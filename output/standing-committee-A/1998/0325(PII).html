The London Metropolitan Archives, formerly the Greater London Record Office, has been run by the Corporation of London since 1960. It performs the important function of looking after the records of the pan-London bodies, the London county council and the Greater London council, as well as miles of court and ecclesiastical records and the records of important London businesses, hospitals and other institutions. That is common cause between the two sides of the Committee.

It is impossible for the London Metropolitan Archives to meet all of London's archive needs, especially at the more local level. It is not resourced to do so, and it would not regard that as being within its remit. Each London borough is statutorily responsible for its own records under section 224 of the Local Government Act 1972. However, local authorities' duties in respect of their records are expressed in very general terms. In effect, the extent of duty is defined by the authority itself. In other words, it is not similar to the power of the Secretary of State in the context of libraries.

National legislation governing local authorities' archives and local studies' collections is contained in the Local Government (Records) Act 1962.1 suspect that my father may have been responsible for that Act, and although it enables all London authorities to make archive provision and create local history collections, it is permissive only. Standards of provision in relation to 1574 access to borough councils, own official records and to material for studies of localities more generally vary considerably between authorities.

Standards of service were summarised in "Towards 2000: the Future of London's Archives", published by the Greater London Archives network in 1993. Of the 33 London local authorities, more than a third have no professional archivist. Some boroughs make no provision for access to the archives created by the local authority itself, much less make provision to take in historic records created by institutions, businesses or individuals in the local area.

Organisations creating historic records of local significance, as well as national bodies responsible for distributing records to repositories, experience considerable difficulties when depositing records in London, because some boroughs remain unable to receive collections of important archive material. There is no duty, as things stand, to take an active role in collecting local material of historical interest and making it available to the country. However, some authorities choose to do so. Camden, Hackney, Hammersmith and Fulham and Westminster local authorities play an active role, and pay tribute to the political colour of those authorities.

Facilities for the study of the locality for the purposes of local business and an understanding of the built environment can enhance and inform public participation in planning and development issues. Some boroughs, which employ professional staff and endeavour to provide a serious service for the public, nevertheless leave those services desperately under-resourced.

Some borough archivists say that they cannot accept the deposit of more material because their small safe storage provision is full. That will prejudice the resources available for future generations. London needs an even standard of provision as a matter of urgency, so that council tax payers in all metropolitan areas can expect the same standards of archive provision from their local council. That does not entail excessive expense, but should meet the standards laid down by the royal commission on historical manuscripts for the storage and custody of records, access to collections and the provision of reasonable opening hours for consulting archive materials. A local authority archive service has little relevance if the public are not given adequate access to the records that it retains.

London local archives are important to retain records of the authority itself, to avoid operational and reputational risk, to facilitate the right of local people to consult council records, to preserve records relating to the activities of communities at local level, to promote records for educational use in local schools, to establish a unique and irreplaceable record of the history and identity of a local community, to create a resource for interpreting the actions of those in local communities, and the physical evidence, including the building, artefacts and structures that they leave behind, and to collect records reflecting the ethnic diversity of the communities that make up each London authority.
