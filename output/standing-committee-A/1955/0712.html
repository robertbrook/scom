The Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. D. Heathcoat Amory)
: I feel that I owe an apology to my colleagues in this Committee for not being present at the beginning of our deliberations and I ask hon. Members opposite to convey my apologies to their colleagues—who I think must all be at Ascot. Mr. Amory
: It is not for the reason alleged by hon. Members opposite, but because I have two functions to perform in two different places at the same time. As Minister of Agriculture one tries to learn how to combine stability with flexibility. I am always trying to push the qualities of adaptability to the limits of possibility, but I try also to combine courtesy to this Committee with the performance of my other duties.

I am sorry that we have not been able to adopt the Amendments which have been put forward by the Opposition, for the reasons given by my hon. Friend the Joint Parliamentary Secretary last Thursday. I am particularly sorry, because I know that hon. Members in all parts of the Committee are convinced that the aims of the Bill are sound. We all want to see the improvements put in hand as soon as possible and we all 52 realise that it is not an easy problem. We are all equally anxious lest practical difficulties of one kind or another may prevent as rapid progress as we should like. I am sorry, therefore, that we find ourselves unable so far to accept the Amendments which have been proposed, but I hope that as we go along we may find ourselves more in agreement.

I want to emphasise two points in relation to these Amendments. The first is that this Bill is something of an experiment. It breaks new ground. We are proposing to put public money into a quite expensive form of improvement for what is very local benefit. We must make sure that in each case the investment of public money is justified—not necessarily justified in the narrow sense of an immediate economic return, but on the broader grounds of the economic and social welfare of the area concerned, I think it is right that we should proceed in this matter cautiously. That, I feel, is what hon. Members opposite are criticising us for doing.

The second point I want to emphasise is that, bearing all that in mind, the Government decided to make available a certain sum—in this case £4 million—from central funds for this purpose. It is easy to say that it ought to be more, or that more could usefully be spent. That may prove to be so. We should all like it to be more, but in this vale of woe I think it is always the job of the Government—a rather unenviable role—in the course of allotting the taxpayers' money, to make a selection from a long list of desirable projects and to do their best to make an insufficient amount of money go round.

We have felt that the limited amount of money which can be allotted in present circumstances to this new type of project can be used most effectively by giving grants towards the cost of improvement of roads. As my hon. Friend pointed out on Thursday, hon. Members opposite are really asking that the whole principle behind the financial contributions made by the Ministry of Transport for the maintenance of roads should be upset. Under this well-recognised and clearly defined procedure the maintenance of unclassified roads is a local responsibility. If what hon. Members opposite are suggesting were to be adopted it would create the most awkward anomalies between such JPEG 53 roads as would be improved under the Bill and other unclassified roads.

We must remember that in many of the areas we are now considering the Government in fact make a contribution to local expenditure through Exchequer Equalisation Grants. I think that is the best way, because the grant is related to the aggregate burden falling on the local authorities. It would be quite impracticable to turn all these improved roads into classified roads, and I am sure that on reflection right hon. and hon. Members will agree.

Where I do sympathise with some of the views which have been expressed is when hon. Members have said that it will be difficult to estimate what the response will be from local authorities until the percentage of grants which are to be made is known. I agree with that view. On Second Reading I said that we wanted to maintain a certain amount of flexibility until we could estimate rather more precisely the likely developments and the kind of schemes which would be put forward. I think that sensible. At the same time, I do not want to create a fog of uncertainty and I am very anxious that during our deliberations in Committee we should give hon. Members all the information we can on how we propose to administer the Bill.

When we discuss the percentage of grants a little later in our deliberations, I think that hon. Members will find that there is really not very much between us. I would also repeat that the quicker sound schemes are put up to us the better we shall be pleased. We do not mind at all how quickly this money is spent. When that is discussed I think it will be found again that there is little difference of sub-stance between us. I hope that in these circumstances hon. Members will not press us on these Amendments because they are really asking us to adopt a principle which would cut at the roots of the whole practice of Government financial assistance to road authorities. I am quite sure that it would be wrong to attempt to do that in this small Bill.
