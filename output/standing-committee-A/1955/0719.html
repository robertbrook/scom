The Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. D. Heathcoat Amory)
: Since our last meeting we have been giving a good deal of thought to this problem to see if there is any way at all in which we can meet the views of those hon. Gentlemen who propose that we should have in the Bill a minimum percentage of grants. I am afraid that the further consideration that we have given to the matter has confirmed our view that that would be a mistake. I shall give the reasons for that conclusion.

First, we can find no single Act of Parliament which provides a precedent for including a minimum percentage rate of grant to a local authority. There are many precedents for the rate to be at the discretion of the Minister, as we are proposing here. There are also precedents for a maximum grant, and for a fixed grant. In our own sphere of agriculture it seems most usual for the rate to be at the discretion of the Minister. In agricultural education, for instance, the rate of grant is at the discretion of the Minister, as it is for land drainage 152 under the Land Drainage Act, 1930. The Agriculture Act, 1947, and the River Boards Act, 1948, also give the Minister that discretion.

Then, under the Transport Act, 1919, the rate is left to the discretion of the Minister. With smallholdings there is a maximum rate of grant, and a fixed rate under the Hill Farming Act. In no case can we find a minimum rate incorporated in an Act. Therefore, it would be an entirely novel principle were we to do it here. I do not say that that would have been an insuperable difficulty—we should certainly have considered it had there seemed to be a balance of advantage in it.

I think that everyone who has spoken in this Committee has agreed that it would be as well to leave the rate flexible. At our last meeting the right hon. Gentleman the Member for Belper (Mr. G. Brown) argued that flexibility was desirable and that is really why we believe that it would be dangerous to include a minimum rate. For instance, we might want to assist a scheme put forward by a local authority which, though sound and desirable in itself, would not contain those agricultural benefits which would justify a high rate of grant.

Such a case might be where a local authority might want to improve a road not only for agricultural but for other reasons—perhaps for the benefit of the tourist industry—to a standard which, for purely agricultural reasons, would be excessive. The cost might be £5,000 per mile and we might feel that, on agricultural grounds, we would not be justified' in contributing more than, say, £1,500 a mile. If a minimum rate were included in the Bill—and I imagine that, to be acceptable to hon. Gentlemen opposite, it would have to be a substantially high rate—we should be precluded from assisting such a scheme. That is an important point. I do not want to keep harping on what I have mentioned several times—that the Bill is really an experiment in a new field—but I am sure that we should be wrong not to give ourselves a bit of room for manoeuvre if we are to meet the various circumstances of the proposals that may be put to us.

One or two hon. Gentlemen expressed the view that a statutory minimum rate would be desirable to enable local authorities to know where they stand. I JPEG 153 do not think that there is a great deal of force in that argument. In the cases which I have quoted where the Minister has discretion, local authorities seem to have the confidence to submit schemes and the work goes forward satisfactorily. Secondly, we have made it plain that we shall welcome consultation with the Ministry at any stage in the proceedings. Unfortunately, we were not able to accept hon. Gentlemen's suggestions for formal consultations in the early stages with the agricultural executive committees, but we do welcome consultation with the Ministry at any stage and we shall consult county committees at a later stage.

Thirdly, there is this point. I have already said, and I repeat, that if the work is to go forward we realise the need for substantial grants to local authorities, particularly for the poorer highway authorities, for these improvements. In fact, I would expect applications for eligible schemes submitted by the poorer authorities—such, for instance, as many of the authorities in Wales—to qualify for the maximum rate of grant.

Doubts in the minds of hon. Gentlemen opposite come back to the question of whether the £4 million will be adequate, and I should like frankly to say how we have worked out our computations. I shall give only four or five figures. The total mileage of roads that we think might be in need of improvement in this category is about 4,500 miles. If all those roads were to be improved in the way we are providing for here then clearly the £4 million would not be enough. We do not believe that applications will be made for anything like the whole of that total mileage.

Looking at the matter realistically we believe that local authorities might apply for between 2,000 and 3,000 miles, which is the figure I mentioned in the Second Reading debate. Assuming that the average grant worked out at about 75 per cent.—that is allowing for unclassified and unadopted roads—and assuming, and we have looked into this again, that the average cost worked out at £2,000 per mile, that £4 million would enable us to make our contribution average 75 per cent. for a total mileage of 2,700 miles. That is the kind of estimate we have made.

154 I would add that if our forecasts are too low, and more sound and eligible schemes are suggested than we think likely, we shall not skimp and cut down our grants in order to "make do." If we did that it would have the effect which we do not want; it would simply mean that schemes would not come forward. We shall go ahead and spend the money as fairly as we can. If the money were spent and some sound and eligible schemes still remained to be dealt with, it would be open to the Minister of the day—and as this scheme covers some years I hesitate to prophesy who he then might be—to say to Parliament "This scheme has been a success. We believe there is more good work that can be done under it and we need more money." That would be the kind of result I should expect to see if this experimental scheme were successful.

One or two hon. Gentlemen have taken me to ask for mentioning on Second Reading that some schemes put forward might be obviously uneconomic. They appeared to think that I should not have said that, but I think that on consideration they will remember that this is primarily an agricultural scheme. It would be easy to think of some schemes which just could not be justified economically; schemes which, however wide—and we ought to consider this on pretty wide grounds—just could not be done because the cost would be prohibitive for the object that would achieve.

In view of what I have said, I hope that the Committee will accept the assurance that we have pondered deeply on this question, and have tried to find some way of meeting the proposals contained in this Amendment. On reconsideration I hope that the whole of the Committee will see the force of the arguments I have tried to put forward, and will agree in this new venture to leave the Minister discretion as to the precise rate of grant he gives for any scheme.
