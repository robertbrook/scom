On 13 December 1990, in Standing Committee F, we were debating the Community Charges (Substitute Setting) Bill. The Minister reminded me of it with his kindly remarks this morning. He said some nattering things about me which embarrassed me in front of my hon. Friends, who did not know that I resembled Father Christmas. In that Committee, we suggested that a breathing space to review the Government's proposals on the poll tax would be opportune. At that time we could have examined jointly how to abolish the poll tax by April 1992. We said then that the Bill was unacceptable, ill-conceived, unworkable and irrelevant to the needs of poll tax payers. I think the same of this Bill as I did of the Bill that became the Community Charges (Substitute Setting) Act 1991; it is the wrong Bill at the wrong time.

We could provide an immediate, sensible solution by amending clause 2. As we shall debate valuation proposals at greater length in clause 5,1 shall curtail my remarks, emphasising only that amendment No. 19 would provide a proper valuation system that takes account of individual property, assesses the worth of that property, takes account of the environment in which it is placed and gives people a 118 basis on which to judge their relevant household payments. That would be much more acceptable than the banding proposals which are included for the sole purpose of avoiding the fair rates solution. There can be no other reason for dreaming up the eight bands and placing households, or dwellings, in a system that permits estate agents to pick out particular sample properties in one locality and then fit the properties of everyone else into the banding system.

I shall not deal with the regional disparities because we shall discuss them later; suffice it to say that no one could have dreamt up a system that would cause such anomalies if he had not been in a desperate hurry and had not wanted to avoid the sensible solution of his political opponents. The proposals will mean that someone could be placed in the same band as someone else in the same street, even though his property is worth a very different amount from the sampled property. It does not take a genius to know that a house next door to a discotheque is worth less than one at the other end of the street overlooking a quiet local park—even though the property may be the same sort of semi-detached house.

Such an anomaly will be repeated up and down Britain and so will cause havoc for the appeals system, which will not be triggered until early 1993. That will mean terrible difficulties for the appeals process and for local authorities dealing with people who feel aggrieved about the band on which their property has been placed. That band could result in their being charged a amount similar to those whose properties are valued £20,000 higher or lower.

Individual valuations would override the problems of banding raised by Tory Members with London constituencies. The problem with the Government's banding system is that in some areas almost all properties will be in the top two or three bands. The 1985 revaluation in Scotland caused enormous problems and there were several appeals, but they have all been dealt with to the satisfaction of the appellants. They can be dealt with satisfactorily under the system that operates in Scotland, and it could continue to operate on that basis.

Research done by, I believe, Glasgow university shows that, on the basis of the banding system, one third of all properties will be just below or just above—£2,000 either way—of each band. It is a mistake to believe that only those put into the band above will appeal. Someone who wishes to sell a house may appeal against a banding that is too low because a prospective purchaser may say, "You are trying to sell this house for £65,000, but it is in the £55,000 band. So why are you trying to do me out of £10,000?" There may very well be appeals both ways. Individual valuation gets rid of some grave anomalies.
