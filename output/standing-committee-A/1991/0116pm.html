Mr. Spearing: We are about to embark upon the first major debate on part II of the Bill which is necessary because of the Government's proposed changes. They would bring into range an entirely different set of considerations that might otherwise be dealt with by a Home Office Bill and a Committee selected for the purpose. I am not a specialist in, nor do I have knowledge of, the issues dealt with in this part of the Bill relating to alcohol taken by people in charge of trains, trams, buses or other public service vehicles that may run on those systems.

However, this may be a good qualification for all of us who do not have the specialist knowledge of my hon. Friend the Member for West Bromwich, East (Mr. Snape) because we are all potential passengers, so are able to represent the interests of our constituents in a more fundamental way than on other parts of the Bill on which we spoke as parliamentarians.

Clause 28 would bring the rules for drink driving by people in charge of public vehicles broadly into line with those pertaining to car drivers. The Minister nods assent. On the face of it, this is a sensible move because, since Barbara Castle introduced breath tests in the late 1960s, there has been at least a legal anomaly between drivers of road vehicles and drivers of tracked vehicles on railways. Col. 252 The Government are proposing a curious inversion of the law whereby historically, the rules for drivers of trains, trams or other public service vehicles was rightly more stringent, if anything, than those that applied in any respect to drivers of private road vehicles.

That proposed inversion of the de jure position is perhaps made acceptable by the rules of employment of such people—especially drivers.

The interesting feature of clause 28 and subsequent clauses is that they may involve people on the permanent way, those involved with signals, those supplying electricity to vehicles or people involved in coupling or uncoupling vehicles and so on. The scope of the clause reaches wider than drivers of road vehicles. It would bring them into line with provisions relating to alcohol in the blood discovered by test and, I presume, to many other complex matters relating to testing, hospitals, the police and so on. This is a complex subject for those with merely a passing knowledge of the issues and has been the focus of much discussion, as no doubt it will continue to be.

The issue that we must decide is whether the proposals are to parallel to a reasonable degree categories of person and quantities of alcohol. Is there to be a scaling down of the quantity of alcohol? We have a range of amendments that would scale down quantity; my amendment would provide that no alcohol should be discernible.

There is a good and logical case for such a provision for drivers and many of the other categories referred to in clause 28. I believe that the public would not find it acceptable if the rules of London Transport or other public transport services allowed bus or coach drivers, who may drive on motorways or around Parliament square, or underground train drivers or guards, to have measurable alcohol in their bodies. I do not believe that anyone would disagree with that. Should the de jure law be brought into line with the de facto rules and made more stringent? I suggest that it should. I suspect that, in the not-too-distant future, a private Member's Bill or legislation introduced by a future Home Secretary will reduce to zero the allowable levels of alcohol of road users. I understand that such a law operates in Sweden and there is an increasing movement in this country—perhaps throughout the world—for a similar law.

We are discussing those who work on railways, tramways, trolleyways and other scientific ways. I am not sure if the proposals will apply to the skippers of boats on navigable waterways. I suppose that they would if such a boat were inside a harbour. From reading clause 27 I am not sure that the Bill will apply to waterways—perhaps we can clarify that issue.

I shall quote from an article in The Independent of 24 December by the medical editor, Celia Hall. It quotes the research of—perhaps appropriately—Scottish reporters. If the report is accurate, we should take note. It states:

"Drivers who have drunk sparingly and would pass the breath test are still more at risk of having accidents, Scottish researchers said yesterday.


After drinking two or three units of alcohol—for the research, a pint-and-a-half of ordinary strengh beer—men's responses to sudden events were 10 to 15 per cent. slower than those who were alcohol-free.


Even after two hours, when blood alcohol levels had almost returned to normal, the slow response rate continued."

The article describes tests that were carried out and quotes the comments of Dr. Richard Hammersley, lecturer with the behavioural sciences group at the university of Glasgow: Image Col. 253

"Basically what we are saying is that people might be OK on an uneventful drive home. But when something unexpected happens then they do less well. In practice they might get away with it or they might not."

Professor Keith Miller, head of the department, is quoted as saying:

"Even quite small doses of alcohol can have marked effects on drivers.


More alarming still is the variation between individuals in their reaction to alcohol. This makes it very difficult to predict the impact of the drug. The idea that there is a 'safe' legal limit is a nonsense."

They are medical opinions and I do not say that hon. Members should accept them. However, the issue could be examined. It is a prima facie case of the zero option, to use a term from a different field of endeavour.

Mr. Snape: I am listening with interest to my hon. Friend's remarks, but will he bear in mind the fact that the rate of alcoholism among doctors is one of the highest in any profession in the United Kingdom? So not all doctors will believe the studies to which he has just referred.

Mr. Spearing: The doctors' profession is not the only one that is cited in the press as being subject to such a risk. I am rather surprised by my hon. Friend's intervention because, whatever rate of alcoholism may occur in certain professions, I should not have thought that people's professional integrity would be affected by what could be called an "occupational risk". Indeed, the reverse might be true. It could apply to politicians and journalists, too.

I understand my hon. Friend's point. I am not saying that the amendment is something that should necessarily go unchallenged. But when there is a danger of something going wrong, we must minimise the risk. Indeed, many years ago someone whom I knew well died as a result of someone else's over-indulgence. That a train driver can jump into his car after coming off duty, having consumed a couple of pints in the social club that may be attached to the depot, and be within the law constitutes an anomaly.

However, the reverse of that might apply. Let us suppose that the company rules state that a train driver must not consume alcohol for a certain number of hours before going on duty. Whatever those X hours might be, he could still be within the company rules and within the law, yet still have measureable quantities of alcohol in his body. If so, passengers might take exception to that, and we must not tolerate such a position. Indeed, I suspect that under the present law, it would be tolerable. Such matters cannot be defined precisely. But surely a rule of no discernible alcohol being consumed would be a rule that is understood and would give passengers and others some assurance and comfort.

Mr. Harry Barnes (Derbyshire, North-East): Will my hon. Friend give way?

Mr. Spearing: Yes, but, as I said, the debate is, in a sense, a mini Second Reading of such a complex topic. I am simply putting forward certain principles in the hope that someone will challenge them. Perhaps my hon. Friend wishes to do that.

Mr. Spearing: My hon. Friend the Member for Derbyshire, North-West (Mr. Barnes) referred to a matter of detail. Despite knowing that such a point might be made, I do not know what the railway rule books state. They might cover various types of food, alcohol and habits. A driver could well be within the company rules if he had consumed on duty and still come within the Bill; I do not know. Perhaps we could be advised of those practical rules. It may take two or three goes to determine what the optimum should be as the Bill passes through both Houses. I think that I had better leave it at that.

I have tried to open up the subject, dealing with the many dimensions that have come to mind; my hon. Friends and Conservative Members may wish to raise others. I do not suggest that I would adhere to that limit with enormous affirmation through thick and thin—it is suggested more by way of exploration. However, I maintain that if there is to be a limit, it would be best set at zero. It would provide confidence, certainty and simplicity to whatever legislation may ensue.

Mr. Roger Moate (Faversham): Amendments Nos. 92, 93 and 94 were tabled in my name. I shall approach the subject in the same spirit as the hon. Member for Newham, South (Mr. Spearing). He was right that it is an exploratory approach to a difficult subject. If we seek sensible rules, the immediate logic would be to adopt the Government's proposal to introduce new limits similar to those in the Road Traffic Acts. On the face of it, that is logical, equitable and I presume acceptable to the majority of people in the industry although I have not received any correspondence. We are entitled to explore whether it goes far enough, but it would be foolish to approach the subject dogmatically. It is an important matter of public policy; I do not believe that it can be resolved by this relatively small Committee. If the principle was to be challenged, the Floor of the House would be a better place to consider it. None the less, this is the right place to explore the matter in detail.

We have before us several proposals. First, is what is proposed in the Bill—that the limit should be 80 mg—comparable to the limit applied to drivers under the Road Traffic Acts. At the other end of the scale is the proposition of the hon. Member for Newham, South for a zero limit. Between those is a proposal made by the hon. Member for Southport (Mr. Fearn), who is not with us this afternoon, for a limit of 20 mg. Lastly is the limit given in the amendments that I tabled which was made at the instigation of the British Railways Board, of 40 mg. The proposed limits are 80 mg, 40 mg, 20 mg and zero.

It is worth our while examining the proposals today, because whatever we decide, we shall return to the subject in future. It is logical to suggest that if we apply such limits to the railways, we should consider whether other public transport, such as public service vehicles, airlines and so on, should be similarly limited. Although we are debating the criminal law, we must remember that those Image Col. 255 organisations have different internal disciplinary codes of conduct; it should not be thought that high limits under the criminal code would mean that public service vehicle drivers should be able to drink happily up to those limits. That is not the case.

I shall deal briefly with the different points. I am sure that none of us will mistake brevity for a lack of awareness that the subject is important to all in the industry and to the general public. The hon. Member for Newham, South suggested a zero limit. Most of us will have received representations from the British Medical Association and I believe that we have all had a letter from the Parliamentary Advisory Council for Transport Safety—PACTS. It is worth commenting on what they say with regard to the hon. Gentleman's suggestion of zero.

The BMA says that it

"would like to see the level reduced to zero as a clear and unequivocal statement that train drivers should not drink and drive. Lowering the limit to 20mg/100ml should be a step towards a zero limit."

PACTS makes the sensible point that 20 mg, which is the proposition of the hon. Member for Southport, is generally considered to be the lowest practicable limit as it makes allowance for small amounts of alcohol in some foodstuffs and medicines and for margins of instrumentation error. It states:

"It would mean that people could easily be over the limit from drinking very small amounts of alcohol and would support the current no drinking and driving message."

That seems a powerful argument against the zero figure proposed by the hon. Member for Newham, South. In other words, the difference between 20 and zero is one that is almost technical; certainly, both are extremely low limits.

British Rail states that it welcomes the principle of the proposed change in the law, but it is concerned that the requirements of safety on railways will not fully be met unless lower limits are prescribed for railway staff. I have a copy of an article from the British Medical Journal which supports that view. It states:

"A train driver's ability to drive is significantly impaired if he has consumed alcohol even where the concentration of alcohol in his blood is less than the Road Traffic Act limits."

One point which is made continually bears examination, although it is a difficult argument. It is that the consequences of accidents caused by train drivers whose ability is impaired by alcohol can be far worse than those caused by drivers of motor vehicles. That is an important point because it is the essence of the case for lower limits for train drivers than for car drivers.

I imagine that there will be strong arguments against that view because the horrendous results of an accident caused by a drunken driver on the road can be as dramatic as those caused by a train driver. Of course, it is self-evident that a train driver is responsible for thousands of passengers, but there is equal responsibility on all drivers of all vehicles. That is the nub of the case made by the British Medical Journal and British Rail.

My amendments would halve the blood alcohol limits. They recognise that the consequences of accidents caused by railway staff are potentially even greater than those caused by drivers of motor vehicles. Therefore, railway staff should be under a particularly stringent responsibility not to drink while at work or shortly before coming on duty. That is a simple proposition, but I well understand Col. 256 that it requires a great deal of detailed examination. I believe that Parliament will return to the matter time and again.

Mr. Spearing: Does the hon. Gentleman have the answer to questions asked by two of my hon. Friends about the time limits within British Rail's current rules for either drivers or others? We should make a difference of category in that respect. Perhaps we can return to the matter later in the debate.

Mr. Moate: I regret that I do not have that answer. I wish that I had it because the relevant internal rules of conduct are important in the context of the debate.

The Minister for Shipping (Mr. Patrick McLoughlin): To help the debate, the Department's medical advice is that civil aviation people should not drink for eight hours before reporting to work to avoid any alcohol in their bodies. British Rail is considering what advice to give. I hope that that answers the question and helps my hon. Friend.

Mr. Moate: That is most helpful and it re-emphasises that we are not talking about standard practice or the rules for drivers. One assumes that if there are disciplinary rules or rules of employment, they will broadly be accepted by both sides of the industry. They could be imposed against the will of the employees, but it is most likely that they would gain general acceptance and approval. We are talking about the application of the criminal law to those circumstances. We have a choice: we can assume that the consequences of a railway accident are so much greater that Parliament should impose tighter rules, or that rules should be the same for all drivers of all vehicles, in which case we would accept the proposition used by my hon. Friend the Minister.

As I have said, we would be foolish to be dogmatic. We could set an example in Committee and could take steps to enforce the rule. However, we should hear the views of my hon. Friend the Minister and, indeed, the hon. Member for West Bromwich, East (Mr. Snape) for whose knowledge and experience of railways—not of drinking—I have considerable respect. Those are my arguments, but we should listen carefully to what other hon. Members have to say.

Mr. Andrew F. Bennett (Denton and Reddish): I support my hon. Friend the Member for Newham, South (Mr. Spearing). I think that the Government's position is illogical. On Second Reading, the Minister more or less said that people should not drive trains or other public service vehicles if they have had a drink. However, he also seemed to say that it would be difficult to maintain fairness in court if there were an accident between the driver of a private car who is just below the legal limit and the driver of one of the trams that will run through our cities who has a considerably lower amount of alcohol but who is above a zero limit. That is not a good comparison, because there is a clear distinction between drivers of private vehicles and drivers of public vehicles.

Mr. Patrick McLoughlin: I shall make a fairly lengthy speech later. I might have used the analogy to which the hon. Mr. Andrew F. Bennett: I shall deal first with public and private vehicles. In the majority of cases, car passengers can take cognisance of how capable drivers are of driving. Although it can be socially embarrassing, steps can be taken to persuade drivers to drive sensibly if passengers do not approve of their driving. On one-man-operated buses, there is a good chance that passengers will, at least, speak to drivers before they board. Mr. Bennett: I do not want to be drawn too wide of the subject. My point was simply that I was not convinced by the Minister's contention on Second Reading that there was overriding logic behind the proposal to set the limit at levels that currently obtain for car drivers. I believe that that limit is unsatisfactory and should be lowered.

The hon. Member for Faversham (Mr. Moate) argued persuasively that the level should be reduced by half. My difficulty with that argument stems from my experience as a car driver. When I go to a place that serves alcohol and choose a soft drink because I am driving, I am inundated with advice from other people that I can drink this, that and the other and still remain below the limit.

Some of that advice is good, but most of it is extremely dodgy. I am also aware that each individual reacts differently to the amount of alcohol consumed and that the speed at which its effects can be felt varies from person to person. Some cynics maintain that a person who practises often enough has greater tolerance than someone who does not. However, that is the difficulty. Even if the limit is set at 20 mg—a very small amount of alcohol—there will still be many stories about how it is perfectly all right according to the law to drink a certain amount. The inevitable result is that some people will consume more than that amount. That is the difficulty with any limit other than zero.

However, I recognise that there is a difficulty even with the zero limit. Alcohol lingers in the bloodstream. As the Minister said, the civil aviation recommendation is that no alcohol should be consumed by a pilot in the eight hours before a flight. But it is possible to consume food or beverages without knowing that they contain alcohol, only to find later that small traces of alcohol are present in the bloodstream. Therefore, I would set the limt at zero, but allow people to claim in defence that they registered less than 20 mg or 15 mg, provided that they could demonstrate that they had had nothing to drink in the previous four or five hours. If we want to make transport systems safe for the public, we must insist that people cannot work and drink.

In the past, some people have drunk at work and, sadly, on occasions, it has resulted in fatalities—sometimes, of the workmen themselves. We must protect the public and, sometimes, protect people from themselves. I urge the Government to set a much lower limit. The only logical limit, which is easy to enforce, is the zero limit, although I would permit the legal defence that a person did not wittingly have alcohol in his or her bloodstream.

Mr. Snape: This is an important subject. We have already heard three thoughtful speeches. We are aware of our limitations as a Committee and as a legislative body arbitrarily to set alcohol limits. The hon. Member for Denton and Reddish (Mr. Bennett) spoke of the need to lower the drinking limit for motorists. As I said to my hon. Friend the Member for Denton and Reddish, it would be anomalous to set different limits for the reasons that I outlined on Second Reading and in Committee.

I agree with the hon. Members who suggested that the ideal limit for people who drive trains, operate signalling centres or carry out maintenance work and other functions covered by the Bill is zero. But advocating and legislating are two different things. As my hon. Friend the Member for Denton and Reddish mentioned, enforcing that zero limit would be fraught with difficulty. I understand the logic behind his suggestion of a zero limit and a defence Image Col. 259 that alcohol was unwittingly consumed. But it would certainly cause the legal profession some joy if we were to enshrine that principle in statute. We have already seen how solicitors and barristers, who have been clever enough to spot loopholes in drink-driving legislation—some real, some imaginary, but all impressive to magistrates—have had their clients cases dismissed yet newspaper reports of the evidence seemed to show that their clients were palpably guilty.

We must keep alcohol and the railway industry in perspective. In the past 20 years, there have been three accidents where the subsequent inquiry revealed overindulgence in drink as factors in two of them and drugs in the other. The driver in the Cannon street accident of 1990 was found to have the product of some illegal substance in his blood two days later. There was an accident at Morpeth in 1984. The driver had consumed some alcohol and took a curve restricted to 40 mph at a much faster speed and drove the train and the first few coaches down an embankment and up to the front door of someone's bungalow. A more serious accident occurred about 20 years ago at Eltham Well hall. The driver's conduct was disgraceful: he had driven people on an excursion to the seaside and spend the day drinking. He wrecked his train and killed himself and several passengers. Some incidents are so unusual that they merit comment. The railway industry is operated mostly by people who know the dangers of excess alcohol consumption and are aware of its consequences for themselves and the people for whom they are responsible.

The hon. Member for Faversham (Mr. Moate) said that he did not know the unions' view on such matters. I took the trouble to find out. The letter suggests other groups of employees that could be covered by the proposed legislation. Some of those suggestions, such as including management, and maintenance and repair staff in the provisions, are incorporated in the Bill. All the grades mentioned in the letter are covered by the Bill. The unions are anxious that any problem is properly tackled and that the provisions for working practices are enshrined in law.

I return to the vexed questions of enforcement and whether a zero limit is acceptable. I remind my hon. Friends that not only drivers and train crews but maintenance staff and signalmen are covered by the Bill.

I said to your co-Chairman this morning, Mr. Lamond, that I would avoid railway reminiscences. There is nothing worse than Members of Parliament who tell stories about life before they were elected to this place. Most of our constituents do not believe that we had any life before we came here. However, I hope that I might be forgiven for transgresing just once more.

During my undistinguished railway career, I was a signalman and a guard and my last job, in 1973–74, was in the train crew control office at Preston. I think that, given the nature of those three jobs, I have fallen out with more engine drivers than any other member of the Committee. I have probably fallen out with more engine drivers than Derek Fullick, the ASLEF general secretary.

By the nature of the railway industry, much of the track maintenance work is done on Saturday nights and Sundays, as we all know when we travel by train on Sundays. It has been customary for many years for the track maintenance gang—the plate layers, as they are still known, whatever their official title is—to meet in the pub nearest the site before heading to the site, usually after the passage of the last train on that track. Those of my hon. Image Col. 261 Friends who do not imbibe alcohol and regard it as a demon to be exorcised from society—I know that some of them north of the border feel strongly on this issue—might regard that practice as reprehensible. "done everything that was reasonably practicable"?

That is a classical debate for lawyers. I do not claim such eminent distinction and shall therefore put the case briefly to the Committee and hope that the Minister will accept my argument.

The amendments are proposed on behalf of the British Railways Board and London Underground. The operator has a defence under clause 29(3) if he has "exercised all due diligence" to prevent the commission on the transport system of any offence under clause 28. The amendments would alter the defence available to the operator of the transport system or the employer. Instead of having to show that he "exercised all due diligence", he would have to show that he had

"done everything that was reasonably practicable"

to avoid the commission of the offence. The defence of "exercising all due diligence" is to be found in earlier legislation such as the Offices, Shops and Railway Premises Act 1963 and the Trade Descriptions Act 1968.

Although there are a number of reported court cases on what is meant by this defence, no consistent meaning has been given. The interpretation is generally left to the magistrates or other courts considering the case. It has sometimes been held that "due diligence" amounts to the same thing as doing

"everything that was reasonably practicable"

but in other cases a lower or higher standard has been imported. On the other hand, the expression to do

"everything that was reasonably practicable"

which appears in the more recent Health and Safety at Work, etc. Act 1974 has a meaning that is well understood. The transport operator or employer would be required to set up a reasonable system to prevent offences being committed under clause 28. The test of a reasonable system would be one where the risk of commission of offences was properly balanced against the cost of establishing the system.

I emphasise that the British Rail Board and London Underground are in no way seeking to lower the standard of care which they would be required to exercise, but it is Col. 270 considered that the proposed amendment would clarify what their duty would be under the new legislation. clause Clause 46

Amendment proposed, No. 97, in page 23, line 44, leave out 'trolley vehicle system'.—[Mr. McLoughlin.]

Mr. Spearing: Why?

Mr. McLoughlin: Amendment No. 97 would remove trolley vehicles from the ambit of the new power of the Secretary of State to give directions omitting speeds and loads on certain transport systems. We are now of the view that as a trolley vehicle will travel on ordinary roads, the speed limit imposed by road traffic legislation will be adequate. Weight limits may already be governed by the drawing up and use of regulations. Her Majesty's railway inspectorate therefore agrees that there is no need for clause 46 to apply to trolley vehicle systems.
