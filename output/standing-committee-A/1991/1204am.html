Mr. Harry Cohen
(Leyton)
: Good morning, Sir Michael, and good morning to the whole Committee.

Members of the Committee will recall that when I was speaking at just before 2 o'clock this morning, I was referring to Labour's approach to local and central Government. I dealt with the subject extensively in my speech, as did my hon. Friend the Member for Dagenham (Mr. Gould), who was often cross-examined by Conservative Members. Our approach mainly comprises restoring the partnership between local government and central Government and restoring trust in local government by using circulars and by calling in local authorities to discuss central strategy and ways in which the partnership between local and central Government can best be forged and put into effect. Overall economic control will be maintained by central Government control of the grant to local authorities. I hope that that will mean that local government will receive more grant under a Labour Government than it has received under a Conservative Government, and that the grant will be better distributed to areas in need.

The other aspect of our approach is annual local elections, which will make councils much more accountable than they are now, or have been over the past 12 years—even though the Government claim that accountability has been one of their objectives. When the Government introduced the poll tax, for example, the opposite of accountability resulted.

Capping does not protect poll tax payers, and it will not protect council tax payers, from high bills. Poll tax payers have not been protected from high bills overall as some examples will show. The Government estimated that the average poll tax would be £178, but that figure was subsequently revised to £278, and later for 1990–91 to £357—an increase of well over 100 per cent. Some authorities had amazingly high poll tax bills: Windsor and Maidenhead and Elmbridge set bills of £449, and Epsom and Ewell bills of £452. Strangely, none of those authorities was capped, even though the Government claim that capping is designed to protect local poll tax payers from high bills. When Conservative-controlled areas such as Windsor and Maidenhead, Elmbridge, and Epsom and Ewell set high bills, they were not capped. Boroughs such as Calderdale, where the poll tax bill was only £296, Barnsley, where it was £329, and Doncaster and Rotherham, where it was £334, were all capped and labelled as spendthrift, yet their bills were much lower. The excuse that capping was introduced to curb high bills does not stand up.

The Government argue that capping is essential to protect council tax payers. We should examine the reasons for high poll tax bills because the causes of high bills will continue under the council tax. There are five main reasons why poll tax bills have been high. The Government assumed a 100 per cent, collection rate, yet even the Prime Minister has now said that the tax is almost uncollectable, and certainly not 100 per cent, collectable. The safety net arrangements introduced by the Government added £75 to some people's bills. In my borough, every poll tax payer in Waltham Forest paid an extra £42—in effect to Wandsworth, which received a subsidy of over £100 per JPEG 809 bill in the same year. The Government considerably underestimated the amount that local authorities would need to spend in 1990–91: the difference between the spending assumptions made in the RSG report and the local authorities' existing spending plans was so great that it resulted in high bills, as the Chartered Institute of Public Finance and Accountancy confirmed. All those factors will be carried forward into the council tax.

We had a big argument in Committee yesterday about standard spending assessments and my hon. Friend the Member for Dagenham gave a few examples of SSS. Basildon is a classic example, where the SSA included only a fraction of its actual debt charges. That results in high bills.

The capping powers are fundamentally anti-democratic and anti-local democracy. They give the Secretary of State enormous, centralised powers. Indeed, in June this year, the Association of District Councils described the powers as "shockingly centralist". The Secretary of State has taken all the power unto himself. The criterior for capping is that if the Secretary of State considers that an authority's budget is excessive or represents an excessive increase on the previous year's he can cap it. The situation will be the same as it was with the poll tax, whereby an authority levying a lower council tax may be capped and one levying a higher tax will not be, just because that is what the Secretary of State considers should happen. There is an appeal process, but, again, that is just an JPEG 811 appeal to the Secretary of State. So, he really has taken all the power—dictatorial power—unto himself. No wonder the ADC described it as "shockingly centralist".

In a letter to the Conservative party newspaper "Newsline" in March 1984, Rosalie Mondiot, then leader of South Oxfordshire district council said:

"What we are concerned about is that this ill thought out bill will … constitutionally damage the recognised right of local authorities to levy their own rates and to be accountable in a local democratically elected manner".

It was recognised by Conservative councillors, when capping was first mooted by the Conservative party that it is against local accountability and against democracy. It is a gross interference by the Secretary of State in the freedom of local authorities.

In electoral terms, it does not seem to make a jot of difference. Most of the councillors on capped authorities are re-elected; their election prospects are not damaged by capping. Generally, it is the people's perception of whether a council is doing a good job—and, perhaps, whether they hate the Government—not the capping itself that decides elections. The damage done by the Government in such circumstances is not borne out in electoral terms. We should return to local accountability instead of bludgeoning it. Let the people see that their local authorities are responsible for services and for their cost. Let them decide what level of bill they want against what level of service. That was once the tradition, but over the past 15 or 16 years, or even longer, Governments have been hostile to local government. It is time to move back to that partnership to which I referred earlier.

Anyone who reads Hansard will see that Conservative Members cannot conceive of local and central Government working in partnership. Local councils have to be nailed down. That has not worked, so the Government are extending those undemocratic capping powers to more local authorities, usually resulting in services being cut. That has not worked. It is against local democracy. It is time that it was abandoned and Labour's alternative was adopted in its stead.
