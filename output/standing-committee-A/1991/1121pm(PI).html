Mr. Ian McCartney (Makerfield): At 1 pm I was on my feet expressing anxiety about the procedures involved in joint and several liability. I was making a point about public utilities, to which I shall return. First, to put the discussion about the amendments in context I should like to read a press cutting from a local newspaper that I retrieved from the Library in the interval. Under the heading "Husband Jailed for Wife's Poll Tax Protest", the article states:

"An unemployed business executive"—

there are a lot of them around these days—

"yesterday became the first man in Britain to be jailed for not paying his wife's poll tax."

The article describes how John Rushton, aged 60, agreed to pay up on his own behalf, but was refused permission to meet his wife's contribution. It continues:

"Mr. Rushton, a former sales and marketing manager of Fen Drayton, Cambridgeshire, had paid his own £330 community charge, but Mrs. Rushton objects to the tax and threatened to leave her husband if, as head of the household, he paid for her.


The magistrates at Cambridge jailed him for 14 days."

Mr. Rushton was eventually released when his friends clubbed together and paid his wife's poll tax.

The debate is about what can happen, and the circumstances of that case illustrate clearly what can happen to innocent victims who are caught up in this legal minefield. What was meant originally to be a personal tax became a family tax. As a result people who pay their own poll tax bills find themselves criminalised and put in gaol because of a protest made by their spouse.

That case was typical of those that provoked the Child Poverty Action Group to issue in June this year a document that deals with the Government's Col. 292 proposals for the council tax. The document makes some general comments, but is really directed at the proposals for accountability and enforcement. The organisation was worried enough to issue a statement, and it will be worthwhile to make it clear to the Committee what the Child Poverty Action Group said about the provisions. The CPAG is one of several organisations that is recognised by the Government as a group which represents people on low incomes, people who have problems under the benefit system and those who need general advice. It also issues guidance to people on how to reach a satisfactory settlement to get themselves out of debt.

In addition, the CPAG campaigns on the effects of the Government's policies on the poor in Britain. When introducing proposals that can affect the daily lives of those on poor incomes, it is important that the CPAG's views are taken into account. Fran Bennett, a representative of the organisation, said:

"we are concerned that at one stage the Secretary of State for the Environment appeared to be considering transferring 'joint and several liability' from the poll tax to the council tax. The use of the term 'liable adult', and the occasional implication that discounts are personal to the relevant individual rather than applying to the head of household, lend some weight to this concern."

In June this year, the Child Poverty Action Group was already voicing its major worry about the Government even considering transferring that provision from the poll tax legislation to the council tax proposals. Fran Bennett continued:

"Joint and several liability currently applies under the poll tax to married and cohabiting couples. As the council tax is a household based tax, it would apply beyond the benefit unit of the couple to other household members. The practical difficulties of operating such a rule—especially if combined with annual (rather than weekly or monthly) liability—would be enormous. And the unit for rebate purposes (the couple) would be different from the unit for joint and several liability (the household). We would urge the Government not to extend joint and several liability, and hope that it is not being seriously considered as an option."

That recommendation was actually underlined by the Child Poverty Action Group. Yet six months later, not only has that advice been ignored, but the group's fear has been realised and the provision has been transferred to the Bill.

We see all around us the advice that was given to the Government. It was that they should learn a lesson from the implementation of the poll tax. The Bill should not be about the transfer of huge parts of former legislation into the council tax proposals. The Government should have listened to administrators of the poll tax and represenatives of those who have been affected by it. Both groups of people advised that the measure should not be in the legislation.

In October, the Institute of Revenues, Rating and Valuation held a seminar to consider the way in which poll tax debts had created a legal and administrative minefield for local government officials who operated the collection arrangements. The seminar did not concentrate only on how the problem of debt collections had arisen. It sought the most effective ways to achieve recovery. It wanted to ensure that the recovery arrangements took into account the civil liberties of those concerned and their circumstances in respect of debts accrued because of the poll tax legislation.

Image Col. 293 The report of the seminar contained a section that dealt precisely with the problems of joint and several liability. Therefore, as late as last month, professional organisations flagged up the severe difficulties that the poll tax legislation was causing them, their members and individuals who were trapped in that legal mechanism. The report of the IRRV seminar states:

"Social security defined a couple. Questions that need to be asked included: Where are the partners residing? Do the couple share a household? What are the living arrangements? Is the relationship stable and how long has it existed?


A married couple must be living together. A man and woman living together might not be living as husband and wife. Similar commonsense questions would be applied to separation."

So, right at the outset such intrusive, detailed questions had to be asked. They might not have been formally required by the Local Government Finance Act 1988, but the professionals say that in practice those questions had to be asked, in order to determine joint and several liabilities. The report continues:

"Landlords, managers and co-owners may also be jointly and severally liable, but not brothers and sisters or individuals living separately in the same property.


Enforcing joint and several liability was difficult with many grey areas. How do you prove people are living together? … It is virtually impossible. If a husband and wife say they are living totally separate lives in one house, you can't prove it"—

That is true for the poll tax arrangements and will be for the council tax arrangements; it is a direct transfer.

"Searching for proof by other means could be successful. Names on the electoral register were unhelpful."

What does the Minister suggest: that we maintain, in some form, or other, officially or unofficially, the poll tax registration lists? Or, will we have, unofficially, to start preparing other forms of registration to ensure that local authority officers will be able to serve liability orders on someone in respect of his or her partner or alleged former partner?

I could go on and on about issues that were raised at the seminar, but I think that it is clear from the increasing concern—clamour, even—of professional organisations that the proposals should be radically amended.

I want to ask the Minister a question on a matter that I raised unofficially with him before our proceedings began. We agreed that, because of the nature of the question, I would ask it officially. It is certainly an interesting matter that needs clarifying. I understand that, under the poll tax legislation, if someone under 18 were married or cohabiting and a partner over 18 left the relationship or the property, the younger person would not be liable to pay the poll tax, because it could not be levied on a minor. Would that apply under the council tax? I think that Members of the Committee would like to know the circumstances in which 16-year-olds would become Col. 294 liable for the tax, because the Bill clearly states that minors would not be liable to pay it.
