Clause 2
THE GENERAL FUNCTIONS OF THE NEW BOARDS

Amendment made: No. 1, in page 5, line 16, leave out "new Board's functions" and insert

"functions mentioned in whichever of subsections (1) to (4) above has effect in relation to that new Board".—[ Mr. Renton.]

Question proposed, that the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

The Minister for the Arts (Mr. Tim Renton)
: We have so far made good progress on this important Bill. However, for the pleasure of the Committee I should say a few words about clause 2.

As members of this well informed Committee will know, the Bill has been eagerly awaited by the national gallery, the Tate gallery, the national portrait gallery and the Wallace collection for many years. I am hopeful that, after the considerate treatment given the Bill on Second Reading, we shall continue to make good progress this morning.

Those four galleries are governed by a range of legislation, Treasury minutes and deeds. The bill will supersede that governing legislation and replace it with explicit legislation that is modelled on the National Heritage Act 1983. It will establish new corporate boards of trustees along the lines of the Victoria and Albert and the science museums. The Bill tidies up a good deal of convention, and the interpretation of previous Acts and instruments that deal with the duties, responsibilities, powers and proceedings of the trustees.

The incorporation of the four boards of trustees will also ensure that the trustees of the four galleries are no longer personally liable when entering into contracts for the supply of goods and services, and also employment. The trustees' liability will be put on the same footing as that for other national museums and galleries. As I said, not only the trustees but the directors and staff of those four galleries who 4 work so hard and well welcome the Bill and hope to see it enacted.

The Government want to provide the galleries with explicit legislation setting out their general functions. The subsections in clause 2 dealing with individual boards have therefore been framed with particular reference to the activities of each gallery. Clause 2 specifies that the general functions of each new board are to maintain, exhibit and grant access to its collection and to promote public understanding and enjoyment of the art with which a collection is associated.

The boards are also empowered to provide education, enter into contracts and acquire and dispose of land and other property. As land is a public asset, the power to acquire and dispose of it is subject to Government consent. The clause also enables the new boards to do what is necessary in pursuit of their functions. The reason is simply that the Government want them to have the requisite powers to manage their collections most effectively. Thus, the clause enables them to charge for admission to their permanent collections and special exhibitions if they so wish and to charge for any goods or services provided by them.

In addition, the Government may decide from time to time that certain Government functions are best carried out by the galleries because of their specialist knowledge. Subsection 2(7) therefore provides that a Minister may direct a new board to carry out functions exerciseable by him.

The clause also provides new boards with the power to provide facilities for others and thus to generate revenue. In that context I shall say another brief word about charging for admission. It is Government policy that national museums and galleries should have the power to charge for admission if they so wish. However, I stress that there is no obligation on them to charge. The decision to charge is a matter for the board of trustees responsible for each institution.

I remind the Committee that the power to charge is not new in respect of these four galleries. As unincorporated trustees, they are already empowered to charge for admission by common law. The Bill merely sustains those powers for the new incorporated boards. The point is sometimes made—not least by Opposition Members—that the Government are trying to force the galleries into making admission charges to supplement the inadequate funding which they receive. In answer to that, the Government's record shows that they are committed to maintaining their support for museums and galleries while encouraging them to develop greater independence and self-reliance.

Revenue raised from admission charges is kept by the national museums and galleries and is then used to improve facilities for visitors—for example, better catering facilities, access for the disabled, signposting and so on. In the financial year 1990–91 the national museums raised more than £7 million from admission charges, and £48 million from all types of self-engendered income in the year just ending. That £48 million compares with £28 million three years ago, and it represents 21 per cent of museums' total JPEG 5 income. That is an important addition to the total revenue of the museums and galleries.

The clause provides an explicit statement of the new boards' functions. It gives them the requisite powers to carry out those functions, and I recommend it to the Committee.

In broad terms, we support the powers given in the clause, but simply stating those powers covers only half the problem. The powers and duties given to national museums—to care for, preserve, and add to their collections, exhibitions, study and research, and to promote understanding—are admirable and essential to fulfil what society expects from these four great national collections. Moreover, if the Government are to fulfil their obligations, they must provide the means to ensure that the buildings are in good order; that they are adequately staffed with high quality people; and that funding and grant aid are substantial enough to allow the duties mentioned in the Bill to be carried out. Over all those areas, however, there are distinct question marks. The ambition embodied in the Bill is admirable, but it is doubtful whether the four collections will have adequate means to achieve it.

The Minister has been told many times that all four collections have very substantial fabric maintenance costs. The national gallery estimates that £20 million needs to be spent on buildings. The Tate gallery's estimate is £27 million. The Minister's ears must be continually ringing with the sound of people telling him that there are holes in the roof of the Tate gallery. It was built on foundations of marshy ground at Millbank, so it is crumbling. Its wiring is also deficient. These problems have been identified for the benefit of the Minister—or his predecessor—by the Public Accounts Committee report and the Museums and Galleries Commission report of 1988, yet the Government have done nothing about them.

So Opposition Members welcome the duties placed on museums by the Bill. There will come a point when the Government will have to wake up to that. The two reports to which I referred highlighted problems in conservation, cataloguing and security. It is about time the Government recognised and dealt with those problems. Unless they do, all the fine words in the clause will be meaningless.

Before we allow this clause to stand part of the Bill, we must have some assurances from the Minister that there is sufficient will on the part of the 6 Government to provide the means to fulfil the aspirations that the clause embodies. Earlier, in the 1980s, the Government's view was forthrightly put by Lord Gowrie—that museums and galleries should stand on their own two feet in the market. Is that still the Government's attitude? The present Minister has never made a definitive statement about museums. Does he believe that market forces should prevail? In his opening remarks, the Minister said that he was enthusiastic about income generation. Will that be the central thrust for the last few weeks of the Government's custody of museums; or is the increased funding of the previous year indicative of a slight change of policy?

The clause contains two main powers which the Committee should spend some time examining: the power to charge for admissions, and to raise income through other means. We deliberately chose not to table an amendment on the power of charging for admissions. The Committee and country know the strong differences between the two parties on this issue, so there is no need to rehearse them now. Because Opposition Members generally wish the Bill well in principle, we shall not obstruct its progress. It would not be for the greater edification of the Committee or the public to go over all the arguments again.

It is important, however, for Opposition Members to put on record the fact that museums have been pressured into charging admission fees, and that that has not been to the benefit either of museums or of the public. It has rather been simply a response to inadequate funding.

I do not think that the Minister has yet come across a director of a national museum who, having introduced charging, would say that he had wanted to, irrespective of levels of funding. All directors of national museums want the maximum number of people to enjoy, understand and be excited by their collections. There is no doubt that the introduction of charges reduced admissions. No one is saying that the initial level to which admissions dropped—an 80 per cent fall in the case of the national museum of Wales—will be constant. Opposition Members, however, believe strongly that charging for admissions is not a good thing. The Minister may say that there is no such obligation on the four museums, three of which, we are glad to say—the Tate, the national gallery and the national portrait gallery—have resisted the temptation to charge, but that is not good enough. If the Government continue to underfund national museums, as they do now, it will be difficult for trustees to carry on resisting the pressures to charge. They will have no alternative.

JPEG 7 It is ironic that charging admissions, which is intended to generate income, generates income on the doors, but depresses income in other areas of a museum's activities. The Government have not adequately reflected on that. They have pointed to an increase in income generation in other areas, but the Minister knows perfectly well that evidence provided to the Select Committee a couple of years ago by Neil MacGregor of the national gallery suggests otherwise. He made it clear that if his trustees required him to charge for admission to the national gallery, income generated by the restaurant, cafeteria and shop would go down. So charging is a self-defeating mechanism to some extent, as decreasing the number of people entering the gallery by admission charges will decrease income elsewhere—in the very areas covered in the clause: sales of books, videos, reproductions and so on. As I said, the Government have never responded to the Select Committee report. They have an opportunity this morning to do so.

Clause 2(9) requires further scrutiny in relation to other possibilities for income generation. The Committee should consider what is happening in this respect in museums and galleries. Because the Government are underfunding them, museums have been required to consider alternative sources of income—for example, letting their premises.

Different museums have a great variety of policies and enthusiasms, but generally a creeping policy is developing. I suspect that the public, and perhaps even the Minister, are not fully aware of it. I should like to draw the Minister's attention to the natural history museum, for example. Last year it earned £419,000 from letting the space in the museum for parties, receptions and the like. The Minister might say that that is a very good thing; but is it furthering the aims of the clause and of the national museums to use museum space for private functions such as 40th birthday parties, 21st birthday parties, bar mitzvahs and other such events? Does the Minister believe that that is making good use, in a way compatible with the aims and objectives of the clause, of the natural history museum's space—not least when we recall that smoking is allowed at private functions? Smoking is certainly not desirable in museums.

It is interesting that other national museums have a different policy. For example, the Tate lets for private functions only to corporate members. It has a corporate membership scheme which is run constructively to bring in corporate sponsorship; space is let only to members. That ties in more closely the commercial exploitation of the space in the evenings with the aims and objectives of the gallery. Lettings by other museums—for example the natural history museum and the science museum, which had 148 lettings last year, 88 of which were fee-paying—do not further the aims of the museums.

It is important for the Committee and the House to recognise what is happening in the interests of income generation. There is a danger that the Government are forcing museums to let their space to anyone, when they should be concentrating their 8 resources on fulfilling the duties and responsibilities of promoting their collections.

The Minister may say that there is no conflict and that income should be generated in any way possible, but there is no doubt that staff time is taken up in administering the lettings, and clearing up afterwards. Resources that are provided to fulfil the obligations and duties of the museums are being devoted to clearing up after 40th birthday parties and bar mitzvahs. If that is the Government's policy, they should make it clear.

Perhaps there is way of resolving and balancing the issue. Thus far, different museums have evolved different policies. If the Government wish them to pursue this line, is it not an ideal subject for a ministerial circular offering guidelines to the museums, to augment and explain the powers in the clause: "You have the power to generate income and to let your space, but that should be done only in a way that fulfils the obligations of the museum; that does not mean private parties where there is smoking and it does not mean any form of letting."?

The Minister may say in reply that there is a case for a ministerial circular or guidelines to the museums amplifying the clause and limiting the types of income generation from sponsorship and evening events. We would welcome that, because it would represent a constructive use of a ministerial circular.
