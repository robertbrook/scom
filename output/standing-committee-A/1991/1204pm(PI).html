Mr. Paul Murphy (Torfaen): To refresh the Committee's memory, I shall begin by repeating one or two points that I made this morning. Under the Local Government Finance Act 1988, an authority must notify the Secretary of State within seven days of its decisions about the demand on its collection fund. That applies also to substitute decisions. However, the Act provides that an authority, having made its section 95 calculations, can reset them. Any substitute calculations cannot be set at a higher level. There may be circumstances in which an authority may reconsider its section 95 calculation, before the level of the council tax is set, and reduce its budget. The Bill proposes that the Secretary of State would ignore that decision for the purposes of designation and treat the authority as if it had not reset its budget. For example, an authority might reset its budget at a level that the Government would regard as excessive but which would not attract designation due to the de minimis provision.

The principle of amendment No. 301 has been discussed several times in the past couple of days. Amendment No. 301 would remove the provision allowing the Secretary of State to include parish or community council spending with district council spending for the purpose of capping. Community charge legislation excludes that possibility. The Bill also excludes parish expenditure, but subsection (6) would allow the Secretary of State, by order, to count parish spending with district spending if he so chooses. Parish or community councils in Wales are independent, legally constituted bodies, able to issue precepts on the billing authority. The billing authority cannot control the level of precept, nor can it influence the parish's spending level.

Statistics on the 1991 community charge from the Chartered Institute of Public Finance and Accountancy show several instances in which total parish spending formed a significant proportion of total district expenditure. For example, parish spending in Mid Bedfordshire district council was 19·5 per cent, of district expenditure, and in Huntingdonshire it was 31·3 per cent. The Government must understand that, although the national consequences may be minimal, the local impact Col. 842 may be significant in a few cases. It is illogical to propose capping criteria in such circumstances. If the Government wish to control parish spending, specific criteria should be introduced for parish councils.

The objections to this part of the Bill have already been discussed. The Minister said that these clumsy and cumbersome measures had been proposed chiefly because deals would be struck between parish councils and district or borough councils to avoid the Government's expenditure limits. That is claptrap. No deal has ever been struck. In many cases, the parish or community councils and the district councils are at loggerheads about something or other and are unlikely to be able to conclude any such deal.

In Wales, parish councils are called community or town councils. They vary in size considerably. In my constituency, the smallest town council covers a population of fewer than 2,000, whereas the largest covers about 40,000. Consequently, the difference in precepts and in what they do is enormous.

The Government, only recently, when they published their consultation proposals on the future structure of local government in Wales—and, I suspect, in England—made great play of parish and community councils playing a much larger role in local government in times to come. This spiteful legislation will do nothing to influence the course of such reforms or to increase the confidence of those who work in parish government.
