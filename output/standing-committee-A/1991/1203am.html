Local Government Finance Bill

Clause 30
AMOUNTS FOR DIFFERENT CATEGORIES OF DWELLINGS

Amendment proposed [28 November]: No. 236, in page 19, line 41, after "below", insert

"and having considered the incidence of council tax on the residents in its area".— (Mr. Murphy.)

10.30 am

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Parliamentary Under-Seceretary of State for the Environment (Mr. Robert Key)
: We had an interesting and comparatively short debate on Thursday evening about the amendment moved by the hon. Member for Torfaen (Mr. Murphy). In his strong contribution, the hon. Member for Leyton (Mr. Cohen) raised the issue of the reaction to the council tax of the Institute of Fiscal Studies. The IFS figures show that under the council tax 37 per cent. of households will lose, 37 per cent. will gain and 26 per cent. will be broadly unaffected compared with how they are affected under the community charge. I agree in general with those figures. The Opposition and the IFS say that the wider range of tax bills will affect not the balance of gainers and losers but the amounts of the gains and losses. I am sure that the hon. Member for Leyton will accept that.

The amendment would require the billing authority to consider the incidence of the council tax on the residents of its area before setting the amounts of council tax. That may seem reasonable, but it reveals a misconception about the clause which, despite its importance, is not concerned with the exercise of any significant discretion by the billing authority. The main decisions about the budget requirement and the amounts of council tax are to be taken by a billing authority in accordance with clauses 32 to 36 and by major precepting authorities under the equivalent clauses, clauses 43 to 47. Clause 30 simply requires the billing authority to calculate overall amounts of council tax for dwellings in each valuation band in each part of its area by adding amounts of tax calculated already by the billing authority and by the major precepting authorities.

Therefore, this is not the right moment to consider the incidence of the tax. Having said that, I have no argument with the view that the local authority should consider the impact on council tax payers when deciding its budget requirement and the resulting level of tax. That is elementary good sense, which we try to encourage. However, it is not what Opposition Members have in mind. It is not for the tax-setting authority to consider how the tax might impact on a particular household or individual. That is a matter for the design of the benefit system and the discount system, which are outside the amendment's scope.

Given the general agreement about the importance of a local authority considering the impact on council tax 678 payers and given that this is not the moment to enter into that argument, I ask the Committee to join me in resisting the amendment.
