Mr. Spearing
: It is a privilege to initiate the first debate on this important Bill. I shall not be brief, and I do not apologise for that because what I say in our debate on these amendments will save time later.

I thank the Minister for his comments. I wrote to tell him and his advisers of the general drift of my remarks and to give cohesion to the amendments that I have tabled.

Amendment No. 33 states:

"after 'section', insert 'and section 3'".

The first part of the Bill, according to the explanatory memorandum, seeks to establish

"a system of Ministerial orders in England and Wales for authorising railway, tramway and other guided transport schemes; canal works; and other works which would interfere with navigation. It provides a means of avoiding the need for special enactments for these purposes."

In other words, legislation that was hitherto "private" will be replaced by statutory instruments. I ask the Minister what constitutes an "order"? That is the term used in clauses 1 and 3.

The word "order" is an important term. It appears in the proposal in lower case but it comes from an authority. An order must be obeyed; it is something from which there is no escape. If there is a dispute about whether it is being obeyed, it is for a court to decide whether it should be. It is an engine of authority, and I make no complaint about that. Until now, the authority has been the House. The high court of Parliament decided on sometimes complex changes in the law, as the courts judge an appeal. We are replacing what was done with the advice and consent of Parliament assembled—to use the age-old phrase in the preamble to any Bill—with the views and wishes first, of a Minister of the Crown, a member of the Executive and, potentially, with an authoritarian person or body. Anyone who wanted to introduce authoritarian institutions to this country would have only to table a one-clause Bill expanding the power of statutory instruments. That is no secret. It could be done relatively easily and would comply with constitutional and legal requirements, although I hope that no members of the Committee would go along with that. Therefore, power would be transferred from more than 600 persons to at least one person.

There is only one small safeguard, to which my hon. Friends may wish to refer. Time and again, we read in the Bill that the order is

"subject to annulment in pursuance of a resolution of either House of Parliament."

In plain language, it is a negative instrument. Unfortunately, a negative instrument is not always given time on the Floor of the House. It might get less that an hour and a half after 10 o'clock. There is only a tenuous possibility of Parliament having control over any exercise of such an instrument by a Minister.

We are substituting the Privy Council's authority and the Crown's prerogatives for the prerogatives of Parliament. That is why I described the Bill as constitutional and procedural.

Private legislation, understandably, is seen as archaic and time-wasting. It provides opportunities for hon. Members to use Standing Orders to delay legislation that a tiny minority do not want. It can entail weeks in Committee listening to well-paid people in wigs. It takes JPEG 9 up the time of hon. Members who might otherwise be engaged in their proper duties. The hon. Member for Tatton (Mr. Hamilton) is here today. He is only too well aware of the constitutional and procedural matters that attended the committee that considered the King's Cross Railways Bill, which he chaired. If we were making a film of our proceedings, we could set the scene in an out-of-date, fusty Dickensian Parliament with a Jarndyce v. Jarndyce type case in which everything was tied up in ridiculous knots. That is not a difficult picture to paint.

In the past, anyone could petition a Bill. Indeed, privately promoted legislation is still done by petition. Petitioning is a fundamental democratic right. "Erskine May" states that private Bills conferring

"particular powers or benefits on any person or body of persons—including individuals, local authorities, statutory corporations or private corporations—in excess of or in conflict with the general law."

In other words, they are out of kilter with what is universal throughout the realm and are entertained only if there is good reason for necessary legislation other than public Bills, including the so-called private Member's Bill, which are general legislation.

Private Bills are required because nowadays they are of general public benefit. Although it may mean temporarily overriding private rights with proper compensation or protection, the general benefit is beyond doubt. In the past the procedure may have been misused. For example, the Enclosure Acts had social repercussions. Later, private powers were used for building roads and for collecting the relevant fees at turnpikes. It seems that, with bridges and the possibility of new turnpike roads, we have returned to that era now. The canals and the railways were also examples of the same procedure, to which nobody objected because of its benefit to society. Parliament had to ensure that such legislation did not tread underfoot the private rights of people, corporations or the benefits of the realm and that proper compensation and protection was provided. Those of us who have been involved with the subject—I note that most hon. Members in the Committee have been—will know that the result was the familiar phrase "no prejudicial affection". That was what had to be proved in the evidence given to a private Bill Committee. In the end, a sort of justice was achieved and that is why we must be careful.

Amendment No. 34 would provide an intermediate stage in addition to the relatively short and narrow statutory instrument—we get a bit suspicious when such documents are too thick. Three questions spring to mind about a private Bill. First, is it necessary? Secondly, is it in the broad public benefit or does it confer undue benefit on private persons or corporations? Thirdly, is there justice and equity for people who might be disadvantaged in the small scale? In other words, we must take account of the macro-balance and the micro-balance.

The transfer of such judgments to the Executive, as the Bill proposes, is an enormous constitutional step. Amendment No. 34 would provide that the Bill require a second document. A statutory instrument is a trigger mechanism, and amendment No. 34 states that the order:

"shall specify a scheme, or schemes, of work in accordance with the description specified in Schedule 1 or any other section of this Act."

10 We are not just dealing with an order but with what, in statutory terms, is a scheme of work—a full description of the works and surrounding conditions which must be executed to fulfil the purposes of the Act. The amendment suggests that schedule 1 be converted into a description of what must be included in such a scheme of work.

Amendment No. 34 would change the existing title of schedule 1 to cover a description of matters to be included in any scheme of work. At present, schedule 1 contains a common-sense list of 18 matters that would be expected to be involved in the discussion before a statutory instrument is laid before the House by the Minister. Those matters are usually listed somewhere in the private Bill, but that list is not always complete. Schedule 1 is headed

"Matters within sections 1 and 3".

I suggest that we add to the list, sufficient maps and plans of the various works, a section dealing with environmental assessment, which is not evident in the Bill, and a table to show properties involved. That is already used in private Bills as a schedule of properties.

The list is minimal, but we can add to it. There is no reason why a description of works should not include other matters that need to be included on a particular project. Such a statutory requirement, which is described in statute, will be essential in the course of inquiries that the Bill will set in train.

The Minister has been helpful and sent us a summary of order-making procedure. In effect, that sets up, under the aegis of the Department of Transport a parallel system to existing planning inquiries with which we are familiar. I am happy to give way to any hon. Member in the development of my thesis.

Some public inquiries are small and others are extensive. Problems sometimes arise. Inspectors from the Department of the Environment administer such inquiries, but not without some difficulty and controversy. The Bill gives power to the Department of Transport to set up a similar and parallel system of public inquiry. Unless there is a specification on a description of works, public activity cannot be focused properly at the inquiry to give it the necessary bite. The parallel planning procedure includes a planning application. The Secretary of State's request to the inspector to investigate the matter covers all aspects involved in a planning application. There should be a parallel library of documents which comes within the description of the works.

I have no doubt that the Joint Committee, whose recommendations formed the Bill, had in mind the analogy of planning applications. When a large planning application or a compulsory purchase order is made for some project, perhaps for a school or power station, the procedures are relatively well known. People have asked, not unreasonably, that if we can follow the procedures for those applications, why cannot we follow them for private Bills?

People have said that if we can have a planning application for a motorway, why cannot we have one for a road, a tramway or a waterway? The hon. Member for Faversham (Mr. Moate) nods his head. The idea is attractive, but we must draw a distinction between a mode of transport and a system of transport. The hon. Member for Faversham looks puzzled.

If we cross New Palace Yard, the road is a public space. Parliament has dedicated the road outside to the public. We are all implicated in the raft of legislation, which has been passed and modified by the House and which deals JPEG 11 with different aspects of that environment; for example, the ownership of the road; the maintenance of its surface, the construction, use and licensing of the vehicles that pass over it; the highway code; criminal law; the courts; the police and so on. An infrastructure of law, custom and practice is understood and taken for granted.

However, a very different sort of law and civic infrastructure exists for a transport system. A transport system requires additional mechanical devices, a system of access or non-access for persons. It sometimes requires complex machinery; it needs civil engineering works, which require considerable maintenance. The system also requires the development of techniques—the House has historically kept abreast of those, especially those on railways, airways and waterways.

Our Committee meets two days after the problems in the Severn tunnel and all the consequent reports on the Floor of the House about system accidents. Apart from occasional motorway pile-ups, we do not have reports of system accidents on roads.

The law for transport systems usually includes a range of legal constraints that do not apply to motorways or ordinary roads.
