The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Mr. Robert Key): What a pleasure it is to be back in Committee. It seems but a twinkling of an eye—if not a shut eye—since we were last here.

I am grateful to the hon. Members for Sheffield, Brightside (Mr. Blunkett), for Leyton (Mr. Cohen) and for Makerfield (Mr. McCartney) for having raised many points late last night, which I shall try to deal with before I come to the substance of the amendments.

There is sometimes an aura of mystery surrounding tribunals. There are no proposals to change the present arrangements, and I think that it would be helpful if I explained them. Opposition Members were telling scare stories about the likely number of appeals and about the way in which cases will be handled by tribunals. Members of what are currently the valuation and community charge tribunals—they will become valuation tribunals—are appointed by county councils, metropolitan districts and London boroughs. Members are unpaid, although they can claim a financial loss allowance. They come from all walks of life. Relatively few are experts in legal or rating matters, although many are councillors or ex-councillors. It is important, for the sake of the tribunals' credibility as independent and impartial bodies, that members' appointments are not seen as political. The valuation tribunal system has existed for about 40 years and it has an enviable reputation for its impartiality and fairness. I thank and congratulate the members, presidents and professional support staff of the tribunals.

This summer, I spoke at the national conference of valuation and community charge tribunals which was held at a remarkable hotel near Coventry. Coventry—or Warwickshire—is in the heart of England and I was surprised that the hotel had Neptune as its theme, with lots of flowing fountains Col. 478 and baths. Never mind, it was exciting to be in the heart of England, especially as I was addressing such distinguished people who play an important role in our national life. The tribunal service provides training for its members and its staff in the legal issues with which they have to deal: rating and liability law. The Department of the Environment funds that training and spent about £100,000 on it in 1991. The clerk of the tribunal is professionally trained and his or her main role is to advise members on the law on any case, before them. The clerk takes no part in the decisions of the tribunal, which are the sole responsibility of the members, but his or her presence gives members access to advice from a legal expert.

The tribunals have a long history of service to the community. They have an enviable track record. Research has shown that appellants are almost always satisfied with the way in which their cases have been handled. I am confident that the tribunals will continue to provide a high-quality service, when they come to deal with council tax valuations.
