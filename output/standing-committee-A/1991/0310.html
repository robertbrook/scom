[MR. GEOFFREY LOFTHOUSE in the Chair]

Cardiff Bay Barrage Bill

10.30 am

Mr. Keith Raffan
(Delyn)
: On a point of order, Mr. Lofthouse. I received notification of my membership of the Committee about half an hour ago. Is that in order?

The Chairman
: It is in order. The matter is not for me, but for the Committee of Selection.

Mr. Ray Powell
(Ogmore)
: Further to that point of order, Mr. Lofthouse. As you quite rightly said, it is a matter for the Committee of Selection, but such lack of notice is inconvenient for members of the Committee. I take the point that the hon. Member for Delyn (Mr. Raffan) made. If all hon. Members who represent Welsh constituencies—and other hon. Members appointed by the Committee of Selection— were entitled to receive an invitation to the sitting, we should surely have been given time to do some preparatory work before debating the Bill. If the fault lies with the Committee of Selection, the Government or the Clerk's Department, that should be explained to the Committee. We should have been given the choice of either accepting the invitation and participating or of rejecting the invitation and deciding to meet on another day. We should all have been properly invited to the Committee and given sufficient time to consider all the matters that relate to the Cardiff Bay Barrage Bill.

Of course, the Bill has been in Parliament long enough and has been talked about at such length that all Members of the Committee should have a certain amount of knowledge about it. However, not even the hon. Member for Delyn could not have an intricate knowledge of the amendments or have digested them properly in the few minutes that followed his being notified of our sitting.

Hon. Members have other commitments today. For various reasons, several Members representing Welsh constituencies were not originally included on the Committee by the Committee of Selection. They want to participate in today's debate on the Bill. However, they were notified only last night or first thing this morning when they opened their mail that they had now been selected. That is inconsiderate of the Clerk's Department, the Government or whoever is responsible. The Government have been negligent throughout the proceedings on the Bill. If they are at fault, the Committee should be informed so that the matter can be fully debated.

The Chairman
: Order. I have already informed the Committee that that is a matter not for me but for the Committee of Selection. However, I have sympathy with some of the points that have been 4 made. If the Committee wants to move a dilatory motion, I would be prepared to accept it.

Motion made, and Question proposed,

That the Committee do now adjourn.—[ Mr. Morgan.]

Mr. Rhodri Morgan
(Cardiff, West)
: I want to say a few words on that matter. This is the second time that the Bill has been subjected to what one might call an attempt to rush its fences while it has been under the tactical and masterful control of the Under-Secretary of State for Wales. We shall all miss his presence—and, in our more sado-masochistic moments, his hyena-like cackle—in the next Parliament. Last July, just one week before the summer recess, the Under-Secretary attempted to introduce a Bill, although there was no point in doing so because the House was about to adjourn. The Standing Orders Committee, with a Conservative majority, quite rightly threw out the Bill. I am grateful to the Conservative Members on that Committee for doing that.

The same is happening now. One week before the House adjourns for a 99.9 per cent certain general election, we have been asked to rush our fences, with the Bill being brought to Committee on Budget day. That is an extremely unwise move because we shall not be able to give the Bill proper consideration while we have other matters on our minds—the Budget and the impending general election.

We have been dragged here this morning because, when such matters are rushed, procedural mistakes tend to be made. Standing Order No. 86 was not considered. As a result, members of the Committee found out at 6.30 p.m. last night, at the earliest, that they were required to be in Committee today. In my view, the only proper course of action is to adjourn the Committee.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. Nicholas Bennett)
: The speech of the hon. Member for Cardiff, West (Mr. Morgan) was full of his usual collection of nonsense. This is not a matter for the Government; the Committee of Selection determines who shall sit on a Committee. It has determined that all hon. Members with Welsh constituencies shall sit on the Committee. The fact that the Committee of Selection made that decision yesterday is something for which it must answer. It has nothing to do with the Government. The Bill has been debated extensively, both in its present and previous forms. The former private Bill was debated for 25i hours on the Floor of the House. It was also examined in detail—

Mr. Allan Rogers
(Rhondda)
: On a point of order, Mr. Lofthouse. Which previous Bills were discussed in the House is not the business of the Committee. The Minister should deal with the problems of this Committee. He has not discussed the Bill at all.

The Chairman
: Order. I will decide that. I think that the Minister is developing an argument about why the Committee should not be adjourned.

The former private Bill was debated for 25i hours on the Floor of the House. It was also examined in detail by two Select Committees, for 27 days in this House and 14 days in another place. This Bill was debated for more than four hours on Second Reading, and a House of Commons Select Committee recently completed 14 days of further detailed examination.

: Lie.

(Upminster)

Members who have spoken. I may have voted in a certain way at the end of various debates, but I have always been open to argument and discussion. If the Minister continues to press his point, I shall continue to say what he really is.

Mr. Bennett

Member for Rhondda, who interrupted me when I started to detail how much examination of the Cardiff bay barrage proposal has taken place, objects when I say that he is trying to prevent me from putting the facts to the Committee. That is a fair description of his action. The hon. Gentleman is not fair minded and open on this issue; he has been opposed to the Bill from the beginning. He has spoken against it and voted against it in the House. That is not my idea of a fair-minded and balanced view.

Mr. Donald Anderson
(Swansea, East)
: The manner in which the Minister speaks in support of the Bill is likely to influence not only those who have consistently voted against it but those who, like me have consistently voted in its favour. Although, coming from Swansea, I was brought up with a certain view of Cardiff, I have consistently supported the Bill. The way in which the Minister is now introducing it is rapidly influencing me against it.

Mr. Bennett
: If the hon. Member for Swansea, East (Mr. Anderson) is so easily influenced in relation to a Bill affecting the future of Cardiff by the fact that the Minister is responding to the hon. Member for Rhondda, who interrupted a simple recitation of the number of hours spent in consideration of the Bill, the Opposition are on their way to trying to delay the progress of the Bill. The Bill is most important to the future of Cardiff. It is a Bill that needs to be debated in Committee. It has been debated 6 extensively on the Floor of the House. In my view, we could complete its Committee stage today if that were the Committee's wish. If some hon. Members wish to obstruct the Bill—as we know some do—it will be in their hands if the Bill does not go forward today.

Dr. Kim Howells
(Pontypridd)
: We are being asked to debate a Bill for which, as members of a Standing Committee, we are not prepared. That is a simple enough assertion. Half an hour ago, I opened my mail and found a card. I was expected to rush to the Committee without knowing what amendments had been tabled. It is nonsense to expect us to make an important decision that would affect the capital city of the Principality, the ecology of the area and our attitude to the whole question of Welsh estuaries and barrages without our having the necessary documents and briefs.

It is ludicrous for the Minister to expect that we should come to Committee armed with all necessary information merely because the Bill was debated on the Floor of the House for 25 hours. The situation changes from week to week, as he knows. We have no choice but to support the motion tabled by my hon. Friend the Member for Cardiff, West. (Mr. Morgan).

Mr. Raffan
: All Members of Parliament who represent Welsh constituencies realise that the Bill is important. I am from the north of the Principality, so the Bill does not affect me directly, but I have taken an interest in it and am strongly in favour. The last thing that I wish to do is to do anything to damage its progress.

I do not know who is responsible for today's cock-up but at such short notice we cannot possibly debate in detail, and in the intelligent way that it deserves, a matter that has serious consequences for Cardiff and for the Principality generally. The idea is absurd. My hon. Friend the Minister will not be surprised to hear me say that his contribution has not helped; it will turn those who favour the Bill against it. I am strongly in favour and find myself in a quandary. I do not wish to delay progress, but we have not had sufficient notice to prepare properly to debate this complex and detailed measure.

Mr. Denzil Davies
(Llanelli)
: I agree with the hon. Member for Delyn (Mr. Raffan). It is not a question of agreeing or disagreeing with the Bill and its many facets. It has 30 clauses, which makes it complicated. I am told that there are almost 50 amendments on the Order Paper. The customary procedure of the House is that, perhaps after the first sitting of a Committee, the usual channels informally agree to an end-date for the Committee stage and both sides tend to work within that constraint and that timetable. That would be the proper way here. It might take three, four or five sittings but no one is being obstructive, so it could be agreed. In the House we debate constitutional reform—a Bill of Rights and other measures which I do not favour all that much. If Parliament is asked to push through important legislation in one day hon. Members will of course JPEG 7 argue that that is not the constitutional and proper way.

I assume that the Government are responsible for bringing the Bill forward at this stage: after all, they are in charge of Government business, but it is a most inopportune time. Let us by all means debate some amendments if we have to. The most sensible course, though, would be to adjourn, agree a timetable and debate the Bill in the usual way.

Mr. Richard Livsey
(Brecon and Radnor)
: Some of my ancestors helped build Cardiff and were involved in the city's business life. They would turn in their graves if they knew that the Bill was to be considered in the space of a couple of hours and would reach the statute book perhaps with glaring errors in it. We need reasoned debate and time, and today is not the right time. I support an adjournment of the sitting for commonsense reasons.

Mr. Gareth Wardell
(Gower)
: It is great to be present when there is all-party support for the adjournment of our proceedings on the Bill. I take the same view as right hon. and hon. Members who have spoken. It is ludicrous to have before us a Bill that is in desperate need of amending but to have no opportunity to amend it. For example, under the Bill, cases may need to go to the Lands Tribunal so that compensation problems can be discussed. Yet no legal aid is available to people coming before that tribunal, so it will disadvantage many people on low incomes. Many other issues arise.

What is happening here is wholly unacceptable to the people of Wales. They will find it amazing if the Bill is driven through. If it is, democracy will be the loser. I support my hon. Friend the Member for Cardiff, West (Mr. Morgan), we should adjourn the Committee.

Sir Nicholas Bonsor
: While I agree with the hon. Member for Cardiff, West (Mr. Morgan), about the Committee of Selection and the extraordinary way in which members of the Committee were chosen, it is difficult to accept the assertion that the question has had insufficient attention paid to it. I had the good fortune to Chair the Select Committee, and it sat for five weeks taking evidence on the issues. Other hon. Members were also present. Approximately 83 petitions were submitted, and the Bill was amended substantially. I am sure that hon. Members who represent Welsh constituencies will have followed the proceedings of the Select Committee very closely. I do not accept that they have arrived in this Committee in a state of unprepared ignorance.

Almost all the evidence submitted to the Select Committee suggested that everyone wanted the Bill to proceed on to the statute book. That is exactly what it should do, in a proper form, as amended by the Select Committee.

If the hon. Member for Upminster (Sir Nicholas Bonsor) is suggesting that we, as members of the Committee, are to be precluded from tabling amendments—[ Interruption.] We certainly cannot do it in one day. Is the hon. Gentleman saying that we should get on with it? Is he suggesting that we should table amendments now and debate them today?

Sir Nicholas Bonsor

Members: "No,"] Then I withdraw the allegation—except to say that the Government Whips have worked hard to put pressure on individual members of the Committee. We have seen them operating. The Minister wants the Bill to proceed in one day, but that simply is not on. It is entirely unacceptable for us to be asked to serve on a Committee without having had the opportunity to table amendments.

The hon. Member for Upminster said that the vast majority of people in the Principality supported the Bill. That could be established by a referendum; but I doubt whether the majority of the people in Cardiff would be in favour of it. Whichever way it goes, we do not know. What we do know is that a substantial number of people's lives will be affected and that some are unsure about the compensation arrangements and therefore against the Bill. Views can develop as time progresses.

This is a Standing Committee, not a Select Committee. If the Bill goes through normal procedure and is adopted at the end of the day, so be it. But we cannot do our task in one day, as the Minister wants, or in a couple of weeks, as the hon. Member for Upminster wants.

Members on both sides of the Committee.

Mr. Paul Murphy
(Torfaen)
: The whole question of the way in which the Bill has been dealt with in the House of Commons has been touched upon by the Minister. The hon. Member for Kingswood (Mr. Hayward) said that it was necessary for the Bill to be considered now. If any obstruction has occurred, it is entirely the Government's fault. Some months ago, the Government failed to ensure that there were sufficient numbers of hon. Members to vote for the Bill, and it collapsed. They failed to get the hybrid Bill right in the summer. They have now failed to go to the Committee of Selection to ensure that there is a special procedure whereby all Welsh Members of Parliament are allowed to sit on Committee considering Bills such as this as was arranged in the 1970s for the Committee considering the Development Board for Rural Wales. All hon. Members should have been given sufficient notice of this Committee.

The Minister appears to be disaster prone with this Bill. That reminds me of the description given by Mr. Disraeli of the difference between a disaster and a catastrophe, when he referred to Gladstone and the Thames. A disaster is when the Minister falls into Cardiff bay and a catastrophe is when he is pulled out.

In all seriousness, the points made about why the Committee should be adjourned have nothing, by any stretch of the imagination, to do with obstructing the Bill. Members who represent Welsh constituencies are rightly expected to attend, they should have been given sufficient notice about what they could table and what would be considered by way of debate.

The important amendments on the amendment paper deal with the quality of water, groundwater levels and flooding, the absence of the question of birds and wildlife in the Select Committee's report, and a host of other matters—which the Select Committee rightly wanted this committee to consider. In all seriousness, we cannot say to the hon. Members who represent Welsh constituencies that because they received notice of this Committee only this morning, that they cannot participate. That is unfortunate and I sincerely hope that we shall return a procedure by which all these matters can be considered.

Many of the important amendments before us were prompted by Cardiff city council, so we must bear in mind that it is in favour of the barrage. We must give proper consideration to these and other amendments. The only way that we can do that, in fairness to all hon. Members who represent Welsh constituencies, is to adjourn.

Mr. Bell
: I rise with some trepidation in the cauldron of emotion about the Bill. I sat on the Select Committee which considered the Bill and I was able to consider the subject objectively and dispassionately because I am disinterested in local political terms. In the Standing Committee there is a confluence of interests and pressures. A general election is coming up and there is great drama in the Committee and pressure to make political speeches that are unconnected with the Cardiff Bay Barrage Bill. This is a Government Bill and the Government want it to go through before the general election. It has been argued from both sides of the Committee that the matter has been thoroughly aired during the past five or six years and that democracy has been at work in Cardiff for more than six years, but there has not been a centime of investment in the Cardiff bay barrage as a result of that debate.
