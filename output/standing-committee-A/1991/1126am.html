Clause 8
LIABILITY IN PRESCRIBED CASES

Amendment proposed [21 November], No. 96, in page 6, line 22, at the end, to add the words—

'(7) A person who is liable in respect of any chargeable dwelling under subsection (3) above shall have a duty separately to notify each resident of—

  1. the amount that that resident shall pay to that or any other person in respect of the council tax for that dwelling; and

"The rent charged in such places will be what the market can bear and, ultimately, regardless of whether the tax element is shown separately or not, it is unlikely to make any difference to the amount of rent that is being charged."—[ Official Report, Standing Committee A, 21 November 1991; c. 347–350.]

That is an extremely cynical view to take when dealing with private tenants' rights.

Landlords retained £400 million—possibly more—in rent when rates were abolished. We are now moving from the poll tax back into a property tax and we wish to protect tenants from being ripped off again. We want them to know exactly what the rent is and what the council tax element is in the total amount that they are required to pay. As we pointed out on Thursday, that is not unreasonable. The Government specify how local authorities should present that information to those who now pay poll tax and to those who will pay the new council tax.

We expect no more and no less for private tenants. We do not believe that private landlords should be shielded from the rights of tenants to have information or from ensuring that tenants are not discriminated against, so that the amounts raised do not exceed the actual rent in council tax imposed on such tenants.

We shall divide the Committee on the amendment, which we think is fair. No arguments have been made that invalidate the rights of private tenants or the purpose of the amendment. Conservative Members who vote against it will rue the day when they denied tenants the rights that the Government are continually parading in other sections of the community, the rights that I should expect to be built into any citizens charter—the right to know, the right to see at a glance what is council tax and what is rent and the right of the public purse not to pay out more than is necessary for whatever benefit entitlement is received by tenants.

Amendment No. 126, like clause 11 itself, takes us to the heart of the question of discounts and to what the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland has referred to as the combination of a personal and a property tax. Opposition Members consider that it could be described equally well as a combination of a head tax and a roof tax. It is the combination of a personal discount and the element of a head tax that leads to the most fearful complications, and it is the cause of many of the problems with which the Government are wrestling.

It cannot be said that the Government have not been warned. We in the Opposition have done our best to warn the Government that they are dicing with death, as it were, but so have many other voices from the world of local government. The Audit Commission has expressed reservations, the Local Government Chronicle has urged that the whole crazy notion be dropped, and even the citizens advice bureaux have expressed, through briefings issued to the Committee, their great anxiety about this aspect of the new tax.

It is remarkable that neither on Second Reading nor so far in the Committee's proceedings have we heard Ministers argue convincingly for the inclusion of these provisions in the Bill. That is partly because Ministers are unwilling to bring to the surface the underlying arguments. As a result, we hear arguments that lack both conviction and credibility because they are not the real arguments.

The first possible argument in favour of discounts is that they would be a way of relieving people, on income grounds, from a liability that they would otherwise have. That argument clearly does not hold water, and, to be fair, Ministers have not advanced it. Indeed, they have glorified in the proposition that discounts have nothing to do with income or the ability to pay. Ministers brush evidence of unfairness aside, saying that discounts have nothing to do with fairness or ability to pay. As we shall argue on 354 another group of amendments, even if the Government could be persuaded to consider ability to pay, it is clear that the discount system is not the right method of dealing with the problem. We believe that the rebate system is a more accurate, better targeted way of helping people who find it difficult to pay their bills.

The second argument has already been advanced: the discount system is proposed to take account of the fact that a single-person household will make fewer demands on local government services than a two-person household. One or two Conservative Members are puzzled by the argument. I am too. However, the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland advanced that bizarre argument strongly. Even if we take it seriously as a genuine attempt to compare the demands on the local authority of one family with those of another, the discount system would be the crudest possible attempt to nod to that principle. It would certainly not measure such demands.

In a previous debate, my hon. Friend the Member for Glasgow, Cathcart (Mr. Maxton) pointed out that pensioner couples often make fewer demands on a local authority than a single parent with four children. The discount system is an extremely crude measure. It would not even put into effect the principle that people should be assessed according to their demands on local authorities.

Measuring demand is in any case an odd basis for a council tax. We have never imposed direct taxation on the principle of a contract between the taxpayer and the state or local authority. Income taxpayers do not pay tax according to their varying demands for nuclear weapons or miles of motorway. That would be nonsense. People pay income tax according to their income and their ability to meet that tax obligation. It would be almost unprecedented to claim that, for the council tax, a notional contract existed between the individual taxpayer and the local authority. That reflects not a serious principle of taxation, but a residual, almost primeval loyalty to the market. The Under-Secretary of State thus reveals that he is no great pioneer of tax theory, but a backwoodsman who cannot conceal his abiding adherence to the principle that no transaction is valid unless the market has sanctioned it.

I am sure that the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland will abide by his principle. Ministers believe that, somehow, even the tax relationship is negotiable. If, therefore I, as a council tax payer, could show that I had reduced my demands on the local authority, I could, logically, demand a reduction in my bill. That is wrong. Even if that was seriously advanced as a basis for the tax, the discount system would be a curious interpretation and a rudimentary, ineffectual reflection of the principle behind it.
