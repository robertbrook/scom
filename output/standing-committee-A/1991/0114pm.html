The Minister for Shipping (Mr. Patrick McLoughlin): I had begun to explain some of the main points in relation to amendment No. 127. In preparing the proposals, we gave careful thought to the issue of consultation before application for an order is made; amendments Nos. 5 and 57 propose that such consultation should be the subject of statutory rules.

There is no doubt that early consultation by prospective applicants for orders is essential. We considered making specific statutory provision for consultation, but we do not believe that that is necessary or that it represents a feasible way of achieving the objective.

Col. 178 Our difficulty is defining accurately what is meant by consultation and what would fulfil an obligation to consult. Consultation should ideally be an ongoing process rather than a single act, which makes it difficult to describe in statutory terms suitably meaningful requirements.

The new procedure will be used for a wide range of schemes, from minor modifications to a railway to new main lines, from small scale canal works to tidal barrages. It would be difficult to prescribe rules for consultation that would be sufficiently comprehensive to include all those who might want to contribute to the process and, at the same time, be flexible enough to suit all the different sorts of application.

As we have heard at some length, clause 6 enables the Secretary of State to lay down the preliminary procedural requirements with which applications for orders under the Bill will need to comply. Clause 23 enables someone who wishes to challenge the validity of an order to take procedures in the High Court within 42 days of the publication of notice that an order under clause 1 or 3 has been made. The right to challenge an order under clause 23 will apply whether the ground for the challenge is one of substance or merely procedural, for example, if the application for the order did not comply with the procedural requirement laid down in rules made under clause 6.

The amendment is of a technical nature. It was brought to our attention by British Rail and I am sure that the Committee will agree that it is on the right lines. It is an attempt to reflect one of the more valuable features of the current practice of the House on private Bills. Under the Standing Orders of the House there is provision for examiners to consider whether promoters of a Bill have complied with the requirements of the Standing Orders. Third parties are entitled to appear before the examiners to complain of any non-compliance with Standing Orders. However, a Committee on a private Bill thereafter may not inquire into compliance with Standing Orders that lay down the initial procedural steps relating to the Bill. Once the Bill has been enacted, the validity of the Act cannot be called into question.

The Bill would allow subsequent challenges to be made on whether the original rules had been complied with. The amendment is designed to ensure that, once an order has been made and an application has been considered, that technical question cannot be reopened. That is in the interests of those making the orders and everybody else concerned. It will remove an element of obscure judicial uncertainty during that process.
