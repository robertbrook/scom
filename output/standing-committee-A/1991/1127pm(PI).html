Mr. David Bellotti (Eastbourne): The House is probably the only place in the world where one day I can speak rapidly to get 600 or 700 words into a minute and a half, and on the following day, I can be offered a three-and-a-half hour break during a 10 minute speech. The Committee was considering weekly and fortnightly payments. I said that recent evidence shows that the highest non-payment of poll tax is in areas of high benefit dependence, and, therefore, of low income. This link has been clearly established by local authorities and voluntary organisations.

It is important that people who are invited to make payments make them from income. If they are receiving income weekly or fortnightly, it is easier to make payments weekly or two-weekly. When payments and income do not match, difficulties occur. A person who is being pressed to make monthly payments with only a weekly income often finds that difficult in the week of the demand. Monthly payments for people on low pay are not only difficult, they are inappropriate. Similarly, there is no reason why everyone who is paid monthly should pay council tax weekly. Payment times should vary. People who receive a weekly wage or fortnightly income are more likely to pay immediately from this income if they have an exact figure rather than a calculation or an amount to put away for later in the month.

Local authority practice in collecting poll tax is interesting because it will be relevant to collection of council tax. Kirklees council introduced community charge weekly payment scheme and this has resulted in a marked improvement in its rate of collection. Winchester council has also agreed to collect community charge payments with council rents, which has helped to keep payment regular. Unfortunately, the vast majority of local authorities will accept payment only on a monthly basis. Therefore many who might have been encouraged to pay regularly have difficulties and have discovered that their local authority is not friendly towards the customer.

To give the Committee an idea of the magnitude of the problem, I offer a statistic from the citizens advice bureaux. Between February and May 1990, CABs dealt with 1,394 inquiries about problems with paying the community charge and a further 1,558 inquiries over methods of payment. This demonstrates that a sizeable problem exists and that we ought to encourage local authorities to deal with it.

If we gave weekly and fortnightly payments the same status as monthly payments, we would provide councils with great stimulus to arrange such schemes for payment.

The Committee no doubt agrees that local authority practices should be tailored to meet the needs of the people. If the Government wish to encourage payment and avoid debt, they will support the amendment.
