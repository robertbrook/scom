Mr. Portillo
: It would not save any time, because I have to deal with points made during the debate.

In an intervention, rather than during his speech, the hon. Member for Makerfield referred to the taper hitting the thrifty. He misunderstood. In any social security benefit, we must ensure that the maximum amount of benefit available is paid to those who are at the lowest level, usually defined as those on the income support level. As people's incomes rise above that level, they are gradually eased off the benefit. [Hon. Members: "Eased off."] There is no way that the problem can be avoided. The alternatives are a taper—a gradual reduction of benefit payable as income rises—or a cut-off point, below which one receives benefit and above which one does not. In jargon, a cut-off point is called a cliff edge. It is most unsatisfactory, because if one is a fraction above the permitted level, all benefit is lost and one is worse off than if one had not had that increase in income.

The best method is to apply a taper. We have to decide the appropriate level of the taper. Until now, the maximum amount of benefit has been 80 per cent, leaving the claimant to pay 20 per cent of the bill. The claimant has been eased out of benefit at the rate of 15p for every £1 of extra income. But, because the new benefit scale will run to 100 per cent, a different rate of taper is appropriate.

We can have arguments above how the taper and the different levels of benefit relate. Running benefit to 100 per cent and removing benefit at 20p for every additional £1 of income is broadly equivalent to giving benefit up to only an 80 per cent maximum and reducing the amount of benefit by 15p for every extra £1. If one had wanted to achieve exact arithmetic equivalence between the two, the new taper would have been 18.75 per cent.

Several Opposition Members raised a point from the briefing given to them by the Child Poverty Action Group. They referred to an example of a 922 particular person on a particular level of income who, when faced with the same bill under the council tax and the community charge, would be worse off under council tax benefit. Of course, the level of one's bill and income matter. That example has been carefully chosen on the basis of two assumptions: first, that the income is at a certain level—in income support terms, that would be relatively high—and, secondly, that the bill is the same under the council tax as under the community charge.

Most people's bills will be different under the two systems. We have concluded from the family expenditure survey that, broadly speaking and on average, the benefit will be available up to the same income level under the council tax as under the community charge. Where people's bills are higher, another element will have to be brought into the equation. We are in favour of transitional relief to reduce council tax bills that are higher than community charge bills.

Of course, the Labour party is implacably opposed to paying transitional relief in those circumstances, but the Government are in favour of it. Transitional relief initially will reduce higher bills, and the benefit will be payable upon that. In any case, people with higher bills may often be able to receive benefit up to a higher level of income. In any case, because the council tax will be based on a property as well as a personal element and because a number of social security claimants will live in low-value properties, those claimants may well receive a lower bill in the first place.
