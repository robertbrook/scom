Mr. Murphy: I beg to move amendment No. 198, in page 90, line 1, leave out paragraph 15.

The amendment's purpose is simple: to stop information collected by local authorities for the purposes of the council tax from being sold on to others without the consent of the people involved. There are two reasons for that. First, the principle is wrong. Third parties should not be given such information without the consent of the individuals involved. The Minister may argue that paragraph 15(2)(b), which prohibits the passing on of information about a named individual, will be an adequate safeguard. It is not. In our age of high technology and computerisation, much information on individuals is already held by third parties and could be correlated with non-personal information collected for the purposes of the Bill.

Secondly, there is a practical reason for opposing the sale of such information. Even without assuming that it is possible to correlate names with otherwise non-attachable information on property, several abuses could result from the sale of information. Owners of exempt vacant properties could be targeted by unscrupulous estate agents or rental agencies. Groups such as students could be subjected to mailing from those keen to exploit their known lack of money. Knowledge of property banding would allow quasi-income group mailing.

If we assume that names can be correlated to the information, the problems are worse. Owners of exempt vacant dwellings could become the unwitting victims of credit card and other frauds. Council tax defaulters could be harassed by bailiffs performing other debt collection work. Householders on benefit could be credit blacklisted. There are many other reasons why paragraph 15 is unnecessary. No benefit will accrue to local authorities from having to sell such information. It is a matter of principle and of practice. I hope that the Government accept the amendment.

Mr. Nicholas Bennett: The hon. Member for Torfaen (Mr. Murphy) made the assumption that names can be correlated. That is a false assumption and his argument therefore collapses. Schedule 2 paragraph 15(2)(b) does not refer to named individuals but uses the phrase "not personal information." It specifically excludes the naming of any individual or the citing of any information about individuals. It allows the statistical information that is aggregated to be sold to companies that wish to use it. There are many reasons why a company might want such information on a particular area. Paragraph 15 protects Col. 546 the individual. There is no way in which the aggregation of statistical information can be used in respect of named individuals. To ram the point home, it is worth looking at those movements. In the north between 1983 and 1990 the average house price moved from band B to band C as it did in Yorkshire and Humberside. In the north-west it moved from band C to band B and back to band C; in the east midlands it went from C to B to C and then to D—four movements within the period. In the west midlands, the average price moved from C to D; in East Anglia, from C to E to D; in the south-west, from C to D to E and back to D; in the south-east from D to E to F and back to E; and in Greater London from E to F and back to E.

The average-priced property would have moved between bands in 13 out of the 32 boroughs, depending on the year in which the valuation was undertaken.
