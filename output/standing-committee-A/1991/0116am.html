Mr. Peter Snape (West Bromwich, East): I beg to move amendment No. 78, in page 8, line 11, at end insert—

'(aa) he shall give that person a statement of the reasons for the modifications;'.

The amendment is virtually self-explanatory, so I shall be extremely brief. It would ensure that people who comment on modifications to a proposal were aware of the reasons for them. It is sensible and eminently reasonable although, in the great volume of papers before him the Minister will probably find many reasons why the Government do not regard it as such. I shall eagerly await the Minister's comments on this progressive and helpful proposal.

The Minister for Shipping (Mr. Patrick McLoughlin): In view of the brevity with which the hon. Member for West Bromwich, East (Mr. Snape) moved the amendment, I shall respond similarly.

The intentions of the amendment are undoubtedly worthy, but I am not convinced that it is necessary to deal with the matter in the Bill.

Modifications will arise usually as a result of argument at the public inquiry, so often all the relevant parties will be aware of the issues: clause 13(3) makes sure that they are. It is only common sense that the Secretary of State should explain the reasons for his proposal to people who are likely to be affected by a modification. Not to do so would mean that they would come back to him asking for more information which would cause unwelcome and unnecessary delay. It is simply administrative good practice.

The amendment is unnecessary because clause 13(3) envisages that the Secretary of State will announce that he is is minded to make an order but that a modification is desirable. Clause 14 more or less forces him to give reasons for his intentions to make the order and it is unthinkable that he would not give reasons for a proposed modification.

I ask the hon. Member for West Bromwich, East to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Snape: It appears from the Minister's explanation that the purpose of the amendment will be met, but if it is unthinkable for a Secretary of State not to give reasons for a proposed modification surely it would be sensible to enshrine that unthinkability in the Bill. Nevertheless, I am prepared to accept the Minister's assurance that that is the intention of clause 14 and, on that basis, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Andrew F. Bennett (Denton and Reddish): I beg to move amendment No. 31, in page 8, line 16, at end insert—

'(d) no Order varying the recommendation of the inquiry shall be made unless such Order has been approved by a resolution of each House of Parliament.'.

The amendment deals with the crucial question of the sovereignty of Parliament as opposed to the powers of the Secretary of State*and a public inquiry.

In their consultation document, the Government proposed that issues of national significance—those with which Parliament should deal—should go before a public inquiry. Parliament would then put its seal of approval or disapproval on the scheme. I understand that various groups have told the Government that the public inquiry procedure might be brought into disrepute if a public inquiry were held and recommended that a scheme should go ahead and then Parliament turned it down. I realise that is difficult to decide who should have the final say— Parliament, or the public inquiry and the Minister—so, I am not complaining that the Government have decided that Parliament should approve the principle, that an inquiry should follow, the inspector makes a recommendation on the basis of the outcome of the inquiry and the Secretary of State then signs the order.

If Parliament agrees the principle and the inquiry and the Minister approve it, that is straightforward, but what would happen if the inquiry reached a conclusion different from that of the Minister? The opportunity should exist for Parliament to have a further say. I accept that that could be difficult.

An inspector might say that a scheme was good, but required one or two minor modifications. The Secretary of State may examine and perhaps reject the modifications, or suggest slightly different ones. I accept that those modifications might not affect the overall principle and would not, therefore, need to be considered by Parliament, but sometimes the differences between the inspector and the Secretary of State could be substantial. A means of giving Parliament a final say in such cases should be built into the mechanism. In those cases, the scheme will be different from the one that Parliament originally approved.

Amendment No. 31 draws the Government's attention to the difficulty that would arise when variations to the decision made by Parliament occurred in the inspector's report. I understand that the Minister would not wish to come back to Parliament with a minor variation from the original scheme. It is difficult to draft an amendment that would pick out a significant change rather than a minor variation. However, that does not mean that it would be impossible for parliamentary draftsmen to find a solution.

Where the Secretary of State reaches a totally different decision from the inspector, it is essential that Parliament has a second chance to deal with the matter. It would be absurd if Parliament approved a scheme, the inquiry presented detailed evidence, the inspector recommended that the scheme should not go ahead because of detailed problems but the Secretary of State approved it without giving Parliament the opportunity to reconsider it in the light of the objections presented at the inquiry and highlighted in the inspector's report. If the Secretary of State reverses the inquiry's decision, Parliament should have the opportunity to have another go at the matter. Such cases will seldom occur.
