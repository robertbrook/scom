Members who spoke to amendments in Committee understood those amendments only after the sitting was over. Having discussed the sittings motion all Tuesday morning and listened to good arguments for not sitting on Thursday afternoon, we then discussed for a further two hours various concessions relating to inland waterways. I am 106 grateful for the opportunity to discuss in detail some of those concessions.

The amendments deal with consultation, and new clause 6 would raise the status of the Inland Waterways Amenity Advisory Council. Is the Minister prepared to concede some of the points that we made during our Tuesday afternoon discussions? I should also like to hear his views on the shopping list outlined during our first sitting by my hon. Friend the Member for Denton and Reddish (Mr. Bennett). It might be helpful for me to remind the Minister for our worries and our wish to rectify the fiasco that has arisen because of the lack of consultation with the inland waterways bodies.

Does the Minister accept that consultation should take place with IWAAC, which is a statutory body, before an order is made that will affect matters coming within its brief? Since the Transport Act 1968, the role of IWAAC as a statutory body, whether under the Department of Transport or the Department of the Environment, has been respected by Government except when considering consultation on this Bill. Bearing in mind that another Bill is going through another place, that highlights the dangers that the Minister will encounter by refusing to accept the need for consultation with a statutory body.

I want clarification of section 110 of the 1968 Act, which establishes IWAAC as a statutory body to advise and make recommendations on the development of amenities and recreation on the waterways—controlled by the British Waterways Board—and enables it to object to an order made by a Secretary of State that affects its brief. That is relevant to new clause 6. Can the Minister assure us on that point?

I wish to consider the wider issues that I hope that the Minister will cover in his statement. Under the Bill, almost anything could happen to waterways. They could be sold or closed down by statutory instrument with minimal opportunity for consultation or appeal. That causes anxiety. It has led many people who care about inland waterways to write to members of the Committee and stress that, although the Government have disregarded their views so far, they wish those views to be taken into account now.

I am pleased that the Minister intends to make a statement. However, I shall outline the problems that the Bill presents for users of inland waterways. I stress again the anxiety caused by lack of consultation with the statutory bodies set up by the Government to advise on inland waterways issues.

IWAAC was not consulted. I hope that the Minister will consult not only IWAAC, but other organisations, especially the British Marine Industries Federation and the Association of Pleasure Craft Operators on Inland Waterways. The inland waterways organisations realised the implications of the Bill late because, until the Bill was published, there was no indication that the measure would have a widespread effect.

The consultation document referred to provisions for the temporary closure of waterways and for works JPEG 107 to construct other transport systems if the powers over waterways in the Bill had been confined to that, with safeguards about the length of closure and consultation, the inland waterways bodies would not have campaigned against the Bill. I stress that the parliamentary agents for the inland waterways organisations advised them that the Bill could be used by British Waterways and other organisations to gain orders that would affect general powers over waterways. However, there may be an overlap between the Transport and Works Bill and the private Bill, being promoted by British Waterways, in another place.

Will the Minister commit himself to amending clause 3(1)(a)? Will he examine the words "the construction or use" of inland waterways? Will he return on Report with new phraseology, relevant to the operation of an inland wateway: Will he commit himself publicly to wording that makes it clear that no provision of the Bill will override the inland waterways provisions of the Transport Act 1968?

Waterway users were worried about the wording of clause 3(1)(a) because they believed that the words "or use" could imply that inland waterways that were classified as commercial or cruising under the 1968 Act could be downgraded by order. The lack of safeguards throughout the Bill's references to inland waterways causes great anxiety.

The Inland Waterways Association is worried that clause 3(l)(b)(i) could lead to a permanent loss of navigation rights on inland waterways. The association stresses that rights of navigation still exist on British Waterways rivers and on some independently owned waterways. The association can live with temporary closures of waterways, on which a public right of navigation exists, for works to construct new transport systems. However, the Bill should include appropriate safeguards about consultation, appeal and length of closure. The association has campaigned in the courts to protect navigation rights. That is one of its published aims. It is unacceptable for the Bill to have a permanent effect on public rights of navigation. It is important that the Minister makes a commitment to temporary changes, but he should not propose permanent changes in the way in which things can be done.

The Minister should also clarify his commitment to subjecting parts of the Bill to affirmative resolution by the House. Labour Members have pushed for that, and I look forward to the hon. Gentleman's response.

Inland waterways users are worried about any change in the governing legislation which at present requires Parliament's consent. Private Bills are subject to the full scrutiny of Parliament and the expertise of hon. Members. The waterways are a national system and boats cruise for surprisingly long distances. An increasing number of newly retired people put a year or two aside to cruise the entire system. Any reduction in the size of the system, whether permanent or temporary, affects the whole system and cannot be regarded as purely a local matter. I hope that the Minister will agree that the Bill should be subject to the affirmative resolution of both Houses.

108 I hope that the Minister will make some concessions on clause 3 and on the matters raised by my hon. Friends at the outset of our deliberations. There is much anxiety that, if the Bill remains unamended, a future Conservative Government would downgrade and close waterways and other transport systems and do away with the canals. I assure the Committee that no Labour Government would do that, but it is well known that British Waterways has been told to maximise profits so that money can be put back into its operations. No Labour Government would contemplate for one moment putting that sort of pressure on British Waterways. However, we fear that the present Government would have no scruples about doing so.

I remind the Committee of the main thrust of this part of the Bill. Essentially, it will replace the private Bill procedure for the authorisation of works with an order-making procedure underpinned, where appropriate, with public local inquiries. The advantages for those with interests in proposed works are considerable. First, those who wish to press for changes in the works, or object to them, will not have to satisfy the stringent rules for locus standi which have to be met by anyone who wishes to appear before a private Bill Committee in the House or another place. Secondly, there will not be the same requirement to employ counsel and pay fees that apply in Parliament. Those and other developments will be of considerable assistance to inland waterways interests.

As railways and canals, for obvious geographical reasons, are often close together, railway works may 110 impinge on a canal. If inland waterways interests need to object to a proposed work promoted by a railway or other undertaker covered by the Bill, who would otherwise have been able to proceed by private Bill, they will be able to use the same, simpler procedure as is used for highways proposals. The same would apply where some proposed new tramway in an urban area has implications for the canals.

There are considerable advantages for those interested in inland waterways from the new procedure. The inland waterways interests, however, have been concerned that there might be some unexpected disadvantages as well. This group of amendments deals with those fears.

The fears of the hon. Member for Stoke-on-Trent, North that the Bill is intended to enable the Government and British Waterways to sidestep existing controls are groundless. There is no hidden agenda in the Bill. It is concerned only with the limited purposes that I explained on Second Reading.
