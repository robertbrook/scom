Due to the generosity of your tea break, Mr. Leadbitter, and the excitement that it caused among some of my hon. Friends, at least one of them has failed to speak to clause 14. He had every intention of enlightening us on the clause, and I was fascinated to find out how he could speak about it for more than 30 seconds. That pleasure now awaits us in respect of another clause.

Amendment No. 191 deals with appeals and tribunals. Although it is not a seemly hour to debate such matters, it behoves me to draw the Committee's attention to the important issues on the way in which appeals will be handled. It may not be widely known outside the House of Commons, even to aficionados of the local government press, that people will not know what their house valuation is—they will be aware only of the band in which it has been placed. Their only way of finding out would be to be aggrieved in the belief that if the property's price had been moved by just over £1,000, it might have fallen into a lower band, thereby saving them about £50 in some instances—certainly 16 per cent. of the amount due. Consequently, many people who would otherwise not be worried because they knew the value of the property that had been designated for valuation purposes and were satisfied that all was fair and above board, might decide to appeal.

The appeals process is likely to be inundated with people who believe that by issuing the appeal they will be put in a lower band and have to pay less council tax. [Laughter.] I did not realise that I had said anything amusing, Mr. Leadbitter. But whatever it was has obviously tickled the fancy of the hon. Member for Thurrock (Mr. Janman), or perhaps it was something under the table, in which case I would rather not know what it is.

In some authorities where the compression of valuations is great, the lower bands take on great Col. 460 significance. In Tameside in greater Manchester and Wigan respectively, 84 per cent. and 74 per cent. of dwellings will be in those two lower bands. People will need to know how close they were, if they are in band B, to being in band A, because it will make a difference to the amount that they will have to pay. There will be many appeals. Many of the appeals from the revaluation undertaken in the non-domestic sector are still being dealt with.

The significance of a batch of appeals being made at the same time is the closeness to the due date for the introduction of the property-poll tax or council tax. That could cause administrative chaos. The property banding will not be published until late in the year and the appeals process will begin in January 1993. As a consequence, shortly before the introduction—if the Government get their way—of the council tax or property-poll tax, there will be an avalanche of appeals. There will also be considerable confusion over the discount system, which we shall deal with in later clauses. Those factors will create a hiatus that will add to the confusion caused by the bitter end of the poll tax.

There will be a confluence of activity; the poll tax will be grinding to a standstill and the collection rates will be causing chaos for local authorities in terms of liquidity and of the problems of chasing outstanding amounts. Those amounts will by then be a debt crisis of gargantuan proportions that could affect the national borrowing requirement. Local authorities will have to draw on the financial sector in order to pay salaries and meet outstanding debt bills caused by the drying-up of the revenue sector because of the imminent demise of the poll tax and the difficulties of collection.

The Government have declined to abolish the 20 per cent. minimum contribution. I expected the Secretary of State to announce with a flourish, during his statement yesterday on the revenue support grant, that, as the Prime Minister had earlier in the afternoon been so proud of the £4 million that the Government—he claimed and I have no reason to doubt him—have allocated to providing extra help in supplementary benefits towards meeting mortgage payments so that people can stay in their dwellings, an equal sum had been found to fund the abolition of the 20 per cent. contribution. That would reduce the administrative chaos. That would in turn help the liquidity and revenue flow of local authorities in the last year of the poll tax, and would ensure that the administrative resources tied up in the collection of the poll tax could be freed to deal with the introduction of the property poll tax or, I hope, of our fair rates proposals.

Administrators would be free to deal with the numerous inquiries that they will inevitably receive because of the introduction of the new tax. Those inquiries will come, quite rightly, from the valuation of property. People will ask on what basis they are in band B instead of band A. A property with a takeaway shop next door might be put in band B, but the owner might decide to appeal because he or she feels that the property was worth less because of the takeaway next door.
