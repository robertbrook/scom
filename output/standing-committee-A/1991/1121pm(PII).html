Mrs Michie: This is a probing amendment to highlight the problems faced by people who rent from private landlords, and to discover whether the Government recognise that often a landlord will pass all or part of the council tax for which he is liable to his tenants. We want to know if the Government will do anything to protect tenants.

When the poll tax was introduced, those who rented in the private sector had the charge imposed on their rent. The landlord benefited from the abolition of rates. Pressures on available property for renting meant that most tenants had no choice but to accept the situation. Will the Government consider introducing a tenants' band, a discount for tenants or perhaps changes requiring the landlord to pay the council tax? None of those things will be plausible or easy to implement. A report to Parliament six months after the implementation of this part of the Bill would enable us to discover whether tenants were getting a raw deal.

Mr. Key: I am grateful to the hon. Member for Argyll and Bute (Mrs Michie) for raising this important issue. Like me, she knows that many people have had unfortunate experiences with landlords, often in the private sector. It is for individual owners or landlords to decide how taxation will be reflected in rents. Landlords account separately for some costs but not for others. They do not have to account separately for overheads such as gas, electricity and water when determining rents, and it is not appropriate that they should be under a duty to account separately for the council tax. However, there is an important distinction which I shall make shortly.

Col. 334 The hon. Lady knows that the effect of her amendment would not be what she or I would wish. It would require the Secretary of State to report to Parliament on every individual who did not own their property but was liable for council tax. That would be impracticable as it would mean identifying every tenant in England, Scotland and Wales and asking them to provide details of their personal finances. It would, in effect, be a national census of tenants.

The council tax has many benefits, one of which is ease of administration. The simple, non-intrusive discount system would be lost if local authorities were asked to provide names and addresses of tenants. The hon. Lady may be suggesting that local authorities should keep a register, which we certainly do not want.

It will be for households to decide how a bill, which may include the council tax, should be divided between those living in a property. They must make the appropriate financial arrangements among themselves. The best approach for houses in multiple occupation is to make the landlord or owner of the property liable for the council tax. We intend to do that under clause 8. It will be up to the landlord or owner to arrange contributions from residents towards rent, as with other overhead costs. In such a case the residents would not be liable and would not fall within the scope of the hon. Lady's amendment.

Although I am sympathetic to the hon. Lady's point, it is up to tenants to ensure that they are aware of their liabilities and know what their landlord proposes in respect of the council tax and whether he intends to include a direct liability for the tax, which is levied on him as the owner rather than on them as the residents. A tenants' band is impractical. It would negate the objective—to relate payment to the property's value for the half of the tax that is based on property values.

Similarly, it would be difficult to create a discount system for tenants, rather than the system that we propose. I am grateful to the hon. Lady for raising an issue that will affect many people across the country. We are aware of that, but it is not in the scope of the Bill to trespass on landlord and tenant legislation. I accept the hon. Lady's point but I resist the amendment.

I was not sure whether we would reach this amendment tonight. When the Minister of State replies to the debate, I hope that he will be able to say—perhaps for the first and last time on the Committee—that the Government accept that our amendment is fair and reasonable and non-ideological, and that they agree to it. [Interruption.] I am grateful for that burst of applause from behind me.

I think that the Government will agree to the amendment because they have often pressed that when council tenants receive a combined rate and rent bill, the rate element—the tax element—should be identified so that people clearly understand what they are paying to their local authority and it is not hidden—that was the expression used on Second Reading.

A Conservative Member said that tenants were often misled into believing that there had been a rent increase when in fact there had been a rate increase. He was slightly muddled because it turned out that there had been a rent freeze, so it was not possible for tenants to have misunderstood. Nevertheless, the point was valid. Everyone should know when he or she is paying tax. It should be clearly distinguished from other payments, such as rent. That is accepted by Conservative Members as fair and reasonable for council tenants and we, too, accept that principle. We now accept that when people receive council tax bills, the tax element should be distinguished in the bill however it is paid, and whether it is paid weekly, monthly or by standing order.

For private tenancies, primarily multiple-occupation tenancies, the landlord should distinguish that element of the bill due for the tax that he is paying on his tenants' behalf. That, again, is a reasonable principle. It is a simple way to identify liability and we are not opposed to the proposed Col. 346 method. We could not be, because that is how it has been done previously and how we want it done under our fair rates.

There remains, however, a major problem. When the poll tax was introduced, many private tenants continued paying the same amount of combined rent and rates even though the rates had been abolished. There is no dispute that some landlords pocketed the difference between the rates and the rent. Some private tenants still pay the old rates to their landlords and the poll tax to the local authority. We should not tolerate that iniquity. In my wildest nightmares, I imagine that some landlords tell their tenants that they will pay the poll tax en bloc at the town hall and that the tenants will end up paying the old rates, the poll tax and the new council tax.

I do not wish to exaggerate but this is a serious matter. When the new tax is introduced—whether it is the council tax or Labour's fair rates—there is a real danger that tenants will again be at a disadvantage and that they will end up paying more than they should because they will not be able to distinguish between the rent and the various additions. They may realise that the rent has increased, but not how much local tax is being levied on them as individuals living in particular properties.

That is a serious matter. It is believed—there is good evidence to substantiate it—that as much as £400 million of rates was paid by tenants to landlords after the rates had been abolished. About £40 million was paid in Scotland and as much as £90 million in London, where there are large tranches of private rented tenancies.

We must not allow that to continue. The Government have expressed some sympathy for tenants but have declined to issue an enforceable regulation to make it illegal for landlords to behave it that way. Whatever our feelings about private tenancies, there is no reason why such unfairness should be allowed to continue under the new tax. There are several reasons, the most obvious of which is equity. People should know what element of their rent is made up of tax and what the increase is to enable them to assess which way to vote in local elections. All of us, including Ministers, entirely accept that principle. It is called accountability.
