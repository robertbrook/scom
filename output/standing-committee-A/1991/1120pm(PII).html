Mr. Cohen: It falls to me to be the after-dinner speaker for the second night running. During the break, I read a reference to the Minister for Local Government and Inner Cities in the diary column of the Evening Standard. I do not know whether the hon. Gentleman saw it, but he was described as a member of the "no combing back group".

As for the poll tax, its parting will not be such sweet sorrow. Amendment No. 34 highlights the problems that will be created by a banding system. It proposes that the council tax should be in proportion to the rateable values of properties, thereby making it more fairer and accurate than a banding system.

There is considerable confusion about the effect that the banding system will have in London. The results of a survey carried out by the London Research Centre showed that 35 per cent. of properties in London will fall into the top bands covering properties whose value is in excess of £88,000. The Government calculate, however, that 55 per cent. of London properties are expected to be worth in excess of £88,000. That is a great difference. The Minister should be truthful; it seems that the Department of the Environment figures are wrong.

Which calculations are right? What will be the effect of those figures on London, especially if the Government are wrong? I wish to highlight the problems caused by introducing the banding system in haste without knowing its exact implications for London. If the survey is right, not so many taxpayers will come into the high bands as was first feared. That would have created a problem as between those in the middle band and the top band. The problem will not be so severe if the survey is right, but higher tax rates will be set and, overall, those in the lower and middle bands will receive higher bills as local authorities in London make up the money lost to them as a result of that calculation.

Over the past few years, house prices in London have varied considerably. Let us compare house prices in 1987 with those in 1991. More properties would have been placed in the higher bands if the valuation had taken place in 1987. The bands are unstable, unreliable and out of line with actual house prices in London. If those properties were sold subsequently, there would have had to be new valuations, which would have created a severe problem when trying to assess the depressed 1991 values.

In 1990 the Halifax building society compared the regional bands within greater London for 1987 with those for 1990.

I shall deal briefly with the problem of disabled people. I say "briefly" because I had hoped that we would discuss this matter on an amendment of its own, but I think that there will be other opportunities in Committee to do so. My objective throughout the framing of the council tax —I speak as an ex-Minister in the Department of Social Security—has been to ensure that we do not require disabled people to pay more, on account of their disability, for adaptations to their property. That led me in August to issue regulations—and a press notice—allowing valuers to disregard certain improvements to properties to make them suitable for disabled people if such improvements would otherwise have increased the value of the property, but to take them into account if they reduce the value of the property.

Organisations representing disabled people have suggested that we have not covered every conceivable situation and that some categories may need further consideration. For example, people who use their wheelchairs at home need turning space and storage space for the wheelchair. I make no commitment this evening, but serious discussions with groups representing disabled people are still taking place. I shall continue to approach the problem in a spirit of determination: that physically disabled people should not have to pay more on account of necessary alterations to their property. I hope that I shall have more to say on that matter at a future stage.

My hon. Friend the Member for Torridge and Devon, West was worried about agricultural properties, which have a lower market value because they are tied to agricultural activities. If a property has a lower market value because of legal constraints on its use, its lower value will be reflected in its valuation for the council tax. For example, a property that is subject to a planning consent restricting its use to occupation by agricultural workers is likely to have a lower market value than a similar property with no such restrictions.

My hon. Friend was also worried about farmers. The valuation will take account of the fact that a farmhouse is part of a working farm. Many working farmhouses have a lower market value than a similar house that is used for purely residential purposes. My hon. Friend will, I know, strongly support the provision to limit bills—three times as much for the most expensive property against the least expensive property in the district—because, if nothing else, that will be an important safeguard for people who happen Image Col. 219 to live in expensive properties. I hope that she will be reassured about the way in which the use of the property is likely to be reflected in the market value that it will be given by the valuer.
