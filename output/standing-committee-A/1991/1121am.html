Mr. O'Brien
: The amendments would change the basis of liability from residence to occupation, in accordance with the General Rate Act 1967, which I consider to be significant in the implementation of any new tax, and in dealing with issues of recovery and insistence on payment. That Act embodies a wealth of case law that is used in pursuing liability for payment of local taxes. Until 1 April two years ago, local taxes had been raised by the rates for nigh on 300 years. Much administrative experience and information was gained from the procedures used for the levying and collection of rates. The General Rate Act 1967 provides the most efficient base for implementing a new tax.

Clause 6 proposes that liability for payment of the new tax should be based on residence in, as opposed to occupation of, a property. Local authority treasurers and financial advisers to local authorities have argued strenuously against proposals based on residence.

The council tax technical sub-group, which consists of officers of the Department of the Environment and representatives and officers of local government, was created to find the best way of introducing the new tax and making it work smoothly and efficiently. Those are the new sub-group's terms of reference.

The poll tax has caused problems because it is based on residence. Magistrates courts are overwhelmed with cases of non-payment brought by local authorities. In August this year, the Home Office issued a notice to local authorities and to the local magistrates courts advising on best practice in the use of bailiffs. The advice was issued because of the overwhelming problems created by the nonpayment of poll tax, of which there are pages of evidence.

Many cases have been brought before the valuation and community charge tribunals by people exercising their right of appeal, and conflicting decisions have been made. The tribunals are not law-making bodies and their decisions cannot be used in a court of law.

260 There is a case on record of a poll tax payer in Bradford who challenged the community charge officer about the definition of the main place of residence. That is the only case that has come before the High Court and it leaves much to question.

We should consider the history of how local government finance has been applied. The General Rate Act 1967 refers to occupation as the best way of helping local government with the introduction of new taxes. In the Bill, the Government propose that liability to pay the new tax be based on residence in the property, not on occupation of it. The Minister should seriously consider changing that proposal, as the amendment suggests.

There is no definition of sole or main residence in the Bill, in the Local Government Finance Act 1988 or in previous legislation. The Bill does not state how "sole or main residence" should be interpreted; there is a void in that respect. That will create problems for local government officers who will have to administer the provisions.

Yesterday morning, at column 148, I raised the question of the inclusion of a garage in the residence. I asked whether, if a large double garage was not connected to the house, it would be included under the heading of "residence". The Minister replied in good faith and referred me to clause 3(6):

"The Secretary of State may by order amend, or substitute another definition for, any definition of 'dwelling' which is for the time being effective for the purposes of this Part."

The Minister said that the Secretary of State could say that the double garage was a dwelling and would therefore be included in the provisions, but everyone would ridicule the idea that a double garage was a residence.

I referred to the work of the council tax technical sub-group which considered an identical issue. The sub-group says that, where a garage is separated from the dwelling by a sufficient distance, it should be regarded as a separate hereditament and would not give rise to the council tax on its own account. It would be for the valuation officer to determine whether a garage should be included in the hereditament and that decision would be subject to appeal. So the council tax technical sub-group says that it is a remote issue and would have to be clarified by the courts.

Under the General Rate Act 1967, that double garage would come under the term "occupation" and not "residence" and would not be interpreted as a place of residence as the Minister said yesterday. The sub-group set up by the Department of the Environment considered that matter and we should be fools not to take note of what the experts say. Whether the definition should be changed from "residence" to "occupation" should be considered in depth. The sub-group's report is interesting and the Minister should study it.

No interpretation of the main residence was given in the Local Government Finance Act 1988. We cannot rely on case law because there are no cases to refer to, but I hope that reference can be made to cases brought to valuation tribunals. In a case before JPEG 261 the High Court in Bradford, a seaman who lived in Bradford stated that he spent most of his time at sea and that therefore his main place of residence was not in Bradford, where he was being held liable for the poll tax, but at sea. He challenged the Bradford community charge registration officer on that issue. The case went to the local appeals tribunal which decided in favour of the seaman and against the community charge registration officer. The officer appealed to the High Court, which decided that a boat could not be described as a sole and main residence by somebody working on it. He was liable in the area where all his furniture and belongings were kept. The court overturned the decision of the local appeals tribunal.
