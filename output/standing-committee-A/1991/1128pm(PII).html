Clause 21
POWERS OF ENTRY

Mr. Maxton
: I beg to move amendment No. 222, in page 16, line 44, leave out "24 hours" and insert "7 days".

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to discuss the following amendments:

No. 252, in page 16, line 45, at end insert—

"(2A) A local assessor when exercising his power under subsection (2) above shall provide the occupier with the option of a visit out of normal working hours if the occupier so wishes.".

No. 253, in clause 89, page 59, line 35, leave out "24 hours" and insert "7 days".

No. 254, in clause 89, page 59, line 36, at end add—

"(2A) A local assessor when exercising his power under subsection (2) above shall provide the occupier with the option of a visit out of normal working hours if the occupier so wishes.".

Mr. Maxton
: I am not convinced—it has been put to me by the assessors in Scotland—that a statutory obligation to give 24 hours' notice or any other notice should not appear in the Bill. A surveyor should be able to turn up at the door, ring the doorbell and, if the householder is happy, to do the job without having to give notice. If there has to be notice, however, it should be seven days, not 24 hours. Two people in employment and out at work when the notice arrives through the post—it will arrive at 9 o'clock in the morning—will not find out about it until 5 or 6 o'clock in the evening when they come home. That is not 24 hours' notice; it is no more than about 12 hours. It is not long enough; they are not able to take time off work to ensure that someone is present when the surveyor arrives. We suggest a more reasonable timescale. The amendment cannot be considered a political obstruction. It is not a matter of principle. It deals with the realities of life. I hope that the Minister might have regard for that fact.

In conclusion, the valuer or surveyor should not produce his authority "if required"; the Bill should simply provide for that person to produce his authority.

Mrs Ray Michie
: I put my name to the amendment, as did my hon. Friend the Member for Eastbourne (Mr. Bellotti), and I tabled a similar amendment for Scotland. I agree with the hon. Member for Glasgow, Cathcart (Mr. Maxton) that in 656 this day and age, giving 24 hours' notice by post that their property is to be inspected is unrealistic. It shows that parliamentary draftsmen are living in the age of the non-working spouse or, indeed, of domestic servants. As the hon. Member for Cathcart pointed out, many households are not like that any more and 24 hours' notice does not give enough time for people to arrange time off work or to move their appointments, or even arrange for a neighbour to look after the house. On the islands in my constituency, one certainly could not guarantee that the post would be delivered within 24 hours. Far from it; some islands do not have the facilities and the boat calls on only three days a week. We live in the days of the citizens charter but the clause gives a power to state officials that is almost arrogant. The amendment reminds the Government as well as the councils that they are there to serve the people, not the other way round.

Amendments Nos. 252, 253 and 254 would give the option of inspections outside the working hours. Why should someone lose a day's pay to help the workings of the tax? Again, I remind the Committee of the citizens charter, which is supposed to ensure that services are provided in a manner and at times convenient to the customer or the client, whatever term the Government use. It may be more expensive because of the overtime payments required when a valuation is made outside working hours, but that is another price that must be paid for a property tax. If the Government believe in service to the people, it is essential that they allow valuations to be made when it is convenient for the householder.

7.15 am

Mrs Fyfe
: I hope that the Government will accept that this is a valuable amendment. If someone is at home when the valuer calls, he is likely to object no more than he would if the meter reader was on the doorstep.

As hon. Members have said, 24 hours' notice is no use for people who are out at work. Someone could arrive to carry out the work before the householder was even aware that they were required to be there. People cannot just disappear from their jobs. Many people cannot phone in and say that they are going to take the rest of the day off, because if they did so they would lose a day's pay. If seven days' notice were required, people would stand more chance of making a flexible arrangement with a colleague to take over their responsibilities for one day.

It would be helpful if the valuer could state whether he would call in the morning or the afternoon, to save the frustration of a person waiting in all day for the valuer who arrives at half-past five.

There is nothing party political in the amendment. If the Government really want to show that they are serious about meeting the needs of working women, they will reconsider the proposal.

Mr. Harry Cohen
(Leyton)
: I welcome the amendment and hope that the Minister will reconsider the clause, which confers a strong power—the power of entry. That has serious implications for civil liberties. Before a power of entry is given, it is necessary carefully to protect people's rights.

JPEG 657 Subsection (2) states:

"At least 24 hours' notice",

but that is not good enough. Some officious authorities will give only 24 hours' notice, and that is appalling.

It is inappropriate in this day and age, when both members of the household may be working or have commitments to fulfil—taking the children to school or looking after elderly parents, for example. There is no time to make alternative arrangements if only 24 hours' notice is given. It is worse—the 24 hours' notice is by post. The post can take 24 hours to arrive at its destination—two or three days if it is second-class post. This is not a satisfactory arrangement, and it is tied up with the penalties in subsection (4) which says that there could be

"a fine not exceeding level 2".

Level 2 is a penalty of up to £100. That is set to increase under the Criminal Justice Act 1991 to £500. It is therefore not right for such short notice to be given. I ask the Minister to reconsider these provisions.

Will the Minister also reconsider subsection (3), which provides that the person from the valuation office must "produce his authority"? That is reasonable, but his authority could be just a card and a photograph. There is a serious problem in some areas where someone simply rolls along, produces a card, claims to be an official and demands to be let in. He then robs the house or intimidates the resident in some way.

There is a danger, if the clause is passed as it stands, that a person will turn up with his card and photograph, claim to be from the valuation office and demand to be admitted to value the house under section 26 of the Local Government Finance Act. People will be intimidated and it will aid those who want to carry out robberies. I hope that the Minister will tighten the clause so that the authority is more powerful and will deter those who might try to use it to commit a robbery.

I cannot see the point of providing for penalties and fining people £500. What could be done if a valuation officer was prevented from entering a property? We obviously need a longer notice period to give the resident a chance to let the valuation officer in, but if he consistently refuses to let him in the valuation officer could threaten to set the valuation in a higher band. That in itself would encourage the resident to admit the valuation officer. I cannot therefore see the benefit of tough penalties in these circumstances. All that makes a strong case for the Minister to take the clause back and to tighten it to protect our civil liberties.

Will the Minister think about that?

Mr. Portillo

The provision represents the bottom line. If there has been difficulty or resistance when the valuer wishes to enter the property, the provision can be invoked. It is a formal position before a serious power of entry can be exercised.

Mr. Maxton

Mr. Maxton
: Subsections (5) and (6) are a relic from the original proposal put forward by the Scottish Office and should not continue to be part of the legislation. When the Government decided to abolish the poll tax and to introduce the council tax, the Scottish Office's original proposal was that the valuation in Scotland would ignore the assessors who are employed already in local authorities in Scotland. The valuation in Scotland would be handled in exactly the same way as it would be in England and Wales and so be carried out by Commissioners of Inland Revenue who would employ private surveyors and estate agents.

It was pointed out to the Under-Secretary that that was a mistake and that it was absurd to ignore the great expertise of assessors in Scotland who also had the staff in place to do the job. After the Scottish Office received representations and the Under-Secretary and his advisers met the assessors, the Government changed their mind, but they did not go all the way. They decided that the assessors would do the work—even though they are employed, independently, by local authorities—but that they would still be under the control of Commissioners of Inland Revenue.

I do not see the purpose of that. Assessors in Scotland have, in the past, carried out valuations without any bother and have kept a balance. The bands are different in Scotland, so there is no necessity for uniformity between the countries. Assessors have created uniformity across Scotland in the past without having to be controlled by the Inland Revenue. Therefore, I suggest that the Under-Secretary accepts our amendments.

Clause 30
AMOUNTS FOR DIFFERENT CATEGORIES OF DWELLINGS

Mr. Murphy
: I beg to move amendment No. 236, in page 19, line 41, after "below", insert

"and having considered the incidence of council tax on the residents in its area".

When I taught, the last lecture on a Friday afternoon was always the most difficult and this is a somewhat comparable situation. The amendment would require billing authorities, before setting their council tax, to have regard to the incidence of the tax at that level 668 on the residents in its area. Its purpose is to ensure that the socio-economic consequences of the council tax are neither forgotten nor submerged beneath the weight of financial pressures placed upon local authorities by the Government. For the purposes of the Committee, it is also to probe the Government on the extent to which it has assessed the number of gainers and losers in the council tax, their status and means, and the extent of any gains and losses.

Members who served on the Committee that considered the poll tax Bill, which went on for three months, not three weeks, will recall, as you will, Sir Michael, as you chaired many of our meetings, that we studied the number of gainers and losers from the poll tax.

The amendment would make every local authority—every billing authority—consider how the council tax within the banding arrangements will affect the people in its areas. There is no doubt that the tax will have different effects in different local authorities. The council will have no discretion in determining how the tax affects its inhabitants, but it can find out what the banding arrangements will mean when the council tax is set.

We debated the question of the bands and their proportionality. There was only a 3:1 ratio between those at the top and the bottom of the bands. We considered that the poorest would be losers and the richest would be gainers. It is important to ensure that before a council sets the final figure of the tax, having taken away precepts, grant and the non-domestic rate, it should consider what will happen to the people in the area.

There will be a fundamental difference in philosophy between Conservative Members and ourselves about the nature of the bands. We consider that differences within the bands will mean that it still is not a fair tax. The old problems of the poll tax where the rich gained and the poor lost, will remain. Therefore, it is incumbent on the local authority carefully to consider the effect that the council tax will have on local people before setting a figure.

In the run-up to the introduction of the poll tax in 1990, many local authorities calculated the number of household and individuals who would be gainers or losers. The majority of each lost, particularly those in middle and lower income ranges. The failure of the poll tax was that it was not related to the ability to pay it. If the council tax is to have any meaning, local authorities must ensure that it reflects people's ability to pay.

Comparable analyses have yet to be undertaken by local authorities. That may take some time because of the absence of reliable information on capital values, household sizes and individual status. However, this week, the Institute of Fiscal Studies produced a study that considered the effects of the council tax within the eight bands set by the Government. The study compares the numbers who will gain or lose in the switch from the poll tax. It also gives the number of gainers and losers if the Government were to alter their proposals and have more bands, which would make liability more closely proportional to bandings on actual values rather than JPEG 669 banded capital values. The report is called "The Proposed Council Tax".

Mr. Key
: It has not been sent to us. I have not seen a copy.

Mr. Murphy
: I shall make sure that the Minister gets a copy as soon as convenient.

The report stated that the council tax is a hybrid tax. It is neither a poll tax, the rates, a property tax or an income tax; in many respects, in its effects on the inhabitants of an area, it is all of those things. The report concluded:

"It is partly a poll tax, as the first and second liable adults each contribute to the bill. It is partly a property tax, as property values also affect bills. But unlike a pure property tax, bills will not rise in proportion to values. The different elements interact. For instance, the size of the 'poll tax' element depends on the property value, so one cannot put precise values on the contributions of each part, but it would be hard to argue that the tax was more than half 'property tax'.


In addition, once properties are in the bottom and top bands, property values have no further effect on tax payable. In some areas most properties fall into just two or three bands. For instance, the DoE (1991b) estimates 65 per cent of properties in South Buckinghamshire and Barnet to be in Bands F and G, and 63 per cent of Manchester properties to be in Band A. In such areas, the 'property tax' element will be largely invisible in terms of local variation and the tax will look more like a hybrid of a household tax and a poll tax."

The Government have made a choice between two principles, that of protecting those on the lowest incomes and that of giving accountability to local voters affected by local spending. The latter will not be breached, not only for those who receive full rebates, but for all those—perhaps 13 per cent of households—receiving rebates. In such cases the new tax will be more like an income tax than anything else.

In summary, the report states that the council tax will be quite a mixture. It will be part household tax, part poll tax, part property tax and part income tax; and that it would be wrong to see the tax wholly or, indeed, mostly, as a property tax. The report's findings are quite dramatic. The Institute of Fiscal Studies found that under the Government's proposals, 27 per cent of families will gain and 37 per cent will lose. If the number of bands were extended—our suggestion last week, which the Government foolishly decided against—there would be as many losers as gainers but the tax burden would be shifted towards those on the highest bands. In other words, it would be shifted towards those who were able to pay.

The report's third conclusion about making liability more proportional to bandings would leave 43 per cent of families better off, but only 32 per cent worse off. If capital values were used, the basis of liability would leave 41 per cent better off and 33 per cent worse off, those at the bottom gaining most. The results of that study make it clear that the council tax will not fully reverse the shift in the tax burden brought about by the poll tax. The Government's refusal to make liability proportionate to bandings or to use actual capital values as the basis of liability will ensure that gains made by the best-off in 1990 are retained as far as possible.

670 That is a damning indictment of the Government. An independent professional organisation comprising economists and accountants has concluded that the council tax will, in many respects, be no better than the poll tax with regard to people's ability to pay. The Government rejected the poll tax, first, because it was not collectable and, secondly, because it was perceived by the majority of people not to be fair. The study rightly maintains that the council tax will be almost as unfair as the poll tax.

Under the amendment, when deciding their budget local authorities would consider the effect of the tax on individuals in their areas. They have no discretion to alter the tax but they can examine how the tax will affect different people within the different bands. If they conclude that the tax is very unfair, they can either reduce the tax and, as a consequence, cut local services or they can increase local services to help those who would be disadvantaged by the implementation of the tax. I have raised an important principle, and I hope that the Minister understands that the relationship between the tax bands and poverty is very real. If the Government are serious about abandoning the poll tax as a result of its lack of relationship with the ability to pay, I trust that they will seriously consider the amendment.
