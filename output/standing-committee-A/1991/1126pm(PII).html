Amendments Nos. 127 and 167 would remove the discount for single people and replace it with one for people on any one of a number of benefits. Amendment No. 184 would make corresponding provision for a second adult in a household.

The amendments reveal the Labour party's position for what it is. It is unfair and unjust, and I hope that all single householders in Britain will take careful note of what the Opposition are proposing. Amendments Nos. 127 and 167 would remove the 25 per cent. discount for a single-person household. That would take us back to the most unpopular aspect of the old rating system. It is not fair that a single person should have to pay the same amount of local taxation for living in a property identical to a neighbouring house that contains two or more adults living in identical circumstances. That is a matter of principle on which there is a clear divide between the Government and the Opposition, and I am happy to go into any election campaign defending that division.

One of the strongest criticisms of the rating system was that single people in identical circumstances and living in houses identical to those of other people in their road—not millionaires, whom the hon. Member for Glasgow, Cathcart (Mr. Maxton) always goes on about, but people in the ordinary streets of Britain where the vast majority of people live, in three-bedroom detached and semi-detached houses—paid the same. Single people living cheek by jowl with couples or with households of three or four people of working age paid the same amount in rates. We considered that unfair and I am delighted that the Government are going to do something about it.
