Anyone looking at that Act would not see some of the subsections referred to in schedule 3, because they were part of subsequent Acts that amended the 1964 Act. We are not just talking about consolidated procedure; I do not know how many Acts are involved—it will be many—so perhaps the Minister, with the help of his experts, could enlighten us.

Paragraph 1(3) could mean the virtual closing of a harbour. It refers to the closing of "part of the harbour", but closing part—perhaps 85 per cent.—can have far-reaching effects, as can the changing of facilities, the disposal of property and the inclusion of uses for leisure, other than sea-going ships for cargo or passengers.

Paragraph 4(c) contains what to any parliamentarian is a most objectionable feature—a provision for the modification of

"any Act or of any instrument made under any Act".

That is any Act of Parliament—general, private or whatever. That is, by any account a tremendous increase in scope.

I believe that that was recommended by the Select Committee, to bring legislation into line with the concept that private harbour Bills should come within the scope of the Act and be transferred to the statutory instrument procedure. The statutory instrument procedure, which we discussed under part I of the Bill, is complicated enough, but is not schedule 3 even more complicated?

The provision would transform the statutory basis of some 600 harbours and ports in the United Kingdom. I accept that many of them are not like Tilbury, Southampton, what is left of Newcastle, Greenock, Glasgow or any of the medium or small ports that are still operative. King's Lynn is an example of a port that has been kept going by ingenious mechanisation. They are usually small harbours, which were significant in the past and are still significant for leisure, recreation and the environment.

Coastal areas are often renowned for their scenery, and include mudflats and many sites of special scientific interest. By their nature, many ancient ports and harbours are sensitive to the relationship between human beings, human endeavours, earlier industrial and navigational techniques and the natural environment. Consequently, 350 great loyalties are felt, not only by people who have lived there—perhaps for generations—but by those who visit those ports and harbours or navigate around them. I do not know whether private Acts cover harbours such as Padstow, Barnstaple and Rye or those on the east coast ports in Essex.

354 The Joint Committee on Private Bill Procedure recommended that

"the permitted scope of Harbour Orders under the Harbours Act 1964 should be broadened so as to allow general powers to be included and a wider range of developments to be authorised".

That was contained in recommendation 9. The Committee mentioned in particular the authorisation of recreational harbours.

Harbour orders can already be used for many matters in connection with harbours. There are some matters where it is not clear how much can be done by a harbour order which need clarification, and some matters which clearly fall outside the scope of harbour orders but which are essentially of local significance and are capable of being dealt with under the harbour order procedures, with safeguards for objectors to make their point and, if those objections are sustained, for a public inquiry before the Minister reaches a decision.

Schedule 3 implements the Government's response to the recommendation of the Joint Committee on those points.

The hon. Member for Newham, South referred to the criteria that the Minister must apply when considering an HRO. These have been expanded in specific respects. They do not give the Minister carte blanche. First, it is made clear in paragraph 1(2) of the schedule that matters relating to the recreational use of a harbour can be dealt with by a harbour revision order. This implements the specific reference in the report of the Joint Committee to the desirability of making provision for recreational harbours, including marinas, in the harbour order procedures. A similar provision is made in paragraph 2(2) to amend section 16 of the 1964 Act, which covers harbour empowerment orders.

Secondly, we will also make it possible in paragraph 1(3) of schedule 3 for a harbour revision order to be made which provides for some diminution in harbour facilities. Strictly speaking, this could not be regarded as meeting the criteria in section 14(2)(b) of the 1964 Act. The Minister must be satisfied that the provisions are nevertheless desirable and this must be seen in the context of section 14 as a whole. The matters which can be dealt with in this way are the closure of part of a harbour, a reduction in the facilities or the disposal of property not required for the harbour.

It will not be possible to seek powers for the complete closure of a harbour under this provision. That would involve the consequential dismantling of the regulatory navigational and conservation functions of the harbour authority which have generally been conferred by Parliament. We consider that a proposal to close a harbour completely should be a matter for Parliament to decide rather than the Secretary of State. A harbour authority will not be able to get rid of its statutory regulatory functions by order.

Thirdly, section 14(3) of the 1964 Act permits the inclusion of provisions

"requisite or expedient for rendering of full effect a provision of the order framed to achieve any of the said objects"—

those set out in schedule 2 to the Act or in subsection (2A), which covers the repeal of an obsolete or otherwise unnecessary local provision. Paragraph 1(4)(a) of the schedule replaces the words quoted with

"any other provisions of the order".

JPEG 355 This is intended to cover the case where provisions are included dealing with the reduction of harbour facilities.

Fourthly, section 14(3) of the 1964 Act permits the inclusion of supplementary, consequential or incidental provisions

"requisite or expedient for the purposes of the order".

Paragraph 1(4)(b) of the schedule brings section 14(3) into line with the similar provisions in section 16(6) which covers harbour empowerment orders. This will remove all ambiguity and make it clear that there is no additional constraint on harbour revision orders which might, for example, prevent the inclusion of safeguards for environmental matters in a harbour revision order.

The 1964 Act provides for the inclusion of provisions repealing or amending provisions in local Acts. Paragraph 1(4) of the schedule widens the power to enable the order to disapply or modify the application of general statutes, but not to repeal or amend them. Harbour revision orders, unlike private Bills, cannot be used to modify or disapply provisions in general Acts or subordinate legislation of general application, except in the special case of section 43(3) of the Docks and Harbours Act 1966.

For example, a harbour authority may wish to disapply the general development order for the purposes of a harbour revision order, as sometimes happens in a private Act, and to deal with the planning authority on the development as a whole, instead of relying on the GDO for some parts and planning permission for others. The effect of paragraph l(4)(b) will be to give greater flexibility, especially where part of a proposed development is subject to normal planning permission, such as with recreational harbours where there might be a related housing scheme. It must make sense to have a single inquiry where the subject matter is linked like that. In that situation, the local authority and the Secretary of State will want to look at the whole scheme. They will not want the GDO to apply to some parts if that does not make sense.

There may be other legislation of general application which it is relevant to modify or disapply in the circumstances of an individual harbour order. A similar provision will appear in part I of the Bill when clause 5(3) is amended on Report. Applicants would have to satisfy the appropriate Minister that any such modification or disapplication was relevant to the particular purposes of the order and he would have to satisfy himself that it did not override safeguards established by Parliament to protect the public interest.

The amendments to the Harbours Act 1964 are intended to do what the joint committee asked for by making specific changes to ensure that harbour orders are capable of dealing with a wider range of matters, which hitherto have been dealt with in private Bills.

That makes more sense and local people understand the implications far better. Parliamentary agents often promote HROs and we expect that they will be involved in the order procedures 356 under part I. I do not envisage any drastic ending of the roles of Members of Parliament or parliamentary agents. Overall, the schedule meets the requirements that the House wanted. Of course, it is never possible to please and satisfy everybody, but the schedule embodies a general approach to how these problems should be considered. I am sure that it will improve the position both for the House and for those who want to object. I hope therefore that the Committee will allow the schedule to stand part of the Bill.
