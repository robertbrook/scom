Lord Reay
moved Amendment No. 25:

Page 6, line 12, at end insert:


("() A person infringes a registered trade mark if in the course of trade he sells or offers for sale goods which by virtue of their name, shape, colour, packaging or labelling or any combination thereof, are similar in overall appearance to the goods covered by the trade mark and in respect of which the trade mark is used by or with the permission of the trade mark proprietor thereof and such sale or offer for sale without due cause takes unfair advantage of, or is detrimental to, the distinctive character or reputation of the trade mark.").

The noble Lord said: While moving Amendment No. 25 I should like to declare an interest in that I am a consultant to a large UK food and drinks company which would like to see the amendment adopted. I might add that it is far from being alone. Several other large and well-known manufacturers have actively been seeking a change to the Bill along these lines, and I believe that they have an extremely strong case which I should like to put to the Committee. However, to avoid confusion I should perhaps state first that the CBI does not hold a single view on the subject. I must assume that JPEG 27 major retailers would not necessarily go along with the amendment, and the reasons for that will become apparent to the Committee in due course.

The amendment deals with the growing problem of own-brand look-alikes; that is to say, major retailers marketing their products for their own shelves in packaging designed to resemble that of a famous brand name or brand leader. A supermarket tomato ketchup appears in a bottle of the same size and shape as Heinz tomato ketchup; a supermarket own-brand disinfectant appears in a bottle of the same distinctive shape, with the grip piercing the bottle, as Dettol; own-brand chocolate appears in the same combination of coloured wrapping as Cadbury's Bourneville. The same thing happens with washing-up liquid, shampoos, deodorants and wine.

The retailers are not infringing a trade mark because they are not using someone else's trade mark, nor even something similar to a registered trade mark, which I believe would also be an offence. They are giving the customer the general impression that their product is the same as that of the famous brand by combining several characteristic features of the packaging that consumers associate with that product. Such features are unregistered and, I suggest, unregisterable. The type of writing on the label, the shape of the label, the positioning of the logo, the colour of the bottle and of the lid in combination and so on. There are an infinite number of ways in which, with some combination or another, the retailer can bring to the mind of the customer the idea that he is picking up a brand leader when in fact it is an own-brand product.

The customer is likely to assume that the brand leader has been specially packaged for the retailer by the manufacturer. In fact that is seldom the case. In the vast majority of instances it is the retailer's own wholly different product packaged deliberately to profit from the customer's loyalty to the famous brand, taking unfair advantage of those brands' reputations and damaging the distinctive character of their trade mark.

Under present law it is theoretically possible to bring an action for passing off, but such actions are in reality difficult to bring. The law requires that the person seeking the injunction has to prove that customers have suffered actual confusion. Manufacturers who have attempted to acquire such evidence have found themselves ejected from the premises of supermarkets. Retailers have also been known to retaliate against manufacturers, so actions are in practice rarely brought. I suggest that a new remedy is needed and that the place for it is in the Bill.

I have seen it argued that the remedy lies in the manufacturer registering his packaging. But that misses the point. However many products a manufacturer registered—and many people would be kept extremely busy if that proposal was followed—there would always be the possibility for retailers to create the general impression of a trade mark by combining together features of packaging that were not in themselves registered. Moreover, the practicalities of registering would be compounded by the fact that manufacturers 28 are constantly changing the packaging of their products to keep up with consumer trends. The practice can be stopped only by making it illegal.

The amendment follows the line of current legislation in other member states of the European Union. In France a remedy exists. An action can be brought for parasitic behaviour and United Kingdom manufacturers have brought successful actions against French manufacturers in French courts. In Benelux countries also a remedy exists. Last year Procter & Gamble sued a Belgian paper producer over a product which had a similar packaging to a Procter & Gamble product. In November, a Belgian court ordered the withdrawal within one week of the offending package. In Germany too brand owners regularly make successful use of the German law against unfair competition and trade mark infringements by proving that an act of piracy has taken place and that the retailer is trying to benefit unfairly from the brand leader's investment and good will.

Look-alikes unfairly profit from the good will and consumer loyalty established and maintained at enormous expense by the manufacturers of famous brands. In doing so they deprive those manufacturers of sales and undermine their investment. No one is objecting to own-brand products, but they should not pose as being a product different to what they are. The amendment offers a chance of remedying this manifestly unsatisfactory situation. I beg to move.

Lord Strathclyde
: This is an important subject and it is quite right that the Committee has taken some time to ponder what this is all about. I am grateful to my noble friend Lord Reay.

I was, to some extent, surprised by the reaction of the Committee which did not seem to relate to the point which my noble friend made. I join that consensus.

There are two reasons that the amendment should be resisted. First, the Community trade marks harmonisation directive sets out exhaustively the rights to be conferred by the registration of a trade mark. There is some latitude in that some of the provisions are cast as optional for member states, but we have taken the fullest advantage of those. It is not permissible to add to the infringement rights provided for in the directive, and the proposed amendment would undoubtedly do that.

I have no desire to hide behind the directive. There is a point of principle which needs to be dealt with here. While I recognise the legitimate objection to goods which copy the appearance of established brands, it is difficult to accept that that should be a trade mark infringement. A trade mark owner has the exclusive right to use the mark and prevent its misuse by others. The Bill, in conformity with the directive, extends those rights so that, for example, where the use of the mark on goods quite different from those to which it is registered would take unfair advantage of the mark's reputation, it can be stopped.

However, it is fundamental to infringement that there is an unauthorised use of the mark or something similar to it. However, in me case of look-alikes, the essential fact is that the mark itself is not being used or imitated.

32 My noble friend Lord Lyell suggested that all that may be covered in subsection (3). That is the point that I have just made. Subsection (3) requires that the mark or something similar to it be used. The point of the amendment is that the mark itself is not used.

A trade mark is registered only after examination by the Patent Office to establish that it is capable of distinguishing its owner's goods and does not conflict with a mark already on the register. Infringement rights derive from that registration and the examination ensures that they are based on something that is entitled to be protected. By contrast, rights envisaged by the amendment would be based solely on the fact that the package has been allegedly copied on which the proprietor has chosen to use his mark but has not copied the mark itself. The scope of such protection would be utterly determinate.

Moreover, under the new law, it will be possible to register the distinctive get-up of a package as a trade mark. It has been suggested that that would not solve the problem; that brand owners would have to register each little detail; and that the protection which that confers could be easily available. I am not sure that I agree with that point of view. If the appearance of the package as a whole is distinctive, there is no reason that it should not qualify for registration. Under the provisions of Clause 9, anything which is sufficiently similar to cause consumers to associate the two would then infringe the registration.

My noble friend Lord Reay suggested that rights similar to those conferred by the amendment already exist in other Community countries. I understand that where that is the case, it is under unfair competition law rather than as trade mark infringement. There is no unfair competition law as such in the United Kingdom. The nearest equivalent is the common law action for passing off. The objection is that such an action involves the need to produce evidence of reputation and damage to it. However, that must be the case with any remedy of that kind, including that proposed in the amendment.

The amendment would require it to be established that the look-alike takes undue advantage of or is detrimental to the mark's distinctive character or reputation. I believe that an action for passing off is a powerful weapon and I am supported in that by my noble friend Lord Cawley and the noble and learned Lord, Lord Oliver of Aylmerton.

The scope for passing off is being increasingly broadened by the courts. In particular, it was held last year in the Elderflower Champagne case that there was passing off because, although the consumers might not be deceived, the misuse of the name "Champagne" was damaging to its attractive power. That is the kind of protection that I believe that my noble friend Lord Reay is aiming at in his amendment.

To sum up, this amendment would add to the rights conferred by the registration of a trade mark going beyond what is permitted by the directive, one of the aims of which is to ensure that these rights are substantially the same in all member states. Moreover, while they are sympathetic to the problems that this amendment seeks to remedy, the Government find it hard to accept that one could be sued for trade mark JPEG 33 infringement without actually using the mark at all. I have to say that, although I was impressed by the examples of my noble friend Lord Reay-for instance, Cadbury chocolate bars, Dettol and tomato ketchup—I do not think that, generally speaking, the consumer is confused and I believe that he does make a real decision as to which brand to buy.

Finally, as I said, passing off provides a powerful remedy. I very much hope in the light of what I have said, and indeed what the Committee has said, that my noble friend will feel able to withdraw his amendment.
