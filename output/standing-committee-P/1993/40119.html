Clause 16 [Rights and remedies of exclusive licensee]:

Lord Reay
moved Amendment No. 43:

Page 8, line 39, after ("mark") insert ("and unless the licence provides otherwise").

The noble Lord said: I should like to move Amendment No. 43 and speak to Amendment No. 43A in the name of the noble Baroness, Lady Hamwee. I think her amendment sets out to do more or less the same as I am trying to do, and I am sure that she will explain how her way differs from mine.

Clause 16 deals with the rights and remedies of an exclusive licensee. As the Bill is drafted, an exclusive licensee is given all the rights of the trade mark owner as if the licensee were himself the proprietor of the mark. This amendment would enable the trade mark owner to contract out of the rights and remedies conferred by the Bill on the exclusive licensee through negotiations with the licensee at the time of granting the licence. Without this opportunity, the trade mark owner could find himself at a disadvantage. For example, a trade mark owner might wish to keep to himself his right of enforcement in order to retain control of his trade mark.

According to the Bill as it stands, the licensee would have the right to bring an infringement action without consulting the trade mark owner, even though the owner might have to be brought into the action at a later stage. Such infringements might be at odds with the way in which the trade mark owner himself wished to protect his trade mark and at odds with his enforcement policy.

Clause 16(1) as it stands is also at variance with provisions in other clauses of the Bill, in particular Clause 17(5) and Clause 18(2) and (7) which deal with various forms of licensing under which most of the rights and remedies can be contracted out of. I refer Members of the Committee to page 9, lines 35 and 40, and page 10, line 25, where they will find the form of words of my amendment or a similar form of words enabling contracting out to take place.

I see no reason why the parties to an exclusive licence should not also be able to contract out of the provisions of Clause 16(1). I should like to hear what the Minister has to say about this, and I beg to move.

Baroness Hamwee
: As the noble Lord, Lord Reay, realised, my amendment seeks to do what his does—rather less economically with words, I regret. I too wanted to explore the relationship between an exclusive licensee and the proprietor. "An exclusive licence" in the meaning according to Clause 24 is a licence: 62

"to the exclusion of all other persons including the person granting the licence".

I look forward to hearing why the parties cannot regulate their own relationships as the other clauses provide.

The Minister of State, Department of Trade and Industry (Lord Strathclyde)
: The Bill, of course, already gives the parties the choice whether to agree an exclusive licence, with all the rights which stem from it, or a non-exclusive licence, as these expressions are defined in Clause 24. The latter can be as restricted or as open as the parties determine. As it stands, the Bill defines what an exclusive licence is and the rights stemming from it, allowing the parties in Clause 17(5) to agree on how the concurrent rights of the proprietor and exclusive licensee will be exercised. That seems to me to be a sensible approach. But my noble friend Lord Reay and the noble Baroness, Lady Hamwee, raised points which I believe deserve further reflection. I shall therefore look at whether, in view of what my noble friend and the noble Baroness said, a change is necessary. In the meantime, I hope that they will withdraw their amendments on the basis of that reply.

Lord Reay
: I am grateful to my noble friend for his assurance that he will look further into the matter. On that basis, I beg leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Lord Strathclyde
: I am grateful to the noble Baroness for her remarks. Like her, I agree that it is worth spending a little time on the clause. I should like to explain in some detail our thoughts behind the clause so that there is no doubt in anyone's mind.

The clause provides for a remedy for a threat to take infringement proceedings where the person threatened is not the primary infringer and the person making the threat has no grounds for doing so. It introduces a remedy for those aggrieved by such threats and defines the relief available to them. It brings trade mark law into line with that for patents and designs and implements a commitment made in paragraph 3.31 of the White Paper.

68 The person aggrieved by the threats will not necessarily be the person against whom they were made. They may, for example, have been made to a manufacturer's customers, and since they may have no interest in defending a possible infringement action they may simply switch suppliers. It seemed only fair that the person who loses business in this way should have a redress against such threats where they are groundless. An alleged primary infringer—the person who himself applies the trade mark to me goods or is the importer of the goods to which the mark has been applied—cannot, however, take advantage of this provision. If he considers that threats of action are groundless, it is up to him to decide on the likelihood of an action ensuing or succeeding and on whether to defend it.

I believe that this is a fair provision. It is clear that a big company can use its position to frighten people by the threat of legal action. My department receives complaints from small companies which feel outgunned in this way. As everyone knows, legal proceedings are expensive—very often extremely expensive. Anyone such as a shop selling goods which are alleged to be infringing who does not have an interest in defending the action is likely to respond by simply switching to another supplier.

It has been said that this provision inhibits the trade mark owner from pursuing counterfeit goods. It must be stressed that this remedy is available only against the groundless threat. The final subsection states that a mere notification—a letter that just hints of infringement proceedings—is not a threat for the purposes of this provision. This subsection is for the avoidance of doubt. We believe that the same applies also to letters which simply ask for information—for example, concerning the source of goods—again provided that the communication does not contain either explicit or veiled threats. I hope that that explains the situation to the satisfaction of the Committee and I trust that the clause will now be agreed to.
