TRADE MARKS BILL [H.L.]

The Committee met at half-past ten of the clock.

[The Deputy Chairman of Committees (The Viscount of Oxfuird) in the Chair.]

Clause 74 [ Fees]:

On Question, Whether Clause 74 shall stand part of the Bill?

The Earl of Harrowby
: I should like to draw the attention of the Minister to Clause 74(1) and (2) (a), where we appear to be giving somebody—it is not clear who—an open-ended authority to charge exactly what he likes. I do not believe that that can be intended. It is rather an important point of principle.

The Minister of State, Department of Trade and Industry (Lord Strathclyde)
: Clause 74 enables the Patent Office and the Trade Mark Registry to require fees to be paid in respect of matters under the Bill. The Patent Office is required to recover its costs through fees and as an Executive Agency must make an average annual return of 6 per cent. on capital employed.

As the last Report to Parliament by the Comptroller-General shows, the Patent Office provides a cost effective service to its customers. Levels of service have improved but fees have been held at the 1991–92 levels, and international comparisons show that the levels of service provided by the UK Patent Office and the fees charged are very competitive. I believe that that demonstrates that the Patent Office is not intent on using this clause other than responsibly, and in any event the fees to be charged under the Bill are subject to agreement by Ministers and to a negative resolution procedure in another place.

The Deputy Chairman of Committees
: Before we continue, can I ask everyone to keep as quiet as possible because it is extremely difficult for the Hansard reporter to hear what is going on when people are shuffling papers.

The Earl of Harrowby
: It was extremely difficult for me also to hear what the Minister said. In fact I missed half of it. I shall obviously have to study what he said very carefully. I cannot believe that it is right to have in a Bill, whatever the conventions, practice or current regulations of the Patent Office are, an authority which is totally open-ended. Some reference should surely be made in the Bill to those restrictions; otherwise one day the department may be segregated from the Patent Office but it would still be operating under the Bill. However, there would be no restrictions on it. I can foresee the day when, like the motor tax, the Chancellor of the Exchequer will be using it as a convenient opportunity for raising funds in the normal course of taxation. One must guard against that.

Clause 86 [ Fraudulent use of trade mark, & in relation to goods]:

The Deputy Chairman of Committees
: I must warn the Committee that should Amendment No. 57ZB be agreed to, Amendments Nos. 57A to 57P inclusive cannot be called due to pre-emption.

Lord Strathclyde
moved Amendment No. 57ZB:

Page 35, line 17, leave out subsections (1) to (4) and insert:


("(1) A person commits an offence who fraudulently—

  1. applies to goods or their packaging a sign identical to, or likely to be mistaken for, a registered trade mark, or
  2. sells or lets for hire, offers or exposes for sale or hire or distributes goods which bear, or the packaging of which bears, such a sign, or
  3. has in his possession, custody or control in the course of a business any such goods with a view to the doing of anything, by himself or another, which would be an offence under paragraph (b).


(2) A person commits an offence who fraudulently—

  1. applies a sign identical to, or likely to be mistaken for, a registered trade mark to material intended to be used—
    1. for labelling or packaging goods,
    2. as a business paper in relation to goods, or
    3. for advertising goods, or
  2. uses in the course of a business material bearing such a sign for labelling or packaging goods, as a business paper in relation to goods, or for advertising goods, or
  3. has in his possession, custody or control in the course of a business any such material with a view to the doing of anything, by himself or another, which would be an offence under paragraph (b).


(3) A person commits an offence who fraudulently—

  1. makes an article specifically designed or adapted for making copies of a sign identical to, or likely to be mistaken for, a registered trade mark, or
  2. has such an article in his possession, custody or control in the course of a business,
knowing or having reason to believe that it has been, or is to be, used to produce goods, or material for labelling or packaging goods, as a business paper in relation to goods, or for advertising goods.


(4) For the purposes of this section a person acts fraudulently if he acts—

  1. with a view to gain for himself or another, or with intent to cause loss to another, and
  2. without the consent of the proprietor of the registered trade mark.


(5) A person does not commit an offence under this section unless—

  1. the goods are goods in respect of which the trade mark is registered, or
  2. the trade mark has a reputation in the United Kingdom and the use of the sign takes or would take unfair advantage of, or is or would be detrimental to, the distinctive character or the repute of the trade mark.


(6) It is a defence for a person charged with an offence under this section to show that he believed on reasonable grounds that the use of the sign in the manner in which it was used, or was to be used, was not an infringement of the registered trade mark.").

The noble Lord said: In moving Amendment No. 57ZB, I shall speak also to the other amendments in the grouping. Clause 86 of the Bill as it stands aims to tighten up the law against counterfeiting. Comments made during Second Reading and subsequently, however, have led the Government to believe that Clause 86 goes too far. As well as deliberate counterfeiters, it would also catch the person who 96 infringes a trade mark inadvertently. We have therefore tabled an amendment which replaces subsections (1) to (4) of the clause.

The intention of the amendment is therefore to focus the offence more directly on deliberate counterfeiting. Therefore the clause no longer proceeds by reference to "infringement", the inclusion of which would read into Clause 86 all the provisions of Clause 9 (which deals with the civil infringement of a registered trade mark), with a resulting considerable increase in the complexity of the offence to be proved. Moreover, it is proposed that the defence that the person charged believed on reasonable grounds that he was entitled to use the sign be substituted for the reference to belief that the use was licensed by the proprietor.

We also take the view that the reference to "similar" to a trade mark may be inappropriate, since it imports the notion of likelihood of confusion into this clause. As well as making the offence too broad, this could be difficult for a criminal court to handle. We therefore propose that this ought to be changed so as to require a test of degree of resemblance to the registered trade mark more stringent than merely confusion. Instead it should be something closer to deception of the customer. Therefore, we propose that the test should be that the sign is "likely to be mistaken for" the registered trade mark.

Where a sign identical to, or likely to be mistaken for, a registered trade mark is used on goods in respect of which the trade mark is registered, this is to be taken as amounting to deliberate counterfeiting. However, counterfeiters also attempt to exploit the reputation of well-known brands by, for example, putting trade marks which are famous for luxury goods, such as perfumes or cosmetics, onto garments. We propose therefore that, where the goods are not goods in respect of which the trade mark is registered, but the trade mark in question has such a reputation that use of the mark in this way would take unfair advantage of, or be detrimental to, the distinctive character or the repute of the trade mark, that also should be caught by the provision.

We also propose making the defence under this provision less restrictive. As drafted, subsection (5) of Clause 86 requires a person charged to have a reasonable belief that the use was licensed by the proprietor of the mark. Even if "licensed" is construed as simply meaning "permitted", the Government accept that this is a very restricted defence. We therefore propose replacing it by a requirement for a reasonable belief that the use was not an infringement. This would cover the use of someone acting in ignorance, or in a genuine belief that what he was doing did not require the consent of the proprietor, rather than taking part in deliberate counterfeiting.

Before the Government were able to table this amendment, the noble and learned Lord, Lord Oliver of Aylmerton, tabled a series of amendments which also aim to improve Clause 86. The new amendments pre-empt them. I know that the noble and learned Lord cannot be with us today, but I understood him to have said that he was satisfied with the way in which the JPEG 97 Government have drafted this new amendment. I hope that it meets with the approval of the Committee. I beg to move.
