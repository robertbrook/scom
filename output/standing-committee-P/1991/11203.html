The Committee met at a quarter-past three.

[The Chairman of Committees in the Chair.]

The Chairman of Committees (Lord Aberdare)
: I shall make a few remarks on our general procedure which may be helpful to the Committee. The procedure is as far as is possible the same as for a Committee of the Whole House. Your Lordships should speak from a standing position and observe the same degree of formality as we do in the Chamber.

The record of proceedings this afternoon is being made from a tape-recording. Therefore if you would please try as far as possible to talk into a microphone it would help the Hansard reporter.

If there is a Division in the House it is for the Committee to decide whether we adjourn for a fixed time or until the result off the Division is known. Previously we have found it best to adjourn for 10 minutes and I would recommend that arrangement if Members wish to divide when the Whole House divides. Members of the Committee are, however, fairly equally divided between the parties and may feel that it is not necessary to go to the Division of the Whole House. If they do wish to attend, I would recommend that we allow 10 minutes.

Noble Lords

The Chairman of Committees
: If we have a Division in this Committee the doors are locked six minutes after the Question has been put and challenged unless all Members of the Committee are present. A Division in this Committee will be broadcast throughout the House on the annunciators and Strangers are not required to leave the Committee Room when a Division is called. Six minutes after the Question has been put, or when all Members of the Committee are present, the Chairman will say, "Lock the doors". As soon as the doors are locked he will put the Question for the second time. If it is still challenged, the Clerk will read out the names of Members of the Committee and each Member will reply either "Content", "Not Content" or "Abstain". Then the Chairman will announce the result of the vote in the usual way, the doors will be unlocked and we shall continue with the Committee stage.

Lord Young of Dartington
moved Amendment No.1:

After Clause 1, insert the following new clause:

("Role of Charity Commissioners

.In section 1 of the Charities Act 1960, for subsection (3) there shall be substituted—

"(3) In order to strengthen their role the Commissioners shall (without prejudice to their specific powers and duties under other enactments) have the functions of—

  1. generally fostering and supporting charities;
  2. encouraging charitable giving;
  3. drawing attention to needs which could be more fully satisfied by charities;
  4. advising the Secretary of State on the policy, including taxation policy, which should be adopted towards charities;
  5. drawing attention to the good practice of particular charities from which others could learn;
  6. promoting the effective use of charitable resources by encouraging the development of 8 better methods of administration, by giving charity trustees information or advice on any matter affecting the charity and by investigating and checking abuses." ").

The noble Lord said: I should like to begin by reading the subsection of the Charities Act 1960 which this amendment seeks to deal with. I think it is the only section of that 1960 Act which deals with the general functions of the Charity Commission. It reads:

"The Commissioners shall, without prejudice to their specific powers and duties under other enactments, have the general function of promoting the effective use of charitable resources by encouraging the development of better methods of administration by giving charity trustees information or advice on any matter affecting the charity and by investigating and checking abuses".

To my mind at any rate the case for the amendment is fairly straightforward. The Bill as it stands has, it appears to me, an unduly negative cast to it. Anybody who came wholly afresh to this scene might easily imagine that charities are riddled with abuse and that a Charity Commission is needed in order to check that abuse.

The Explanatory Memorandum of the Bill starts by saying that its whole focus is to strengthen the role of the Charity Commissioners. However, it then goes on to say that it will do that by extending their existing power to investigate charities and to check abuses. The first new Bill on charities for 30 years should not have such a complete emphasis on abuse as that, important though it is. The role of the Charity Commissioners certainly needs to be strengthened but not just so as to increase their regulatory powers. There is surely an old-fashioned pre-Thatcher assumption here about the role of the state which is being buried in the Bill; it is that organs of the state are to regulate and nanny enterprise, not to foster it.

The Government have in other of their policies recognised that charities have a far more important part to play in a modern society than they used to have. They have been helped by tax concessions and by government grants. Charities are being seen as partners both by the state and by private business, so is it not anachronistic that the Charity Commissioners as the chief and chosen instrument of government policy should be cast here just as a regulatory body? Let the commission be associated with the general run of charities which are doing a good job and not with the field of abuse. Let it be the friend and supporter of charities and it will be of much more use to society. If it were not just a policeman but also a friend and comforter of charities, I think its necessary regulatory functions would be more readily accepted.

So to the detail of the amendment. It starts with the statement taken from the Explanatory Memorandum about the general objective, which is to strengthen the role of the Charity Commissioners. Then comes paragraph (a), which is the first addition to the clause. It would give the commissioners the function of generally fostering and supporting charities and make it quite clear that their function was to do this in general and not just in relation to any one particular charity which might call upon their help.

The second function, which would be a new function, or at least stated here for the first time if it got through in an Act of Parliament, would be to give JPEG 9 them the function of encouraging charitable giving. I think that they would speak on this subject—obviously a very important subject—with an authoritative voice in support of the Charities Aid Foundation, the NCVO and other bodies which are already promoting charitable giving. The commissioners could give advice to solicitors who are helping people draw up wills; they could buy some of the giant European foundations like Volkswagen, Agnelli and others which may extend their sphere to Britain after 1992, and they could also do the same with many great North American foundations.

In many other ways too they could lend support to all those who are trying to increase the amount of charitable giving there is so that the charities that exist now and the charities which might exist in the future would have more sources with which to work.

Moving on to paragraph (c), the function here would be to give the commissioners the power to draw attention to needs which could be more fully satisfied by charities than they are at present.

The new Chief Commissioner of Charities, who has made such an excellent start, spoke recently in a lecture named after Lord Goodman about the far-sightedness of Joseph Rowntree who set up a charity which had as its general objective the relief of poverty. He pointed out that the shape that poverty takes and the kind of needs that particular poverties give rise to are constantly changing and need to be scrutinised, and that there is always a need for new ways in which old purposes can be served. I think it would apply to many fields of charity as it would be of very great value to society if the commissioners had a function to point to gaps in what is at present provided.

Paragraph (d) speaks for itself. If this function were operative all charities would have a spokesman when it comes to taxation and other policies, and I am sure that most charities in the country would greatly welcome it if they thought that the Charity Commission was acting in this way on their collective behalf. Paragraph (e) could mean that the commission had a particularly useful role in spreading good practice and not go in the other direction from what we hope it will do in checking abuse. It would seek out good practice—it will hear of good practice anyway—and let other charities know what that good practice is so that they may consider whether they wish to adopt it.

Paragraph (f) only repeats what is already in the 1960 Act and I think that if this amendment were accepted it would be necessary to repeat that. This is my submission. I beg to move.

Lord Renton
: Perhaps I should confess, as my noble friend Lord Ferrers said at Second Reading. I have a responsibility with regard to the 1960 Act but that does not close my mind to improvement. Indeed, I welcome the Bill as a whole.

I greatly appreciate the work which the noble Lord, Lord Young of Dartington, like other noble Lords on this Committee, has done in helping charities. I wish I could go along with him in supporting this amendment. If I may say so, it seems to me good in parts, and I hope that the Committee will bear with me if I go into some detail in considering it. But, taking it in the broad, I am very much afraid that if we added this to the law it would cause confusion, controversy and some doubts in interpretation.

I now come to the detail. Let me take each of the paragraphs in this new subsection in turn. In Paragraph (a) the word "fostering" is innocuous. I certainly would not object to it. The word "supporting", however, has certain implications. If the Charity Commission, which has no financial powers—it has never been voted money for paying out to charities—was asked to support charities in general or in particular, a controversy would arise at once as to whether its support was to be financial, which is what so many charities are seeking, or what other kind of support it would be? I should be chary of having the word "supporting" put in.

"Encouraging charitable giving" is fairly innocuous. It is something which the Government do anyway and which Chancellors of the Exchequer—especially the present Prime Minister when he was Chancellor of the Exchequer—have made provision for by giving really great incentives in charitable giving (for example, in the Finance Act of, I think, 1989) to enable anyone who gives anything from £800 to £1½ million to deduct it from his general taxable income. That is very supportive and encouraging of charitable giving. As I say, paragraph (b) is fairly innocuous.

However, I am very worried about paragraph (c):

"Drawing attention to needs which could be more fully satisfied by charities".

Where would the priorities lie? There are vast numbers of suggestions that could be made and we enter in then to the realm of party political discussion and, indeed, controversy as to which needs should be more fully satisfied by charities, which should be less fully satisfied and so on. That is not something which I think the Charity Commission should be asked to do.

Then I come to paragraph (d):

"advising the Secretary of State on the policy, including taxation policy, which should be adopted towards charities". That is the only paragraph in the subsection which I would be broadly in favour of. But I rather doubt whether it is necessary to state it in the Bill. We then come to paragraph (e):

"drawing attention to the good practice of particular charities from which others could learn"

I think that is inviting a comeback. "Particular charities" would mean named charities. If it were to say in its annual report, in very broad terms, that there are some charities which have a good practice for doing this and that, and that it is a pity some other charities do not follow it, that would be all right. But for us to have it as part of statute law that specific charities should be mentioned from which others, the lesser brethren, should learn is something which we should somehow try to avoid.

I therefore come on to paragraph (f):

"promoting the effective use of charitable resources by encouraging the development of better methods of administration".

Innocuous down to there, but they are then invited to go on,

"by giving charity trustees information."

: I think I understand what the noble Lord, Lord Young of Dartington, wanted in putting down this amendment. He gave some very good reasons why he thought it should be included.

The Charity Commissioners will act in a similar way in regard to their responsibilities. They will be friends of the charity world but periodically they will have to take action against some people.

My noble friend Lord Renton made a clear exposition, if I may say so, of why we would also have doubts about having these words in the Bill. My noble friend said he thought it would cause confusion, controversy and doubt. I think that is correct. I think that some of the functions of the Charity Commission should not, as he said, be made obligatory. Of course, the amendment would give the Charity Commissioners a number of functions which are additional to those which are specified in the existing Section 1(3) of the 1960 Act, as well as elsewhere in the Act and in this Bill. I do not think that these extra functions are appropriate to the commissioners and in some respects they could give rise, as my noble friend said, to a conflict with the commissioners' existing statutory responsibilities which are essentially those of supervision and regulation.

The first duty which the noble Lord seeks to give to the Charity Commission would be that of generally fostering and supporting charities. That sounds very reasonable, but of course the commissioners' discharge of their functions as a whole contribute to the support of charity in general and the charitable sector in particular by enhancing public confidence in the effectiveness and accountability of charities. I think that a specific duty to foster and support charities does not sit easily with the commissioners' duty to take firm and even harsh action in respect of particular charities.

In paragraph (b) he seeks to encourage charitable giving. Again, if public confidence is enhanced, people will be encouraged to give to charity. Once again I do not think that it would be appropriate to give the commission a specific responsibility to encourage charitable giving as this implies that it ought to promote charitable giving. That is something which I think is best done by the charities themselves and by their supporters. The Government play their part by providing fiscal incentives and new ways of giving JPEG 13 such as gift aid and payroll giving. The Government in fact give substantial sums of money to charities—some £2.4 billion in all and £800 million in tax relief.

The next point the noble Lord made was to draw attention to needs which could be more fully satisfied by charities. My impression is that the charitable and voluntary sectors are very good at identifying and meeting new needs. We have only to look at the number and diversity of charities concerned with AIDS which have come into being in the past few years. The Charity Commissioners' area of expertise does not extend to what is not being done by charities, as opposed to what is being done.

The noble Lord also seeks to get the Charity Commissioners to advise the Secretary of State on policy, including taxation policy to be adopted towards charities. My right honourable friend the Home Secretary and his colleagues will of course always take into account the experience and expertise of the Charity Commissioners when they formulate their policies towards charities. That is inherent in the Home Secretary's relationship with the commissioners and with their general role and functions.

That is shown up by the example of the commissioners' involvement in the preparation of the White Paper and indeed on this Bill. Of course, we will always invite their contributions but in the end taxation policy is a matter for my right honourable friend the Chancellor of the Exchequer. However, that does not mean to say that charities and the Charity Commission cannot give their opinions.

Then we come to the suggestion that the Charity Commission should draw attention to the need for good practice of particular charities from which others could learn. The commissioners issue and will continue to issue guidance on good practice in matters which are within their remit. Would not a general duty to advise on good practice be going too far and too wide? In our discussions we have mentioned the development of self-regulation within the charity world to complement the statutory regime. Spreading examples of good practice is very much a part of that. The duties consequent on registration are not onerous and we have indeed taken considerable care to try to keep the load as light as possible for small charities. As there are positive advantages to charities by being registered I hope that Members of the Committee will consider it desirable to keep the provisions already set down in the Bill.

Lord Richard
: I should like to say a few final words. Use or occupation could imply casual use or occupation. It does not necessarily imply regular use and occupation. It certainly does not need to imply any form of legal instrument as a result of which there is use and occupation. It is the sweeping nature of the phrase that concerns me. It could, for example, include the village cricket team, if that happened to be a charitable trust, which does not own any land but which plays every week on the local pitch. In so doing it would be "using" land. The team would be a charity within the terms of this subsection that had the use or occupation of some land. Is it seriously suggested that the team ought to be registered on that ground? Perhaps it should be registered on other grounds, but I would suggest not on that ground.

Lord Renton
: But has the noble Lord any evidence that the expression "use or occupation of land" has given rise to any difficulty?

Lord Richard
: I am bound to say that the amendment is not something that I dreamed up in my own head. It emerged as a result of representations which were made to me by some charitable organisations that this could cause difficulty and might do so and that the legislation would be better without it. I cannot put it any higher or lower than that.

I do not detect any great groundswell of support for the amendment in the Committee.

Earl Ferrers
: I wonder whether I can help the noble Lord. He said that representations had been made to him about this. The provision has been in the 1960 Act for the past 31 years and we have had no representations made to us that this has been an inconvenience: in fact rather the reverse.

Lord Richard
: Perhaps those making the representations thought they would get further making them to me than to the noble Earl. However, the fact of the matter is that it was felt that, if the amendment were accepted, the situation in respect of small JPEG 39 charities would be improved and one danger - namely, that casual use and occupation of any land might mean that a charity had to be registered although its income was less than £1,000 a year - would be avoided.

However, as I said, I do not detect a great deal of support for the amendment and I ask the Committee's leave to withdraw it.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.
