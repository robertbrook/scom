Clause 58 [Professional fund-raisers etc. required to specify institutions benefiting and arrangements for remuneration]:

[Amendment No. 73 not moved.]

Lord Swinfen moved Amendment No. 74:

Page 49, line 44, at end insert:


("the format of this statement to be established as is prescribed by regulations made by virtue of section 62(2) (d), after the broadest possible consultation with charities, the NCVO, the ICFM and other appropriate bodies.").

The noble Lord said: In moving this amendment I shall speak also to Amendments Nos. 78 and 80. The way a clear and accurate statement is worded will be critical to the success or failure of fund-raising. There should therefore be the broadest possible consultation between the Secretary of State and the charitable sector as to how this statement might be worded. I beg to move.

The Minister of State, Home Office (Earl Ferrers): I appreciate the anxieties behind this amendment. The requirement for a clear and accurate statement appears to be a daunting one. Fund-raisers may be unsure in particular circumstances whether any statement they might make falls within the law. I have been exploring ways in which the requirements of Clause 58 might be mitigated, and I intend to introduce amendments to that end before the next stage of the Bill.

In the meantime, I do not think that my noble friend's amendments are the best way forward. It seems to me that the Secretary of State would be faced with an impossible task in determining in regulations exactly what information should be disclosed by a professional fund-raiser or a commercial participator in any given circumstance. There are a multitude of different forms of fund raising, and I do not see how the Secretary of State could legislate for all of them.

It seems preferable to keep this requirement a general one, so that the fund-raiser or participator himself can satisfy himself in his particular circumstances, whether or not any statement made by him meets the requirements of Clause 58. I do not think that that should be difficult. I agree with my noble friend's concern that the Government should consult widely in drawing up regulations when the Bill becomes law. I have said before, and I am happy to repeat now, that we will consult to ensure that regulations under Clause 62 of the Bill are as near right as we can get them.

Lord Swinfen: I thank my noble friend for his assurances. I beg leave to withdraw the amendment.

Viscount Astor: These amendments seek to make a significant change to the arrangements in Clause 59 Image Col. 231 which will allow donors, in certain circumstances, to obtain a refund of a donation or payment made to a professional fund-raiser or commercial participator.

My noble friend Lord Ferrers explained during the Second Reading of the Bill why we think these provisions are important. They provide an additional safeguard for the interests of the donor who may respond generously to an emotive appeal but who later realises that the donation he has made is financially untenable for him. Clause 59 therefore provides a cooling off period for the donor who makes a payment with a credit or debit card in response to appeals made by television, radio or telephone. As my noble friend said at Second Reading, such appeals can have an emotional impact which clearly justifies such an additional safeguard.

Clause 59 will allow a donor to claim a refund whatever the amount of his donation. The amendment tabled by the noble Lord, Lord Beaumont of Whitley, would limit the availability of a refund only to those cases where a donation was made in excess of £50.

I am sure that it would not be right to limit the effect of Clause 59 in this way. A donation of £50 made on the spur of the moment, in response to an emotional appeal, may well cause severe financial problems for a person of modest means. To limit the availability of a refund under Clause 59 in the way suggested would be inequitable and would be to prejudice the interests of many donors. However, we have some sympathy with the view that professional fund-raisers of commercial participators should be able to deduct expenses properly incurred in connection with processing refunds, and I shall come forward with an amendment to achieve that before the next stage of the Bill.

There is, however, one respect in which it might seem an anomaly. The provisions to be applied to broadcast appeals by professional fund-raisers do not also apply to broadcast appeals by charities acting on their own behalf. That is provision contained in Clause 59 for a cooling off period where a donor makes a payment by credit or debit card. This allows the donor in certain circumstances to cancel his donation or payment. As I have said, broadcast appeals on television or radio can be very emotive. People respond generously. They might then realise that they have responded too generously and cannot, on reflection, afford the amount that they offered. The provision provides a safeguard for the interests of the donor.

Is it right that the donor should have a safeguard only where the appeal is made by a professional fund-raiser? Charities can play on the emotions too. We shall consider whether we need to put the broadcast appeal by a charity or charities on the same basis as that made by a professional fund-raiser. On that point we are also considering whether the 14 days should be reduced to seven days in Clause 59(1). We seek to find the right balance for the protection of the donors and in the interests of commendable fund-raising by professionals and charities themselves.
