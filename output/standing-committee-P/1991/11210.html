Clause 27 [ Annual returns by registered charities]:

[ Amendment No. 47 not moved.]

[ Amendment No. 48 had been withdrawn from the Marshalled List.]

Viscount Mountgarret
moved Amendment No. 48A:

Page 24, line 8, at end insert:


("() Nothing in this section shall apply to a charity the gross income of which in its last financial year did not exceed £25,000.


() The Secretary of State may by order amend the above subsection by substituting a different sum for the sum being specified there.").

The noble Viscount said: We discussed earlier the principle of the level at which a charity with a small amount of income may be involved in the various provisions of the Bill in relation to accounts and annual reports. Members of the Committee were not entirely in favour of the principle. The purpose of moving this amendment is to inquire of my noble friend the Government's view about exempting charities on a low level of income. There is no reference to a level of income in this clause in relation to the necessity of making annual returns.

It seems that any registered charity with whatever level of income at its disposal, large or small, will have to make annual returns. I question whether the necessary expense involved in having to make those returns should be borne by a very small charity with a low level of income.

The level which I have suggested in the amendment is open to question. It may be too high. I have an open mind about it. However, I should like to know my noble friend's view about the principle that charities with a very low level of income should be required to make annual returns. I beg to move.

The Minister of State, Home Office (Earl Ferrers)
: The amendments tabled my noble friend Lord Mountgarret and the noble Lord, Lord Ennals, would take the exception from the accounts provision of the Bill one stage further for charities with an income of £25,000 or less. That would remove from such charities the requirement to prepare and submit to the commissioners an annual report and return.

There is little additional burden on trustees of registered charities if they are asked to write a short report on their activities and to make an annual return of information about their charity. That would include matters of names and addresses of trustees and so on and a requirement to send the documents with the accounts to the commissioners. The commissioners are given power to dispense with the requirement for an annual return in the case of a 136 particular charity or class of charity if they should think fit. Doubtless that power could be exercised to relieve the burdens on certain small charities if that seems sensible at some future date.

We have given a lot of thought to the financial levels set in the Bill because of the variety of charities and the level of their activities and sources of income. We think we have that about right. We have provided for the Secretary of State to be able to alter the figure of £10,000 by order if that proves sensible in the light of experience. Therefore, I hope that my noble friend will agree that that is a reasonable level at which to set the requirements.

Viscount Mountgarret
: My noble friend referred to a level of £10,000 and I did not quite catch what he said, and I apologise for that.

Earl Ferrers
: We have provided for the Secretary of State to be able to alter the figure of £10,000 by order if it proves sensible in the light of experience.

Viscount Mountgarret
: I am grateful to my noble friend but in this clause there is no figure to alter. In order to be consistent, perhaps the Government will consider inserting a figure of £10,000. That would be consistent with the level in Clause 21. Is my noble friend prepared to consider that?

Earl Ferrers
: The point is that charities which have an income of less than £10,000 can produce the simplified form of accounts. Charities with an income of over £100,000 must produce audited accounts. Therefore, there are two levels. I believe it would be unnecessary to have an additional level of £25,000.

Viscount Mountgarret
: I am grateful to my noble friend. I am glad to know that the commissioners will have power to dispense with such a requirement. The matter has been discussed. I believe that there is a degree of sympathy for the principle but I can understand that it may be undesirable for that matter to be written into the Bill. I beg leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

"to provide any person who so requests in writing with a copy of the charity's most recent accounts".

Persistently to be in default in relation to those obligations is made a criminal offence by Clause 28. That would seem a little hard unless the trustee who was to be prosecuted actually knew that there had been this default. In those circumstances it seems to me that the word "knowingly" inserted in the appropriate place would clear up that matter and make it absolutely clear that only the trustee who has been personally in default will be caught by this subsection. I beg to move.

Earl Ferrers
: I can understand what the noble Lord is aiming at with this amendment. The proposed offence is already very carefully worded so that trustees who are merely careless or forgetful cannot commit the offence. It requires that the default, whether it be failure to submit the annual return or failure in connection with the provision of accounts, should have been persistently committed. A trustee who fails once to fulfil one of those requirements will not be committing an offence. It also requires that the default must have arisen "without reasonable excuse". The trustee who has a reasonable excuse for being in default will again not be committing an offence. He will be committing an offence only if he is both persistent in his failure and has no reasonable excuse for his persistent failure.

It is difficult to see what the amendment would add because in order to have persistently defaulted a trustee would have to have ignored reminders without a reasonable excuse. If he had ignored reminders and had no reasonable excuse I cannot see how he would be in the position in which the noble Lord, Lord Richard, would wish him to be of not "knowingly" being in default. The point is that if these reports have not been submitted the person will have 10 months in which to submit them. If they have not been submitted it is likely that around six weeks after that there will be a reminder from the commissioners. If there is still no report there will be a number of reminders. It is only at the very end when after all those reminders the trustees still have not fulfilled their obligations that action can be taken. Therefore I do not think that the word "knowingly" would have any relevance, though I understand why the noble Lord has moved the amendment.

Lord Renton

In any event, if it is a question of the word "knowingly" implying knowledge of the law, we all know that lack of knowledge of the law is no defence anyway.

Lord Houghton of Sowerby
: This is an example of where the lawyers get to when they want to lay on guilt with a trowel. This person must be:

"knowingly, without reasonable excuse… persistently in default".

If he is all those things, he is sure to be guilty. I remind the Committee that the great mischief of the term JPEG 139 "without reasonable excuse" is to be found in the law of value added tax. We have special tribunals which sit all day, every day, every week to consider pleas of reasonable excuse. If one reads those cases one is astonished at the length to which administration can go where there is no apparent discretionary flexibility about imposing penalties.

One case last week involved a small child of two or three years of age. He got at his father's papers and pushed them all down the lavatory. The Customs and Excise held that this man did not have a reasonable excuse and that he ought to have taken more precautions to see to it that the child did not do any such thing. The father went to the tribunal, bless your life, which decided that it was not a reasonable excuse that that happened. So surely he will keep the lavatory door locked in future. This is another example of where we get to when we are trying to provide for degrees of delinquency. The term "without reasonable excuse", if it is to be repeated and repeated, will require another layer of the judicial system to decide questions in dispute.

If a person is knowingly and persistently in default without reasonable excuse he knows he is in default. It is a question of whether he has a reasonable excuse. In this case he must be in default not only once but several times.

I ask the lawyers to consider what they are doing to the poor citizen who has got into a mess with his affairs. They try to catch him on every side. We ought to reduce the length of the formula which will either send a defaulting person to prison or subject him to other penalties.

In relation to Clause 26, on lines 25, 26 and 27 of page 23, the obligation is again placed upon those trustees to comply with the request. Therefore, in the statute the obligation is perfectly clear, it is on the trustees as a whole, whether or not each individual trustee knows about it.

I am sorry that my noble friend Lord Houghton of Sowerby thinks that I am imposing obligations upon 140 the citizenry.
