    The main area of uncertainty must inevitably be over the whole question of the hours and conditions of work. Here the various aspects of the transport industry have different concerns. Let us take, for example, the current situation regarding the maximum hours of driving in a day. In this country a lorry driver is allowed to drive for 10 hours, with no overall requirement in any seven days. Under EEC Regulation 543/69 that was cut to eight hours, but with nine hours twice a week, and with the further proviso that it should be 48 hours total in a week, but only 92 hours in a 14-day period.

    As I understand it, the latest proposals are to do away with the 92 hours in 14 days, but to restrict the weekly number of hours to 46. This can only mean a considerable additional burden. It will make it extremely difficult for many firms to operate on anything like their present scale of charges.

    There are two considerations here. The first is the economic well-being of the country. If we increase transport costs considerably—one estimate is that they already represent about 10 per cent. of overall price levels in this country—we shall add a totally unnecessary burden to the country at a period when it could well do without it.

    Secondly, lorry driving is no longer popular with the women of this country as a job for their husbands. There has been a movement over the years, perhaps coinciding with the development of Women's Lib, towards wives wanting to see more of their husbands. This applies to Members of Parliament just as much as to lorry drivers. Over the years, there has been a growing resistance to husbands being away from their homes during the week. Further restrictions on driving hours will make it extremely difficult for firms to avoid many more overnight stops and periods when husbands will be away from their families, and—dare I say it?—I think that it will lead to increased domestic friction.

    However, one of the greatest difficulties is not just over the number of hours worked in a week, and the cost, but the proposed maximum length of the working day and the maximum hours to be JPEG 17 worked in a week.     Where the difficulty arises is that if we take the limit of 12 hours, and 14 hours twice a week, with the Commission's proposals for 60 hours on regular services, or 65 hours in any seven days, we get into a strange situation. Many drivers of coaches, in particular to seaside resorts or other tourist centres, will find that they will be working virtually a four-day week. Yet, many of the hours during which they are theoretically working will be wasted either sitting in a traffic jam—when I accept that they are fully working—or, when they arrive at their destination, while they are waiting to bring back the occupants of their coach.
