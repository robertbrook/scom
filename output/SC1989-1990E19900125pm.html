Mrs. Alice Mahon
(Halifax)
: I beg to move amendment No. 405, in page 7, line 32, at end add—

"(7) This section shall not have effect until all second level registered nurses employed by the NHS who wish to take the conversion course leading to first level registration of the UKCC register have done so.".

All information about the position of nurses in the foreseeable future, from the Royal College of Nursing and from the Government, shows that there will be an increasing and alarming shortage of nurses, and that due to the demographic time bomb we must keep as many trained nurses as possible if we are to avoid real shortages later in the century.

I shall briefly outline the background to the problem that enrolled nurses are facing now. Nursing recruits who opted for second, rather than first, level training did so because some preferred a two-year to a three-year course and others because they did not have the necessary academic qualifications. Different demands exist in different parts of the country for academic qualifications. For example, London teaching hospitals often require recruits to have A-levels to become first-level nurses. However, in provincial hospitals, further away from the main teaching hospitals, one or two O-levels will do. We want to see the end of other divisions in nursing.

During the 1950s and 1960s, many black nurses were recruited from the West Indies. Often, they were directed on to the two-year course, not realising its limitations or that the certificate that they would be given would not be recognised in their own country. In some cases it was felt that discrimination was the reason why black nurses were persuaded to become second, rather than first, level nurses. When nurses failed the State Registered Nurse examination—as it was known then—they were automatically put on to the enrolled nurse roll.

I want to stress that enrolled nurses have played a vital part in running the NHS. It is true that, in practice, enrolled nurses have been used interchangeably with staff nurses on wards, especially for psychiatric and geriatric nursing and for unsocial hours at night. Therefore, they have a right to feel bitter about the way in which they have been treated. They stuck in a no-woman's-land of training and they have been subjected 478 to regrading exercises, which many of them consider to be degrading.

There are 97,000 enrolled nurses employed in the NHS, and many want to take the conversion course to obtain first-level status. An Institute of Manpower Services survey conducted in 1989 used 3,014 respondents. The following table shows the percentages of enrolled nurses who wanted to undertake conversion courses:

"Age %
18–25 58%
26–35 40%
36–45 26%
46–55 18%"
The IMS survey showed, therefore, that about a third of enrolled nurses wanted to undertake conversion courses. Another third said that they might wish to do in future, but did not know at the moment.

My DHA funded conversion courses for five years and only 10 enrolled nurses have converted. That problem is spread across the country. The survey showed also that 50 per cent. of all enrolled nurses were dissatisfied with their grades.

Before 1983, enrolled nurses had to take a 97-week course leading to first-level registration, but following discussions on Project 2000 the United Kingdom Central Council introduced 52-week courses, the first ones being approved in 1985–86. At first, the ratio of students to tutors on the courses was 10:1. Sadly, that has now been changed to 15:1 because of the shortage of money.

In order to convert, enrolled nurses, or second-level nurses as they prefer to be called, have to meet the EC nursing directive requirements during the course by, for example, gaining experience in psychiatric, community and maturnity nursing. The rules also allow schools to develop flexible courses. They can give credits to nurses for specific courses, for education and for experience. We must remember that these nurses have been holding down responsible positions in the NHS for many years, and it is good to know that flexibility to include experience is now included. In addition, schools may use open and distance learning. In theory a maximum of six months' remission can be obtained.

Despite this and all the good words and worthy intentions .there are still only 1,000 to 1,500 conversion course places available annually, and these are being chased by 30,000 enrolled nurses. There is much dissatisfaction, and morale is low among those nurses.

The hon. Member for Ross, Cromarty and Sky (Mr. Kennedy) and I attended a conference of enrolled nurses a week or two ago. I wish that hon. Members here had heard the passionate speeches from those nurses about their dissatisfaction, and how they were being ditched by the NHS.

Another factor causing bitterness and unease is that most DH As accept only enrolled nurses from their own districts, which makes life more difficult for nurses with health authorities that do not provide conversion courses. I talked to the Royal College of Nursing about this problem and it is adamant that all DHAs should be required by Government directive to provide conversion courses for enrolled nurses. I hope that the JPEG 479 Government will take note of that view on the basis that the RCN is a body to which they should listen. That is the least that the Government can do.

I repeat that there is an increasing and alarming shortage of nurses. The DHAs are endeavouring to retain the nurses that they have, because they are conscious of the problems that will face them in a few years' time. If enrolled nurses are to be retained in the NHS, their skills must be recognised and they must have career prospects. If they cannot get on a conversion course, they feel, justifiably, that they are on a ladder to nowhere. They feel that they are wanted only when no one else is around, but then they are ditched and told that there are no prospects for them.

Under Project 2000, a qualified nurse will be aided by a support worker, so it is vital that enrolled nurses are not caught betweeen the highly skilled nurse with first-level training and the assistant at the bottom. The Department of Health's own document, "A Strategy for Nursing", recognises the need to enhance the career qualifications of enrolled nurses, but so far it has not provided the resources for that purpose. That is why the amendment is important, and I hope that the Minister will take it very seriously.

In conclusion, the RCN and other nursing unions are worried that self-governing hospital trusts will be under no obligation and have no incentive to provide conversion courses. The trusts will have the right to employ whatever nursing staff they wish, and they will be able to offer differing rates of pay. My hon. Friends and I are worried that there will be a top layer of skilled first-level nurses and under it there will be a huge pyramid of nursing assistants who will receive just the vocational training course.

Trusts may take the view that as enrolled nurses are currently carrying out the duties of first-level nurses, there is no need to convert them at extra cost. They may feel that the cost of conversion should fall on the NHS hospitals under DHA control. That would be bad news for the Health Service, because we need to keep as many nurses as possible. In some areas, especially if self-governing hospitals are in a monopoly position, potential returners can be lost to the service for ever if there are no conversion courses, and nurses may decide to opt out of working for the NHS altogether.

We, like the nurses' unions and the RCN, would welcome any information that the Minister can give us about the trusts and their obligation towards conversion courses, and I urge the Minister to accept what, in my view, is a very reasonable amendment.

In spite of the inherent advantage, some NHS trust managers have already indicated that they will attempt to force consultants to use NHS trust private facilities exclusively by inserting the requirement into consultants' contracts, and offer a bonus or a donation to research funds that is based on the number of private patients admitted.

I consider that undersirable for a number of reasons. First, it would deny the patient choice. The Bill is all about extending choice for NHS patients. The same opportunity should not be denied to private patients. They should not be forced into a particular unit because the consultant is obliged by the terms of his contract to use it exclusively. It would be ironic if under the Bill, which extends choice to NHS patients, private patients were denied choice.

Secondly if consultants receive an incentive or even if a condition of service requires the admission of patients to a particular unit, GMC regulations insist that consultants who have a financial interest in a unit must declare it to patients before admitting them. The Government must look into that.

Thirdly, consultants may find themselves in trouble with the Inland Revenue. If a contract specifies that their private practice should be limited to a percentage of their income, and take place in a particular unit, that will be regarded as a condition of employment rather than a separate activity. Their private practice income will be taxed at a higher rate rather than as a separate business activity. It may be seen as a restraint on trade and interference with the individual rights of consultants to decide, in their private practice, what is the best for their patients. They will not wish this restraint to be imposed upon them.

The most important element is the limitation of choice for patients and for consultants. A Bill which extends NHS patient choice should not restrict private sector patients' choice nor restrict the consultants' choice of where to perform operations.
