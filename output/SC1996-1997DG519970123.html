Draft Plant Protection Products (Basic Conditions) Regulations 1997 and the Draft Control of Pesticides (Amendment) Regulations 1997

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mrs. Angela Browning)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Draft Plant Protection Products (Basic Conditions) Regulations 1997.

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to consider the Draft Control of Pesticides (Amendment) Regulations 1997.

Mrs. Browning
: The regulations for discussion today complete the United Kingdom's implementation of Community rules for the authorisation of agricultural pesticides. They also make minor, but worthwhile, clarifications and amendments to the United Kingdom's continuing national pesticides regime.

Before describing the new legislation, I shall set it in the context of the Government's overall approach to the control of pesticides. The Government recognise that, although pesticides can bring many benefits, they are potentially hazardous. They are therefore determined to ensure that all pesticides used in this country are used safely, thus protecting people and the environment.

Pesticides safety was first put on a statutory footing with the passage of the Food and Environment Protection Act 1985. The powers provided by that Act were put into effect by the Control of Pesticides Regulations 1986, under which a scheme was established to ensure the safety and effectiveness of pesticides. Under those statutory arrangements, pesticide companies must provide a wide range of scientific data for scrutiny by the Pesticides Safety Directorate or the Health and Safety Executive and the independent experts of the Advisory Committee on Pesticides.

Only when the advisory committee is satisfied, on the basis of that expert advice, that a product can be used without unacceptable risk to people or to the environment, will Ministers give approval to the product. Approved pesticides are subject to routine review and may be reviewed at any time if evidence emerges concerning their safety.

In 1991, the EC made its own legislation for what are termed "plant protection products"—broadly, agricultural pesticides. Other products controlled by 4 our national pesticides legislation will be picked up by the proposed European Community regime for biocidal products. I am pleased to say that the main elements of the EC arrangements for plant protection products set out in Council directive 91/414 draw heavily on UK experience. The EC scheme therefore provides for a system of authorisation to ensure that plant protection products on the European market are safe and effective.

The eventual result of the EC legislation should be a common set of rules that deliver consistent high standards of protection for people and the environment and provide pesticide manufacturers and users with a level playing field. However, EC rules will apply to a given chemical only when the Community has evaluated or reviewed its safety. The review process for existing chemicals is a very large task and the transition from national to EC rules will, therefore, take a considerable number of years to complete.

The detailed rules for the authorisation of chemicals and products set out in directive 91/414 were implemented in the UK in the Plant Protection Products Regulations 1995. However, it was not possible to provide in those regulations for control and enforcement measures, which the directive requires, but does not spell out in detail. It is the provision of those measures that is the principle purpose of the Plant Protection Products (Basic Conditions) Regulations 1997, which are before the Committee today.

Because the transition from regulation under the UK rules to regulation under EC rules will take a long time, it is sensible to make the control and enforcement regimes accompanying the two systems as similar as possible. The control and enforcement measures to be established for the EC regime are, therefore, modelled as closely as possible on the present UK arrangements, which have proved effective and are familiar to industry and enforcement bodies alike.

The drafting of the EC control and enforcement arrangements provided an opportunity to review the Control of Pesticides Regulations 1986 in the light of experience. We saw advantage in making a number of generally minor amendments and clarifications to the 1986 regulations, which is the purpose of the draft Control of Pesticides (Amendment) Regulations.

I shall describe the provisions of the regulations in a little more detail. The draft Plant Protection Products (Basic Conditions) Regulations would enable an effective control and enforcement regime to be applied to the marketing and use of plant protection products. As under the 1986 regulations, the advertisement and storage of plant protection products would require approval. The new regulations would also apply general conditions to the advertisement, storage, sale, supply and use of any product subject to the EC regime and would allow for the seizure and disposal of plant protection products following contravention of the regulations.

JPEG 5 All those provisions follow the blueprint of existing UK rules. Existing powers under the 1985 Act to prescribe codes of practice and to provide powers for enforcement officers will apply to products regulated under the EC regime.

The draft Control of Pesticides (Amendment) Regulations would clarify the scope and content of the 1986 regulations. Most of the amendments are minor and technical, but a few are worthy of specific mention. The new regulations would specifically exclude pesticides acting by physical means—devices such as glues that stick pests or greases that repel them. The rigorous arrangements applied to normal chemical or microbiological pesticides are not appropriate to that small sector of the market. We have, therefore, taken the opportunity to deregulate.

Following detailed public consultation, we are also taking the opportunity to achieve substantial clarification of the rules as they apply to adjuvants. Those are substances without significant pesticidal properties of their own that, when used with a pesticide, significantly increase its effectiveness. The existing rules on adjuvants have given rise to some confusion as to how and to what extent they are controlled.

The draft regulations would make it clear that an adjuvant may be used with a pesticide only if that adjuvant is on the authorised list and is used in accordance with the conditions given in the list and the conditions of use of the pesticide. The regulations would also make it clear that Ministers may remove an adjuvant from the authorised list if it is marketed outside its authorisation, or if human safety or environmental safety concerns are identified.

The Food and Environment Protection Act 1985 and the Control of Pesticides Regulations 1986 put in place a thorough and effective statutory system for the regulation of pesticides. We have started the transition of provisions for agricultural pesticides from those UK rules to EC arrangements that will provide similar safeguards.

The draft Plant Protection Products (Basic Conditions) Regulations would complete the UK's implementation of those EC rules. By following the existing UK regime for control and enforcement, we can be confident that we shall have a system that is workable and well understood. The draft Control of Pesticides (Amendment) Regulations would make minor clarifications to the UK regime. I commend both sets of regulations to the Committee.
