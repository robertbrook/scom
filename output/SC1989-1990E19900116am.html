Clause 1
REGIONAL AND DISTRICT HEALTH AUTHORITIES

Question proposed. That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Jim Cousins
(Newcastle upon Tyne, Central)
: I welcome the opportunity to discuss clause 1. It represents a remarkable system for potential jobbery and fixing; the Minister would appoint people who would then JPEG 145 appoint managers, and together they would appoint everyone else. Labour Ministers have already drawn attention to that.

We have not yet discussed the linked ability, in lines 16 and 17, to abolish or change the boundaries of health authorities by decree. It is typical of the Government not only to seek to create the potential for jobbery and fixing, but to create an atmosphere of abolition and administrative reorganisation, so that they might coerce people into obedience. We are deeply disturbed by that, and its consequences can already be seen in the NHS. There is an enormous wastage of the general managers who were appointed under the general management scheme.

In lines 16 and 17 of clause 1, the Government propose to abolish district health authorities by decree or to vary their boundaries without consultation. That would be a further attack on the integrity and performance of NHS management. It would also have serious implications for involvement and consultation with communities, and for the boundaries of district health authorities, which should reflect community identity.

Lines 16 and 17 of clause 1 are not trivial: they lie at the heart of the Bill. They would give the Government disturbing additional power.

If the Bill is not amended, district health authorities will lose many of their functions. They will manage far less. There is already a loss of morale among some sections of district health authority management, for which there is only one traditional corrective; additional money. We will await with interest the Government's announcement on pay rises for NHS management.

Lines 16 and 17 also raise the issue of what will happen to community health councils if the district health council is abolished. There has already been one example of an amalgamation of two district health authorities in London which led to the amalgamation of their associated community health councils. I refer to the formation in West London of Parkside health authority, which covers an area of considerable diversity and stress. It was unfortunate and a great administrative error that when the health authorities were amalgamated the community health councils were too. We seek an assurance that if the Governement take the power to abolish district health authorities, the position of their associated community health councils will be respected.

Lines 16 and 17 are also significant because of the proposal for self-governing trusts for opted-out hospitals. There is no shadow of doubt that that will further reduce the responsibilities of district health authorities. The Government made that point in paragraph 3.20 of the White Paper:

"The establishment of NHS Hospital Trusts will mean a substantial change in the responsibilities of DHA's which were responsible for their management. As more and more proposals for establishing NHS Hospitals Trusts come forward. RHAs will need to consider the viability of existing DHAs and the possibility of sensible merger with neighbouring Districts."

But lines 16 and 17 do not refer to regional health authorities having a cushioning or buffer role in that respect. They merely give Ministers the power for immediate abolition of health authorities or a 146 variation of their boundaries. That is inconsistent with the White Paper. The Minster needs to spell out clearly this morning—it is not mentioned in the Bill—what evaluation regional health authorities will be asked to undertake before they decide.

In my health district in Newcastle, three of the four hospitals have expressed an interest in forming self-governing trusts. What is the role of district health authority in that context? That question is being asked by members of the district health authority and their employees.

In the neighbouring district health authority, there is a proposal for whole district opt-out, an extraordinary proposal under which, for a time, the general manager rides two horses. He is both the candidate manager of a whole-district opted-out NHS trust and the actual manager of the district health authority from which he is to opt out.

In Darlington, a small health district, there is a situation that is common throughout the country. A single district general hospital proposes to opt out and become a self-governing trust. What will be left of the pattern of services in that district health authority?

We know the likely consequences. North Tyneside health authority minutes show that secret discussions are taking place between Newcastle and North Tyneside health authorities about amalgamating because of the changes. People—especially health authority managers —are entitled to expect a process of consultation, not the abolition by decree of, health authorities.

The proposal will not strengthen NHS management; it will undermine it. It will centralise, not decentralise, the key power to abolish health authorities by putting it in the hands of Ministers. It will put at even greater risk the connection between district health authorities and the people who look to them to provide services.

I hope that the Minister will respond to these matters.
