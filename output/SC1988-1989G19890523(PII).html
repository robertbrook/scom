Clause 36
SCHEDULE E: UNPAID EMOLUMENTS

Amendment made: No. 136, in page 26, line 38, leave out from "be" to end of line 5 on page 27 and insert

"charged to income tax only by reference to the year of assessment in which they are received.".—[ Mr. Alan Howarth.]

Question proposed, That the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Norman Lamont
: The clause provides transitional relief to prevent income earned before the introduction of the receipts basis being taxed twice, once when earned before 6 April 1989 and again when received after 5 April 1989. It does so, when relief is claimed, by reducing the emoluments chargeable under case I or II for years before 1989–90 when they are received after 5 April 1989, and by providing that they should be charged instead only under the new basis for the year of receipt. It includes provisions designed to prevent taxpayers normally assessed on a receipts basis, or on the non-statutory accounts basis, from obtaining entitlement, or increased entitlement, to transitional relief by having their earnings for 1988–89 assessed on the strict statutory earnings basis. That is to say that a person cannot move from the accounts basis to the earnings 194 basis to prolong the transitional period. We have already discussed the rationale of the measure and the following two clauses also bear on it in the converse sense of preventing incomes slipping out of the tax net as opposed to being taxed twice. The transitional relief works in both directions.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 36, as amended, ordered to stand part of the Bill.

Clause 38
SCHEDULE E: EMOLUMENTS ALREADY PAID

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Lamont
: Clause 36 provides transitional relief designed to ensure that the same earnings are not assessed twice, once on the old basis and once on the new. Clause 38 deals with the obverse where earnings chargeable to tax under the restated cases of schedule E would, but for this clause, fail to be assessable for any year because they were received before the new receipts basis came into force. The clause prevents JPEG 195 such income from being outside both old and new rules by deeming it to be received on 6 April 1989, therefore assessable for 1989–90. If the earnings are chargeable under case III of schedule E, but received in the United Kingdom before 5 April 1989, the clause similarly deems them to have been received in the United Kingdom on 6 April 1989, therefore assessable for 1989–90.

The earnings to which the clause relates should be within the charge to tax. It would be wrong if they were fortuitously exempted.

Clause 40
SCHEDULE D: COMPUTATION UNDER CASES I AND II

Mr. Lamont
: I beg to move amendment No. 141, in page 29, line 2, leave out from "made" to "for" in line 4 and insert

"of profits or gains which are to be charged under Schedule D and are".

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to take Government amendments Nos. 143, 145 and 147.

Mr. Lamont
: Clauses 40 and 41 achieve the same end. Clause 41 relates to investment and insurance companies. Clause 40 contains consequential changes for employers following the introduction of the receipts basis. The clause provides that where remuneration is still unpaid nine months after the period of account in which it was earned and for which it was charged, it is not deductible in arriving at the taxable profits of the employer for that period, but instead shall be deducted in arriving at the profits for the period in which it is paid.

At present all employees' remuneration is deductible by the employer for tax purposes for the period of account in which it is earned and for which it is charged. The employee is correspondingly assessed to tax on that remuneration for the tax year in which he earned it. There is therefore a link at present between the assessment of earnings on the employee and the relief to the employer.

196 However, when we move to the new receipts basis, that link will be broken, as the employee will be taxed only when he receives the remuneration. It is necessary to have a new rule to limit the extent to which assessment on the employee, and relief to the employer, can get out of step. Without the provisions, an employer could obtain a deduction for remuneration which was allocated to an employee but which remained unpaid for a long time.

As long as the remuneration is unpaid, the employee cannot be taxed under schedule E. Arrangements of that type could be created where, for example, the employee is a close relative of the employer. This will interest the hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, East (Mr. Brown). In this way, the employer could reduce his own tax liability although he has spent nothing and there is no corresponding schedule E liability. That would clearly be unacceptable.

The great majority of cases in which remuneration is paid shortly after it is earned will not be affected by the provision. Where there is some delay in paying remuneration, we are allowing nine months after the end of a business's period of account within which remuneration can be paid and still be deducted in the normal way. That should allow for any reasonable delay and means that postponement of the deduction will happen very rarely. But where there is exceptional delay, it is only reasonable that, if the business has not paid the employee and still has the benefit of the money, it should not be able to claim tax relief until the remuneration is paid.

As the Committee knows, not many businesses are able to provide their accounts and tax computations within nine months of their accounting date, so most will be able to reflect the remuneration position accurately when they submit computations of their tax liability. But to allow for employers who may be in a position to prepare and submit their accounts and tax computations to the inspector in less than nine months after the end of their accounting period, the clauses provide that remuneration that is unpaid when the computation is made shall not be allowed in computing the profits for tax purposes but, if any of that is subsequently paid within the nine months, a claim can be made for the computation to be adjusted to allow the remuneration. Therefore, the effect is to get the deduction on the employer's side and the tax on the employee's side in step, subject to the nine-month limit.

Amendment Nos. 141, 143, 145 and 147 are technical. They make it clear that the calculations to which the rules apply are all calculations of taxable profits made in accordance with the schedule D rules that apply to individuals, partnerships and companies.

In the second group, amendments Nos. 142, 144 and 146 ensure that the rules apply to all the types of earnings—

Mr. Lamont
: As I said before when we discussed clause 40, it was designed to stop the deduction on the employers side getting out of step with tax on the employees side. Clause 41 does exactly the same for investment and insurance companies.

Amendments Nos. 150, 151, 152 and 156 are technical amendments. They replace, where appropriate, the phrase "period of account" with the phrase "accounting period". Profits for corporation tax purposes are calculated by reference to accounting periods as defined in tax legislation. It is therefore appropriate for the Bill to refer to an accounting period in connection with profits liable to corporation tax. However, when fixing the deadline which determines when a deduction is to be due it is necessary to focus on the period for which the company actually draws up its accounts, which is called the period of account. The company may have a 15-month accounting period, for example. That is 200 why it is necessary to distinguish between a period of account and an accounting period. The amendments recognise the distinction between the two phrases.

Mr. Norman Lamont
: Clauses 47, 48 and 49 provide an exemption from the benefits in kind charge which might otherwise arise where an employer provides personal physical security facilities for an employee who is under a special threat to his security by virtue of his employment. That relief is matched by equivalent reliefs for self-employed taxpayers in clauses 111, 112, 118 and 119.

The relief is of limited application and prevents charges to tax, chiefly under the benefits in kind 214 rules, in certain exceptional circumstances. The Government recognise that for some employees the particular work on which they are engaged may mean that they are exposed to a threat to their personal security from terrorists and other groups who resort to violence. In the face of such a threat, employers may incur expenditure in seeking to protect their employees. Where such expenditure results in a tax charge on the employee, the Government intend that it should be relieved.

The benefits in kind legislation, which applies to those who earn £8,500 a year or more, is widely drawn so as to charge tax on

"benefits or facilities of whatsoever kind"

provided to employees by virture of their employment. Similarly, where expenses are reimbursed to employees by employers, a tax charge normally arises which can be relieved only if the underlying expense is itself

"wholly exclusively and necessarily incurred in the performance of the duties"

of the employment; that is a deliberately stiff test.

It follows that where security facilities are provided for employees outside the workplace, a tax charge—possibly a substantial one—would be the normal result. That would apply even where there was no intention on the part of the employer to remunerate or reward his employee in incurring the expenditure, or where the "benefit" arising might not readily be recognised as such. The Government do not think that that sort of charge should be made in the very special circumstances envisaged in these clauses.

I stress that the relief is intended to apply only in exceptional circumstances. There must be a "special threat" to the "personal physical security" of the employee emanating from his "particular employment". One cannot simply obtain tax relief for normal security burglar alarms or for floodlighting one's house. It must be a specific threat that arises from one's employment. It is intended that the relief should go only to those who face a real threat to their person and that such threat is directly linked to their work. Such circumstances are most likely to occur where the nature of a person's employment means that he is a potential target for the activities of terrorists and other similar extremist groups. For relief to be given in relation to any personal security expenditure, it must have been incurred by the employer with the sole intention of improving the employee's security.

The relief will not cover expenditure for security measures against the sorts of general criminal threat which all citizens may face to one degree or another; nor where expenditure is incurred primarily in meeting a threat to property; nor where the perceived threat is unconnected with a person's employment. Any intention on an employer's part to reward his employee by meeting security expenditure will disqualify it.

Clause 111
SECURITY: TRADES ETC

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Lamont
: The purpose of this clause, and of clause 112, is to allow expenditure other than capital expenditure incurred by an individual or partnership of individuals—this answers the question asked by the hon. Member for Kirkcaldy—on certain assets and services provided for security purposes to qualify for tax relief as a deduction in calculating profits. Capital expenditure on security assets of this nature is to qualify for specific relief under clauses 118 and 119. All four clauses are designed to give self-employed people broadly the same tax reliefs as those which are being provided for employees under clauses 47 to 49.

To qualify for relief, the expenditure must have been incurred with the sole object of meeting a threat to an individual's personal physical security, arising wholly or mainly by virtue of the particular business activity being carried on.

The threat must be a special threat—that is to say, it has to be a threat of a kind which is somehow greater and more serious than would be faced by the generality of individuals in a similar line of business. The relief is aimed at those to whom there is a special threat through their work from the activities of terrorists, extremists and of other groups of people who might resort to violence.

Essentially the relief is aimed at expenditure incurred by a business man on security protection at his own home. Expenditure on the protection of his business premises will generally qualify for relief anyway. We do not need to make special provision. Expenditure on protection at a business man's own home would not, generally, qualify for relief as the law stands because there is a private purpose behind 222 such expenditure. The new rule will ensure that relief is due notwithstanding the fact that there is an element of private benefit, and notwithstanding the fact that the expenditure may also benefit the individual's own family or household. The sort of expenditure covered will include rent paid for alarm systems, special lighting and so on.
