[MR. BILL OLNER in the Chair]

Draft Social Security and Child Support (Decisions and Appeals) Regulations 1999

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Angela Eagle)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Social Security and Child Support (Decisions and Appeals) Regulations 1999.

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to consider the draft Child Support (Miscellaneous Amendments) Regulations 1999 and the draft Child Support (Miscellaneous Amendments) (No. 2) Regulations 1999.

Angela Eagle
: I welcome you as Chairman, Mr. Olner. I have never served on a Committee under your Chairmanship. I hope and trust that this will be the first of many such enjoyable occasions.

The draft Social Security and Child Support (Decisions and Appeals) Regulations 1999 provide for the new arrangements that will be introduced from the second half of this year. The draft Child Support (Miscellaneous Amendments) (No. 2) Regulations 1999 contain some consequential amendments to the child support regulations, all of which are derived from the powers provided by the Social Security Act 1998. The draft Child Support (Miscellaneous Amendments) Regulations 1999 make a small number of amendments to the secondary legislation, which provides the structure of the child support scheme. For the most part, they correct minor errors and clarify the intention of the legislation in cases of doubt.

The changes in decision making and appeals fit in with the broader context of the Government's plan for overhauling the social security system. Principle 8 of the Government's Green Paper on welfare reform seeks to deliver welfare to people in a flexible, efficient and easy manner by seeking to deliver an integrated service across the country. The changes before the Committee will constitute one of the key early initiatives of the active modern service programme. Clearer processes, which are focused on the needs of the individual rather than on administrative convenience, should result. That approach will improve services and allow a more cost-effective and quick delivery.

First, I shall address the changes to decision making and appeals, where change is long overdue. The current system of determining claims and resolving disputes is too long and complex. People have a right to expect their claims and applications to be dealt with correctly and in good time. When they wish to dispute 4 a decision, they expect to see it resolved swiftly wherever possible, or progressed rapidly to the appeal stage.

The draft regulations provide for an improved decision making and dispute resolution system, a simplified appeal system where five different types of tribunals are replaced with a single jurisdiction, and the introduction of a modern, accountable appeals service. To assist with the Committee's deliberations, I shall explain the content of the regulations in more detail.

Hon. Members will recall that one of the main provisions of the 1998 Act was that all initial benefit and child support decisions will be made by officers acting on behalf of the Secretary of State. That replaces the current system where there are a variety of different types of decision maker.

The current inflexible system of reviewing decisions often forces people into a frustrating and time-consuming appeals process. Clients often appeal against a decision, simply because they are not aware of any other way, in practice, to get their decision checked. The regulations overcome such problems by replacing the existing provisions for the review of benefit and child support decisions with a more efficient system for dealing with disputes and correcting wrong decisions at an early stage. Where decisions are clearly wrong, the agencies will put them right quickly with the minimum of fuss. The same straightforward and simple rules on how and when decisions can be changed will apply to all benefits.

Regulation 3 allows decisions to be revised and replaced with new ones, for any reason, where a dispute or appeal is made within one month of the decision being notified. The regulation further provides that some decisions may be revised after the one-month time limit so that we can be sure that any errors are corrected effectively. Regulation 30 allows an appeal to lapse where someone appeals against a decision at the outset, and the decision is revised in his or her favour. There will, however, be fresh rights to dispute and appeal the revised decision which regulation 31 confers.

Even where errors are identified outside the one-month time limit, and the decision cannot be revised, a decision may be superseded under regulation 6. Superseding decisions, as regulation 7 details, will generally take effect from when the decision is made or when the application was made. Decisions can also be superseded under regulation 6 where there is a relevant change of circumstances since the original decision was made. In benefit cases, in accordance with regulation 7, a decision will be superseded from the date of change, provided the change is notified promptly, otherwise the new decision will take effect from the date of application. In child support cases, a superseding decision following a change of circumstances will take effect from the date of application.

In planning for the introduction of these new arrangements, the agencies have taken the opportunity to begin to improve their notifications. Our aim is that the notification of a decision should use language that JPEG 5 is easy to understand and should explain the rules and decisions given. The notification will invite clients to contact the agency if they are not satisfied with the decision or if they have a query. They will be given the opportunity to discuss it and to present any new relevant information. The agency will then reconsider the case and either confirm the original decision or issue a new one. In either case, their right of appeal is protected.

I shall now deal with the provisions on appeals. Current delays in the handling of appeals are unacceptable. Hon. Members will know only too well from their constituency postbags that across the country people wait, on average, seven months for their appeals to be heard. Many wait a year or more. That is unacceptable and the Government are committed to delivering significant improvements in performance. In developing provisions that will deliver the necessary improvements, I wish to acknowledge the vital help received from Judge Harris, the president of the independent tribunal service and his colleagues.

The regulations introduce a number of important changes to appeals.

First, the regulations simplify the confusing jumble of time limits that currently operate—time limits that are difficult for appellants to understand. There are 28 days for this appeal, 42 days for that appeal, 3 months for that application, and so on. In addition, where appeals are lodged out of time, different rules govern their acceptance or rejection and the period during which such applications may be admitted. In short, the arrangements are a mess and we propose to replace them with a single one-month limit for each stage of the process. Various regulations—for example, regulation 3 for revisions and regulation 31 for tribunal appeals—achieve that.

Secondly, regulation 36 ensures that tribunals have the necessary expertise to deal with the range of issues that may come before them. Social security and child support law can be complex and change rapidly, so all tribunals will include a lawyer. However, some appeals raise medical issues or questions of disability and others raise complex financial questions. Tailoring the composition of the tribunal to the issues raised by the appeal will ensure that tribunals are properly equipped to decide the relevant questions. A disability benefit appeal, for example, will be heard by a three-person tribunal, which will include a lawyer, a doctor and a disability expert, while a retirement pension appeal, raising questions of fact and law, would normally be heard by a lawyer sitting alone. That approach will allow the appeals service to concentrate expertise on the cases that need it and speed the service it offers to all appellants. For the most part, it is these regulations that will determine the composition of any particular tribunal. However, where the exercise of discretion is involved, I should like to make it clear that it will be the president or his judicial staff who will decide how a tribunal will be composed. While on the subject of tribunal composition, I should like to clarify the position on appeals relating to incapacity for work. All 6 such appeals will be heard by a lawyer and a doctor, and we are looking again at the wording of regulation 36 to ensure that that is made clear.

Thirdly, 10 per cent, of appeals received by the independent tribunal service have no prospect of success. For example, child benefit is not payable for children over the age of 19, and an appeal in those circumstances simply cannot succeed because the law is explicit. The same is true in the case of the disability living allowance mobility component in relation to a child under the age of two. It is essential that we build in arrangements to ensure that such cases do not undermine the ability of the appeals service to handle other appeals speedily and effectively. However, there will be safeguards to ensure that appeals are handled properly. People will have the opportunity to argue in front of a tribunal that their appeal is not misconceived, if they wish to do so.

Fourthly, I want to make it clear to the Committee that the Government are expanding the role of tribunal clerks from that which we envisaged when the Bill was debated. We have done that after discussions with Judge Harris. Currently, tribunal chairmen must spend time dealing with clear-cut interlocutory matters, including the disposal of particular appeals. That time would be better spent on hearing appeals and helping to ensure that delays and backlogs are reduced. That will mean a speedier service for all appellants, so we have decided that clerks may handle matters involving a simple assessment of straightforward facts. Regulation 46 gives effect to that. It is, of course, essential that clerks can properly perform these duties, and Judge Harris has kindly agreed to assist with the training and guidance provided for clerks. Clerks will not strike out appeals in any other circumstances. I want especially to draw the Committee's attention to these changes because of their importance and because they are a change from our earlier proposals.

I now turn to the Child Support (Miscellaneous Amendments) Regulations 1999. As I said earlier, they make a small number of amendments to secondary legislation, which provides the structure of the child support scheme. For the most part, they correct minor errors and clarify the intention of the legislation in cases of doubt.

Hon. Members know that we are considering in depth all aspects of child support provision and of the Child Support Agency. We set out our proposals for change in the Green Paper "Children First: A New Approach to Child Support", which was published last July. We have consulted widely and in a few weeks' time we will publish our plans in a White Paper. However, the scale of change needed to bring about radical reform means that it cannot be done overnight. In the interim, we intend to make changes to pave the way for radical change and to make improvements between now and the introduction of the new scheme.

This package of regulations marks a step in that direction. The difficulty that the Child Support Agency has had in obtaining maintenance from self-employed non-resident parents has been well documented. More than 80 per cent, of such parents do not pay their full JPEG 7 child support. Non-resident parents are reluctant to provide information on their earnings, partly because we treat their evidence differently from the Inland Revenue. Child Support Agency staff need special expertise to enable them to interpret the complicated information that such parents provide. We are doing something about that in the regulations.

Regulation 6 enables self-employed parents to provide the figures that they use for self assessment of their earnings for tax purposes for use in calculating maintenance. Only when they cannot provide them, such as when a new business is involved, will the agency have to continue to use the old, cumbersome arrangements.

Regulation 3 defers the reintroduction of fees until April 2001. Fees for the services provided by the Child Support Agency have been suspended since April 1995. That suspension was due to be lifted this April, but while the agency has made significant strides in improving its service to customers, it does not yet provide the quality and speed of service that we believe would make it right to levy a charge for that service. The Government have therefore decided to defer the reintroduction of the fees for another two years.

Regulation 6(7) restores the policy intention in respect of the valuation of property transferred before 1993 in lieu of child maintenance, where a decision by the child support commissioner has resulted in the value being less than was originally planned in the 1995 changes.

Those are the most significant changes in the package. Hon. Members will be relieved to hear that I do not intend to burden them by going over the regulations one by one, especially as they are for the most part technical. I shall be happy, however, to answer questions about any of them.

The decision-making and appeals changes deliver the first element of the Government's objective of modernising welfare delivery. Nearly everyone in Britain is affected at one time or another by the service that we provide through our agencies. The regulations provide a strong foundation for the Government's Welfare Reform and Pensions Bill and future child support reform. They support a welfare state that is flexible, efficient, and easy for people to understand and use. I commend them to the Committee.

Mr. Quentin Davies
(Grantham and Stamford)
: It is a pleasure to welcome you as Chairman of our proceedings, Mr. Olner. This is the first time that I have appeared under your chairmanship.

I thank the Minister for setting out the Government's proposals. The Conservative party has no objection to the child support changes to which she referred, such as the revaluation of assets that are transferred as part of a couple's settlement. The same is true of fees; the Minister is right that the Child Support Agency is as far away as ever from achieving the standards of service and precision that would be 8 required to begin to justify charging the public fees. There is legitimate doubt about whether it provides acceptable value to the public.

Our concern is with the Social Security and Child Support (Decisions and Appeals) Regulations 1999; they form a large part of the regulations and fundamentally change the rules governing the appeals available to those who consider that the social security and welfare system has not treated them justly.

After nearly two years of the new Labour Government, we are getting used to being pretty sceptical about Minister's statements and press handouts. They are always couched in the same bland and apparently entirely harmless language—the PR newspeak of new Labour. It is only when we being to look at the detail and the small, fine print that we realise that what appears at first sight a gain for the citizen or the cause of good government is in fact the exact reverse. So it is, I am afraid, with the bulk of the Minister's comments.

There was, however, one thing about which the Minister was remarkably frank. She said that the Government's proposals in the Social Security and Child Support (Decisions and Appeals) Regulations 1999 "fit in with" the Government's other welfare policies. That is all too true. The Welfare Reform and Pensions Bill is being considered in Committee. Among other pernicious aspects, it attempts to claw back more than £1 billion from vulnerable people— widows and the disabled. That is notwithstanding the fact that the presentation of the Bill made it appear to be good news for the vulnerable citizen. The reverse is the case. The regulations are indeed part of that agenda. The intention is to make appeals more difficult to load the dice further against the appellant who will, in many cases, be a vulnerable person. He may deserve a better deal from the social security system—he probably does—but he will be much less likely to get it.

There has been not so much a harmonisation or unification of procedures in the different independent tribunals, but a move in every case to a structure that is more favourable to the Secretary of State and less favourable to the appellant. As the Minister mentioned, an appeal has to be made within different periods in different parts of the social security system; now, however, the period will be a maximum of one month in every case, subject to narrow restrictions. It is clear what is happening: we should be under no doubt that the intention is to make it less easy for individuals to appeal.

Some of the people affected are not especially sophisticated or professionally advised. How could we expect somebody who is living on social security to be professionally advised? That is a cruel idea. Some charities help and do excellent work. There are the various disability charities, the citizens advice bureaux and so forth, but they are under great burdens and often cannot respond as rapidly as might a lawyer who was paid £300 or £400 an hour to work on someone's behalf. It may, therefore, be extremely difficult to get the kind of help that is required and to formulate an appeal within a month.

JPEG 9 What is more, the regulations will make it more difficult to ensure that one's appeal has been formulated correctly. A draconian provision allows clerks to throw out appeals as misconceived if, for example, a required document is missing. All kinds of new mantraps have been set for the unwary, and most of the people who the Government are trying to clobber in this way are by definition unwary. They are vulnerable people rather than sophisticated or wealthy members of society who can more easily defend themselves. This is a cynical attempt to tighten the noose around them, and it is pretty unpleasant.

This is a bad day's work for the interests of justice and good government. One of the virtues of having different appeals for different aspects of the social security system is that panels build up expertise—their members understand disability, pensions, child support or whatever area they are dealing with. It is unrealistic to expect that a more limited number of individuals sitting on panels that deal with a whole range of social security issues can keep up to speed so effectively with all the regulations and laws that are associated with different aspects of social security. Even more importantly, people will not have the experience of dealing with many claimants in a particular situation—for example, mothers or people with a specific disability. They will not see the same sort of case all the time and be able to develop a human understanding of the situations of such people. It is important that an appeals system in a humane country should be staffed by people who attempt to look beyond the mere facts and figures of the formal documentation or medical reports that are submitted to them to envisage how the individuals concerned can run their lives in society. That process will be eroded.

I should like the Minister to explain further two aspects of the matter. First, regulation 21 is gobbledegook. I do not know whether you understood it when you read it, Mr. Olner, but I certainly did not. It states:

"(1) For the purposes of section 25(3)(b) (prescribed cases and circumstances in which a decision may be made on a prescribed basis) a case which satisfies the condition in paragraph (2) is a prescribed case.

  1. The condition is that the claimant would be entitled to the benefit to which the decision which falls to be made relates, even if the appeal in the other case referred to in section 25(1)(b) were decided in a way which is the most unfavourable to him.
  2. For the purposes of section 25(3)(b), the prescribed basis on which the Secretary of State may make the decision is as if—
    1. the appeal in the other case which is referred to in section 25(1)(b) had already been determined; and
    2. that appeal had been decided in a way which is the most unfavourable to the claimant."

I defy any normal human being to understand that. It would be very much in the public interest if the hon. Lady employed people who have the gift of writing clear English in formulating such regulations, especially given the pernicious agenda of which the new regulations are part. We are right to be suspicious of the meaning of this regulation until it has been thoroughly explained to us, and I would be grateful if she would do that.

10 The second matter that we must explore in some detail requires, I fear, no explanation. Regulation 36 is a good example of new Labour speak. One can read it several times without realising that it provides for some appeal tribunals to consist of one person. An appeal tribunal consisting of one person is a contradiction in terms. It is not a tribunal, but an administrative decision made by an individual—a potentate established by the Government to try to prevent people from claiming social security benefits. Regulation 39 refers to:

"The chairman, or in the case of an appeal tribunal which has only one member",

a phrase which is repeated in several subsequent regulations. But it is quite disguised by the verbiage of regulation 36. We see, again, a nefarious purpose, which sets at naught the whole concept of a panel or tribunal. It is a mockery, disguised as it is under this unacceptable level of obscurity.

I think that we have understood the sense of paragraph 36 all too well. It is one of the features that will lead Opposition Members to vote against the regulations. I still do not understand regulation 21, and I think that it is extremely important that the people who are likely to be targeted by it understand it. I should therefore be grateful for a clear explanation in simple English from the Minister.

Mr. Quentin Davies
: The hon. Gentleman made an inadvertent slip in his logic—very uncharacteristically, if I may say so. He says, quite plausibly, that the right hon. That bureaucracy carried on from the previous Administration, to whom it never submitted these pernicious proposals. JPEG 11 Perhaps someone in the bureaucracy conceived some of these matters; perhaps Ministers conceived them. I think it most likely that Ministers seized on some of the worst, most autocratic and arbitrary proposals put forward by their own advisers because it fitted the Treasury agenda of reducing public expenditure.

The Chairman
: Order. This intervention is becoming a speech. I call Mr. Webb.

Mr. Webb
: Thank you, Mr. Olner. I am trying to recall whether, when the Social Security Act 1998 from which the regulations spring came before the House in November 1997, Conservative Members supported it. I have a vague recollection that they did.

We support these child support regulations. We welcome the attempt to track down self-employed absent parents and to use their income as declared to the Inland Revenue. As soon as parents with care say, "My ex is self-employed", one's heart sinks, because we all know how difficult it is to get money out of the self-employed. If these regulations help, we warmly support them.

We have a number of concerns about the substantive social security regulations in front of us. Many were voiced by us alone when the Social Security Act 1998 was going through Parliament. I shall not detain the Committee at great length, but there are three areas that I want to touch on.

The first is the composition of the appeals tribunals. Many concerns have been voiced by welfare rights advisers who have been presented with the content of the new regulations and will have to implement them. I hope that a listening Government will take those concerns into account. On the composition of the appeals tribunals, the hon. Member for Grantham and Stamford said that there can by one member. In the case of child support appeals, there is a feeling that there should be at least one person from each gender— one man and one woman—on the tribunal. The Child Poverty Action Group cites the example of a woman who has been subjected to domestic violence who might feel that leaving her child support arrangements in the sole care of a male on his own on a tribunal would not be to her advantage. Such males may be above reproach, but justice needs both to be done and to be seen to be done. One rather doubts that that would be the case in these circumstances.

I therefore share the concern of the hon. Member for Grantham and Stamford about one-member tribunals. Certainly in the case of child support appeals, I would want to see at least one member of each gender on tribunals. That would follow the model used in cases of disability, where there is someone who has experience of disability on the tribunal; likewise, in child support cases there should be someone from each gender.

The second concern is time limits. If claimants are to have only one month in which to appeal against a decision, they will need sufficient information on the day on which they are notified that their benefit claim 12 has been turned down—they should not have to wait several weeks to obtain the full information. Moreover, as has just been said, the information that they do receive must be in plain English. Committee members will all have seen forms that use tick boxes— for example, applicants may be asked to tick the relevant box from a list including mobility allowance, disability and care component. It makes very little sense. Will the Minister assure us that when people are notified that they have been turned down for benefit, they will get a letter in plain English, as well as one citing all the relevant Acts and sub-clauses?

A month seems too short a period. The Child Poverty Action Group cites parts of the country where it can take six weeks to get an appointment with an experienced welfare rights adviser. In those circumstances, it is clearly unfair and unrealistic to suppose that people can respond in an informed manner within a month to the news that they will not get their money. The Minister might reply that there is a safeguard—that in "wholly exceptional" circumstances, the one-month period can be extended. But we are very concerned that the words "wholly exceptional" may be drawn very narrowly and that legitimate reasons for delay such as sickness might not be taken into account. Will the Minister assure us that the definition of "wholly exceptional" will be drawn as widely as possible and interpreted in a relaxed way?

A category of appeals for which the one-month limit might be deemed far too short is appeals to the social security commissioners. Such appeals are by definition limited in number, but they are very technical because they set a precedent and touch on a complex point of legal dispute. The idea that people should have only a month in which to decide whether to lodge such an appeal seems entirely inappropriate. Those are complex legal matters with which I suspect many hon. Members would struggle.

My third and final concern relates to striking out, an issue that we raised during the Committee stage of the Social Security Act 1998. I was more alarmed by what the Minister said today than I was by what was in the original Act. She said that clerks are to have even greater powers. I am sure that she will correct me if I am wrong, but as I understand it, clerks do not have to have legal qualifications and yet they will be able to look at a paper and say that the appeal is ill-conceived. In the Standing Committee examining what would become the 1998 Act we discussed a case which on the papers appeared completely ill-conceived. It was allowed to proceed because the striking out provisions were not law and the commissioners eventually found in the claimant's favour. However, any clerk looking at those papers would have thrown the case out immediately.

The Minister may say that there is a right of appeal, but regulation 48 prescribes that such an appeal has to be made within 14 days of the notification that the appeal has been struck out by the clerk. It is not clear whether that is 14 days from the date of posting or 14 days from the date of receipt; given the post, that could be an important distinction. Will the Minister clarify whether that 14 days starts from the day that JPEG 13 notification lands on the doormat or the day it is posted? That could take up a good third of the time available.

All that has to happen is that appellants have to be notified by the clerk that their appeal against having their benefit turned down has been thrown out. In response, they have to say whether they want to argue in person or have a preliminary oral hearing. I suspect that that is another hurdle being put in the way of people trying to appeal.

I fully accept the Minister's point that the previous system needed streamlining—it contained anomalies and it was difficult to overturn obvious errors. We therefore welcome certain aspects of the reforms. However, the striking out provisions strike at the heart of justice. They allow an official to say, "You have no case"—not even a one-person tribunal in an oral hearing, where a claimant is present to make the argument, but just an official who will send a letter. Many benefit claimants may well receive that letter and understand it to mean that they have lost their appeal. That is most worrying.

We have a number of reservations about the regulations which follow from our reservations about the Social Security Act 1998, which we alone opposed in Parliament, and unless the Minister can give us a satisfactory answer to those points, we will oppose the regulations.

Mr. Tony Baldry
(Banbury)
: It is a little hard to criticise officials or Ministers because the regulations are complex—they deal with complex matters.

A constituency like mine in middle England is not full of welfare rights advisers. If people have problems, they tend to come to me or go to citizens advice bureaux. I want to ask the Minister about promulgation. How can we ensure that necessarily complex regulations are translated into guidance notes for volunteers—who are often very professional—who work in citizens advice bureaux and for similar advice organisations on the regulations and other matters relating to the Child Support Agency? Unless we can be confident that people can obtain straightforward advice on the regulations, they will be put at a substantial disadvantage.

The regulations are no more or less complex than any other part of the English law, but in other areas of English law, such as landlord and tenant law, people usually have access to legal aid. Solicitors have a vested interest in interpreting the law and ensuring that it is understood by their clients. That is not the case in this area of law, for which legal aid is not generally available.
