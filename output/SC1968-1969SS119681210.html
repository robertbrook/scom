Mr. Anthony Stodart
: I beg to move, Amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 5, at beginning insert '(1)'.

I very much doubt whether the great public outside will appreciate the significance of this Amendment, but this is the way we often do things in Committee. We start with a paving, drafting Amend- 4 ment. The point made by myself and several hon. Gentlemen during the Second Reading debate was that we believed that the right hon. Gentleman the Secretary of State already had the powers for which he was asking, under the 1954 Act. This was strenuously denied by the Under-Secretary, although it is not unfair to say that he did not altogether carry the Committee with him. He maintains that the present Law Officers have advised him that the Bill gives the Government an authority which they do not have. Although this is contrary to the advice once given to us, it is a matter which we must accept and I will not say any more about that.

Four positions have been taken up over this Bill. There is first the Government position. They wish to retain the present system with such an insignificant change, that is, the change described by the hon. Gentleman, flowing from this Bill that one has to split hairs to find and understand it. Secondly, there is the position of the Under-Secretary of State, which is different from that of the Government, because he quite clearly wants to fix 1973 as the appointed day, with no reservations. That is his personal wish about possible postponements. Thirdly, there is the position taken up by the R.S.P.C.A., which is unable to accept any of those balancing factors put forward by the right hon. Gentleman in 1965, and by several hon. Members on Second Reading. The R.S.P.C.A. wants the date of 1973 advanced, with no reservations. Lastly there is the position of the National Farmers' Union of Scotland, which dislikes the Bill because under it the Secretary of State can advance the date of the ban without an alternative trap necessarily being available. The N.F.U. wants Clause 2(1)( b) made much more positive.

It is not surprising that there are widely differing views on a matter which has been before Parliament, either in debate or Parliamentary Questions, many times over the last twenty years. Every Secretary of State during that period has had it before him and each has taken an identical line. In saying that, I include the present Secretary of State, so far as his most recently expressed view in Parliament goes.

In an endeavour to reach a workable compromise, entirely in line with all JPEG 5 that has been said by Government spokesmen in the course of six Parliaments—until the hon. Gentleman deviated from that line a fortnight ago—we have put down these seven Amendments, all of which are closely linked. Without its companions, the intention of each one is destroyed.

It might be of benefit to the Committee if I make clear what it is we want to amend. The Bill proposes that the power of the Secretary of State to make an order, as he can and does, authorising the use of the gin trap for the catching of foxes and otters anywhere in Scotland, shall cease in a little over four years' time, on 1st April, 1973, subject always to the Secretary of State receiving the consent of Parliament to withdraw the Order before then, or, and this is a point which made a very much more severe impact upon the Committee, to postpone the withdrawal of the Secretary of State's powers indefinitely. On Second Reading, Clause 2(1)( b) got a pretty substantial mauling. Through these Amendments we seek to be much more positive and practical.

There has been entirely consistent thinking during the last twenty years by all right hon. Gentlemen who have dealt with this problem, beginning with the right hon. Member for East Stirlingshire (Mr. Woodburn) and coming down to the present Secretary of State. All have said that the only obstacle to following the lead set by England in 1948 has been one of geography and topography. As the present Secretary of State remarked three years ago, the banning of the gin would cause serious losses on our hills and sheepland, and he said that these factors had to be balanced. I am in complete agreement with that. I find it surprising that the hon. Gentleman departed from that doctrine on Second Reading by declaring his total indifference whether a humane alternative will replace the gin in 1973.

Amendments 2 and 3 would restrict the Secretary of State's authority, for permitting the use of the gin, which he at present uses in respect of the whole country, to the crofting counties and to the livestock rearing lands of the northern counties. It would no longer be permissible to use the gin for otters, but only for the catching of foxes.

6 Amendment No. 4 defines what are the crofting counties and it is unnecessary for me here to go through the list. It also defines the meaning of livestock-rearing land. This is well enough known in farming circles, but I might say that it is defined statutorily as land which consists predominantly of mountain, hill and heath. It therefore takes in those wide spaces of hill land which are outside the crofting counties but in which, to gas, shoot or snare foxes is an almost impossible proposition.

Amendment No. 5 would have the effect of terminating the Secretary of State's right to authorise the use of the gin in the whole of the west of Scotland, that is, in the whole of Lowland Scotland, south of Argyll and Perthshire and Angus, as well as on the low ground of those northern counties, as from 1st April, 1970, rather than 1st April, 1973. It is perfectly practicable to do that in country which is more like the English countryside, where it is already banned.

Amendment No. 6 removes the powers given to the Secretary of State in the Bill to advance or postpone the appointed day. As Amendment No. 5 advances this by three years, we think it unnecessary to allow for a further advance beyond that. Nor do we think that within the Lowlands of Scotland there is any need for postponement.

The Amendment to the Title is entirely consequential.

Hon. Gentlemen may ask why we are asking for the retention of the gin in the areas in the north till such time as the Secretary of State feels able to remove it. It is solely in order to retain the balance of the various factors involved, the killing of livestock by foxes on the one hand, the pain suffered by foxes in a gin on the other. It is the balance to which the right hon. Gentleman referred in 1965. There is no doubt that since he uttered those words things have got worse and the balance has gone further out of adjustment. I would have no quarrel with what the hon. Member for Central Ayrshire (Mr. Manuel) said on Second Reading, that everyone can see that the fox population has increased since the Secretary of State said how important it was that we must keep the gin because of our geographical features.

JPEG 7 There are those who regard myself and others as last-ditchers, trying to hang on to the gin trap, if not in the whole of Scotland then in the Highlands and in the hills. We approach it from the other angle, and want to forbid its use over as large an area as possible, and to retain it only where absolutely necessary. My calculations have led me to understand that the effect of the Amendments would be to limit its use to an area of no more than half the land surface of the country.
