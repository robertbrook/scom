AGRICULTURE

Motion made, and Question proposed,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered

  • Class V, Vote 2, Department of Agriculture and Fisheries for Scotland;
  • Class V, Vote 4, Department of Agriculture and Fisheries for Scotland (Agricultural Grants and Subsidies); and
  • Class V, Vote 6, Department of Agriculture and Fisheries for Scotland (Agricultural Price Guarantees).—[ Mr. Buchan.]

    • Mr. Anthony Stodart
    : Mr. Robertson, I hope you will forgive me if I say something to you which I do not recall having said to any Chairman before. That is that I profoundly regret that you are in the Chair this morning, because that will not enable you to repeat that speech which I have remembered for many a year, which you made from this position in July, 1964, on the very same subject of Agricultural Estimates.

    It is right that we should start our debate on the Scottish Estimates by considering the agricultural industry; an industry with a very great record of productivity, increasing, as an example to all other industries in the country, as each year goes by; an industry which could make a very large contribution to the difficulties in which the nation finds itself with regard to its balance of payments. If I make a speech in which political bite is more absent than usual it is because the problems facing the industry merit serious and constructive thought.

    By and large, I believe that the industry is well equipped. The farm labour force, what there is of it—and I shall 4 have a word to say on that later—is first class. The Young Farmers' Club in Scotland is one of the best movements in the world. The farm improvements scheme has been a godsend to the industry, and has completely changed the face of the countryside and allowed the buildings in the latter half of the twentieth century to keep pace with the inventions which move about the fields. Housing varies between the geographical areas and between types of farming, but much of it is very good indeed.

    What is short is labour, cash and confidence. There has been a drop in the total labour force of an alarming nature. It is interesting to look back and note that in the five years between 1954 and 1958 the labour force fell by 6 per cent., but in the equivalent period from 1964 to 1968 it fell by 16 per cent. It is now down to 48,000, of whom slightly fewer than 40,000 are full-time regulars. Hitherto, mechanisation has filled the empty places, but I think most farmers would now admit that on most farms we are down to rock bottom of labour substitution. It is true that at the Royal Show today there is to be seen, I believe for the first time, a driverless tractor, and I suppose it is possible that, in this world of electronics, machines may move about the fields controlled from that armchair in which farmers are notoriously known to sit, but certainly, till that day comes—and I would myself be doubtful whether we shall on most farms, and particularly in the livestock sector, see any great ability to reduce labour more—I believe the situation to be critical.

    In, I think, the latest volume of Scottish Agricultural Economics, there is a study of the reasons why labour is short, and I believe I should be right in saying that the general conclusion is that wages are the main reason. Wages are nothing like so low as some people make out, and the statutory minimum figure is, in most cases, entirely academic, but, even so, there is, to my mind, no doubt that agricultural wages are unattractive and unworthy of one of the great industries of the country. The industry must be enabled to pay more, which it would wish to do. The hon. Gentleman the Under-Secretary scored what was a perfectly valid debating point in reply to a Question of mine the other day, when he said that of course there was nothing to stop any farmer paying as much above the JPEG 5 minimum as he liked. That is perfectly true, and, indeed, most farmers pay at least £1 above the minimum, and many pay £2 or even £3. Even so, the gap between that and the wage for the same sort of responsibility in industry is still too wide, but till the industry gets better prices for its products it cannot pay the wages which it ought to pay.

    The second commodity in short supply is cash, and there is a shortage for two reasons. First, not enough is coming out of farm profits; and, secondly, the banks are right up to the ceiling which the Government have imposed on their loans. The Minister of Agriculture told us the other night in the debate in the House that he had made representations to the banks in England and Wales. I wonder whether the hon. Gentleman would answer a specific question. Has his right hon. Friend made similar representations to the Scottish banks, and will they, the banks, be able to meet the request for special consideration which he has made? I am extremely doubtful whether they can, unless the Government allow agriculture to occupy a special position as an important import saver in exactly the same category as those who win our exports.

    Mr. W. H. K. Baker
    : Would my hon. Friend not agree that those remarks apply equally to the fishing industry?

    Mr. Stodart
    : I have no doubt that they do. but I rather doubt whether you, Mr. Robertson, would wish me to go into that today.

    If I may substantiate what I have said about the profits being made, I know that farmers' figures—not the physical figures, but the arithmetical figures—are always viewed with some scepticism, but in one of the newspapers the other day there was an interesting report quoting one of the recent econorrmic surveys made by the West of Scotland College, and the first few lines read as follows:

    "Dairy farmers in the West of Scotland who have fewer than 125 acres are making no more profit today than they did 12 years ago—about £850 a year—according to a report by the West of Scotland Agricultural College just published."

    That seems to tie up very closely with figures which I quoted from the North of Scotland College in a debate in the House earlier this year, to the effect that 40 per cent. of the farmers whose accounts were made up by the College in 6 1966 made a profit of less than a farm worker's wage; and that, at that time, meant less than £600 a year. All this must show that the financial reserves in the industry are low.

    There have been recent losses—not so bad in the arable sector as those south of the Border, but I quoted last week a figure which I had been given by the National Farmers' Union of Scotland, and said that cases had been heard of in which the lambing percentage in this spring had been as low as 20 per cent. The hon. Gentleman the Joint Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture said in his reply that he felt sure that must be exaggerated, but I have checked again and am told that that is, in fact, the case. That being so, in this spring's lambing we are up against something very nearly approaching, if not overtaking, the lambing disaster of 1947.

    I think that confidence is at a low ebb because farmers are not convinced that the expansion policy of the Government is a genuine one. If farmers really felt that the Government meant business and wanted expansion, if import saving—and I think this is a reasonable test—were given the same credit priorities as export-winning, there would be more confidence. The reserves are not yet so low that the industry could not mount an expansion programme; but expansion is meaningless, unless it is accompanied by import substitution. If we expand production but go on bringing in the same amount of imports, chaos will merety ensue.

    It is disturbing, after all we have heard about import saving, to read the latest market survey of the Meat and Livestock Commission, spring-summer, 1969, which says this:

    "Imports of meat during the first quarter of the year were much higher than during the first quarter of 1968, mainly because of larger imports of lamb."

    This is at a time when we are trying to save on imports. It goes on:

    "Lamb imports, mainly from New Zealand, were 6 per cent. higher than in January to March, 1968."

    I do not think that the Government have been converted to import saving, so far as British agriculture is concerned. I do not think they know how the expansion, to which they have put their name, is to be financed. Can the Treasury do it, and if the Treasury cannot, as I believe it cannot, and if the expansion has to JPEG 7 be paid for by the consumer, is not a substantial contribution to our balance of payments problem worth a marginal increase in food prices? But, believer as I am in an expansion policy, I believe we have to keep our feet on the ground in thinking about it, and not get carried away like, if I may say so, the Scottish Nationalist Party appears to have been carried away, if one is to take its statement and policy seriously.

    It has said that output should be raised by 40 per cent. The present value of Scottish agricultural output is £200 million. Therefore, a 40 per cent. increase leaves an increase of £80 million worth. It also says that the total cost to the taxpayer is not to differ very much from the present figure. There is to be a massive reclamation of millions of acres of bog land, deer forest and sheep runs. It is unarguable that £80 million worth could not be got from what I would describe as the existing conventional areas of production. The N.E.D.C.'s extremely careful survey gave less than £25 million as the possible Scottish contribution. This must mean that the difference between the £25 million and the £80 million, the £55 million of the Scottish National Party's increase, must come from reclamation, and this must be done for £24 million, as the total cost of support is not to differ much, and also, according to that party's statement of policy, the production grants are to remain. Not only has reclamation to be done at very heavy cost—and if the Minister was unable to give as accurate and precise figures, at least he hinted that the cost of reclamation per acre is very heavy—but the acres reclaimed and stocked must be grant aided with hill cow, hill sheep and calf subsidies. Although every viewpoint on a matter of this kind merits the most careful scrutiny, and although I do not believe that expansion could not conceivably be done, I would say that it is physically impossible to see it to the tune of a 55 per cent. increase over present levels for £24 million.

    I would like to say a word about the hills and uplands. This is a sector in which, I dare say, the Government would claim they have shown more interest than almost any other, but it is becoming clearer and clearer to me that the policies 8 they are following there are not being effective. The net income that the farmer draws at the end of the day is what really matters. It was at its peak for this decade so far in 1964-65, when the net income of hill farmers was £1,578, compared with £1,322 today. The net income of upland farmers in 1964-65 was £1,308 and in 1967-68 it was £1,121.

In other words, subsidies from the taxpayer are going up and up and up and the net income to the farmer is coming down and down and down. This cannot be the proper way to do things. The subsidy given entirely to the cow and the ewe gives no incentive to raise the percentage rates of lambing and farming. We should consider transferring some of the subsidy on to the lamb or the calf in order to give a farmer incentive to jack up his lambing percentages.

    To subsidise stock and neglect the land which is carrying the stock is the wrong way of doing it. It shattered me to see in an Answer I received yesterday that the application of lime has fallen to three-quarters of what it was four years ago. If there is one thing the hills need desperately it is the application of good lime. It is no use allowing the land to become sourer and sourer, and unable to carry as many head of stock as it used to, and merely jacking up the subsidy rate on the stock, because all that is happening, as the figures show, is that at the end of the day the income is coming steadily down.
