Mr. Kennedy
: I beg to move amendment No. 318, in page 5, line 46, leave out "ownership and".

If there are to be NHS trusts, as outlined in the Bill, which will be charged with the management of hospitals, that should remain distinct and separate from ownership of hospitals. We have been told again that hospitals will not be opted-out of the NHS but will remain very much a part of the NHS. If that is true, I cannot understand why the Government are hostile to the amendment, which would simply confirm that the ownership of hospitals and related land or buildings would remain firmly under the auspices of the NHS and could not be disposed of by hospital trusts.

We have heard all the factors that are supposed to be part of self-governing hospitals, and phrases such as corporate spirit, rewarding excellence and managing staff, but none of those is dependent on the ownership of land. Ownership implies the right to sell and to dispose of assets, the proceeds of which would presumably go to improving the work of the self-governing hospital, but the land was probably bought and paid for by the DHA with money that could otherwise have been used for the whole community. Therefore, resources raised from sales should surely go towards improving the health care of local people, and not just to providing resources for specialised treatment, which may have far less immediate relevance to local needs.

The Secretary of State should have powers to prevent a sale, but the self-governing hospital trust should have to justify to the relevant health authority its decision to dispose of assets. There is a deep suspicion that the words "ownership and", standing alongside the management function of the hospital in the clause, could lead to asset-stripping and the longer-term posibility of privatisation. People will be anxious and will ask why the provision is in the Bill, as it does not appear to be central or necessary to the management of a hospital trust. I hope that the Minister will reasssure us on that point.

The important issue underlying the amendments is accountability. To appreciate that, it is important to sketch out how medical education is funded. It involves two Government Departments. Part of the cash comes from the Vote of the Department of Health Vote, via health authorities. It pays for the service element of teaching and, to some extent, research. The service element includes hospital facilities and NHS staff. The other part comes from the Vote of the Department of Education and Science via the Universities Funding Council and is intended to cover clinical academic staff who teach undergradutates and undertake research.

We discussed the knock-for-knock system about which Glasgow university's principal expressed anxiety. That is the term used to describe the way in which university clinical teachers and NHS consultants in university teaching hospitals are paid by only one employer while doing work for two. Whether consultants are academic or from the Health Service makes no difference from the patient's viewpoint. In univesity hospitals, some patients are under the care of consultants employed by the NHS who have honorary teaching contracts. This means that they are paid to look after patients but receive no pay for teaching students. Other patients are under the care of university professors such as readers or senior lecturers. They are employed by the university and, in a sort of mirror image, have honorary NHS contracts. They are paid to teach students and undertake research, but do not receive pay for looking after patients. There are two categories plus two votes.

The relationship between universities with medical schools and the NHS is extremely close—indeed, it is inextricably linked. On that basis, it is not surprising that universities are unhappy about what they regard as the inadequacy of having only one representative on the board, which is what the Bill specifies.

If the universities have a shared responsibility with health authorities and with the NHS trusts that are to be set up under schedule 2, they must have a real share in decision making. Near my constituency is Hammersmith hospital which comes under the aegis of the special health authority, and the Royal Post Graduate Medical School, which is part of the University of London. The Bill asks what will happen to the relationship between a university and a health authority if the latter becomes a trust and is therefore required to place contracts and try to obtain value for money.

The amendments suggest that the Bill lacks adequate safeguards to protect the university's interest in research and in training doctors. They stiffen up the Bill and by providing those safeguards. If, for the sake of argument, the Hammersmith special health authority became a trust, its relationship with the University of London would not be adversely affected. The university would also have the access to the hospital's facilities that it needs to train staff. At the heart of the amendments is the worry that universities will not be able to carry out their role if hospitals go down the contract path too fast and too far.
