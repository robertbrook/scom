Education (Assisted Places) (Scotland) Amendment Regulations 1996

4.30 pm

Mrs. Helen Liddell
(Monklands, East)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Education (Assisted Places) (Scotland) Amendment Regulations 1996 (S.I. 1996, No. 1808).

I first apologise to the Committee for the fact that my voice is not as loud as usual. The downside is that I have a sore throat—the upside is that it will make my speech considerably shorter.

The measure before us seeks to extend the assisted places scheme to include children under the age of 11, among other things. This will see the further expansion of a scheme which is already to be doubled in size over three years, and that of course will be a futher burden on the British taxpayer.

In view of the fact that only one Scottish Conservative Member is present, it might, as a matter of courtesy, be as well to point out that in Scotland the education system is significantly different from that in England and Wales. In Scotland, the overwhelming support for state comprehensive education means that 96 per cent. of all children in Scottish schools are in the state sector. Even those who can afford to send their children to private schools choose to send them to state sector schools.

The very fact that the assisted places scheme exists is a straightforward acknowledgement by the Government that they view the state sector as second best. The arguments that they use in support of the scheme largely relate to the fact that it gives children "from poorer families" an opportunity to attend private schools. Those of us who have had the benefit of a good Scottish state education—and I include the Minister among that number—know that there are few countries in the world that can provide an education of the quality of the Scottish state sector.

The extension of the assisted places scheme to those in primary education has not been broadly welcomed in Scotland. That should be no surprise. How can it be, when an extra £9 million has been given to the expansion of a scheme that already benefits private schools to the tune of £11 million—by contrast with the drastic cuts in local authority education budgets of £60 million last year—4.7 per cent.—and this year, a further £21 million?

Mrs. Liddell

I should welcome some answers from the Minister. Over the past couple of years, I have asked him several questions about the assisted places scheme, and I have received no answers. When he responds to my remarks, will he tell the Committee how much of the additional £9 million that is being made available to extend the scheme will de directed towards children JPEG 5 under 11? I believe I am correct in stating that the scheme has been extended since September 1996. Will the Minister tell the Committee how many under-lls have so far entered the assisted places scheme and how many of them were already in the private sector?

Furthermore—this relates to the point made by the hon. Member for Wantage (Mr. Jackson)—there is great concern in Scotland about the fact that the expansion of the scheme will benefit a disproportionately tiny minority of Scottish pupils—only 0.4 per cent. of the 4 per cent. of children in the private sector. What about the 96 per cent. of children who are in the state sector? I am sure that they would benefit from the sums that the Minister has in the past claimed could add up to £20 million, if that amount were allocated to the state sector.

In 1995, average expenditure per pupil on the assisted places scheme was £3,500. That compares with only £2,700 per pupil in state schools. Those are the Government's own figures from Her Majesty's inspectorate's "Scottish School Costs 1995-97". In Scottish primary schools, average expenditure per pupil in 1995 was £1,700. What is the estimated figure for pupils in the assisted places scheme? Will it be significantly higher, as it is for secondary schools?

We debated this matter about 14 months ago in relation to a similar statutory instrument. At that time, the Minister claimed that the scheme does not provide a subsidy to private sector schools. He has failed to explain why a number of schools each benefit from more than £500,000 a year. I know that that figure is attractive to the Minister—after all, he managed to find £500,000 for one tiny school in Dunblane. But if private schools are to benefit to the tune of £500,000 from taxpayer's money, why cannot such sums be taken from the scheme and used directly in the state sector? A school in Edinburgh received £975,000 in the last academic year, when only 11 per cent. of its pupils were on the assisted places scheme. That seems to be a classic case of misdirection of resources.

The Minister's usual response is that the scheme gives children more equal opportunities, regardless of their parents' income, and that parents recognise that the scheme offers their children the chance of better academic results, but the figures do not bear that out. If one analyses the figures, particularly those that were made available in the Scottish Office press releases, it is obvious that about 52 per cent. of those currently benefiting from the assisted places scheme were already in the private sector. If that is not a classic opportunity for subsidising private schools that are, in some cases, failing, I do not know what is.

There has been a recognition in the educational community in Scotland that the assisted places scheme is keeping open schools that would otherwise have long since closed. I have met representatives of the Scottish Council of Independent Schools and individual schools in the independent sector, a number of which are worried about schools that do not provide the quality of education which they are providing and are able to continue as a result of the subsidies which are available through the assisted places scheme.
