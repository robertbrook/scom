Mr. O'Neill
: The amendments are concerned with the fees in regard to the assisted-places scheme. We seek to ensure that the scheme, which we consider to have been somewhat carelessly constructed, is examined. The first aspect that we should like to consider this morning is boarding fees.

The question of boarding schools is rather vexed in education circles. Many of us feel that an integral part of a child's life and development is that which he shares with his immediate family. It may be deleterious to a child's development to take him away from his home environment and place him in a rather austere institution which robs him of his parents' affection. That is as important at the time of puberty as at any other time—perhaps even more important.

Nevertheless, we recognise that many of the schools in the scheme—although not all—hitherto have not taken in day pupils. The exclusion from the scheme of boarding fees may result in schools having to provide extra facilities. We should like the Minister to tell us which schools, which at present have only boarding pupils, will make alternative arrangements to supplement the assisted places scheme by paying boarding fees so that pupils can enjoy—if that is the right expression—the full range of alleged benefits that such schools afford.

We say that in the knowledge that boarding schools are viewed with some suspicion regarding the contribution which they make to the well-being and development of young people. We recognise that in certain circumstances boarding schools are, at best, an unhappy compromise. There are people who work abroad where the educational provision in English is not as satisfactory as they would wish. Therefore, they are obliged to send their children to Britain to be educated.

There are occasions when children can stay with aunts and uncles or with grandparents. The family unit can be retained as they can attend a day school either within the local authority area or in the private sector of education.

However, we are not dealing with that group of people today. We are dealing with people from modest backgrounds who will be offered the opportunity of attending those schools. I say "offered the opportunity" in a general sense because as yet the Minister has not given a clear indication about the method of assessing the suitability of those young 558 people for the scheme. However, we shall work on the assumption that in the first instance they are acceptable to the school. We must know which institutions in the scheme will offer boarding places, assuming that the money will have to come from school funds.

We know that some of the schools at present offer only boarding education. They do not provide for day school facilities. We should like to know which schools involved in the scheme are seeking to make financial provision to support the young people. We should also like to know about those schools which hitherto had only boarding pupils and are now introducing day pupils as an extra.

The question of boarding fees raises certain problems. We recognise that some schools offer extracurricular activities which are important to an all-round education. For children to participate in such extra-curricular activities, they would be expected to live in the school. If children have to make a 50-mile round trip—the Minister has said that that is the distance which children should be expected to travel if they are to participate in the scheme—and the schools have a wide range of extra-curricular activities which can be entered into only if the children are boarding, we consider that the assisted places scheme will not meet the full prospectus. The scheme will not meet its full educational objectives.

Consequently, we have grave misgivings about the exclusion of boarding fees. We do not wish to see any more money spent on the scheme than we can afford; it should be the utter minimum. We made a point, somewhat ineptly the other night, when we chose a figure which was no higher than that which was made available to grant-aided schools in 1979 during the lifetime of the previous Labour Government. If young people are to have access to the full facilities, we question the Minister's exclusion of boarding fees.

The amendment is a probing amendment which we do not intend to push to a vote, but we think that this is a legitimate point which shows the Government's confused and simplistic approach. They have not thought through the full educational implications of the scheme and certainly not about those institutions which have provided boarding facilities only.

We want to discover the Minister's thoughts—if any—on this matter. The exclusion of boarding fees may have been thrown in as an afterthought—"We cannot spend too much money, so we cannot pay boarding fees." An amendment in the names of some of the Minister's hon Friends deals with travelling expenses, where it is considered that it may be better that the child should stay at the school rather than travel back and forth every day. That amendment will doubtless be reached some time today. Our amendment is not concerned with travelling only. It is perhaps indicative of the Government's views that they are preoccupied with travelling, as a purely material matter, whereas our amendment is rooted in consideration of the educational aspects.

We have grave misgivings about the use of boarding education. We recognise that in certain circumstances young people's education cannot be pro JPEG 559 vided by any other means in the United Kingdom —if parents are abroad and if there are no relatives and grandparents who can look after the children so that they can attend day school at the same time as enjoying the full benefits of family life. The Opposition are not sympathetic to boarding schools, because we feel that they prevent children from benefiting from the full range and depth of family life.

Nevertheless, if schools which hitherto have offered only boarding places will be offering something akin to second best under the scheme, we want to know how the Minister has arrived at such a compromise with the schools and how it would be dealt with under the scheme. This is why we have moved the amendment.

Amendment No. 131 concerns other charges by a school in addition to fees. The amendment would have the effect of excluding

"such other charges, if any, as may be prescribed by regulations".

We have seen some of the other charges mentioned in regulations. In debates earlier in the week, when we strayed close to being out of order, we raised the matter of the other charges. We touched on academic expenditure and quoted the example of the provision of science courses.

Not many new textbooks have been produced in the past two or three years for A-level Latin and Greek. Not many cassette tapes are required in the language laboratories to master Cicero, Livy, Virgil or other classical writers. Certain subjects, therefore, require less expenditure by schools on the provision of materials. However, expenditure is required for technical subjects where materials have to be provided—timber, metal—and the initial outlay on equipment involves a considerable amount of money. The same applies to business studies where pupils expect to have access to typewriters and word processors. For computing courses there have to be many computers. In the State local authority-funded area a great deal of money is now being spent to assist young people to come to terms with modern technology. That takes money, and the sort of schools that provide those facilities are usually in the State sector. But extra charges are sometimes levied in the private sector in the form of a higher rate of fees per term. At other times those charges are met out of the imposition of an additional levy on each pupil who embarks on those courses.

The other evening, the Minister did not give us a satisfactory answer about definition concerning the academic charges that relate to course content. There are also charges for other subjects such as musk; lessons, games, membership of societies, and so on. The Minister has been understandably coy in his replies because he does not know how much would be entailed. Therefore, we hope that, having had the luxury of a couple of days' grace in which to sort out those matters, he can be more specific than he was previously.

Amendment No. 133 is also a probing amendment. We are happy for this provision to be included because the various prospectuses show that there are wide variations in fees. That can result from many 560 aspects such as the wealth of the school in respect of its historical accumulation of endowments which enable the fees to be reduced. The school may have a high level of expenditure on new equipment, new dormitories and new buildings so that in that instance, the financial resources must be extended, and it is therefore necessary to charge higher fees. The schools may be run not just as charities but in such a way as to ensure a surplus from year to year. There are also problems with the effect of charges such as value added tax, which rose to 15 per cent in June 1979.

Clearly, all that will result in differing fee levels, and we are happy that the Secretary of State has the power to consider seriously the level of fees to see whether it is excessive.

We ask the Minister to explain what he means by the word "excessive". We know that sometimes the Secretary of State's view of what is excessive is somewhat different from that held by, for example, Lothian region on rates rises. The word "excessive" could be used rather more generously in the interests of fee paying schools than it is in the interests of Labour local authorities, which merely seek to provide a reasonable education service for ratepayers and their children. Therefore, amendment No. 133 is a probing amendment and we should not like subsection (8) to be removed from the Bill.

We were surprised that the Minister did not seek to underpin subsection (7X£), but it is significant that one of the hallmarks of the Government's approach to legislation is that they should seek to cavil and cajole the local authority sector wherever they can and leave the private sector virtually without great obligations. The paragraph is concerned with the provision of examination entrance fees in respect of candidates from a school but makes no clear statement—such as was included in the parents' charter—of the rights of parents to insist that their children be allowed to take particular courses and be entered for particular examinations. We have sought to extend those rights to cover this point.

We do not feel that the Government have been fair with the parents of children who will take part in the assisted places scheme. They ought to state specifically that parents of children in the scheme should be treated in exactly the same way as parents who send their children to local authority schools.

We recognise that on occasions parents have a somewhat inflated sense of their children's abilities and that they might present them for courses and examinations which may not be suitable. In all our discussions the Government have never given a clear indication of the required ability range of children and the manner in which that would be determined. We had some doubts when we discussed the matter in regard to the local authority sector, but since it is included in parental rights in that sector, it should be included in the private sector, especially as the funds are to be provided by the Excheqeur.

All our amendments in this group are probing amendments. We do not expect to have to vote on them. But we think that these areas should be explored, because the regulations to which they refer were published only on Monday—a point about JPEG 561 which we have already complained—and a Prayer has been tabled in order that the amendments may be considered by the House within 40 days.

Because of our opposition to the assisted-places scheme, we shall seek to oppose the statutory instrument containing the regulations at the appropriate opportunity. Nevertheless, it would be helpful for our debate to hear replies from the Minister to some of the matters that we have raised on this part of the regulations.

I shall make no further comment on the amendments. We shall seek leave to withdraw the amendment after we have heard the ministerial reply, which I hope we shall not hear until some of my colleagues have had an opportunity to make additional comments.
