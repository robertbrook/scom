Erskine Bridge Tolls Extension Order 1991

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Erskine Bridge Tolls Extension Order 1991.

The Erskine bridge, which stands on the River Clyde nine miles downstream from Glasgow, was built at the request of the Clyde Navigation Trust and Renfrew-shire and Dunbartonshire county councils, the local authorities in the area at the time. The local authorities accepted that the bridge would have to be tolled.

Erskine bridge serves as the strategic route for traffic travelling to the north of the River Clyde from the M8 in the east, the M74 in the south and the A77 in the south-west. It provides a high quality and increasingly important link within the trunk road network in Scotland.

The Erskine bridge opened to traffic on 2 July 1971. In its first full year of operation, some 1·9 million vehicles used it. By 1976 the figure was 2·9 million and by 1989, 6·4 million. The average annual growth rate between 1976 and 1989, the latest period for which comparable national figures are available, was 6·8 per cent. That was much higher than the national average of 4 per cent. That high increase has continued and in 1990, 6·85 million vehicles used the bridge. The current national road traffic forecasts suggest that road traffic in Great Britain could increase by 112 per cent. between 1991 and the year 2025. As Strathclyde has a lower average car ownership than the country as a whole, we expect that traffic on the Erskine bridge will continue to increase at a greater rate than the national average as the gap in car ownership narrows.

The Erskine Bridge Tolls Act 1968 sets the initial tolling period at 20 years. That period ended on 1 July 1991. The Act empowers the Secretary of State for Scotland to set tolls, if he wishes to do so, up to the level which, over the tolling period, would recover the cost of building the bridge and provide for its maintenance over the remainder of its lifetime. That is in line with the principle adopted by successive Governments that the user should pay for the benefits that estuarial crossings provide. That policy has been confirmed in this House on several occasions.

The Secretary of State for Scotland is not required to set tolls at such a level. It was recognised, even before the bridge was opened in 1971, that it would never be possible at Erskine to recover the full costs of the bridge because of the relatively low levels of traffic, the relatively short distances that traffic would require to 4 divert for alternative Clyde crossings and the economic situation of the area served by the bridge. The limited objective was set, therefore, of trying to recover as much as was reasonably possible from tolls while at the same time having regard to the economy of the area.

The 1968 Act also empowers the Secretary of State to extend the tolling period by periods of five years. He may do that if the full cost of building the bridge and providing for its future maintenance, has not been recovered over the existing tolling period. Because tolls have been set below the cost recovery level, the conditions under which the tolling period may be extended have been met. The Erskine Bridge Tolls Extension Order, which we are debating today, extends the tolling period for a further five years, commencing on 2 July 1991.

The long-term financing of and arrangements for accounting for the Erskine bridge are under consideration. Although the benefits of the bridge are being enjoyed by a growing number of commuters, tourists and businesses, it remains the case that the full cost of building and maintaining the bridge cannot be recovered from tolls revenue. That is because raising the toll to the levels that would be required to allow the bridge to break even would act as a deterrent to the bridge's use. Traffic would be driven away from the bridge and on to the already heavily used Clyde tunnel and Kingston bridge. There would thus continue to be a substantial revenue shortfall at Erskine. But it is right, and in line with successive Governments' policies, that we should continue to recover a contribution to the costs of the bridge from those who benefit from it. Therefore, we decided to extend the tolling period by the five years that the Act allows. We also propose, subject to completion of the separate statutory procedures, to increase toll levels to a flat rate of 60p from 1 April 1992. The required statutory procedures for implementing the increase have already been set in motion.

The proposed level of toll continues to take full account of the economy of the area. If, for example, we had sought to keep the tolls in line with original level set in 1971, they would now be 98p at today's prices. If we had sought to maximise revenue from tolls, without regard to the economy of the local area, the level of tolls would be between £2 and £3.

We are seeking, however to impose an increase only from 40p to 60p. We recognise that a steep increase now, 20 years after the opening, to, say, £2 per crossing would be highly disruptive to the patterns of travel and trade that have been established. The increase to 60p will mean that tolls in real terms remain well below the levels originally set in 1971. We do not expect that the toll increase to be applied will result in any significant loss of traffic using the bridge. Our view is supported by an examination of patronage in 1989, the year the tolls were last increased, from 30p to 40p, and the years immediately before and after 1989. There was a steady increase in traffic using the bridge. In 1988 about 5.75 million vehicles crossed. In 1989 the figure was 6.37 million and in 1990, as I have already said it was about 6.85 million.

By extending the tolling period by means of the order, the Government are maintaining the principle JPEG 5 that the user should pay for the benefits that he or she enjoys. It is our intention that toll levels set in the extended period should strike a reasonable balance between recovery of costs and the need to take account of the difficult economic circumstances of the area that the bridge serves.

I commend the order to the Committee.

I looked up some of the debates that led us to this position. It is interesting, given current circumstances, to see how the debate has evolved. When the Erskine bridge was being built in the late 1960s there was already anxiety in Scotland about the effect of tolls, as both the Forth and Tay bridges had been tolled. There was a clear feeling that although bridges were marvellous advances for the Scottish economy, tolls were and would increasingly become inhibitions on the benefits to be gained from them. Lord James Douglas-Hamilton

I said that the Erskine bridge was built in response to local demand. The hon. Gentleman cannot escape the reality that the Erskine bridge was started under a Labour Government. If there had been no local demand, I presume that they would not have gone ahead with the project. If there were no tolls on the Erskine bridge, it would almost certainly not be there today.

The Erskine bridge plays an increasingly important role in the Scottish trunk road network. It is our policy to toll estuarial crossings to recover building and maintenance costs, on the principle that the benefits should be paid for by those who enjoy them. We cannot, for the reasons that I have given, recover the full costs. We take into account the economic situation of the surrounding area. Our proposals to extend tolling by five 14 years and to increase the tolls to 60p are moderate and in line with our policies. In real terms, tolls will remain below the level originally set in 1971. I commend the order to the Committee.
