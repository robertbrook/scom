10.41 a.m.

Mr. Teddy Taylor
: I think that my hon. Friends were absolutely right to raise the points that they did. Quite apart from the fact that the printing difficulties are impeding the work of Members of Parliament on important issues such as this, there is also the fact that the shambles over the provision of documents adds further uncertainty to an industry which is already facing a great deal of uncertainty. For that reason we very much regret the situation and hope that steps will be taken to try to prevent these difficulties arising in future.

The attendance of hon. Members in this Committee—sometimes our Committees are not well attended when we 8 are considering Statutory Instruments of this sort—shows how important to the industry as a whole and to those concerned for the interests of our fishing communities are the general issues behind the orders. We have before us a total of five orders. Although I doubt that many of us will have objections in principle to the idea of conservation which lies behind the orders, there is no doubt that many of us are most concerned. Unquestionably these measures will add to the hardship which existing conservation measures have caused to our industry and to those who process fish.

The fact that the industry has supported such measures shows the commitment of the British industry to maintaining our fish stocks, but it would be wrong for the Government to disregard the fact that all these conservation measures cause hardship to British fishermen and to those who process fish.

I have three questions for the Minister arising out of the orders. First, as we have been waiting for a very long time, will the Minister make a statement on whether the Government plan to make further efforts to help the fish processors who are being hard hit by the conservation measures limiting the amount of fishing being done? A number of firms have suffered a great deal. We have had redundancies and further problems are arising as the temporary employment subsidy is being phased out.

This problem has not arisen suddenly. It is one about which the Government have been well warned. Is the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland able to give us some indication of whether he has any further plans to assist fish processors who are being hit very hard by conservation measures?

My second point relates to the scope of the conservation measures before us. The Minister will be aware that, despite the hardship that the restrictions cause, the industry has been united in supporting conservation measures. Although it is true to say, as Mr. Gilbert Buchan said in the recently published Scottish Fishermen's Federation newsletter, that the problem of communications in such a scattered industry remains formidable, there is little doubt that the industry as a whole supports conservation measures and believes that in some respects they should be tightened up.

JPEG 9 Have the Government given consideration to adding to these conservation measures, and, in particular, will they reconsider the question of the suspension of the introduction of the 70 mm. net, together with the restriction which should have introduced the one net, one voyage rule. There is no doubt that the industry places a great deal of emphasis on the importance of the introduction of a one net rule, which would help to put an end to one of the biggest abuses of the net regulations.

The third general question relates to herring, which is dealt with in one of these orders in which further restrictions have been introduced. The industry supported the measures that the Government took on herring because of its concern about the elimination of the herring stock. Has further scientific consideration been given to the movement of herring stocks and is there any possibility of a limited quota being introduced for herring in the near future? This is a matter of particular importance to Scotland, where a great deal of hardship has been caused by the restrictions.

I think that, in general, the Opposition can say that, together with the industry, we wholly support the need for these orders. We believe that there is a need perhaps for the further tightening up of conservation by the introduction of the two further measures that I have indicated. I hope that the Government will be able to give us a clear assurance that, if we are unsuccessful in getting the kind of general agreement that the industry greatly needs in the discussions in the EEC, it will be the Government's intention to review the whole nature of our present conservation measures and to consider the introduction of further conservation measures—in particular, the one net rule and the 70 mm. minimum.

In addition, I hope that the Government will give us a full assurance that they will do all they possibly can to ensure that the negotiations upon which we are about to embark once again will result in a fair agreement for the industry as a whole.

Members with any knowledge of the fishing industry have supported these conservation measures in the past. We shall continue to support them, as the fishermen have supported them, but the Government must be more forthcoming on the effects which they are having and on the actions which they themselves intend to take.

In order to ensure that the TAC of 9,000 tonnes for the fishery was not exceeded, the Government sought on 17th August the Commission's approval for a national measure. This regulated fishing for the main 1978 season in the fishery by means of a licensing scheme for United Kingdom and Isle of Man vessels to take not more than 8,100 tonnes, and a closure of the fishery no later than 24th September for the remainder of the year.

They say that stocks may be recovering and that it would do no harm to have a limited catch in the Minch, in particular. I am sorry, but for both scientific reasons and reasons of presentation it would be most unfortunate if it were thought in EEC circles that our industry was so desperate that it was willing to breach or go against what at the moment is sound scientific evidence.

The hon. Member for Fife, East (Sir J. Gilmour) asked about Cylde herring. We have a similar difficulty there. Fishing will be continuing, but we have been JPEG 35 asked to supply the scientific evidence to justfy our claim that it is a separate stock. We have done that and the matter rests there. There will be Clyde herring available, mainly for Clyde fishermen, next year as there has been this year. That is the state of play at the moment.

Most of the other questions have been answered by my right hon. Friend. The hon. Member for North Angus and Mearns (Mr. Buchanan-Smith) raised one of the most important questions: Why are we not restructuring our industry when, apparently, other countries in the EEC are doing so? I can only say, first, that obviously each industry in each country is different. The approach of each Government may be different, and I would not have thought that the need for restructuring was the major problem at the moment.

The hard, cold economic facts of the future of the fishing industry—I am sorry to have to say this to my hon. Friends whose constituencies are bearing the brunt of the troubles—seem to point to a diminution in deep-sea or long-distance catching effort. Everyone accepts that, including the unions that are involved. They have been marvellous in their responsibility as we have a real problem here. But it is still not clear. There are too many imponderables as well as the negotiating situation which also is difficult. There are too many imponderables for us to make dramatic pronouncements about what help might be available for the industry.

Of course, there will need to be discussion about what form assistance might take in the fairly near future in an effort to ensure that we have an industry that will match the availability of fish, where it is, and the kind of fish, depending on the settlement of the common fisheries policy. So it comes back to a matter of judgment as to whether we should go overboard to try to evolve structural changes within the industry that perhaps might not be the right ones.

I am not trying to score a debating point—certainly not with the hon. Member for North Angus and Mearns—but I think it is a slight exaggeration on the part of the firm operating out of Granton to say that if it had had the same assistance as the West German Government 36 were giving it might still be in existence. That is, at most, a debatable point.

I can understand the feelings of people who have gone out of business after a liftime of operating from a port such as Granton. But, again, I think that Granton must be included in the category of Grimsby and Hull which have been hard hit by the change in the fishing opportunities around our shores. It is one of the facts of life that we have to accept with some regret.

I want to conclude by referring to some of the interesting points that always seem to be raised by the hon. Member for Fife, East. I seldom know the answers to his questions, whether on agriculture or on fishing. My right hon. Friend had a go at answering the one about the dead fish and I shall not attempt to improve on what he said. I do not want to take advantage of the fact that there is no hon. Members from the south-west here at the moment but, at best, there is some doubt about the stories coming out of the southwest. I do not want to be too nationalistic, because I know that the other side of the coin is that everyone is talking about the pirates from Scotland. There are genuine problems that concern all of us, and what my right hon. Friend said was perfectly true: we are aware of the problem and we are concerned about it. It does no good to have fishermen fighting with fishermen. We have enough problems on our hands without that. I am sure that good sense will prevail.

As for blue whiting and industrial fishing, there are problems here but nevertheless it is a resource that is available and one that we should exploit.

I cannot comment on the Rockall order. I must confess that I am not the one who pinched it from the Library, or I might have been able to comment on it. I am sure that it has something to do with the west coast restriction and haddock catching, but I shall send an answer to the hon. Member in due course.

Finally, as regards the sum of£2¼ million for four new boats, I have no detailed information. I do not know why two should get grant and two should not, but I shall look into that and advise the hon. Member. In general terms, it highlights the difficulty in the industry. Do we restructure? Without being smug and complacent, I can say that the fishing JPEG 37 fleet, and particularly the Scottish fishing fleet, is reasonably modern and efficient. Nevertheless, there should be some replacement even in a period of restriction, so there is nothing inconsistent in a few new boats being built with grants. The question is: why have only two got a grant?

This has been a useful debate, but I am surprised that one point has not been raised. I shall give this gratuitously. I cannot guarantee that it will be this week, but we are reaching a period when there is a possibility of reaching some kind of agreement. The hon. Member for Cath-cart was unusually fair in what he asked for. He asked for an assurance that there would be a fair agreement. That is the most temperate language I have ever heard from the hon. Member on any subject. But I appreciate that, as there is no question of a sell-out. We think that we have improved the negotiating atmosphere. That is all that I could claim on behalf of my right hon. Friends as of today. What will happen, or whether or not the atmosphere will change, I do not know, but for us to continue as we are doing to press for exclusive 12-mile limits with the problems of phasing out of historic rights—and what is a historic right?—for us to be going for some kind of access preference, to put it no higher than that, without talking about 50 miles or anything else —for us to define that and be within Community thinking is a difficult exercise.

I repeat that there is absolutely no doubt about our determination to get a good agreement, and there is no doubt, as I have said before, that the combination of my right hon. Friend the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food and the Secretary of State for Scotland makes a formidable team for negotiating skills, stubbornness and cleverness. Therefore, all I can promise is that we want not to be tough but to fight hard to get a fair settlement. I think that we have the knowledge and the skills to reach one, and I am delighted that we have the support, by and large, of the Committee.

I give one other assurance. There is a real problem—I am sure that the hon. Member for North Angus and Mearns was involved in negotiations—in that we 38 have to get the support and the backing of the industry for whatever agreement is put before us. We have also to get the support and backing of the House. How we advise the House, whether before the settlement or after, is a difficult problem in a negotiating situation, and I hope that hon. Members will be sympathetic in the way in which we shall do our best to communicate with the House on what is happening.
