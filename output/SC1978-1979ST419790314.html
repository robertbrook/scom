NATIONAL HEALTH SERVICE (PRESERVATION OF BOARDS OF GOVERNORS) ORDER 1979

10.30 a.m.

Mr. Patrick Jenkin
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the National Health Service (Preservation of Boards of Governors) Order 1979 (S.I., 1979, No. 51).

The Committee will recognise that the time for praying against this order has elapsed but the procedures of the House allow us, nevertheless, to discuss it, which is all the Opposition ever intended. There has been, I fear, misunderstanding in some quarters, as there always is when an Opposition table a prayer against a statutory instrument. I sought to allay that by combining the tabling of the prayer with the issue of a press notice to the entire medical press, explaining that we did not intend to vote on the order but merely to discuss it. However, that did not prevent anxieties from arising. I published a second press notice when it became known that the order was to be debated here this morning and I hope that all concerned will realise that we intend merely to debate the order, to explore the Government's intentions regarding the further time allowed by the order and to make one or two observations.

The order concerns 12 groups of specialist hospitals in London, among which are some of the most prestigious hospitals in the world. Merely to read the list of hospitals to be found in Appendix A of the Government's consultative paper on the London postgraduate hospitals is to realise that I do not exaggerate.

The hospitals under discussion include the Hospital for Sick Children at Great Ormond Street, at which the wife of the Prime Minister has the honour to be the chairman of the Governors; the National Hospital for Nervous Diseases at Queen Square; Moorfields Eye Hospital, perhaps one of the world's leading eye hospitals; the Bethlem Royal and Maudslev Hospitals, for psychiatry; the Royal Marsden Hospital, which has a pre- 4 eminent place in the treatment of cancer, and a number of others. Each of these hospitals is linked with a corresponding institute of postgraduate medical education and research, such as the Institute of Cancer Research and the Institute of Psychiatry which together represent a valuable resource not only for the patients but for medicine and the National Health Service as a whole.

These institutes are federated into the British Postgraduate Medical Federation which itself is one of the schools of London University. Here again, we have an organisation that straddles the difficult but productive boundary between the National Health Service and the universities.

In the Government's consultative document about the London specialist postgraduate hospitals, published last September, the role of the hospitals and their related institutions was described in these words:

"The institutes and hospitals between them make an important contribution to postgraduate medical education in London, and to specialist and post-certificate training of nurses and paramedical staff. Although their contribution to medical education is a small part of the total in the country, it is nevertheless significant nationally and, indeed, internationally."

I stress, in particular, the international reputation of the institutions.


"They carry out much important work in the fields of specialised medical and scientific research, and the development and trial of new treatments. This work too is of national and international importance. Clinically the hospitals serve as national referral centres, though on average between 85 per cent, and 90 per cent, of their patients are drawn from the Thames Regions. Many of them provide a substantial local service, sometimes by formal agreement with the AHAs concerned."

As one reads through the consultative document, and some of the predecessor documents that were produced by the Department, one gains the impression, perhaps wrongly, that there is an almost wistful regret that these institutions exist at all. An impression is given that they constitute an untidy, illogical and tiresome excrescence on the administrative scene and that their existence interferes with the neat, orderly, logical pattern of hospital care in London, tidily arranged in sectors, districts, areas and regions, each serving its adjacent population according JPEG 5 to some overall master plan in the DHSS, neatly wrapped and tied up—if I may be excused a bad pun—with "lengths of RAWP" which are devised in the Elephant and Castle.

This view of the postgraduate hospitals emerges from the next sentence, from paragraph 1.7 of the report:

"Whether or not separate specialist hospitals of this kind would be established now, given complete freedom of choice, may be debatable."

One may ask, whose freedom of choice—the patient's or the administrator's? I suspect that what is there thought of is that of the civil servants in the DHSS.

I approach the problem of this order and the issues that lie behind it from exactly the opposite end. I start by believing that the London postgraduate hospitals are part of the glorious heritage of British medicine—that they stand for some of the greatest advances in the history of British medicine, that they set standards of quality in treatment and research which bear comparison with those of similar institutions anywhere in the world. I believe that their destruction would be an incalculable loss to our system of medical education in Britain and so, ultimately, to the quality of care of patients throughout the National Health Service.

It matters far less to me that these hospitals do not fit neatly into the DHSS administrative system than that they should continue, under whatever system of governors, to provide not only the skilled specialised treatment for some of the most troublesome complaints to which mankind is prone, but also the means of further medical advance for the well-being of mankind as a whole.

Treatment, education and research are inseparable because, for medical education and medical research, clinical material—that is, patients in need of treatment—is essential. The link, therefore, between the hospitals and their institutes is a key issue.

The Government's consultative document has described how these hospitals were left out of the 1974 reorganisation, and how the boards of governors continued to exist under an order made, I was interested to see, a few days before the 6 General Election of February 1974. I cannot help feeling that the Ministers involved must have had other things on their minds at the time. Nevertheless, the order was made and the reorganisation of the hospitals was postponed for five years. The five years expired last month, and the order that we are now considering continues the postponement for a further three years.

The assumption that underlay the 1973 Act, under which that order was made, was that five years would be long enough to decide how best to integrate the specialist postgraduate hospitals into the new organisation which was bringing together the hospital service, the community service and general practice into a single, integrated scheme. After prolonged, intensive consultation—nobody could complain that the Government have not consulted widely and over many years—it is fair to say that we are now no nearer a final solution than we were five years ago. There are three reasons for this.

First, the reorganisation itself—this is not the time to go into it in detail—has proved very traumatic, and it is now widely recognised that further changes—I hope they will be evolutionary changes—are necessary in order to get the best from the resources provided for the National Health Service. The new structure is believed to be too elaborate and too bureaucratic, and it is felt that there are too many tiers of management. This is now recognised, to the extent that the Government of which I was a member bear some share of the blame. That must be readily acknowledged.

The Royal Commission set up to examine the finance and manpower of the NHS has received much information about structure and management, and its three-year report is expected during the summer.

Against this background it has been difficult to reach a clear view as to how the London postgraduate hospitals would fit in, because of the uncertainty of the future structure of the NHS itself. This is particularly true because they are in London. London has its own problems, which are the subject of a separate study by the London Health Planning Consortium. So, the background against which this consultation has been made is itself uncertain.

JPEG 7 Secondly, the reorganisation of the NHS structure involved changes in the system of management, and moved to the system of consensus management—team management. Here again, it would be difficult to find large numbers of people in the NHS who are so convinced of the merits of this new system of team management that they would be willing to adopt it holus-bolus. Only one of the 12 hospitals —Queen Charlotte's Hospital in West London, a very small hospital—has moved towards the system of consensus management which was built into the reorganised Health Service. Therefore, the boards of governors have felt reluctant to embark down a road which would inevitably lead them into a system of management whose merits are far from proven yet in the NHS.

The third reason stems from the nature of the hospitals. They are heterogeneous in size and nature. They are geographically widely separate. Many have branches which exceed substantially in size she original central London hospital, the branches being located well out into the suburbs and beyond. They serve populations from all over the country. One must remember that the four Thames regions stretch from the boundaries of Hampshire to the east coast of Kent, from Hertfordshire and Bedfordshire down to the English Channel. Even if one says that 85 per cent, of the patients come from the Thames region, that still represents a substantial slice of the country.

Against this background it is extremely difficult to see how these hospitals could sensibly be integrated within the NHS system as it applies in London. Yet the Government have decided that they must be integrated eventually. But, like Peter Abelard confronted with the prayer to forswear sin, the Government add the words, "But not yet."

The functions of the London Postgraduate Hospitals Authority are well set out in paragraphs 3.29 to 3.32 of the consultative document. The main point is that the hospitals would be brought together for planning and resource allocation, and that there would be an intermediate tier between the hospitals and the Department which has hitherto administered them direct.

The final stage would be some form of complete absorption of the hospitals into the NHS structure by a method as yet undecided; that would be the stage at which the boards of governors themselves would disappear. It is made clear in the Secretary of State's foreword to the consultative document that the interim stage would not involve the abolition of the boards of governors.

I suspect that you would draw me up pretty sharply, Mr. English, if on this order I went into the longer-term possibilities. That clearly must be left for another occasion. But as this order specifically provides for the retention of the boards of governors for the continuing interim period, I think we are entitled, in debating this order, to do two things—first, to ask the Minister how he intends to deal with the issues which arise in the time provided by the order. The interim LPHA solution sits very uneasily alongside the existing structure of the NHS in London. Why does he believe that this interim position will be any better than the one that exists at present? Why is there any necessity to do anything in the meantime? What will be the game? All change results JPEG 9 in frictional costs and in upheaval. Why is he satisfied that the benefits will outweigh the disadvantages?

The argument is not fully set out in this document. The Government assume that the proposed change will be better than the present position. In his preface the Secretary of State writes:

"… I feel progress must be made towards overcoming the most important weaknesses"—

which are not defined—

"of the present arrangements, particularly in the field of planning and resource allocation."

There seem to be three reasons why one should question that judgment. First, this is, without doubt, an extra tier of management, coming at a time when there is a good deal of disillusion about tiers of management in the NHS. It will involve extra costs when the finance for the Health Service is already under severe strain and it could well give rise to delays in decision-making, when decision-making in the NHS is under strong criticism.

Secondly, regarding resource allocation, these hospitals will not fit into the RAWP process unless somehow the 85 per cent, of patients which come from the four Thames regions provide some kind of excuse whereby a substantial part of their resources will flow via the Thames regions. As I understand it, that is not envisaged at all. The RAWP process, based upon the formulae apparently under review by the DHSS, is based upon populations, morbidity, mortality, and a whole range of other factors. It is exceedingly difficult to see how the RAWP process—presumably this is what the Secretary of State had in mind when he talked about allocation of resources—can have any relevance as it is applied to the rest of the Health Service in being applied to these 12 hospital groups.

The hospitals are at present funded outside RAWP and that is not unique. The four special hospitals—the Broadmoors and Ramptons and so on—are funded outside RAWP. There is the example of the Common Services Agency in Scotland which is funded outside RAWP and provides common services for the Health Service in Scotland. There seems to be no inherent objection to funding outside RAWP something which does not fit neatly into the RAWP system.

10 However, as I understand it, the London Postgraduate Hospitals Authority will itself be funded outside the strict RAWP formula. One asks, therefore, where is the advantage of putting this separate layer between the Department and the hospital? How will that help the planning and allocation of resources?

The third reason to question this arrangement is that I foresee difficult relations between the LPHA—the new, intermediate tier—and the continuing boards of governors. If the boards of governors have a strong representation on the authority, so that in a sense they come to dominate it, there is a grave danger that the "big boys"—the Bethlems and the Great Ormond Streets—will gang up to do down the "small boys"—the skin hospital, the Eastman Dental hospital, and the other small ones. That certainly is a fear that I have seen expressed.

The alternative is that the new authority has a strong chairman and a strong team of officers, and that the boards of governors do not have a dominating influence. There is a risk there that it will undermine one of the real advantages of the system of boards of governors—that a high degree of independence and autonomy can lead to effective local administration, swift decision taking, good local leadership, and happy relations within the hospitals that are served.

Which is it to be? If there is to be the intermediate system, how will it overcome either one of the disadvantages?

The Minister of State, Department of Health and Social Security (Mr. Roland Moyle)
: It might be helpful if I intervene at this Juncture, since the right hon Member for Wanstead and Woodford (Mr. Jenkin) said that one of the advantages of a debate on this order now, in spite of the fact that the time for praying had passed, would be to probe the Government's intentions with regard to the future of these hospitals and to see how we shall use the time which has been given to us by the order.

JPEG 15 Much of the background to these postgraduate hospitals and their related institutes has been set out by the right hon Gentleman in his opening remarks. To summarise, there are 12 postgraduate hospitals in London, each having an associated postgraduate institute which is linked through the British Postgraduate Medical Federation as part of a London University school. The work they do, besides healing, involves advanced research into medicine and postgraduate education. At the same time—this is particularly important from the point of view of the patients—they have a service role.

Over the years many solutions, principally inspired by a desire to combine the postgraduate hospitals' advanced role with the service requirements of the National Health Service, have been pro-posed for their management, but none has been adopted. The attempt was last made in the run-up to the 1974 reorganisation, but by the time it was proposed to put that reorganisation on the statute book no solution to that problem had been worked out by the right hon Member for Wanstead and Woodford.

As each hospital is directly managed by a board of governors, the last Conservative Government merely extended the life of the boards of governors for five years in order to give time for a solution integrating the postgraduate hospitals into the National Health Service to emerge That five years concluded on 21 February this year. It is therefore necessary to extend the life of the boards of governors. No solution has yet emerged to the problem but there is progress, as I shall relate, and it is a little pessimistic to say that we are no nearer a solution now than we were in 1974.

In the meantime, the hospitals have continued much as they have always done. That is to say, the boards of governors are accountable to the Secretary of State who appoints them. They are financed directly by the Secretary of State and that places management functions on the Department of Health and Social Security. These functions have been discharged with some skill, but they impose a strain on the Department as DHSS staff are not trained or organised for a management role.

16 The hospitals are outside the resource allocation system of the National Health Service and at a time of tight allocations, this may mean the misapplication of scarce funds. Although the hospitals consult the various health authorities in London, both region and area, they are not part of the National Health Service planning system. Again, the danger of misapplication of scarce resources arises. The hospitals have no close links with local authorities or community health councils. I think that close links with local authorities are important in the present working relationships between the Health Service and local authority social service departments.

The management of the hospitals, particularly some of the smaller hospitals, remains small-scale and unadapted to modern conditions. Clearly, our predecessors were right to recognise a need for change and we agree with them.

Hon Members may ask—as the right hon Gentleman has already asked—why the Government have taken five years to consider the problem, only to sec a further extension of the life of the board of governors. It appears that we agree about the effects of the reorganisation of the National Health Service in 1974. That was what I understood the right hon Gentleman to have said. I think he will not be surprised when I say that, for the first two-and-a-half years following that reorganisation, the Department and Ministers were inevitably involved in trying to sort out the immediate consequences of that reorganisation.

It was therefore not until 1977 that we were able to turn our attention to the complex issue of the future management of the postgraduate hospitals. Since then, we have completed an extremely careful analysis and consideration of the issues involved in the management of these hospitals. The right hon Gentleman has already paid tribute to the amount of consultation and discussion that we have undertaken with the interests concerned, in particular the representatives of the 12 boards of governors.

In 1977 and 1978 we held two major conferences with representatives of all the health and academic interests where together we discussed ways and means of ensuring adequate arrangements in the future management of these hospitals.

JPEG 17 The 1977 conference concluded that there was a need for change in the arrangements for the hospitals. Arising from discussion at the 1978 conference there has emerged a broad measure of agreement which was reflected in the consultative document—to which the right hon Gentleman extensively referred—entitled "The Future Management of the London Specialist Postgraduate Hospitals", which was published last September. In between the two conferences my right hon Friend and I have visited the great majority of the postgraduate hospitals and discussed the problems with staff and boards of governors concerned.

The central issue in these discussions is a complex one: how to preserve the special service and educational role of these hospitals—our admiration for their roles in this respect is shared by the right hon Gentleman: I would not depart by one iota from the words he used to praise them—and to preserve those special service and educational roles while, at the some time, bringing them under the structure of the National Health Service which. at the moment, is organised on a geographical basis through health authorities with considerably devolved powers. But at this juncture we still need time to effect the changes. That was one reason for bringing the order forward.

The principal reason for preserving the boards of governors was that unless the order were confirmed the boards of governors would automatically disappear and management would automatically transfer to the area health authorities, within whose boundaries are the specialist postgraduate hospitals. That would have meant reallocation of the units, which I am sure nobody would have wanted. While the disappearance of the postgraduate hospitals within the boundaries of some AHAs might not have been too damaging for some boards of governors, there was general agreement among all those consulted that the complete integration of all postgraduate hospitals within the NHS on an arbitrary geographic basis would be inappropriate.

That sets out the background to the laying of this order in a way that will enable the Committee to understand the issues. I have also put before the Committee the arguments which support the 18 passage of the order. However, I have spoken of discussions, formal and informal, of conferences and discussion documents, of proposals and responses. The right hon Gentleman for Wanstead & Woodford and members of the Committee will have rightly concluded that there is much more to this than I have so far outlined. Therefore, although it does not directly bear on the passage of this order but on how we shall use the time allowed under the order, I feel the Committee would be helped and, in a sense, is entitled to know how the minds of my right hon Friend and myself are moving in coming to a conclusion about the future of these hospitals.

The starting point for consideration of this issue is the case for changing existing arrangements. The consultative document sets out the case for change. It points to the urgent need to plan for the patient services provided by the postgraduate hospitals within the context of London as a whole. The document also makes it clear that the present management arrangements in some of the boards of governors—particularly the smaller ones—are less than satisfactory and therefore need strengthening. At the same time staff working in the hospitals have been left in limbo since 1974, which has adversely affected their morale in these hospitals during the last five years. I think it fair to say that broadly the comments we have received on the document accept that there is a case for change. The question to be answered is, what form should change take, and how best can it be accomplished without damaging either the hospitals or their staff?

Three main options were examined, but in each case they presented particular difficulties. The options were, first, to place the management of the postgraduate hospitals under the area health authority to which they most naturally relate; secondly, to establish special health authorities accountable to regional health authorities; thirdly, to establish a single special health authority directly accountable to the Secretary of State.

We came to the conclusion that it was not possible at this juncture to decide which of these alternatives would be appropriate to particular hospitals because, in the first place—this is important—we cannot accept that there is an argument JPEG 19 for saying that all the hospitals should be treated, ultimately, in the same way. We recognised that there was a strong case for beginning with a single postgraduate hospitals authority containing all 12 hospitals, for granting it delegated powers, for remitting to it specific tasks and for such an authority to be an agent for change. I think that that is the most important aspect of the whole exercise.

It could be argued that we should set up an authority of six of the larger postgraduate hospitals. Secondly, we consider that the proposed authority has an important role to play as an agent of change.
