Mr. R. J. RUSSELL
: I beg to move, in page 1, line 7, to leave out the words "any county or county borough," and to insert instead thereof the words "the county of London."

This Amendment brings us right up against the real position with regard to this question in this country at the present time. Those of us who have watched the development of this discussion are well aware that had it not been for happenings in London we should not, in all probability, be here discussing this Bill. The introducer of the Bill in the last Parliament left no doubt as to that position, and every speaker either in the House or in Committee has repeatedly referred to the conditions in London and the necessity for something to be done in consequence of those conditions. Therefore, in submitting the Amendment that this matter be confined to London in the first instance, we are opening the way for the first step in the solution of a very vexed problem. London is different from anywhere else. It has preculiarities far beyond those of any other city, not only in this country but in the world. Week by week we are crowded with visitors who come here on business, on pleasure and for social activities, and it may well be, having regard to that influx of visitors, that there is a demand which does not arise elsewhere. We have no wish at any time to interfere with the responsible Government of such a place as London. If the host has certain sentiments, and the guest, albeit a paying guest, desires other things, it becomes very difficult indeed for the host to refuse his guest the things desired, and, although we may not like it, as a matter of courtesy we 1660 are almost compelled to do something to meet the wishes of our guests.

If the visitors who come to London in such large numbers demand that there shall be something in the nature of Sunday cinemas or similar entertainments, courtesy makes it difficult to deny them. Then, again, we cannot deny that in London itself there is a demand for these things. That demand may seem very small when expressed in percentages, but in such a vast area, and with such a vast population, although the percentage may be small the actual numbers may be very large, and at any rate that number has been sufficient in the past to demand something on the lines of what has happened in London. I do not doubt that it is because of that substantial demand that the Loudon County Council felt themselves compelled to do something. If there is such a demand there would seem to be some reason for the Government to take action to protect the London County Council against attacks for having done that which at the present moment is illegal.

Conditions in London are peculiar from the point of view of the population and overcrowding. No other place that I know of can show such figures in regard to population as London. In certain areas the density of population is remarkable. In Bethnal Green there is a density of over 145 persons to the acre, in Fins-bury of 129 to the acre, in Holborn and Islington over 106, in Shoreditch of 158, in Southwark 153, in Stepney 141. Seeing that sanitary and health science rather indicates that the population ought not to exceed 29 to the acre, when we have reached the position shown by the figures I have quoted we must realise that we are up against a very serious problem. And yet I submit that for the Government to suggest that because of this state of affairs we should allow Sunday cinemas is to take a very dangerous line. The very last thing the Government should do is to introduce palliatives to bad health conditions, and yet here we are up against the conditions that I have mentioned. We do not want to stabilise bad conditions, but during the period whilst those conditions are being altered, it may be necessary to do something along the lines that have been suggested and, at all events, we want to make it quite clear that we are not out JPEG 1661 to compel people to continue as long as posible under unhealthy conditions or to prevent them getting out of those conditions as quickly as possible.

There is another point. London is so large and so varied that it is almost voiceless in a matter of this kind. You could not for a moment get a referendum on a subject like this from the whole of the people who are affected. You could not possibly get an intelligent return from such a vast aggregation of people. It is different in that respect from other places, and for those reasons London has cinemas open. The fact is there and we have to face that fact, But none of these conditions applies elsewhere. In other towns you have not the same difficulty in consulting the inhabitants, the same congestion that you have in such a vast area as you have in London nor that vast aggregate of demand, although it is a small percentage, that you have in London. There is the real reason why we should first of all deal with London. It may be argued that this is unjust to other towns. I submit that it is not. If the people of another town wish to have Sunday entertainments, and if you have the precedent of London established, they can get it. All that is required is that the municipality should promote a Bill or, what is more likely, put a Clause into a Bill that they are promoting for other purposes. Then they must have a town's meeting, where the people can express themselves. The meeting can demand a poll, and they must express themselves in the poll.

No matter what may be our opinion, we have no right to say that, if the majority of the people in any district say by a poll that they require certain things, they shall not have them. The way is open to them without any alteration of the law. That being so, the way is open to us definitely to say that we apply this to London. All the arguments for the Bill are peculiar to London. Even the hospital demand in London is far higher than it is elsewhere. Here the national inflow makes the hospital provision a tremendous problem, and the hospital provision even itself is different here from what it is elsewhere, although it is difficult enough elsewhere.

But there is a further reason why I hope the Committee will accept the Amendment. There has been a great deal 1662 of talk both here and outside of a real desire to settle the question. No matter what the Press says, any hesitation in settling it will not come from this side of the House. We want the question settled, and I believe the whole country wants it settled, on reasonable and wise lines. You cannot expect us to agree to some of the conditions that are in the Bill. We cannot hand over a sacred institution unless we have some protection against the profiteering of those who care nothing for sacred institutions or anything else except profit. We have in this the first Amendment which it seems to me should receive general acceptance in the House and in the country if we confine this in this first place to London and then leave it to other places to take the ordinary method of a Clause in a Bill; and, having a precedent already established, it throws upon the people of these other districts the responsibility and the option of taking such action as they feel to be wise. Then there is the very simple matter of protecting the worker, and everyone who has discussed the Bill has agreed that the worker ought to be protected. Then you have to make charity a real thing, and everyone has agreed to that also being done. Then we want some protection with regard to the character of the films shown. The Amendment to this Amendment which appears on the Paper will safeguard the community in regard to that. Then I should like, if possible, to see the Bill so arranged that it can give, either in London or elsewhere, some further protection to the people in regard to dog-racing and other activities. All along those lines I believe we can almost at once come to a settlement of the question and, because the Amendment is the first instalment, I hope the Committee will accept it and let us get down to the business of carrying the Bill through.

This Amendment gives my hon. Friend the Under-Secretary the opportunity of making a statement. That is the point of view of an opponent of the Bill, and for those who support the Bill I suggest, as a reasonable attitude in the present circumstances, that they might say, "I believe entirely in the principle of the Sunday opening of cinemas, and I realise that unless legislation is passed soon, a most unfortunate situation will arise in London in October; and, therefore, in order to get through a Measure which at least saves the worst effects of that situation from developing immediately, I am prepared to realise that such strong opposition to the principle of local option enshrined in this Bill exists that it is better to accept something of that character." I shall no doubt be accused, and we shall all be accused over these things, no matter what line we take, of yielding on this question of Sunday observance, but I would say to hon.
