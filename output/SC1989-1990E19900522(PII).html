On more careful examination, I find that the points made by the hon. Member for Slough and by me are complementary, not contradictory. I am surprised that the Chief Secretary did not pick that up in his response. Our point is that it is the overall limit for qualifying for small companies relief that is important, rather than the constituent parts of the group of companies.

The hon. Member for Slough argued that if a group of companies made a £200,000 aggregate profit, small companies relief should be available on all the profit, even though it were distributed unevenly among the four constituent companies. Taking a group of companies, four constituent member companies and a £200,000 aggregate profit, the hon. Gentleman argued that all four companies should qualify for taxation at the small companies rate. That might not happen, but it would happen if each company had a profit of £50,000 so that the aggregate figure was divided equally among all four companies.

If the distribution were unbalanced and one company had a profit of £90,000, another a profit of £90,000, a third a profit of £20,000, and the fourth a profit of nil, only the company with a profit of £20,000 would qualify for its entire profit at the small companies rate. The two companies making a profit of £90,000 would qualify partly at the 35 per cent. rate and partly at the 25 per cent. rate. The hon. Member for Slough highlighted a genuine anomaly. He suggested that the overall sum of £200,000 should be the trigger point for small companies relief.

The argument that I was advancing approached the problem from a different angle. I was trying to ensure that we do not allow companies to massage their profits between different members of a group to avoid a relatively small amount of tax. However, I was making the same point in principle that it is the overall sum that is important. If a company is earning massively over £1 million profit in all, it should not be able to place some of its profits in small companies at a low rate of £10,000, £20,000 or £50,000 to get a tax advantage. Mr. Allan Stewart
(Eastwood)
: I beg to move amendment No. 10, in page 13, line 36, at end add

"(3) In Schedule 6, Paragraphs 3(1) and 3(2) of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988, the figure of "18,000" shall be deleted and "10,000" substituted."

This amendment concerns the definition of a car used preponderantly for business purposes. As hon. Members will know, where a director or an employee earning more than £8,500 per annum is given a company car which is also available for his private use, he is assessed on a scale charge. The law attempts to recognise that certain vehicles are less of a perk and more of a necessary tool of the business by providing that the scale charge is half if there is preponderant business use.

Since 1980, preponderant business use of a company car has been set at 18,000 miles. In recent years the scale charge has increased. It was doubled in 1988 and after a one third increase last year it is proposed under the clause to increase the charge by one fifth this year. The underlying principle of the benefits legislation is to charge the employee with the cost to the employer of providing the benefit. In the case of a car the cost per mile depends on the total.
