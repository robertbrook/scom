The Committee knows that in a large number of cases involving motoring offences under the Road Traffic Act there is a high percentage of pleas of guilty by letter.

Careless driving is an offence under section 3 of the Road Traffic Act 1972. By and large people convicted under this section do not bother to give up a day's work to attend court, particularly if there is no danger of disqualification, which would almost certainly bring a personal appearance, and they plead guilty by letter.

I have often sat in court and heard a sheriff reading what is a quite explicit plea of guilty. But there is no admission of previous convictions attached to it. At that stage the sheriff says to the court, "I suppose that we had better continue it", rather grumpily. I am not sure whether it is because the sheriff is reluctant or because he cannot dispose of the matter until there has been a specific admission of the previous convictions. Can the Solicitor-General tell me?

Clearly, it would be an enormous convenience for the court if it could railroad the matter through—that is perhaps a pejorative way to put it—if it could get the conveyor belt running smoothly and not have to hinder progress by going back to the accused, who no doubt in the vast majority of cases, when the matter is pointed out, will accept that the previous convictions are correct.

I can understand the reason for the clause, but. I do not shut my eyes to the dangers that could result. In our criminal courts, previous convictions are enormously important determinants of sentence, and a record of any substance will strongly influence the disposal of the court. Enormous statutory safeguards—advantages, in a sense—are built in for the first offender but, apart from those, when it actually comes to disposal, the most effective plea in mitigation is to say, "My JPEG 1066 Lord, my client has never been in trouble before. Previous unchallenged convictions on the record will affect the sentence. If in fact those convictions are false and have not been challenged merely because their significance was not appreciated by the accused, he may receive a much more severe sentence than would otherwise be the case.

I wonder whether it is beyond the wit of man to design a pro forma not necessarily setting out all the previous convictions but if the accused is to plead guilty—I know that we go some way towards this by having a pro forma on the back of the complaint, which can be returned—specifically drawing his attention to the schedule of previous convictions, and enjoining him in forceful terms to tick it or to accept it, when sending in his plea. I hope that the Solicitor-General for Scotland accepts that it might be dangerous to give that power to the court. If we ensure that there is a proper pro forma and that people's attention is drawn to the fact that a generalised letter pleading guilty is not enough, and that they must either use the pro forma with its specific section asking "Do you admit the previous convictions?" or add a specific rider one way or the other to their plea in their letter, I think that we might be able to avoid the need for clause 39.

The Solicitor-General for Scotland (Mr. Nicholas Fairbaim)
: I have to advise the hon. Member for Glasgow, Garscadden (Mr. Dewar) that the wit of man did conceive of a pro forma which was introduced in 1978 by act of adjournal. At present there goes with the complaint, schedule 4, "Notice of Previous Convictions":

"In the event of your being convicted of the charge(s) in the Complaint it is intended to place before the Court the following previous conviction(s) applying to you."

They are then listed.

Schedule 5 says:

"Cross out whatever does not apply:—

PART 1 I plead NOT GUILTY…
PART 2 I plead GUILTY
delete as appropriate I admit the previous convictions listed against me
I admit the previous convictions except (list those previous convictions not admitted)
I do not admit the previous convictions listed against me."

Therefore, what the amendment of the hon. Member for Renfrewshire, West (Mr. Buchan) seeks to provide already exists.

The difficulty of the present situation is that, either by design or by default, many people when they send back their plea of guilty fail to admit, or deny, or qualify the list of previous convictions. The result is that it is mandatory on the court to have another diet and to serve a warrant on the person requiring him to be present at that diet. Occasionally the sheriff's clerk will write to the person and say "You did not fill up schedule 4 when you returned your plea of guilty. Would you now please do so?" A fortnight elapses during which there is no reply, so the person then has to be cited to attend. It is a cumbersome, expensive and wasteful process, and it is in a minute number of cases—practically never—that a challenge is made to any conviction.

Therefore, as Thomson recommended, in summary cases there should be a mandatory assumption that the convictions are admitted. Of course there will still be the pro forma, so they still ought to be admitted. But in the absence of those admissions, a point raised by the hon. Member for Garscadden previously, there is a problem if one fails to admit one's convictions or fails to fill up the pro forma—for instance, if a person has two convictions in the past two years and eight months and can postpone his plea of 1069 guilty for another four months, the totting-up procedure does not apply, because he has tholed his assize. If a person puts in a plea of guilty during the remaining period, he would have three convictions within three years and automatically he would have to be disqualified.

"It would require to be made clear to the accused in the notice served on him that the previous convictions would be held to apply to him unless he expressly denied them. We agree with this view and we recommend that a statutory rule should be introduced on the lines proposed."

I understand the concern of the hon. Member for South Ayrshire (Mr. Foulkes). However, it is not as if the previous convictions are produced to the court and never served on the person who is pleading guilty. If he pleads guilty he must have been notified of them. He has an opportunity when filling in the plea of guilty to admit, deny or qualify them. That is part of the plea that he has to fill in.

I understand the equitable concept. If the accused, who must have read the short pro forma while filling it in, does not admit or deny the convictions, he must surely be taken to have read it and not to have any comment. As the hon. Member for Garscadden said, if he has to be brought to the court to admit or deny the previous convictions it will probably mean a day off work. On the balance of equity more than on the grounds of expense and convenience I believe this to be an equitable change in the law.
