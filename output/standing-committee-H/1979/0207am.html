Mr. Prescott: The principal amendment is concerned with the revocation and suspension of licences. This is an extremely important clause, and it will be necessary to give a great deal more attention to it than we were able to give to clause 21.

The amendments concern the restrictions and sanctions that will be placed on an operator's licence. The sanctions in clause 22, to which the amendment is addressed, are very powerful, in that they Image Col. 1143 will put an operator out of business under certain conditions laid down in the clause.

The essence of the amendment and its implications, since it deals with not only our legislation but Community Directive 74/562, is that they are largely concerned with how one maintains quality control in the industry. Previously we were concerned to do that by a road licensing system. Now we are, quite radically, changing over to an operator's licensing system.

While that is done in the name of freedom—and most of us have made our points about that philosophy—what we are concerned with in clause 22 is the conditions that will control freedom—a sort of organised freedom—to maintain the public safety that is necessary. It can be seen that most of the contents of subsections (1) and (2) have been rolled together into our amendment in a new subsection (1). We are concerned at the possibility of an operator continuing in business when he does not fulfil the necessary requirements, particularly with regard to professional competence. The Bill states that a person may have an operator's licence Col. 1144 if he fulfils the qualifications, or he may employ, another person who fulfils the qualifications on professional competence to act on his behalf. Professional competence is an important matter, as indeed are the other two qualifications. "the occupation of road passenger transport operators in national and/or international transport operations".

That is clearly in line with the provisions of the Bill. Our amendment deals with the matter of a suspension

"where failure to satisfy the requirement as to professional competence arises out of the fact that the transport manager of the business in question has ceased to work for it or is no longer of good repute".

The question of "good repute" will be determined, as laid down in the Bill, at the discretion of the commissioner. For example, the commission of criminal offences or safety offences may affect the attitude of the commissioner in deciding on whether someone is of "good repute". Schedule 3 makes clear what is meant by " good repute". It has the same meaning in the amendment. "Good repute" is therefore defined, albeit perhaps in a lax way, so there is some indication to the commissioners as to what should be taken into account in considering the operation of a particular licence, whether any offence has occurred to which the sanctions in the clause will apply being given some reasonable indication in schedule 3.

I turn to the equally important matter of professional competence. Our amendment says that where

"the transport manager of the business in question has ceased to work for it or is no longer of good repute, the traffic commissioners may, subject to article 3.1 of Council Directive (EEC) 74/562, suspend the revocation of the licence for such period as seems to them reasonably required for enabling a new transport manager to be appointed."

The ability of a company to remain in business under the conditions of the operator's licence is here vested not in the company but in an individual. There is the possibility that that individual may by his action remove from a company its qualification to operate under the licence.

Every member of the Committee must be able to think of many examples of cases in which that may happen, some more serious than others. The death of a competent professional manager, for example, would be most unfortunate. It may be a very good operating company, or it may be a company operating much nearer the line in which the qualifications may not be so readily evident.

There is a possibility, during the suspension, that a company will operate without that essential quality of professional management that determines the quality of the service and the vehicles, and the observance of the company's obligations, not only under the legislation, but to the passengers travelling on its buses.
