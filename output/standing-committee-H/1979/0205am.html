Mr. Booth
: We are sorry, in coming to the amendment, to note the absence of the Minister of Transport. The amendment, coupled with amendment No. 138, would give to district councils—and for this purpose district councils can cover borough councils within shire counties—the right to prevent their areas from being made trial areas. That is to say, it would give them the right to prevent the Minister from designating their areas as areas in which people could operate stage carriage services without road service licences.

The clause as drafted provides no right for these bodies effectively to object. It does not even give them the right to be consulted when that important decision is being considered—a decision which in their view may be of absolutely crucial importance to their ability to run their own transport services or at least to have a degree of control over the way in which those services are run. I believe that matter to be of crucial importance.

It is not a narrow party issue that we raise—I wish to make that clear at the beginning. It has the support of the Association of District Councils, which means it is supported by Conservative, Labour, Liberal and Independent council- 922 lors up and down the country who are highly concerned about the effect of the Bill upon the bus services within the areas that they represent. These councils, particularly those that run transport undertakings, have knowledge of that important area of the Bill and of what is involved in running passenger transport services within their areas. They have experience of running such undertakings and they are required to be democratically accountable for the running of those services.

I believe that it has been noticeable in local elections over the last few years how that particular subject has been given far greater prominence in political debate. Therefore, councillors are very likely to feel a sense of concern, obligation and accountability to those who say "Why cannot our service be improved? Why cannot buses be run to a better schedule on this route?" or "Why is a service being withdrawn?"

It is useless to be accountable or to carry such a responsibility unless one has an element of control over those decisions.

County councils can and do impose a number of conditions on district authorities in the way in which they run their transport services. So there is no question of the record showing any great impartiality on the part of county councils in their dealings with districts on this matter. They have interpreted present legislation—as they are entitled to do—as laying upon them responsibilities for planning the network of services throughout the whole of their areas. They have regarded it as entitling them to impose conditions on district and borough councils that are running their own services.

I should like to illustrate that with an example which concerns me deeply, because it comes from my constituency. The borough of Barrow-in-Furness applied to Cumbria county council for TSG support in the running of one of its corporation services, which goes along the coast road. As the constituency is on the end of a peninsula it is probably an ideal case to consider, because it does not have as many problems with cross-boundary services as those that are totally landlocked.

Barrow-in-Furness has, in the view of the members of its district council, an obligation to those who live on the coast road that runs along the side of Morecambe Bay to provide a bus service. That service is not economic and not viable, nor JPEG 923 is it likely to become profit-making. However, I believe that the council is entitled to exercise its right to determine that such a service is required in the public interest.

The council applied to Cumbria county council for support under the TSG arrangements to continue to run that service. The answer that it received was "We shall provide you with an element of support under TSG subject to your meeting conditions laid down by the county council." Those conditions included the district council's raising from fares 50 per cent. of the total operating cost of that service. It was given no discretion whatever about the extent to which cross-subsidisation could operate between that route and others run by the corporation.

It also found that the county council had a policy of requiring municipal undertakings run by district councils within the county to adjust their fares, if it required them to do so, to rates that it had determined in its negotiations with the Ribble service arising from TSG. I mention that because I have checked with councils in a number of other areas who have had exactly the same experience. County councils will use their powers to impose fare structures on district councils as a condition of TSG provision.

I have stated that to make clear beyond any shadow of doubt that the passing of the amendment will not, of itself, prevent a county council from exercising its rights and powers to effect networking arrangements in the county. The amendment will give the district council what I contend is an elementary democratic right to say "If we are to work under the restrictions and obligations imposed on us by Parliament, by a Minister and by a county council, surely we should have the right to object to an arbitrary decision to remove from our area the requirement for a road service licence for a stage carriage service."

Given the restrictions imposed by counties, it would be bad enough trying to maintain and operate stage carriage services in some areas even if one had a clear knowledge of road service licensing conditions. However, without them it will become very difficult, if not absolutely impossible. I believe that the members of Cumbria county council are not necessarily indifferent to the transport services of Barrow, but they just do not have the detailed knowledge of the district council 924 members. In fact, some of them do not know what is involved in running services, say, from Barrow to Stainton, Askam, or Gleaston. Some of them do not even know whether there are bus services for the residents of Stank or Newton or some of the other places in my constituency. The district councils who represent those areas know those problems intimately. Surely they should have the elementary right to say that the county council shall not approach the Minister to make us an area in which services are run without road service licences.

One can appreciate that the Minister has had many thoughts about this clause, not only second, but third and fourth thoughts. Having listened to the ministerial contributions on previous clauses laying down the conditions attached to road service licences, one can clearly see that the Minister would dearly love to abolish licensing altogether. Many of the speeches that he has made have suggested that the theme of his approach is that licensing is a terrible restriction, a horrible Socialist plot to prevent the entrepreneur from running vastly better services than those now operating in the country. If he had the courage of that conviction, this Bill would not have been before us. We should not have had this timid and tentative approach. The Minister would have proposed a Bill to abolish Road Service Licensing throughout the length and breadth of the country.

That contrasts dramatically with what the clause says, namely that the trial areas can only be brought about provided the county area volunteers, and the Minister then agrees. So, presumably, even if all the county areas volunteered themselves as trial areas—a magnificent response to the Minister's approach in this part of the Bill—we should not see them all become trial areas. There is an innate caution, a built-in contradiction between the approach that was used in drafting this clause and the approach which was used in other parts of the Bill. One would imagine that if hon. Members on the Government side had been converted by their Minister, as they appear to have been by their voting record on this Bill, to the belief that road service licensing is highly undesirable, they would see trial areas as entrepreneurial oases in a desert of licensing.

However, if that is their approach, why do they not call upon the Minister to make the desert bloom, to turn it all into an entrepreneurial garden in which such JPEG 925 transport services can flourish? They know very well, however, that in this clause they are up against a fundamental contradiction. I appeal to strike a blow for local democracy in the name of Labour, Liberal, Conservative and Independent councillors throughout the country, who have thrust upon them the difficult task of trying to deal with transport services in their areas.

At least let them be able to ensure that while the Minister has not made up his mind and is under no obligation in the terms of his own Bill to designate trial areas, while he wants to insert "may" rather than "shall", at least local councils should, in view of their experience, their accountability and their intimate knowledge of the passenger transport requirements of their areas, have the right to object and to be excluded from trial areas.

There is nothing partisan in the two amendments. At present, district councils are controlled by Conservative interests, so it is clear that we do not table the amendments in any partisan political way. We are arguing for the principle of consultation. If the county council is to designate any area, it is surely common sense that an obligation is put on that county council to consult the district council involved. After all, the district council is at the grass roots of the matter.

There is a further point. Many county councils are broadly rural but contain large urban intrusions. For instance, the county of Avon has within it Bristol, and the urban area of Bristol dominates the county of Avon. In my own county of Suffolk, the largest urban area is Ipswich—the old county borough of Ipswich, but I leave JPEG 927 Suffolk out of the argument, because I do not make constituency cases one way or other in this Committee. However, it frequently happens that the controlling interest of a county council is rural. Yet in the urban area of any district council, a different set of transport problems exists from the problems in a rural area. Therefore, there must be the obligation to consult.

There may be disagreement between the county council and the district council about the area to be designated. I imagine that there is often a difference of opinion. If that difference cannot be resolved, the district council should have the right to object, and that right should be built into the framework of this legislation.

These two amendments contain nothing strikingly new. There is a sound precedent in the 1978 legislation, which is now in operation and which has been on the statute book for some time. There is a prima facie case, purely on the common sense and justice of the matter, that district councils should be consulted and be given the right to object. That would avoid a lot of trouble, and experimental areas would be more likely to succeed with these amendments than without them.

It is certainly true that our enthusiasm for trial areas is based upon considerable scepticism about the value of road service licensing.
