Mr. Booth
: The aim of this group of amendments is to put some precision and certainty into a clause that is all vagueness and uncertainty. The clause appears to be the parliamentary equivalent of the punter who backs every horse in a five-horse race, and some of them each way. The wording of the clause makes that apparent, even on the most cursory examination. It begins with the words:

"If it appears to the Minister, having determined that all or any of the securities of the successor company held by him or by nominees for him should be offered for sale."

The clause, therefore, begins with one ministerial option. It continues in subsection (3) by saying that

"If the Minister gives an undertaking"

1796 certain things shall follow. In subsection (4) the clause goes on to say:

"If no undertaking is given".

The general impression left by the clause on an objective reader is that either the Minister has not made up his mind on how he wants to deal with the many detailed and important questions that arise—even given that his aim is to fund part of the NFC's pensions—or, having decided upon it, he certainly does not want to tell the Committee or Parliament at this time how he intends to go about it. The Minister appears to be leaving himself a considerable number of options. But, since the choice of those options affects, first, the nature of this legislation, and, secondly, and equally important, the return which the public will get on its assets in the NFC, it is necessary at an early stage in discussing these amendments to have some guidance from the Minister on some clear and precise questions.

If the Minister is to reject the amendments he must explain to the Committee whether it is his intention that the Committee, and subsequently the House, should give him powers to choose from at least three courses which appear to be in the clause, and which would certainly be narrowed and curtailed if the amendments were accepted.

The first appears to be a choice of putting on the market, or requiring his nominees to do so, either all or a certain proportion of those shares which were produced in exchange for the loans and initial capital funding of the NFC at a fixed price and with a guarantee on how far the sale would be used to fund those pension liabilities which the NFC has arising from its employees who had road service prior to the creation of the NFC.

If the Minister does that, the Committee has a direct interest in knowing, before we agree to this clause or seek to reject it, the extent to which these obligations will be funded on the Minister's decision. The price that those shares will fetch, no matter what the nominal value is, must depend to a considerable extent on how much confidence those who buy them can have that there will be a complete funding of this pension obligation. In other words, if prospective purchasers had any reason to suspect that they would obtain a part ownership in a successor company which would not have the road pensions fully funded, they would be likely either to JPEG 1797 reduce the purchase or purchase at a much lower price and thereby devalue an important public asset.

We have made it clear that we are opposed to the concept of selling off the NFC. However, given that the Government, with their majority, will push it through anyway, we see it as at least part of our duty—and we hope that some Government Back Benchers will also see it as part of their duty—to ensure that the public get a proper return on their holding. Without some of the amendments, on the present information that we have from the Government, there must be considerable uncertainty in the minds of people considering the purchase of shares in the NFC. Therefore, it is necessary here and now for the Minister to make a statement about his intention.

If it is the hon. Gentleman's intention that the sale shall only be made when he is prepared to guarantee that there will be a full funding—that would accord with the general view expressed upon that matter on Second Reading—how will he bring that guarantee about, other than in the terms of our amendments? Presumably, he cannot guarantee that the initial sale will produce the full funding.

Is it the case that, under the terms of subsection (3), as qualified by subsection (5), he will not be able to give an undertaking to pay more into the funding than he obtains from the sale of the shares? His own guarantee to make the payment to the trustee of the fund could not be a guarantee to pay more than is obtained from the sale. So, in any case, there will be a doubt in the minds of those who consider purchase, unless the Minister can say something this morning which will persuade us, and many people who have read this Bill and have considered the propositions in it, that there is a way of initially floating the shares which will fully fund the road service pensions.

I say that with a clear appreciation that we are discussing only one of six main categories of pensions involved in British Rail and National Freight Corporation pension arrangements, but I want to keep the issue within the narrow context, believing that there will be ample opportunities on later clauses to deal with the complications which arise from other categories.

Can the Minister give a guarantee, however, in respect of this category? Or are 1798 we right in supposing that any undertaking to pay into funds could not produce more money for the trustees of the funds than could the sale of share option in guaranteeing that some, or even all, of the proceeds of sale, if that were less than the cost of the road service pension funding, would be available?

From that follows the next question, which seems to me to be one of the main criteria by which the amendments should be judged. If the Minister cannot give a guarantee at that stage, is it his intention to delay the flotation until he feels that there is the best chance of making the guarantee? If so, what happens under the provisions of the clause to the maintenance of the funds by the successor company in the meantime? Will the present guarantees under the 1978 Act continue to operate, or will the repeal provisions of the bill take effect so that part of the return on the successor company's holding of shares will have to be used to cope with current funding obligations? If the latter is the case, it will produce a scenario in which the sale of shares can be judged and the extent and cost of the obligations can be better estimated.

So far as I am aware, the last actuarial evaluation of the cost of the obligations took place at the time of the 1978 Act. That must be considerably outdated by now. Therefore, in so far as the explanatory and financial memorandum appears to be based upon that valuation, the Committee has no accurate guide as to the kind of sale of public asset required for the purpose of funding the road service pensions of NFC.

What is the Minister's current estimate of the level of sale now required to cover the road service pensions? I take it that the £25 million mentioned in the memorandum is based upon the 1978 valuation and that a much higher figure would now apply. If £100 million is the total in respect of the NFC, I assume that we admit that part of that will not be within the valuation carried out for the purpose of sale of the shares, as it relates more to initial capital funding and loans than to current working assets.

It would therefore be most useful to have the up to date information. We may then discuss in a more intelligent way how far the amendments would go to meet the problems that I have raised, all of which fall under the broad heading of imprecision and multiple options for the Minister JPEG 1799 that we wish to see narrowed down in any case.

If the assets are to be sold in the way proposed in the Bill, at least two aims would then be achieved. First, the public's assets would be sold at the best price. Secondly, but no less importantly, those who have the duty of running the pension funds of the NFC, in so far as the funds cater for those carrying road service pension rights, will have sufficient money fully to meet the obligations that will fall upon them.

I will deal now in a little detail with the individual amendments and no doubt hon. Members will wish to come back both on the detail and the principle.

The amendments would basically delete the requirement for the Minister to obtain the consent of the Treasury before giving an undertaking to make payments towards the funding of the road-based deficiencies. They would also have the effect of making the giving of such an undertaking mandatory rather than permissive.

The convention is to require treasury approval in financial legislation of this kind, and I see no reason to break with that convention in this instance. The Treasury would expect to be consulted anyway, whether or not that was written into legislation.

The change from "may" to "shall" would have no practical effect since the Minister would retain discretion on the amount of the payment, as can be seen from the last part of the clause.

Amendment No. 310 would delete subsection (2)( b). This requires that the Minister's undertaking to make a payment to the NFC pension schemes shall be conditional upon the amount received from the sale of the shares being greater than the amount of the undertaking. Basically, the clause as drafted prevents a sale from taking place if the amount required for the pension funds is greater than the likely proceeds of the sale. That is a sensible provision. It is clearly nonsense for the Minister to pay more into pension funds than he expects to realise from the sale of the shares. It is therefore right to recognise that in the statute.
