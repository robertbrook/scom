Mr. Dobson
: This side of the Committee takes the view that the part II of the Bill represents a rip-off of public assets to the benefit of private investors. We are disturbed at the prospect of public assets being squandered for private gain.

The main point on the clause is that subsection (4), to which these amendments refer, is a vital part of the rip-off process. Part II could therefore be described as a three-part rip-off. We have the write-off rip-off, the sell-off rip-off and the strip-off rip-off. Subsection (4) is the first of those rip-offs—the write-off rip-off. It covers the writing off of the commencing capital debt of the National Freight Corporation which, 1542 I believe, totals £58.7 million, and outstanding loans to the Government of £41.3 million. If my arithmetic is right that is a total of £100 million to be written off by the Government. That represents either a capital sum of £100 million or, as near as can be, £8 million in annual payments to the Government which will be written off at the taxpayers' expense.

Why do the Government consider the write off to be necessary? The answer is that it will make the National Freight Corporation a more attractive purchase. I can do no better than briefly quote what the Parliamentary Secretary said on Second Reading:

"One of the immediate effects of our proposals is that the company's capital structure will be very much more attractive".

Indeed it will. It would be much more attractive to buy shares in the new National Freight Company—if that is what it will be—when the company does not have a £100 million debt to the Government than if that debt were to remain as it should.

It is rather strange that a Government who say that they are dedicated to cutting public expenditure will, through this subsection, in effect spend £100 million of capital, or forgo—which is another way of spending—£8 million a year in income. It is interesting that such a sum has never been written off before. I understand that such an amount could have previously been written off to the benefit of the National Freight Corporation, but that did not happen largely because of the malignant effects of the Treasury during the period of the Labour Government. I cannot establish the motives that prevented such a write-off under previous Conservative Governments.

However, no private purchasers or property speculators on the make would have gained if it had been written off while the company remained in public control. The proposal is attractive only to those who want cowboy operators to move into National Freight or want to benefit possible private purchasers.

On Second Reading the Minister of Transport stated:

"the NFC has not had the commercial freedom and opportunities open to private firms".—[ Official Report, 27 November 1979; Vol. 974, cc. 1128–1242.]

It certainly did not have that. Few private firms have had a regular and invariable JPEG 1543 demand for £8 million a year to be paid to the Government. But either that has not registered with the Minister or he does not give much weight to it.

The proposal is supposed to be necessary so that the capital structure will be attractive and the NFC will have more freedom and opportunity than before. I do not understand why that is necessary, because in a debate on the NFC's commencing capital debt order in 1976 the present Minister of Transport said:

"the NFC is not run as some vast over-centralised nationalised industry. The corporation is broken down into a federated structure … maximum independence is given to the separate companies … the management in the NFC seems genuinely in no way to wish to avoid commercial realities. The Opposite is the case. The approach of the corporation is … to achieve a commercially viable company, to invest in success and to disinvest in failure … we urge that the NFC should pay its way … are, we believe, pushing against an open door … because that represents the aspirations not only of the top management but of the staff".—[ Official Report, 12 May 1976; Vol. 911, cc. 587–8.]

Nevertheless, he did not feel obliged at that time to advocate that the freedom to pursue the better commercial interests of the company would in any way be assisted by writing off its capital debt.

It is only now, when it is necessary to make the company more attractive to potential private purchasers, that Conservative Members feel it appropriate to write off the capital debt and, in effect, to give away £100 million. That seems a most bizarre approach.

I should also like the Parliamentary Secretary to tell us whether the £100 million in expenditure—that is what it is—will be counted by the Treasury as part of the public expenditure on transport for which he and the Minister are responsible. Will the transport industries be deprived of £8 million a year for many years, or £100 million in the forthcoming year, because of the write-off? It seems to me that the Treasury, if it takes its usual barmy view of public expenditure, would be justified in saying that. The measure represents public expenditure by the Department of Transport. If one had to assess all the possible ways of spending money on Britain's transport industry at present, the lowest priority one could conceive would be writing off the debt of the National Freight Corporation in 1544 order to make it more attractive to private purchasers.

The Minister will claim that such action is justified even in financial terms because the £100 million debt and the £8 million per year income will be exchanged for the securities that are to be issued when the assets are sold off to the private sector. However, we do not know the terms. I suspect that the Parliamentary Secretary, the Minister and their officials do not yet have any idea of the terms on which the securities are to be disposed of.

We are now being asked to agree to the writing-off of £100 million without knowing what we are to get in return. Yet to examine such details is a vital part of the function of Members of Parliament. £100 million will certainly go out of the window but we simply do not know what will come through the door in exchange for it. Is the Minister prepared to tell us the terms on which the shares or other securities in the new company will be disposed of? If not, it would be a gross dereliction of the duty of all members of the Committee to perform their traditional role of controlling the Executive, of controlling public expenditure and of limiting the extra-parliamentary discretion of Ministers if they allow the proposal to go through. I am quite serious about that; it is not just a debating point. If the measure goes through as proposed, £100 million will be written off and—for all we know at the moment—the public may receive in income from the securities sold as little as threepence. They may get £100 million or £500 million. But there is no guarantee that they will get anything.

Therefore the measure before us is not good enough. It would be appropriate for even the Conservative Back-Bench Members—if they are paying any attention to what they are being asked to approve—to demand that the Committee and the House are told what the public will get in exchange for the write-off. It is entirely inappropriate to leave the question of what is to come in entirely to the discretion of Ministers.

Later we shall discuss other amendments that seek to limit that discretion. On this part of the Bill we do not really expect any accommodation from the Parliamentary Secretary on such issues. It may well be necessary for us to make efforts to limit the Minister's discretion under other parts of the clause and under later clauses in this part of the Bill.

JPEG 1545 We are being expected to give the Minister a free hand in the matter of the £100 million that is to be written off. I cannot imagine that that is acceptable to anyone who approaches in a responsible manner Parliament's role in controlling the actions of the Executive and in particular what it does with taxpayers' money.

I hope that the Government will consider the matter further. We shall have another famous period of reflection—I do not expect anything to happen today—and I hope that Back-Bench Members in particular will bring pressure to bear so that the Minister is not given carte blanche. I hope that they will go even further and identify the £100 million of additional public expenditure that they are being asked to sanction, recognise that it is of the lowest possible priority and consequently bring pressure on the Minister to withdraw part II. That would save a great many jobs, the industrial side of the National Freight Corporation and also a great deal of money.

The most dangerous stage in political life and the most dangerous pitch to which one can ever rise is when one believes one's own propaganda. The Government have begun to believe their own propaganda about saving public money, but the clause, the amendment and subsection (4) belie that whole principle, because when the chips are down—as they are now—they are willing to use public money for a purpose, that, if revealed to the public, the public would not agree with. Another matter that gives me hope—and hope springs eternal—is that when these swashbuckling private enterprise people come in to buy the shares of the NFC, being men of such yeoman good sense and financial independence, they will not want the public subsidy. I want to make a last point. How much taxpayers' money we shall recover from the sale of shares, even when the commencing commercial debt and the loans are written off, remains a complete mystery. The shares will have to be marketed at the right time, in the right way and at the right JPEG 1549 price. We know none of those matters, so no one knows how much of our £100 million will be recovered. That is a complete leap in the dark. One may be in favour of a leap in the dark now and again, but not when it is financed with £100 million of taxpayers' money as this is.

Furthermore, even when we get the money back from the sale of the shares, deducted from that amount will be the historical pensions obligations which have to be funded. So we shall not get anything like all of the money back anyway.

To sum up, the amendment seeks to achieve a number of objectives. The first is justice to the taxpayer. If the amendment is not passed, this will cost him tens of millions of pounds. Secondly, the amendment makes true costs, financial obligations, commercial information and commercial truth—that is the point—part of the framework of the sale. The Opposition's third objective in the amendment is to expose what in the end is blatant political sharp practice.
