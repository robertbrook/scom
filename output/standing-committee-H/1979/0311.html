TRANSPORT BILL

Clause 54
ABOLITION OF FREIGHT INTEGRATION COUNCIL AND REPEAL OF CERTAIN PROVISIONS ABOUT SPECIAL AUTHORISATIONS FOR USE OF LARGE GOODS VEHICLES

10.30 a.m.

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. John Prescott
: I see that the arch-disintegrater is not here. I should have thought that the Minister would be here to deal with the integration arguments.

This is only a small clause but a fundamental point is involved which will divide the Committee in a fundamental way. In many ways it reflects the theological difference in attitude between the two major parties and the difference in the attitudes to transport, particularly public transport and freight.

The 1968 Act was fundamentally a new deal for British Rail and it certainly represented a different approach. Other legislation since the war has reflected different attitudes towards integrated transport, but basically it has always claimed to attempt to achieve a better transport system.

The clause deals with one part of the mechanism, as envisaged by the Labour Party, to achieve integration. The clause, in dealing with the abolition of the special authorisation procedures for long distance road transport, also seeks to abolish the Freight Integration Council—and in view of what has happened over the past few years one could say that that is what it has almost done for itself.

Nevertheless, it has always been the express purpose of legislation—the 1968 Act was designed specifically for this purpose—to give a new deal to British Rail and to adopt a new approach to transport problems. The essential principle and express purpose was to end the rivalry between road and rail, to attempt to complement the advantages of these two systems and to integrate them in the 2140 inter-modal concept as embodied in the National Freight Corporation.

The desire was to bring together the advantages of both road and rail transportation and develop them together. The Freight Integration Council had the express purpose of working at the two systems.

In abolishing the Freight Integration Council we are, in fact, announcing its official death, the council having died many years ago. But the other part of the clause dealing with the repeal of quantity licensing, cannot be said to have started— in fact it is being killed before being implemented. There is, in a sense, an abortion as it has not been given the chance to see whether it could be effective.

The two aspects of an essential mechanism were seen by the Labour Government in 1968 as a means by which we could attempt to integrate two essential parts of our transport system—namely, the road and rail industries.

We should address ourselves to a number of valid criticisms that were made by the Minister on Second Reading and during the Committee. To get to the heart of that opposition, I shall quote from his publication "The Right Track", which puts the argument against Labour Party policy. Indeed, part of it seeks to educate the Labour Party on transport. It says:

"Labour's objective is 'to move as much traffic as possible from road to rail and to water.'"

That is quoted directly from our manifesto.

The document continues by quoting from what we said in our consultation document of 1976:

"But what of the popular belief that a dramatic environmental gain would accrue if only we shifted a large amount of long distance freight traffic from road to rail? Alas it is a pipedream."

The phraseology of the then right hon. Member for Grimsby (Mr. Tony Crosland) is well evident from that statement. It goes on to quote the Labour Government's White Paper, which was even more stringent in its attitude:

"A substantial diversion of freight traffic from road to rail is not therefore immediately possible. Nor is it a sensible long-term aim. It could only be achieved by a detailed direction of traffic to the railways. But requiring industry to get approval before moving its goods would hinder its efficiency and mean extra cost to consumers. It would be an unwelcome extension of officialdom."

JPEG 2141 I am sure that on the Conservative Benches a great deal of agreement will be felt with those sentiments.

We are bound to address ourselves to the Minister's argument that if quantity licensing and the Freight Integration Council were not used as intended in 1968 they should be taken out of the Bill. It seems to be the basis of the reasoning that because they have not been used we should unclutter the legislation by taking them out.

An argument that constantly goes on in the Labour Party is that an objective in the manifesto, such as that contained in clause 4, will not necessarily be implemented under a Labour Government. Integration is a little like that. A great deal is said about it at church on Sunday, so to speak, but there is not a great deal of movement towards its implementation. Nevertheless, integration is an important matter and is part of the Labour Party's fundamental thinking. We believe that it is becoming increasingly relevant to the problems of the transport industry.

We do not necessarily believe that all transport problems will be solved by quantity licensing, or that matters hang entirely on that. However, we feel that it is an important part of integration and a small part of the mechanism by which one attempts to inject community values and public interest into the criteria determining the distribution of transport and into all the other matters that the commercial market fails to take into account.

We believe that the arguments about community values and social cost should increasingly dominate the subject of transport.

Since the war transport policy of all Governments has claimed a common objective—namely, a more effective use of our road and rail assets. There has been a dispute about the principles that we adopt to achieve that objective. The marketeers believe that the price mechanism will be the determining factor. Others believe that some form of intervention and control is needed in order to achieve a better balance of distribution and to take account of the community's concern in this vital area.

The Labour Party's approach has been dominated by the British Transport Commission. This was evident in the 1968 Act, when the National Freight Corporation was set up to bring together road and rail, 2142 hiving off part of the rail aspect and seeking to integrate the best transportation systems into the National Freight Corporation. I believe that that was a good concept, and the past chairmen developed it very well. But eventually, for a number of reasons, it sold itself out completely to road transport interests and attitudes.

In the National Freight Corporation's evidence to the Armitage inquiry, there is no longer any pretension to a connection with rail. The NFC argues its case purely on a commercial lorry service basis even against the present Government's proposal to impose further taxes on heavy lorries. It has therefore made a complete move-over, no doubt ready for that part to be sold off to the private sector as desired by the Government and, it seems, to a large extent by the National Freight Corporation also.

The Tory approach is one of denationalisation, but it is evident in the Bill, and indeed in other legislation, that denationalisation relates only to the profitable parts. It is a plundering of public assets and a decontrolling of the mechanisms by which one might introduce some kind of complementation between these aspects.

That has been the difference between the two parties since the war. Neither has been completely successful in the implementation of its policies. I shall not at this stage develop the argument that one system has proved far better than the other. This morning, I hope to convince the Committee that the balance of advantage is moving more and more towards the community determining greater integration of the systems. I think that both parties would agree on that. Even the Conservatives, having originally lurched to the market mechanism in the 1950s, with the Beeching logic and the thousands of cuts in the railways, have moved away from that principle in the decision to retain and to support the rail system at its present level.

At the time of the 1968 Act, transport was dominated by the road industry. That Act, parts of which are repealed by the Bill, resulted from the Labour Government's desire to give a new deal to the railways, to return to intervention and to complementing the systems, and to introduce new ideas as to how that could be achieved. It may be argued that it was a kind of planned competition, because, in the integration of road and rail, particularly by the National Freight Corporation, the objective of transferring traffic JPEG 2143 from one system to the other was not a community objective initse If but could take place only if it were shown to be equally profitable, cheap, quick and efficient. That meant that the argument was always loaded against the rail industry, and was one of the reasons why the NFC was obliged more and more to seek solutions in developing the road sector, as can be seen from the NFC's record.

It should be put on record that, when that policy was developed, in putting the legislation before Parliament, the then Minister of Transport, Barbara Castle, stated clearly that:

"Integration is a practical response to the needs of today and if those needs are to be met we must do some hard thinking about just were we want integration and why, and then decide the how."

She decided in favour of the National Freight Corporation as the way to introduce that policy, and stated that:

"the National Freight Corporation … will be responsible for all freight traffic in the public sector which originates by road, while leaving to British Railways responsibility for traffic originating by rail—whether as complete trains or as wagon load traffic."—[ Official Report, 20 December 1967; Vol. 756, c. 1283 and 1285.]

But there was in that the principle of competition, equal efficiency and equal cost. As I have suggested, that worked to the disadvantage of the rail sector.

When Barbara Castle introduced quantity licensing, she did so with a certain qualification. She said that she would not introduce quantity licensing that required authorisation for traffic over 100 miles. At that time she considered the breakeven point between the advantages of rail and road. She said:

"I have no intention of introducing quantity licensing until the freightliners are capable not only of producing a comprehensive service, but a reliable one."—[ Official Report, 20 December 1967; Vol. 756, c. 1287.]

Despite all its difficulties, no one could doubt that Freightliners developed effectively. One could argue that it could handle much more traffic than it does today. That is an argument worth deploying. The success of Freightliners is such that today it carries 8 million tonnes and has 42 per cent. of deep sea traffic. It has one of the largest concentrations of freight and container traffic on rail anywhere in Europe. It compares favourably with organisations in many parts of the world. 2144 Despite its difficulties, Freightliners has been quite a success.

I have already quoted from "The Right Track", which influenced opinion at the time of the 1978 Act. At that time there was a desire to aim for more harmonised competition. Note that there are always qualifications attached to competition. We are never told of the bald fact of competition, the bald determination of everything on the grounds of price alone. There is always to be a conditioned harmonisation—harmonised competition, or regulated competition. There is never to be limply competition as such.

The 1978 Act developed the concept of fair track costs, which would entail lorries beginning to pay more. The idea was that we should begin to civilise the lorry and make it carry its proper costs. That idea was developed primarily for environmental reasons, because of the damage to the community. One sees the beginning of the introduction of a criterion other than that of cost, the introduction of the concept of consideration for the community and for the environment.

That was the idea behind the section 8 grants, for example. Those have been quite successful. The intention behind them was to utilise rail assets more effectively, thus encouraging more trade to transfer to the rail system. But the successor to the 1978 Act continues the old Tory philosophy of denationalising profitable sectors and leaving the Government to act as a kind of ambulance to the others.

The Government will have to give money to the unprofitable sectors just to keep them ticking over. They will be reduced to having to compete. Whatever is left of the railway system, one is always told that the best way forward is that it should be forced to compete. A competitive atmosphere is enforced although the balance of advantage has always been in favour of the road haulage industry.

Our arguments against the deletion under the clause of certain parts of the 1968 Act do not maintain that those parts have not been used effectively to date. We argue that they are still relevant. Indeed, they are becoming more relevant in view of the problems that will develop over the next 10 years.

Let us look at some of the problems that are beginning to predominate in our transport system. We note that demand JPEG 2145 for transport must grow. It has grown and it will continue to grow. For many years transport has been a growth sector, in terms both of ton-mileage and of units.

: If the hon. Gentleman believes that the Freight Integration Council is so relevant, why during six long years of Labour Government did they do nothing about actually using it? It appears to have become totally moribund.

Mr. Prescott
: That is a fair point. I tried to indicate that that argument must be faced. But if the hon. Gentleman will stick with me, there is a number of arguments that justify our belief. However, we do not believe that it will necessarily be relevant to keep the Freight Integration Council. It has not proved an effective body. I shall make that clear in a minute. One of the Select Committees on which I sat advocated that it be abolished. However, there was a most important condition attached to its abolition. I shall return to that.

First, I wish to make the point that the demand for transport is increasing. It is becoming greater in terms both of ton-milage and of units on our roads. Lorries are growing bigger and covering greater distances on average.

We are grateful for the evidence given by the Department and other bodies to Armitage. We now have much relevant detail, which has been produced over the last month or so, on which to make a judgment about these matters. We are fortunate in being able to debate in Committee the argument on integration, which cannot easily be raised on the Floor of the House.

The demand for transport continues to grow. Bigger lorries are going further into urban areas and that is leading to demands for forms of control. The road share of traffic in this country is increasing. It is now 83 per cent. British Rail takes the majority of the remainder, but it has the capacity to carry more. I think that we should nail the myth that, even if more traffic were transferred to British Rail, it could not carry it. British Rail's evidence to Armitage in the last few months enables us to look at the facts. It is clear that, even if there is no more investment in freight by British Rail, 5 per cent. or 10 per cent. more traffic could be taken 2146 now. That is probably £8 million to £16 million. Without a great deal more investment, it could take up to 50 per cent. more, and carry 250 million tonnes. Even with its present difficulties, British Rail has the capacity to take more traffic.

Nobody envisages a massive transfer of traffic from road to rail. That has never been our argument. We have said that it is in the community's interest that certain traffic should go by rail. That would assist the railway industry and use an under-used asset.

The road haulage industry is creating more congestion on our roads. Heavier lorries are creating enviromental problems. Everyone in the Committee is aware of all those problems, so I do not need to itemise them.

If we believe that rail could carry more, given that roads are becoming increasingly congested and demanding more investment which the Government say they cannot make, clearly the question is how to encourage traffic to go from road to rail transport. What is the criterion of allocation? The market mechanism will not do it, because transport is cheaper by road. Both sides of the Committee have accepted that. The Government's encouragement of the transfer of traffic from road to rail by fiscal measures is an indication of that. That was well stated by the right hon. Member for Yeovil (Mr. Peyton) who, as Minister of Transport in 1972–73, said that he would keep the rail system at that level and give a lot of money to develop it, which he did. I think the allocation was £900 million over several years.

We have agreed that the existing rail system shall remain. The Minister has made clear that he is not prepared to cut it, as I found to my cost at an earlier stage in the argument. There is unused capacity on rail. Roads are becoming increasingly congested. Therefore, it is in the community's interest, apart from that of British Rail, to put more traffic on to British Rail.

If we all agree on that, how can it be done? The mechanism that has been developed so far is to encourage British Rail to take bulk trainload capacity. Most of that market has been saturated, and to that extent British Rail has done a very good job. There is still room to take more of that traffic, but a major contribution has been made.

We say that the market mechanism JPEG 2147 cannot be the single criterion. I draw support from the words of the right hon. Member for Sutton Coldfield (Mr. Fowler) on 23 January 1976 when he had been a Shadow transport spokesman for only seven days:

"I do not suggest that the economic criterion is the only one in transport. Plainly, there are other aspects of transport policy— for instance, the protection of the public, the protection of the environment, and the inescapable need for some public services."— [ Official Report, 23 January 1976; Vol. 903, c. 1752.]

I could probably add safety. I am sure that the Minister would, too.

The Minister therefore accepts, and his policy shows, that there are other criteria than the market mechanism. Hence, the thinking of Armitage. So it is not only competition that will determine what will relocate traffic between the two competing systems.

It can be done in two ways. The first is by licensing and by some form of direction, which has been more evident on the continent than in this country. In fact, in the successful French and German economies, of which we hear so much, licensing and price controls have been used effectively as a means of regulating the transport industry. The Foster Committee report considered some of those examples.

The second method is to give encouragement. The section 8 sidings provision in the 1978 Act was one way of doing that. That is one of the answers to the question about integration that was raised by the hon. Member for Brighouse and Spenborough (Mr. Waller) who asked why a Labour Government did not implement freight integration and quantity licensing. The Labour Government were convinced that the best way of getting nationalised industries to compete with each other was for the market mechanism to introduce the right type of financial discipline. I did not accept it, but that was the Labour Government's view.

Indeed, they went further. They said that they would and give positive financial inducement to attract traffic from road to rail. A study of the section 8 sidings grant provisions shows clearly that 78 sidings projects were agreed at a cost of £21 million to carry 16 million tonnes of traffic. So far only 9 million tonnes have been implemented, but the Government have agreed on traffic amounting to 16 million tonnes. That is a considerable amount. Environmental considerations were 2148 the reason for that. Apparently lorries left one colliery at the rate of one every one-and-a-half minutes, which deserves thinking about when considering putting freight on to rail. It is beyond my comprehension that we have to pay money to nationalised industries to do that, but it is part of the logic of financial incentives being available to everyone.

Because the Government refused this financial help for certain authorities if they thought that the advantages were insufficient, it became a kind of fiscal, not direct, licensing. One had to apply and meet certain criteria, and the Government then gave a licence by giving money.

The Minister made it clear that he intends to legislate by 1983 to ensure that lorries carry their fair share of track costs. It was made clear to Armitage that the bigger lorries especially were not carrying their fair share of track costs to the tune of approximately £200 to £500 per lorry. That is a lot of money. The Minister has stated that he will legislate to increase taxation in that respect, so road transport will begin to carry that extra cost.

If that is done, the allocation of traffic will be affected. The break-even cost band, which in the 1960s was considered to be 100 miles, is now 250 miles or more. The introduction of the 30-ton lorry made it cheaper to carry larger units. If a 40-ton lorry were introduced, clearly the same economics would occur. The cost to the operator would not mean cost to the community, even if the Minister intends to place fair track costs on to lorries.

Foster or Armitage concluded that, if one took account of fair track costs, 3 per cent. or 4 per cent. would be added to the operating cost of a lorry. If one takes also account of proper damage costs, that will add another 3 per cent. or 4 per cent. Therefore, that will increase the operating costs of these lorries by 8 per cent. or 10 per cent. The break-even price between rail and road will then change substantially. An 8 per cent. increase in road costs will mean a greater proportional advantage to rail, because most of the traffic is concentrated in this break-even band of about 200 miles. Therefore, there would be a positive inducement to British Rail.

The Minister may say that under the JPEG 2149 market mechanism, with his inducement, rail would get more traffic and the aim of all of us would be achieved, because he would be integrating the two systems without quantity licensing and without the Freight Integration Council, which he might say we do not need. That is fair argument. But I argue that we must take account of another point which the community cannot ignore. There must be some authority by which the criteria between the two systems are determined. It could be the Armitage Committee which could do exactly what the Freight Integration Council should have done. The council had the power but did not use it, thereby abdicating completely its responsibility in this respect. Its purpose was to advise the Minister because of its many independent experts and its continuity, but it failed rather tragically to do that. It was not because of some fault in the concept or design but because of the quality of some of those who took part, together with certain political ideas about what it should have been doing. That was one of the many problems.

The right hon. Gentleman made clear in 1976 that he believed that it should not be a bureaucratic body. He said that the consumer should make the decision. The consumer all too often operates what he thinks is cheapest and best for him. That is one of the apparent benefits of the system. But we must recognise that a transport system, by definition, is not static. A demand for lorries is growing and if we bring in 40-tonners with four, five and six axles, there will be yet greater demand for them among those who need commercial transportation by lorry.

They will certainly be cheaper for operators to use. There was evidence of that when the 32-tonners took the place of 16-tonners. But price should not be the only criteria. The terms of reference of the Armitage inquiry were:

"To consider the causes and consequences of the growth in the movement of freight by road and, in particular, of the impact of the lorry on people and their environment; and to report on how best to ensure that future development serves the public interest."

Those are wide and comprehensive terms of reference, which cover the whole transport system. Evidence was given to the committee from many sources. No one was asked to consider the consequences of bringing a 40-tonne lorry into Britain in 2150 the context of the EEC. The remit was broader than that. We should now be looking at the broader consequences.

Similar terms of reference should have been given to the Freight Integration Council. I understand why the Minister did not do that, but that is not necessarily a reason for considering that the NFC is defunct in this respect.

The terms of reference spoke of the public interest. The public interest is to be defined on broader terms than the cheapest form of transport available to the consumer at the lowest cost. There is increasing concern about the juggernaut and its effect on the environment, on safety, bearing in mind the contravention of overloading laws which the Minister knows takes place throughout the EEC and, to a certain extent, in the United Kingdom. There is conflict between the community's interests and the interests of commerce. That is why measures are necessary.

The first response was to make the lorry safer. The second was to have lorry routes. What happened to those? The Labour Government's 1978 Bill proposed special tracks for them, and said that if freight could not be put on ribbons of steel it should be put on ribbons of concrete and that a whole new transport system should be developed. The Government considered what it would mean to restrict 16-ton-plus lorries on our roads and they found that to divert them would increase mileage by 20 per cent. and increase the cost, according to the evidence given to Armitage, of £130 million. They felt that that was too expensive. With the cost of tachographs starting at £300 million, the cost of lorry routes seems to pale into insignificance in the context of transport costs.

If the Government say that that is too expensive, and that they will not do anything, they will abandon that area, perhaps to go to the bypass argument. But that argument costs money and of the 500 towns where we wanted bypasses we have only 130 with bypasses and no-nobody seems prepared to find more money for more bypasses or more roads. So we dump the argument and leave it to local authorities. The GLC found that if it wanted to restrict lorries it would cost industry £150 million. Apparently it costs £500,000 and 130,000 gallons more of fuel to bypass Windsor. York is now in the process of trying to protect itself JPEG 2151 because the Government abdicate their responsibility to create lorry routes or direct more of this harmful traffic to another means in the under-used transport system. Therefore, the evidence is growing.

One thing that has changed substantially and rapidly since 1968 is the difference in energy costs. Looking at the evidence given to Armitage, one sees that a 10-tonne lorry has labour costs of 45 per cent. and fuel costs of 8 per cent., but a 32-tonne lorry had labour costs of 30 per cent.—a reduction which was better for the operator—while fuel costs rose to 20 per cent. of the operation. The operator could still say that he could afford that and was getting the price he wanted and making a profit. But the argument is no longer just about the price of fuel but more about the availability of fuel.

I suggest that anybody who wants to examine the argument about energy should read the booklet recently issued by British Petroleum, "Crisis Toady"—no doubt distributed by Saatchi and Saatchi. The booklet says that if it is desired to maintain the amount of growth in oil supplies at the rate over the last 50 years of 5½ per cent., it will be necessary to find two Alaska oilfields every year. Whatever the argument about atomic energy may be, there is just no way in which that can be done. Certainly, for the next two decades there is no doubt that we shall have to depend upon the internal combustion engine, and a dire situation for energy resources will exist in that period. Therefore, energy conservation is essential.

The booklet says that the choice for the community in the rest of the century is either reducing growth because of the lack of oil availability or having more growth with less dependence on oil—that is, by a better utilisation of oil. Road transport is at least three times more wasteful of energy than rail for the carriage of one ton of freight. Rail transport in this country takes only 1 per cent. of our oil resources whereas 50 per cent. goes largely to road transport. Clearly that is a major consideration to be taken into account for the reallocation of traffic. Apart from matters such as costs, damage to roads and the environment, how we conserve our most vital resources of energy must be taken into account. Efficiency has usually meant the cheapest way of achieving an objective and we question why that is no longer the 2152 case. The Foster report on road haulage operators' licensing referred to efficiency as the ability of the industry to provide the range of services for which the user is willing to pay at minimum resource cost to the community.

In the lecture entitled "The Day After Tomorrow", given by Sir Peter Parker in February 1979—that also looked into the argument about efficiency which dominated the Labour Government's 1978 thinking on the transport system—it is stated:

"we are seeking in industry to reconcile the drive for efficiency in business and the equal and sometimes opposite drive for improving the quality of life."

Such arguments create considerable difficulties in the concept that market price should be the determining factor. We are all interventionists of one sort or another, whether fiscal or direct. We intervene to prevent the full consequences of a market system determining transport policy. Tony Crosland was convinced that matters should be left more to the market because apparently he did not like bureaucratic bodies and civil servants, and so on, making the decisions. I believe that he underestimated the consequences of an energy crisis.

There is a matter of judgment. We believe that the best way of proceeding is to introduce community values and not leave it to the market mechanism. We should intervene increasingly to protect the community from the consequences of that. Our transport systems must be used much effectively than they are at present. It seems logical to meet an increased demand for transport with another system that is underutilised. That is the best use of resources.

I should have thought that the proposal would be acceptable to the Government in the context of money supply and public expenditure. Presumably the justification for Armitage is to examine other criteria and to make a decision on those, not simply to leave the consumer to choose a 40-ton lorry because it is cheaper to operate. Indeed, Armitage is challenging the idea that the market mechanism should foist such lorries on us.

Whether one believes in a bureaucracy such as the British Transport Commission, a national transport authority, or even a national transport council, which NFC has been talking about on its death bed, various bodies could be considered. The freight integration was conceived of to JPEG 2153 deal with such matters and to bring continuity, but that did not happen. That is a reflection on those who participated and on the politicians who gave its direction. I should like to read out a recommendation from the Select Committee report on the National Freight Corporation. It said:

"a suitable opportunity should be taken to introduce legislation to abolish the Freight Integration Council which should be replaced by some more effective body."

The Minister is constantly speaking of what Committees of the House have told him, but he only ever takes from them what he chooses. That applied to pensions. We had a big debate about the matter. The Minister did not ask a Committee about an alternative system. He has now done exactly the same with another Select Committee. He has picked out the argument that the council should be abolished but has not gone further and set up another body.

However, he has set up the Armitage Committee and commissioned the Leitch report to deal with what the Freight Integration Council should have done. The concept was right but the application was lousy. That reflects on those who had no faith in it. They include a former Member of the House, Sir Richard Marsh, who was both the Minister and a member of the council. In both capacities he was somewhat disastrous, and that tradition continues with those bodies on which he currently sits, such as that concerned with newspapers. The slogan "Long live the centre" springs to mind.

There is a need for a body such as the Freight Integration Council. It is fair for the Minister to make clear that it did not prove itself, but that is not an argument for saying that there should not be anything in its place. We believe that the Select Committee's point should be taken and the clause rejected.

The arguments against massed quantity licensing have been powerful. Quite apart from the Minister making clear that he is not in favour of it, there is the point that it was never applied. If Barbara Castle had remained Minister of Transport and Freightliners had been developed, quantity licensing may well have been implemented. But for a number of reasons Ministers who followed her were not convinced about the matter. It would be fair to say that although we did not implement 2154 quantity licensing we went onto another tack, with which I have already dealt.

There is later evidence from the Foster Committee, which came out against it. However, I understand that Professor Foster was one of the advisers on the 1968 Bill, which introduced quantity licensing. Now he advises that, on balance, it should not be brought back. However, there is evidence that it can be used effectively. One need only look to the EEC for that. Its rail systems carry a considerably higher proportion—two and three times as much —than ours. As Foster states, that may well be to do with geographical advantages and the longer distances that are travelled. However, the Community has shown that more can go by rail. It can complement the transport system.

Foster says that the decision whether to have quantity licensing is political. He suggests that if it is brought in, environmental protection should be a factor in the issue of licences. He does not seem to be totally opposed to the idea, but implies that if it does come about it should be used to protect the environment.
