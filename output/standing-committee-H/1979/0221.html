Mr. Frank Dobson
: I beg to move amendment No. 307, in page 34, line 28, leave out subsection (4).

We understand that subsection (4) proposes that where any of the subsidiary companies of the National Freight Corporation accrue profits out of which they might pay dividends before the establishment of the successor company, those profits will be available to be paid to the successor company. That means the profits which accrue from public investment, and public expenditure generally, and the efforts of people working in the public sector, will be devoted automatically to making the new successor company yet more attractive to potential purchasers.

We have already heard that in an effort to make the company more attractive the Government proposes to write off £100 million capital debt. They also intend that the Transport Act 1978 grants, which admittedly are small, are likely to continue. In addition, the Minister has said he will further reflect on the possibility of allowing the tax losses which NFC is presently accruing to carry forward to reduce the amount of tax paid by the new company when it comes into existence.

We are faced, therefore, with capital debt being written off, Government grants intended for the public sector continuing into the private sector, and the possible writing off of tax losses incurred in the public sector being used to the benefit of the company in the private sector. All these are to help make the company more attractive. That is simply handing over public money to the private sector. No doubt we will then hear how much more effective and efficient the private sector is—and so it ought to be after having all those burdens lifted from it.

To put the matter in perspective I should put on record the scale of funds which may be involved. We do not have 1750 the figures for 1979. The latest published figures that I have of profits for the NFC's subsidiary companies are those for 1978. BRS made a profit, after allowing for tax, and so on, of £2.5 million; National Carriers made a profit of £2.7 million; Special Traffics, £2.2 million; Pickfords Removals, £2.7 million; Tankfreight £1 million. Two of the subsidiaries, Roadline and Lawther and Harvey made losses. All the other subsidiaries made a profit. We are, then, talking of that money being handed over to the new company rather than being devoted to other purposes.

One might reasonably ask what those other purposes might be. As these profits result from much hard work by a large number of people in the industry, the first call on these funds should be to ensure the proper financing of the pensions of those whose work has made those profits possible. For that reason, the Opposition believe that the profits accrued by subsidiary companies before the new company comes into existence should not accrue to the new company. They should be used for the purposes I have mentioned or taken entirely away from the National Freight Corporation and donated to some other area of public transport expenditure. If the Government cannot make the company attractive enough with the other write-offs and benefits that they are offering—other bits of the dowry that the taxpayer will give to go along with the NFC—it makes their performance even more pathetic.

It is intended to ensure that any dividends paid to the company in respect of shares—particularly from the subsidiary companies—tiansferred as part of the undertaking of the NFC will be available to the successor company as distributable resources. Company law ordinarily deals with a different case—a takeover. There is no reason why income should be capitalised in this instance. We shall not be depriving the public in any way by taking the course that we propose. We are simply ensuring that the company will have a common-sense capital structure. There is no need whatever to lock into that any past earnings from subsidiaries as non-distributable capital which should be available for distribution in the ordinary way. It is a technicality and avoids what otherwise would be an unnecessary and slightly anomalous consequence of a strict application of the Companies Act. I hope that the Committee will agree that the political fears expressed in the Committee are not justified and that this perfectly sensible provision should be allowed to stand.
