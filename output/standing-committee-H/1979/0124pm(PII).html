Mr. Booth
: I beg to move amendment No. 68, in page 49, line 6, at end insert—

"1. The number of passengers carried must not exceed five."

 The amendment is eminently reasonable, but raises serious issues. It arises because the provision in clause 2(2) allows that:

"A vehicle carrying passengers at separate fares in the course of a business of carrying passengers, but doing so in circumstances in which the conditions set out in Part I, II or III of Schedule 1 are fulfilled, shall be treated as not being a public service vehicle unless it is adapted to carry more than eight passengers."

 From that, it is clear that part I of the schedule is dealing with vehicles that are not public service vehicles provided that they carry no more than eight passengers. That is a different provision from the present law.

My hon. Friend the Member for Kingston upon Hull, East (Mr. Prescott) will deal later with the limitations in existing legislation in respect of the pursuit of a business on a hire-car basis. He will also make known our view on the unreality of grouping taxis and hire cars in the proposal. Although they may be covered in theory, taxis are subject, in practice, to other legislation. Therefore the two cannot be considered as comparable in the terms of part I.

Taxis of course operate at fixed fares and generally carry no more than five passengers in the provinces and four in London. Our amendment is designed to get a serious response from the Government to the problem of the unequal relationship 560 that will be created between taxis and hire cars by passing the Bill and schedule in their present forms. Without developing the argument at this stage, I merely state that we are close to the position where it will become impossible to have a sensible business relationship between the two types of passenger service if the law is extended to such an extent.

I readily acknowledge that the Labour Government moved some distance in this direction in their legislation. However, they always put a limit on the number that could be carried in the course of a car hire business. The point that I wish to raise, which will supplement and underlie the main argument that will be developed, concerns the effect of paragraphs (1), (2) and (3) of part I on the new position that will arise as a result of applying the provision to hire cars. I reiterate that it cannot apply to taxis.

The new situation arises because existing provisions are being greatly widened—we are not talking of an increase of one in the number of passengers, but of doubling to eight the number of passengers who can be carried.

The second direct question, given that eight can be carried under the Bill as framed, is, who will initiate the agreement which part I clearly envisages. Part I states:

"The making of the agreement for the payment of separate fares must not have been initiated by the driver"—

 That is clear and specific and no one can complain about lack of clarity in the drafting of the schedule—

"or by the owner of the vehicle, by any person who has made the vehicle available or by any person"—

 who is presumably not the driver-owner or a person who can make the vehicle available under an arrangement—

"who receives any remuneration in respect of the arrangements for the journey."

 Our question is relevant and justified. Who does the Minister say is to initiate the arrangements under the agreement for payment of the separate fare?

Another specific, clear and relevant question concerns the background of the argument on the amendment. How do passengers know of the availability of the service under this provision? The Minister will accept, no doubt, that the journeys that we are discussing have to be made without previous advertisement of the facilities to the public. In fact, the schedule states: JPEG 561

"The journey must be made without previous advertisement to the public of facilities for its being made by passengers to be carried at separate fares."

 That, I admit, throws some doubt on the scope of advertising.

The driver of a taxi is not entitled to drive into a station and organise a group of passengers to divide the fares. A traveller at the head of a queue for taxis is entitled to the next available taxi. He does not have to share it with anyone. The taxi driver is obliged to take that passenger to wherever the passenger stipulates. Therefore, if the driver starts organising separate fares he needs a PSV licence and possibly a road service licence, if he is not careful, because he is running a stage carriage.

If the proprietor, or someone who has a hire purchase interest in the taxi, or some shadowy backer, initiates or advertises arrangements whereby people can travel at separate fares in taxis, that moves him straight into the ambit of our legislation.
