If the law is to prescribe that charges may be made for a certain type of operation, it is in everybody's interest that we seriously consider Col. 344 whether that should merely be a matter of ministerial directive—which is not open to parliamentary challenge—or whether it would be an advantage in terms of accountability and of the protection of the country's citizens if Parliament could examine those charges from time to time to see whether they are right and proper in achieving the Bill's objectives.

I make no more of the matter than that. But that is the crucial point. We have tabled an amendment which raises the possibility of doing that by statutory instrument procedure. Since we have had the merits committees, there has been no difficulty. When issues such as this have come before the House in statutory instrument, generally—I am not making a party point, because I believe that there has been common ground between Governments and Oppositions—unless they have raised extremely controversial matters, it has been possible to refer them to merits committees.

The virtue of having a published list is that the position is made clear. People are more likely to use a legislative provision if their position is clear than if it is in doubt. If somebody can say "I have a 1500cc car, with three passenger seats and a driver's seat. Here is a provision which makes clear that I shall be operating in a completely legal way if I charge my passengers no more than 8p a mile", that makes it more likely than when they do not know whether it is legal or illegal to make that charge unless the matter is tested in court.

Without any wish to be dogmatic or doctrinaire, I suggest that there is a balance of consideration—whether, for the purpose of achieving the Minister's aim, it is better for there to be a clear spelling out of what the scale of charges would be, or whether it is better to leave that to the court. If it is better that it be spelt out, we are left with the question whether it should be left to the Minister to make a directive or for us to retain parliamentary control by making the matter subject to statutory instrument procedure. Those are the two issues which can properly be considered in this batch of amendments against the Government's proposition in amendments Nos. 61 and 62 to withdraw the directive.
