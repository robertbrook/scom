Mr. Albert Booth: I beg to move amendment No. 329, in page 42, line 3, at end insert—

"(1A) In determining what proportion of the relevant fund obligation of each BR or NFC pension scheme has not been funded the Minister shall use the same economic assumptions for every pension scheme subject to the determination".



Its purpose is to avoid the danger of discrimination between British Rail and the National Freight Corporation pension schemes in so far as the Minister will determine the proportions to be covered by funding or by pay-as-you-go.

I attach considerable importance to this aspect because I realise that there is a whole series of conflicting requirements and considerations which the Minister may have pressed upon him when he makes those determinations. It must be established that once a solvency standard has been adopted for one pension scheme, or a set of pension schemes, the same should be applied to others.

As regards the National Freight Corporation, one of the considerations which will obviously be in the Minister's mind is how far prospective purchasers of shares will be satisfied that the funded proportion is adequately funded and, if they felt that it was not adequately funded, how far they might be influenced on what they would be prepared to offer for the shares. Therefore, it might be argued that the Minister had some eye to the effect of the value which a prospective purchaser would put upon a public asset by making a favourable assumption for the purchaser of the shares—that is, making sure that the proportion set to be covered by existing funding was on the low side.

On the other hand, it might be argued by those with a long experience in his Department of dealing with British Rail Col. 2026 pension funds that, if there was a collapse of the funded section in the pension funds administered by the railways, pressures would be brought to bear upon the Minister in the future to make some other financial arrangement. No matter how strongly that idea might be rejected now, with the passing of the years there can be changing views about provisions for railway pensions. The Bill bears eloquent testimoney to that proposition.

There is, therefore, only one completely fair, equitable and reasonable proposition that we can adopt on this issue, and I believe it should be embodied in the Bill—namely, that the same economic assumptions should be made for all of the historic railway pension rights schemes, whether they are administered by British Rail or by a successor company of the NFC.

The wording of the amendment has been chosen carefully and deliberately not to place any restriction on the Minister as regards assumptions other than those of economic output. I appreciate that the rates of death and retirement can vary from one scheme to another, and that the demographic pattern or profile of membership of schemes can vary. Clearly that would have a bearing on the appropriate fund proportion. But it would not be limited by the amendment, which requires only that the economic assumptions should be the same for every pension scheme. It avoids a fear—or suspicion—which would otherwise exist of some preference being given either in respect of the NFC, because those are funds that will pass into the hands of a private company, or other presumptions such as those that I have mentioned. Therefore I hope that the Minister will be able to accept not only the amendment but also the spirit behind it.

They are entirely relevant to our discussion of the amendment. First, the letter stated that the valuation to be made arising from the Railways Act 1974

"will be freely agreed by all the parties involved—the Government, the Board, and the funds—on the basis of the actuarial advice available to them".

Col. 2032 Let there be no argument about this. That undertaking was not written into the 1974 Act, but was given subsequent to the Act. We are now dealing with a period subsequent to that in which there could have been an adjustment in the amounts to be guaranteed by way of funding.

The letter went on to deal with the situation which would be brought about if the Government sought to move in response to the questions posed by the Public Accounts Committee as to whether it was desirable to change from a fully funded scheme to one which might not make such a large immediate call upon public funds. That part of the letter reads:

"However, if policy were to be changed in the light of this review one must, I think, assume that this would be done in such a way as to leave the position of members and pensioners of the funds as secure as it is now, in other words if the change were made the security which is effected by full funding would be replaced by some other equally effective security for the membership."

That letter therefore contained two assurances. First, it said that the appropriate sum to be paid on funding would be freely between the three parties. That is a very different situation from that proposed in the Bill, which gives the Minister the right to settle any dispute as to whether the amount proposed and the proportion is appropriate. Secondly, the letter gave the assurance that if the Government moved in response to the Public Accounts Committee's criticism, they would do so in a way that gave equally good protection to the pension fund membership. I do not believe that we can equate the proposition in the Bill with those two assurances.

It is one thing to say to those who are running a fund "When you have totally agreed with us what the appropriate funding is, it is your responsibility to see that those pensioners are looked after." It is quite another to say "If we disagree with you on the appropriate portion to be covered by funding, we shall determine it for you, whether you like it or not. But you will still be responsible for seeing that the proportion to be covered by the funding Image Col. 2033 is adequate, and if it is not, you will have a duty to those pensioners." That goes to the heart of the reasons for the amendment, because it is an entirely different proposition.

To his credit, the Parliamentary Secretary has always been extremely frank about this aspect of the Bill. On Second Reading he said:

"The actuarial basis is being considered and we hope that it will eventually be agreed between the Government, the Board and the unions. But we cannot guarantee that. Someone has to decide, and actuarial judgments involve a matter of opinion in the final analysis. Certainly at present we would hope to reach agreement, if we can, on the actuarial basis."

But he went on to say:

"there is absolutely no threat to the pension rights of the railwaymen who are affected by these historic rights provisions."—[Official Report, 27 November 1979; Vol. 974, c. 1239.]

But he made clear all along that, in the last analysis, the Government could decide.

The Parliamentary Secretary's statement on Second Reading is very different from what was stated in the assurance in the letter dated 7 December 1977. That said that, on the basis of the 1974 funding scheme, the appropriate amount would be freely agreed between the parties.

In the light of that assurance, it is reasonable for us to expect that if there is a disagreement at some time in the future, there should be outside arbitration on an impartial basis in order to ensure that that undertaking is not breached. If it cannot be freely agreed, I can see only one way in which the settlement could be fair and be seen to be so. That would be if it went to an impartial outside arbitrary body. This proposition is in keeping with the 1977 undertaking. I think that the Government owe it as a duty to those who are having thrust upon them this considerable responsibility for safeguarding the pensions of a large number of pensioners on an entirely different financial basis from that on which they originally undertook that responsibility, one on which the amount of money agreed is no longer within their direct influence, because they will no longer have the assurance that they will operate on an agreed sum. All we ask here is that at least they have the assurance within the Act that if it is not an agreed sum, it will be one that is independently arbitrated.

It is important to realise that clause 47 deals with a comparatively limited number of schemes in which the pension entitlement depends upon two sources—a basic scheme which governs the benefits at retirement date and a supplementation scheme covering annual index-linking thereafter. Two supplementation funds have been established under previous legislation. They are the B.R. (1974) pension fund and the National Freight Corporation (1978) pension fund. Clause 47 deals with the situation in which the basic scheme fund produces a surplus. The clause is concerned with what is a fair use of the surplus produced by the investments of the basic fund. Having listened to the speeches, I realise that there is a belief among the Opposition, which is understandable when one first considers the arrangements, that somehow the Government are trying to have their cake and eat it, that we seek to vary the rules the moment any fund looks like going into Image Col. 2045 surplus compared with the attitude we have taken to the matter of fund managers meeting their obligations out of their investments generally.

However, an examination of what I might call the 1974 settlement shows that that is not actually the case. Up to 1974, there were a number of schemes which set out a basic pension entitlement for which they had some funds, but in which, in practice, over the years, it had become customary and, indeed, expected that each year there would be supplementation to update the benefits in line with inflation. There were no funds that could properly cover that annual index-linking. At the same time—again because of inflation—the investments of the basic scheme funds have in some cases done quite well. Because of inflation, the Government's obligations to supplement the pension benefits have been considerable. Inflation has caused the Government's liability to index-link and to produce a surplus in the basic scheme.
