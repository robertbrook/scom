Mr. Fowler
: I beg to move, That the Bill be further considered this day at half-past Three o'clock.

I regret the need to move this motion. It does not give me any pleasure to do so. But we have made very disappointing progress on the last clauses. They are not, by any stretch of the imagination, the most controversial clauses. Nor is this the most controversial part of the Bill.

I am the first to admit and concede that there are parts of the Bill which are deeply controversial. But no one who reads the clauses we have been debating since 10.30 am yesterday morning can honestly say that they are controversial. I believe that these are sensible public safety clauses, some of which have been drafted with the same words and have the same conditions which have been taken from the legislation of the previous Government.

In those circumstances, it is a matter of considerable regret that these highly important public safety clauses should have made such small and slow progress through the Committee. We have been sitting here virtually non-stop since 10.30 am Thursday, and we have not yet completed four clauses. It would be less than my duty to accept that. It is unacceptable progress from any objective point of view. For that reason, I propose the motion.

Mr. Booth
: I hope that I am normally a reasonable man and talk in moderate terms. But what the Minister now proposes is utterly intolerable and, as far as I can ascertain, totally without precedent. Perhaps you, Mr. Dean, will guide us on the question of precedent.

I cannot find any previous occasion when a Minister has come to a Committee after it has been sitting for this length of time and sought to work, without notice, a procedural manoeuvre which will enable 1414 him, technically, to end the sitting and extend it within a few hours. The Minister is well aware that that is what he is doing. He knows that by doing so he may well make the role of the Opposition an impossible one. He knows that he has had tremendous aid and facility from the Opposition, and more progress has been made on this controversial Bill in one week than can be pointed to in any other Standing Committee.

We are prepared to set this against any reasonable criteria that the Minister likes to pick. He can take Bills going through the House during this Session. He can take other controversial transport Bills as the test. He can compare with one Transport Bill which had 50 clauses the consideration that we have given this Bill. By any of these tests his proposition is totally unreasonable.

Mr. Prescott
: However it is expressed, all my hon. Friends on the Opposition Benches, who have far less experience than my right hon. Friend, feel varying kinds of anger. Whatever that anger may be, I want to make clear from the beginning that we consider that the Chair has treated us with great fairness, and we make no complaint there. We do not complain about the procedures that have been used in this Committee. The Government allege that we are delaying the Bill and are not moving at the speed of other Committees. That may question whether we have been in order. You, Mr. Dean, and other Chairmen have ruled as you and they have seen fit; we are governed by your rulings, so whatever the argument may be, we have certainly been in order.

The argument is about the substance of our contributions throughout this period. People can go to the record to confirm that whole clauses have come out of the Bill, words have been amended and considerable changes made, bearing in mind the ability of any opposition to change Bills. That reflects on the receptive-ness of the Minister to the arguments on the amendments. We have put on record that we do not have complaints about the receptibility to an argument. That has been prevalent in this 19 or 20 hours of debate. It was the same in the debate on Tuesday: at each stage our arguments have been accepted and there have been amendments and further reflections. That has been the nature of the progress on the Bill. The argument has not been of sterile opposition on ideological positions; there has been give and take; there has been movement; there have been changes in the Bill.

The Minister is arguing that he would be neglecting his responsibilities if he allowed the Bill to continue in this manner. The Bill is almost unprecedented in its speed. As I understand it, the House has only ever worked through into Saturday once, and that was unprecedented on a more controversial Bill than this some years ago.

The Minister makes clear that there are important areas on which controversy will continue to arise—on matters of greater substance than is involved in these clauses.

1416 My time in the House is the same as that of the Minister—we both came at the same time—and from my experience, limited by serving in Europe, it has never been practice to judge the speed of a Bill by the controversy of its clauses. I have never heard that principle enunciated by any other Minister or anyone else in the House. Whether or not it is a new principle, brought by the Minister to the Department to reflect the nature of his experience, I must tell him that it is one that no authority in the House will accept.

I have always understood that the responsibility that the Minister talks about is to search a clause to the best of my ability and, if I do not agree with its contents, to change it, and, if I do not agree with the amended contents, to vote against them. That is the purpose of a Standing Committee. I defy any hon. Member here, including the hon. Member for Birmingham, Yardley (Mr. Bevan), to find any part of the Bill wherein there has not been movement, substantial and varying change because the Opposition have carried out their obligations.

We totally reject the Minister's argument. For any parliamentarian to project the argument that where there is acquiescence to a clause it is not to be investigated, discussed and changed is the very antithesis of democracy, and it is a lesson for the Minister to learn. It is an arrogant statement and would be rejected by any thinking parliamentarian in his role in the Opposition of examining a particular Bill.

The argument seems to be—the Minister did not express it in so many words but this is what he means—that the speed of the proceedings on the Bill is somehow unprecedented. He tries to justify an unprecedented action. What is the record of the speed for which the Minister now submits this motion? Is it that the Committee should continue for another 24 or 48 hours, or that it should have gone on till midnight tonight? No doubt it would then have been possible to table a motion and the Minister would have told us that because we had not moved at the same pace another motion would be required to carry us through.

I have not deployed any argument about the important matter being decided on the Floor of the House today, and the opportunity that we are being denied to participate. We are all well aware of the implications of the Abortion (Amendment) Bill, about which probably most of us have received JPEG 1417 more mail than about any other piece of legislation that I can remember. Admittedly I have seen some abortion legislation, but none of it carried the amount of correspondence for me—or for other hon. Members, from what they tell me—as has Abortion (Amendment) Bill. Millions of people are concerned in the actions of their Members at this moment on the most important conscience clause they will participate in or vote on.

Presumably by the rules of this Committee we shall not be denied the possibility of voting in the House, but we shall be denied the chance to listen to the arguments and decide whether the limit should be 24 or 28 weeks, to listen to the experts who have been dealing with the matter and to make considered judgments.

We thought that the motion was to apply from 3.30, but presumably we shall adjourn immediately if the Government win the vote on the motion. Is that correct, Mr. Dean?

Mr. Prescott
: For as long as the Committee takes to deal with this issue, we are faced with a dilemma. Opposition Members could stop talking, vote and use our right to participate in the debate on the Floor of the House. We can accept that capitulation, or fight this other important principle—though it is probably not as important as the vital principle involved in abortion—namely, that if Oppositions take time to scrutinise legislation and take their time on non-controversial clauses, the new maxim for Committees is "If you don't speed up, you will have to go on and on".

Let us put some facts on record. We have been discussing the matter of a timetable with the Minister. I shall put our position on record so that it can be fully understood by every member of the Committee and by anyone outside. What were the facts which led to this breakdown? The Minister says that he is not happy to come to the Committee with the motion. But we have been receiving threats all day that we should be here until one o'clock Saturday and Sunday. We take those threats as they come.

It is not with any great sorrow that the Minister puts the motion before us. Let us get rid of the cant. The Minister was sleeping while we were working on his Bill. His hon. Friends might have slept, 1418 too, because they were not prepared to speak. It is a non-controversial clause. Why should they not go and sleep? They do not want to discuss legislation which we have effectively changed.

How did the breakdown come about? I shall leave the Parliamentary Secretary out of this argument, because the breakdown came with the communication about our discussions with the Minister himself. That, apparently, is where the breakdown arose.

It is known, and it is part of the role of Oppositions and accepted by us, that there comes a time when an Opposition are in disagreement about a Bill, no matter how uncontroversial. A more controversial Bill will extend the area of disagreement. Any Bill that denationalises a nationalised company is as controversial as can be, and a Bill which fundamentally changes the bus industry in with the consequences that we have attempted to point out, is controversial.

Some days ago—probably two weeks; perhaps the Committee will bear with me if I do not remember the date—discussions took place as the result of a joint request across the Committee when the motion was moved that the Committee should meet in the afternoons with open sittings. The argument began to develop: what can we discuss on a timetable?

The Minister was concerned to get his Bill through in a certain time. It was quite a respectable proposition. We were concerned to delay it for our reasons. At the same time, we requested certain matters for co-operation. Those are matters of judgment that each of us can make. Should a Government be prepared to concede something on a Bill to facilitate the demands of the Opposition? It is a judgment that must be made. If it is thrown out and not accepted, one has to consider that situation. But those matters are discussed in every Committee along this Corridor, whether in debates on how many days will be given for the Report stage or on an amendment from a body that is pressing the Opposition to take it up. It may deal with pensions, for instance. Behind that word pensions is an awful lot of bad feeling in certain quarters of this Committee, because of suggestions that some people had prescriptive rights.

What are the facts? It is a matter of record, as hon. Members opposite must admit, how many amendments are on our Amendment Paper compared with the number on some of those Bills. It has not been the Opposition's policy to go for a blanketing of amendments. Hon. Members taught me a valuable lesson that will live for me for the rest of the time I stay here. I was soft. But it is a matter of fact, from the actual record of amendments that have been dealt with in this Committee, that they have been few compared to the amendments on other Bills. In many cases, the amendments were suggested by various interests such as the National Bus Company and others.

There have been comments to the effect that we seem to be putting forward a National Bus case, or a case for another body that gives us amendments. We happen to agree with those amendments, and we put them forward. That is another indication that we did not adopt what is traditionally done in Committee. That may be put down to our inexperience, but I have learnt a lot, listening to the wise counsels of my right hon. Friend. We were caught in that process.

Why did we adopt that approach? Because we were discussing with the Minister the reasonable proposition when the Committee was to finish its business, so that the Minister could say to the usual channels "You will have the Bill by this date." Each side of the House, when it is in government, has to plan its business.

We should be naive if we did not believe that both sides—and I have had periods of Opposition and Government on the Back Benches here—have made attempts through Committees to clog up business. It appears to be a parliamentary process which eventually leads to the loss of a Bill. We have lost Bills by that process. That seems to be a legitimate objective. I am assured by hon. Members on both sides of the House that it is a tactic in which we have all taken part. We have either acquiesced in it or played a positive part, 1420 depending on our attitude.

I return to my comparison with other Committees. What has been the speed of this Committee compared to other controversial Committees that have gone through the night?

In my concern to see how reasonable the Minister's arguments were, I questioned members of those Committees and was told that they went through a second stage—from 4.30 p.m. to 6.30 p.m.—in their sittings motions. Ministers on those Committees did not seek open motions as did the Minister of Transport. He was provocative from the beginning about the motion. We jumped straight from an ordinary sitting into an open-ended sitting. It is legitimate for Government to do that, but I am trying to compare Committees to see whether we have been unreasonable, because that is the heart of the argument.

When we first opened our discussions with the Government I looked at the other sittings. We had nine sittings. Others had started earlier so I had to refer to the record in the Library of the ninth sitting. We were making a fair comparison between the Transport Bill Committee and the Committees on the Education (No. 2) Bill, the Industry Bill and the British Aerospace Bill.

All that information was put to the Minister when we first met to discuss the sittings and it was pointed out that we were terribly slow. I do not think that he mentioned it after that.

At the ninth sitting, cur Committee was debating the third clause. The Industry Bill Committee was on clause I, and I believe that it stayed there until the 11th sitting. The Aerospace Bill Committee was on clause 3, exactly the same pace as us. The Education (No. 2) Bill Committee, to be fair, was on clause 7. At an early stage, agreement had been reached between the two sides which allowed progress on the JPEG 1421 basis of certain understandings. That partly explained why they were on clause 7.

At this point it is fair to ask, what was the size of the Bills in each case? In the Transport Bill there are 57 clauses, in the Education Bill 31 clauses, in the Industry Bill 18 clauses and in the Aerospace Bill 14 clauses. So, proportionately, we have not moved forward at the same speed, but we can argue the opposite view—that because a clause is in a Bill with 57 or so clauses it should not have one-third less time spent on it. It is not my argument, and I do not think that the Committee as a whole would accept the new concept developed by the Minister.

I wish to illustrate that I have considered the arguments carefully. I have with me the notes that I used when the Minister and I talked together. I told him then that his argument was not reasonable and not borne out by the facts, but that that did not mean that we would not talk about a timetable motion. We were not talking about going through the night, because there is no love on this side of the Committee of sitting through the night. There are no special creatures on this side who wish to sit for so long.
