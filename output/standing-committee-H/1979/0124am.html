Express carriages are those used to carry passengers at separate fares, and so on, for less than 30 miles as the crow flies. But the 30 miles as at present defined in the Bill as drafted is the distance from the place where the passengers are picked up. It is possible, therefore, that in one direction the route travelled may, at some point along that route, go more than 30 miles as the crow flies from the point where the passengers were first picked up, although the destination may be less than 30 miles away. Such a journey would be an express carriage. But on the return journey, although exactly the same route is followed, the point that made the outward journey qualify as an express carriage may not be 30 miles from the point at which the return journey commenced—that is, the destin- Image Col. 455 ation of the outgoing journey. That is plainly anomalous, and it can be corrected by the wording proposed in the Government amendment.

In case I have not made the point completely clear I shall give a specific example, with distances. A service takes passengers from A to C, with C being 29 miles from A. The route passes through B, which is 31 miles from A, but only five miles from C. As a result, on the outward journey, all the passengers travel through B. The condition for express services in clause 3(3)(b)(ii) of the Bill is therefore satisfied. However, on the reverse journey from C to A, along precisely the same route, the passengers never travel to any point at least 30 miles from C, which is the pick-up point for the return journey.

That is likely to occur only in a very few cases. We are probably dealing only with a tiny number of journeys, but I hope that the Committee will accept that an absurd situation could arise. We therefore propose the revised wording on the amendment paper.

The effective change is that the 30 miles is now to be measured from either the place where the passengers are picked up or the place where they are set down. So long as at some point in the journey the route takes the passengers more than 30 miles as the crow flies from either the pick-up point or the set-down point, the service will be an express service.

It is important that the definition of express carriage should be intelligible and unlikely to give rise to any anomalous or absurd results, because, as the Bill stands, an express carriage will be taken outside the need for road service licensing. The provider of an express carriage will need an operator's licence. His vehicles will be subject to stringent safety controls, but express carriage services will not need road service licensing and will not therefore be subject to the quantity controls or the power of the traffic commissioners to prevent competition and regulate trade. In discussing later amendments to clause 3, we shall be able to bring out the full significance of that change.

Freeing express carriages from the need for road service licensing opens up great opportunities for commuters and other travellers to provide specific services for their needs. They could cater for long-distance commuting in and out of London, for instance. We also expect to see a great Col. 456 opening up of inter-city express services which would provide probably the cheapest form of public transport between large urban centres.

We see no reason to inhibit competition in that area or to prevent the setting up of new services. We believe that there is a future for the development of low-cost transport between urban centres, and that many people will be tempted to provide such services, once we have removed the present restrictions on express services.
