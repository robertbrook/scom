Mr. John Prescott
: I hope that the Government note that we are assisting them by making up the quorum. Despite all that has been said about time-wasting, it is the Opposition who are providing sufficient members of the Committee for the debate to start. I hope that we shall not hear too much about timetable motions from the Government later, when they cannot now produce a team even to start our discussion.

Before the break in our proceedings, I was talking about the difference between express and tour bus operation. The Parliamentary Secretary made clear—and we are agreed—that if a passenger travels more than 30 miles in a vehicle that will be one of the major qualifications in denning an express operation. There is no requirement for permission from the commissioner, as with present legislation, but only that he should be informed—in a prescribed form—about the nature of the service.

The Parliamentary Secretary said that there may be a commitment to a service. Presumably that means that a bus service may be run from A to B perhaps a certain number of times a week under the conditions of an express service. I am concerned particularly in the light of our amendment concerning tours, at the differences between the two operations.

Let us consider the tour operator. The example readily springs to mind of one who offers an excursion to a particular spot. At present he is required to have a 504 licence for an excursion of less than 30 miles, and under certain conditions there must be agreement from the traffic commissioners.

However, what happens if the distance is greater than 30 miles? Let us suppose that there are excursions to the seaside from the city of Leeds, which would certainly be more than 30 miles. It would be a regular passenger service. Particularly in the summer and at weekend, people would want to visit the seaside town of Bridlington. There might be two forms of traffic— that associated with a day out at the seaside, and that of travelling for business or other reasons, which is not normally covered by tour arrangements.

In those circumstances, the express operator goes to the traffic commissioner and gives him an indication of his intention to start a service two or three times a week from Leeds to Bridlington. He requires no permission for that, but simply says "Mr. Commissioner, I now intend to provide this service."

Another tour operator may decide that he wants to operate only at a profitable time—say on a Saturday rather than a Wednesday. He could be in the same situation that arose in our argument about cross-subsidisation. Another operator who wished to operate a service three times a week might be prepared to provide more services on Saturdays in order to carry on with less profitable business on Mondays and Wednesdays.

I presume that it is now possible—I should like the Minister to confirm this— that a tour operator is not required to give notice to a commissioner that he intends to run a business on a Saturday in competition with the private operator of an express service. If the express operator is required to give notice to the commissioner about services, routes and times, why is the tour operator not required to give such notice?

I readily concede that there are many examples of tours that may not fit into that category, but we are in a grey area where it may be difficult to distinguish between the tour operator's activity and the express service, except that one will have been notified to the commissioner and the other will not. Why maintain that distinction? As my right hon. Friend said, should not the commissioner, on receiving such notice, be able to decide whether there was a duplication of services and whether the express service would be JPEG 505 undermined? Presumably he would then be able to refuse a licence to operate.

Our amendment seeks to show that there is an artificial distinction made between a tour operator and an express service, and we have used the same basic economic arguments that we have employed on other amendments. That may undermine an express service operator who might operate a subsidised service showing his commitment—to use the words of the Parliamentary Secretary—to provide some form of service on the route—presumably taking over from the National Bus Company which regularly operates that route, under a licensed arrangement, which is cross-subsidised by a local authority that gives a transport supplementary grant.

The Parliamentary Secretary will probably agree that that is just one of the anomalies. Information will not have to be given to the commissioner, and the two services will compete, with the likelihood that the man operating a service on Saturdays in summer—when it is profitable—will pull out and no service will be provided in the winter when business is not profitable.

We are debating the extremely narrow point of whether, if an excursion or tour at any point goes more than 30 miles from the point of origin, the operator of such an excursion or tour should notify the traffic commissioners. I think that it all boils down to one point, stated again a few moments ago by the hon. Member for Kingston upon Hull, East (Mr. Prescott). Given that we impose upon an express service the obligation to notify, why do we not impose a similar obligation on an excursion or tour? We therefore come back to the essential difference between an 508 express service and an excursion or tour. I shall come on to the legal definition, but I will begin by trying to put it in lay language.

An express service is a regular, scheduled service going from point A to point B and back again and running to a published timetable. People planning to make a journey between two centres may therefore consult the timetable and find that every Friday afternoon, say, there is a bus that leaves Leeds at 5 o'clock and gets into Bridlington at a set time. Or a service may leave Nottingham every Friday at 5 o'clock and arrive at Skegness at 7 o'clock.

The operator of such a service therefore notifies the traffic commissioners in order to indicate commitment, because we do not want people deciding to run a service one week and deciding not to run it the next week because it makes a loss. There is also a practical difference for the passengers. In the case of an express service, if only one person turns up at 5 o'clock in the old market square in Nottingham, the service still has to run.

Mr. Prescott
: Why?

Mr. Clarke
: Because it is a scheduled service. In the same way a train will pull out of a station with only one passenger on it. The station master will not cancel it because only one person has turned up. It is a scheduled service. It therefore leaves at the set time, and the public need to know that it is reliable and not subject to cancellation. Notification is therefore given to the traffic commissioners and the service runs accordingly.

An excursion or tour is, to use lay language once again, in the nature of a holiday trip or excursion or a mystery tour or something similar. The passengers may get on and off at Nottingham, but they could go on an outing for the day to Skegness and come back again in the evening. All the passengers would go out on the journey and come back again. That is not a scheduled service. It may just be a one-off trip because it is thought that on a particular weekend in the summer there will be enough people wanting to go to Skegness for it to be worthwhile for the operator to advertise and see how many people turn up. Most operators run on the basis, that is perfectly possible with excursions or tours at present, that if not enough people turn up the trip is subject to cancellation and they get their money JPEG 509 back because it is not worth going.

Given that the services are different entities, it is on that basis that we propose in the Bill that the scheduled service needs the commitment and notification. It is really in order to make sure that the public know where they are. With excursions or tours there is not the same requirement at the moment. One does not need the same undertaking that the timetable will be adhered to, because an excursion does not have a timetable. It is by nature a one-off trip that the operator will try to market as best he can.

I agree that there will be occasions when the two will sail rather close together. There may be a passenger who lives in Nottingham but has business in Skegness, or a man in Leeds who has business in Bridlington. I am sure, that occasionally, an individual who has to make a journey for business reasons, works out that the cheapest way of transacting his business in Skegness during the day and getting back home at night is to travel on a holiday excursion. He does not have to join the women's institute or a club to do so. He simply goes to one of the coach operators and finds himself on a coach with a number of people who have buckets and spades and are going to the seaside for the day.

That man cannot be stopped. There is no way that the traffic commissioners can stop a businessman, clutching a briefcase, from joining an excursion because he has business to transact in a seaside town and has worked out that the excursion trip is the cheapest way of getting there and back. I do not see the problem.

The other difficulty that Labour Members anticipate is the problem of competition. What if tours are run with such regularity that they damage the express? With respect, that argument relates to the previous amendment.

I have tried to explain why we believe that regulation of competition—the protection of the interests of the bus and coach operators—is not of paramount importance when discussing long-distance excursions, tours and trips. It is an area in which we wish to encourage competition. As I have indicated by quoting market reasearch in the National Bus Company, we consider that it is an area into which more people will come and where, at present, the market is underexploited and not overloaded. Regulation of competition is regulation for regulation's sake.

The definition of "excursion or tour" is at the root of the matter. Again, the right hon. Gentleman, with his usual diligence, has discovered the definition. The definition clause in this Bill is clause 35, and there one is referred to the transport Act 1968, which contains the involved definition that the right hon. Gentleman read out. As I pointed out to the right hon. Gentleman, when he commented on the inelegance of our drafting, Mrs. Barbara Castle did not do much better when she was in charge of transport legislation.

I assure the right hon. Gentleman that if he analyses that definition, he will arrive at a conclusion of the sort that I have indicated. It is not a scheduled service. It is not a service for which there is a licence, or for which a timetable is a condition of the service. It is one which essentially takes people roughly from one vicinity to another and back again to the starting point. We shall deal with the definition in detail when we reach clause 35, but I think that it will be found that the law will match common sense in the distinction between excursions or tours and express services.

Finally, I was asked whether the very frequency of a tour and excursion arrangement would make it a scheduled service. My present understanding is that frequency has nothing necessarily to do with excursion or tour. We all know that many coach operators advertise that they do an excursion every Saturday to a set place. In practice, that does not give rise to any confusion about the nature of such an excursion to the Yorkshire Dales or the Peak District or anywhere else. The fact that the excursion is regular—perhaps even two or three a day at busy times—makes no difference.

I conclude—and I am prepared to repeat this argument on subsequent occasions to assist the Committee—by pointing out the areas where excursions and tours remain distinct from express services. The reasons are similar to those that I have already given for distinguishing between express services on the one hand and excursions and tours on the other. The reasons for continuing a distinction for excursions and tours are first, to relieve them from the need for notification; secondly for the purposes of clause 9, to give them, when they are going less than 30 miles, a quick and easy way of obtaining a road service licence where they are not competing with stage carriages; thirdly, for the purpose of new bus grants under clause 51, to make sure that they do not apply for those; and, finally, for fuel duty rebate, also found in clause 61, I have been helpful in having got up and clarified the distinctions, although it will merely enable the Opposition to table more amendments to cover exactly the same ground when we come to those clauses. Those are the only places in the Bill where there is need for a separate distinction for excursions and JPEG 513 tours and this distinction, like all the others, follows naturally when one grasps the essential difference between the two types of service.
