FEES FOR GRANT OF LICENCES, ETC

Mr. Albert Booth
: Before lunch we were questioning how far the clause should provide for the collection of a series of fees for the issue of documents, without any guidance on the policy to be pursued by the Minister in formulating the regulations controlling the level of fees. Consideration has been given by the Minister, and those in the industry responsible for finding the fees, on how far the industry should bear the cost of this part of the Department of Transport's work. They have considered whether it should be in part a public service—as some of the issues involved are clearly in the broader public interest—whether it should be seen as a legitimate expense to be passed on and how far the role of the Department is to regulate and control the affairs of those businesses.

The amendment takes a neutral stance on the cost argument. It will aid the Minister to the extent that he will have protection for any regulations that he makes which recoup the cost of providing the services directly required for the provision of documents. He would be hampered if he sought to use his power to obtain fees as a form of revenue raising for his Department. I do not suggest that he would try to raise revenue for any other Department or to improve his own standard of living. I hope that the amendment will give us an opportunity to consider this difficult issue.

The Minister may not agree with the way in which the amendment is drafted but I hope that he will indicate his view on the principle. It would be useful if he 1484 could tell us the current position in his Department and how far it is a cost upon public funds to administer these services, or the nearest equivalent.

I now turn to amendment No. 263, which could be held to take a position slightly different from that of No. 262, if the two are read narrowly. Amendment No. 262 contains the word "approximately", and it can be interpreted as providing the total fees charged or the total costs incurred. If we read it broadly it meets the principle. But amendment No. 263 relates only to the operators' licences. It enunciates the principle that the charge should be proportionate to the maximum number of vehicles that can be operated under the licence. An operator seeking a licence to operate 50 vehicles would expect to be charged twice as much as an operator seeking a permit to operate a maximum of 25 vehicles.

The principle will appeal to Government Members in so far as it will mean that the small operators—and Conservatives claim to be protagonists for the small businessman—will be relieved to some extent by this charging system while the large operators will bear the heaviest financial load. That does not cause any difficulty in the Opposition's view. We like the principle of the broadest shoulders carrying the heaviest loads. There is a further attraction in that it will be a deterrent to those operators who might, as the Bill at present stands, be tempted to apply for an operator's licence for a considerably larger number of vehicles than in their current fleets. There is nothing whatsoever in the Bill to prevent them doing that.

Has the Minister thought about this? It is a slightly different provision from anything in present legislation. The Committee voted against clause 23 which provided for the transferable discs to be run by the operators. Now the operator's licence is the main guide to how many vehicles a PSV operator can run legitimately. It will not necessarily correspond with his number of vehicles. If it fails to correspond because he is running fewer vehicles, it will be legal but if it fails to correspond because he is running more vehicles, it will be illegal. This is an issue, therefore, of some importance. However, there is virtue in having a slight financial deterrent indicated by Parliament to the obtaining of an operational licence for a number of vehicles way in excess currently at the command of the operator—or the JPEG 1485 number which he shows to the commissioners to whom he applies—because they might not be exactly the same. I do not want to join my hon. Friend the Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, Central (Mr. Cowans) in using the transport business to give cowboys a bad name—because cowboys were among my early heroes—but I have to take account that different operators may approach this issue differently.

Two issues are bound up in the second amendment, both of which are worthy of consideration by the Committee.

Amendment No. 264 deals with the method by which the regulations are made. I have a direct question for the Parliamentary Secretary. Does section 260 of the 1960 Act apply to this clause? I have read and re-read section 260 of the 1960 Act—I am grateful to the Parliamentary Secretary for the copy that I am using. That appears to indicate that any power conferred by the 1960 Act upon the Minister to make regulations must be exercised by statutory instrument. Parts of the Bill will become part III of the I960 Act, but no distinction appears to be drawn in the 1960 Act between one part and another. Section 260 will probably apply. If that is so, it seems that without the action suggested in this amendment, there will still be Parliamentary procedure in that the regulations will be prescribed in the statutory instrument. They will be subject to annulment in pursuance of a resolution of either House of Parliament.

The amendment proposes to change that in two ways. It proposes to make it subject only to the control of the Commons. To that extent it seeks to simplify the procedure. It also proposes to make it subject to the affirmative resolution procedure, and to that extent a stiffer hurdle has to be jumped.

I argue before this Committee, which has for some weeks been looking in great detail at the powers which are to be exercised by a Minister if the Bill becomes an Act, that there are a large number of powers in the Bill to be exercised by the making of regulations. Even we, who understand with our detailed scrutiny of the Bill, the case that might be made for these enabling powers, and for leaving many matters undecided by the Bill to be decided by regulations, might have trouble in explaining that to our constituents, who want answers to their questions. We might 1486 find it difficult to explain that, in our judgment, it is right to leave it to a Minister to deal with the matter by regulations as and when the occasion arises.

If hon. Gentlemen are with me so far, I put a second point to them. Constituents will ask: "What control will there be when the Minister makes the regulations? Will he have the opportunity to raise the matter directly with the Minister on the Floor of the House if there is something fundamentally objectionable in the proposal?"

The only way that we can answer that question in the affirmative is by carrying the amendment. That is the only simple and direct way of dealing with the matter. I do not suggest that the Minister has any wrong intent in his use of these powers. Far from it. I appreciate the modern argument for delegated legislation and why so many Acts are on the statute book, put there quite recently, which make sweeping enabling provisions. But I believe there comes a time when every Committee has to make a choice between whether it wants the power to be vested in the Minister with no parliamentary control, whether it wants power to be vested in the Minister subject to his merely making the information available in the form of the regulations, or whether it wants to retain a degree of parliamentary control over some of the regulation-making powers.

We have a simple proposition on which to test that choice. In the clause, a whole series of possibly heavy new fees are involved for a number of people operating in this business. If the Minister lays before the House a perfectly satisfactory set of regulations, I am sure that none of us will challenge them. The affirmation will be obtained on the nod. But if serious difficulties or questions of policy are raised about those regulations, I think it is right for the Minister to be questioned and to give his answers, and for the House to decide ultimately. Those are some of the main issues involved.

I hope that we can look broadly at the policy for financing the fee-raising arrangement and see how far the Minister wishes to have this enormous flexibility. In particular, if the Minister uses the power to introduce payment by instalments to the hilt—one might say licensing on the "never-never"—it should, on the first occasion be examined in a way that gives an opportunity for parliamentary challenge.

My hon. Friend the Member for JPEG 1487 Newcastle upon Tyne, Central (Mr. Cowans) has raised one difficulty with the system of charging which the Minister did not anticipate. I read the clause a few times, and I did not anticipate it. Further difficulties may come to light if such a detailed instrument, covering such a range of charges, is laid before the House subject to affirmation. If that happens, it will be all to the good. The Opposition are not arguing that all legislation should be perfect, that there should be no enabling powers or that the legislation must define precisely every legal requirement.

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Transport (Mr. Kenneth Clarke)
: These are detailed amendments about the fees system. If any hon. Member has general points to make about fees they are probably best left until the clause stand part debate.

Amendment No. 262 seeks to impose a statutory obligation on the Minister to set the level of fees at a level that will

"in general seek to recoup the approximate cost involved in providing the services."

I accept that this is a probing amendment, but it is vague in its wording and would be vague in its effect. It is also not entirely clear whether the right hon. Member for Barrow-in-Furness (Mr. Booth) is suggesting that each fee should seek to recoup the cost of providing the service for that particular form of licence, or whether the fee system as a whole should seek to recoup the whole cost. However, the amendment gives me an opportunity to give a general description of what we have to do.

All the fees will have to be reassessed in the light of the changes that the Bill makes. It may help if I explain the basis on which the present fees system operates. We do not intend to move too far from that. Fees are at present fixed at a level which fully recoups the cost of the system, including 1488 the salaries of enforcement staff and headquarters costs. But there is considerable cross-subsidisation from one service to another. For example, the cost of road service licensing and enforcement is far from covered by the fees charged for that part of the system. PSV driving tests are provided free. Both those services are cross-subsidised from the fees which are receifed from the public service vehicle licence. The system as a whole is entirely covered by the fees raised for various types of licence, but there is substantial cross-subsidisation within the system. Given that we shall no longer have PSV licensing, which is the main source of revenue, we shall have to reassess the whole system, including the balance.

The main changes will be that PSV licences will go, and fewer services of all kinds will require road service licences. There will be no conductor licensing because we agreed in Committee this morning to abolish it. The duration of various licences will be extended. We are taking power to charge for PSV driving tests. Operator licensing will come in, and we shall have to look to that as a source of revenue to replace PSV licensing.

We propose—and I hope that the Committee agrees that it is the right way to proceed—to consult as widely as possible on a new fee schedule that the Department is in the process of drawing up. I doubt whether, in the end, we shall go for a system where we try to recover the exact cost of each type of licence. I anticipate that we shall seek to recover the costs of the system from those applying for and obtaining the licences. We hope to aim at rough equity at least in the differing charges between the size of operator and the particular benefit he receives from the licence for which he is applying.

This brings me to amendment No. 263 which seeks to limit the broad d'scretion in clause 30 by ensuring that we fix a fee for a PSV operator's licence in proportion to the maximum number of vehicles it allows to be operated. I agree entirely with the broad aim of the amendment. It seems fair that in applying for an operator's licence, the operator with a large number of vehicles should be expected to pay a much larger fee overall than the operator who has only one or two vehicles. That would be reproducing one of the present features of the PSV licence. Whether the amendment is the right way of achieving that is somewhat doubtful. JPEG 1489 The snag with the amendment is that it would tie us in each individual case to levy fees for operators' licences exactly in proportion to the number of vehicles in that licence. It might be administratively simpler to have differing levels of fee for differing sizes of operation. Thus the fees would go up in steps. I am not yet familiar with the work being done on this matter, but there might be a particular level of fee of people with from one to 10 vehicles, another for 10 to 20 vehicles, and so on.

The Bill as drafted gives us the necessary discretion to vary the operator's licence fee according to the size of the operation. That is the way we intend to use the discretion, but we should leave more flexibility than the amendment would leave to the eventual regulations that would draft the schedule of fees.

The third amendment—No. 264—seeks to require that when finally we produce a schedule of fees, following full consultation, it should be subject to the affirmative resolution procedure requiring debate on the Floor of the House in each case. The present position was rightly described by the right hon. Member and is retained in the Bill. The regulation-making power will be subject to section 260 of the 1960 Act. That Act is now over 19 years old and its provisions are such that all regulations made under it follow the negative resolution procedure. That means that they are still debatable by the House and can be annulled by a vote of Parliament. But it is necessary for a prayer to be tabled and for time to be given before that debate can take place.

Some regulations are of such importance that it is right that the main legislation should make it essential to have an affirmative vote in each case. But I do not think that our parliamentary colleagues would thank us if we put the schedule of fees for vehicle licensing into such a category. If the amendment were carried, it would mean that every time there was any variation in the fees level for any kind of vehicle licence we would have to take parliamentary time to debate it. In practice it might be a formality because such orders might go through on the nod late at night and cause little interference. But we do not, on the whole, litter up the Order Paper with that kind of regulation.

I think that—given inflation and given the need to respond to representations from the industry or to changed cir- 1490 cumstances as the licensing system evolves—it is better to leave the situation flexible. Regulations should be tabled as statutory instruments subject to the negative resolution procedure so that only those that are most controversial can be brought before the House and annulled if they are not able to command a parliamentary majority.

That is my response to the three amendments. I do not think there is much difference between what the right hon. Gentleman wants and what we aim at. We intend that there will be different levels of fee for operations of different sizes. We intend that the total amount recovered as fees should cover the cost of the service, so that no charge on public funds will arise. We think that Parliament's rights will be adequately protected by the retention of the negative resolution procedure which has been followed for all regulations under the 1960 Act. I hope that I have made our intentions clearer and that the right hon. Gentleman will now feel able to withdraw the amendment.
