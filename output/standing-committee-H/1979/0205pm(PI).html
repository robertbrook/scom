It became clear in our debate this morning that the local authority, as defined for the purpose of this part of the Bill, is the county council. Some district councils might object to not being included in the definition.

If such consultation as is proposed by the amendment is brought into being, I think it possible, or at least conceivable, that the local authority concerned might reach agreement with the district councils and the other bodies listed in the schedule before the application is made to the Minister. That would save considerable time and effort for the Minister in what he determines. If, as he has indicated today, he wishes to select only two or three trial areas and he has about 12 proposals before him, I suggest the easiest way to discharge his otherwise difficult duties is to accept the amendment now. He will be able to look through the applications for 972 designation and pick out the two or three which show, as a result of the amendment, that agreement has been reached.

I appreciate that, as the Minister said, even if the amendment were not carried, it would still be possible for those other bodies or authorities to make representations about a proposal by the county authority. If they did that, the contents of those representations would have to be relayed to the Minister when he was considering whether to make a designation order.

That is a very poor substitute for an agreement. It means that the Minister would be sitting in judgment on whether the objectors or the county authority were right, as an alternative to the proposition in the amendment which would enable them, on discussing their objectives and difficulties with one another, to reach agreement.

I accept that this might take some time. That was one of the arguments advanced this morning. Effective and constructive consultation takes time. A hard and realistic proposition is being put forward. Once the designation area is made it would be two or three years before the decision could be revoked. A great deal turns on that decision and the evidence we have is that if services collapse in an area it is very hard to restore them. Therefore, I urge the Committee to accept that this is not a wrecking amendment, or even a fiercely or strongly probing amendment. It is a serious amendment which is meant to give the Minister's proposition about trial areas a greater chance of success. Failing that it would at least ensure that the proposition operates in those areas where those concerned agree that the attempt should be JPEG 973 made. We are all well aware that resources in these spheres are likely to be subjected to pressure for reduction, and the ready way to reduce expenditure, both in money and manpower, is to extend the period by a month here and a month there, until the present arrangements are severely eroded. Provision for a regular 12-monthly inspection can only be of benefit to the public.

Many amendments have been suggested by the various transport undertakings, and organisations representing them, some could be interpreted as being in the interests of the transport organisations, and possibly some of them as not so much in the interests of the travelling public. However, this new clause is one proposal which the organisations representing the public passenger transport industry strongly support. In those circumstances we should encourage them in this effort to improve safety.
