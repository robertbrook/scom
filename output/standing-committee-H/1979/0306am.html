Mr. Booth
: I hope that it will be helpful if I indicate the background considerations which apply to amendment No. 341 and, to a limited extent, to amendment No. 342. In a sense, they are probing amendments and arise from our inability to discern with exactitude the purpose of the clause. It appears to attempt to keep the law the same by changing the words in the statute. That is to say, it takes account solely of the changes in definition of public services and public service vehicles in clause 3, and recognises that, given that the meaning in law of contract carriage has been changed—as it will be when the Bill has been passed—it will no longer be possible to define eligibility for fuel duty rebate and bus grant in the same way. Therefore, even if it were the Government's intention to retain eligibility for fuel duty rebate and bus grant there would have to be some consequential changes as a result of the passing of part I. However, that only raises the question of whether the Government found, in looking at the new definitions created by clause 3, that it was no longer possible to have the same legal definition of eligibility, or whether it was their intention to narrow the range of eligibility for fuel duty rebate and for bus grants.

I believe that the clause has the latter 2090 effect. The current eligibility is related mainly to the stage carriage services and buses running in them, and the definition of those services is to be found in section 117 of the Road Traffic Act 1960. That definition is different from the definition of "stage carriage" in part I of the Bill. The definition, inasmuch as it bears on the amendments, requires a service to be provided

"at separate fares none of which is less than 21p".

I do not comment on the merits or demerits of the definition, but I insist that, since that definition differs so much from the present approach, one cannot just substitute one definition for the other and leave the same eligibility for fuel duty rebate and bus grant.

Under the definition in section 117 the distance covered by the vehicles in the course of a stage carriage service has no bearing on eligibility, whereas in the definition in part I the dividing line between express carriage and contract carriage—following our amendment to the definition of express carriage—is based on the distance any one passenger is carried rather than the distance between stops. Even that distance is further qualified by the maximum distance the passenger is from the point at which he is picked up or put down in the course of the journey. Therefore, this complex definition bears on the latter part of the legislation.

It is clear that the total distance run by the service has no bearing on existing qualification, now that the separation of contract and express carriage, and this peculiar definition, have a bearing.

The other difference as I understand it—I would like the Parliamentary Secretary's advice on this—is that the present eligibility for fuel duty rebate relates solely to the stage carriage. Therefore, only stage carriage services receive refunds on fuel duty. But that is not so with bus grants. They are now to be paid on buses which will be used wholly or mainly for stage carriage services. Therefore, the clause raises the question of exclusions.

One other line of eligibility is affected. Currently there is eligibility for the fuel duty rebate to employers providing bus services at low fares for their workers. That does not appear to be covered by the definitions in clause 51.

The other issue that clearly arises is tours and excursions which are not, so far JPEG 2091 as I can discover, eligibility under the present law. I understand that because of the 21p fare limit the contention is that there is no need to write in an exclusion since it is likely in practice to exclude all tours and excursions. However, I cannot see how the mechanics of the clause were arrived at. It refers to

"a service as regards which the condition specified in section 3(3)( a) of the Transport Act 1980 — is satisfied".

Had section 3(3)( b) been referred to tours and excursions would have been excluded. They are mentioned separately in the definition of "bus service" in the clause.

There is also the reference to clause 3(3)( a) in respect of the express service exclusion. The two are not picked up in the way that clause 3 leads one to expect. That complex consideration of how eligibility is now divided, and the extent to which the clause appears to limit it brings me to the purpose of amendment No. 183.

The amendment is intended to preserve eligibility for buses provided by employers to carry workers to and from work. I can see nothing in current public transport developments to justify the Government's proposal to remove that eligibility. I see no evidence that public transport services are expanding so rapidly that employers will no longer need to lay on such buses. There is no reason to suppose that in future such provision will be any less expensive than it has been in the past. On the contrary, the indications are that it will be more expensive. In view of that combination of considerations I cannot see why the Government are removing eligibility at this stage. On the basis of my constituency experience I would argue that employers lay on such services only when public transport is inadequate to fill the bill.

To some extent, therefore, the proposal threatens one of two undesirable consequences—possibly both. One is that employers' costs will go up as a direct result of fuel duty rebate being withdrawn. The other is that they will be forced to pass on the additional costs to employees. In many areas the issue of employees' fares is sensitive enough already without changes in fuel duty rebate eligibility making matters worse. I hope that the Parliamentary Secretary will explain to us the thought behind the 2092 exclusion.

A further complexity arises from the widening definition of contract carriage. In Committee, though not on Second Reading, the Government have made clear their intention greatly to widen the scope of contract carriage. They intend it to be possible to run a regular service between fixed points every day as part of a normal contract carriage arrangement. That is a completely new concept in our law. It led me to assume that there would be an option for such services to be provided on a contract carriage basis. But if that is so the Government must tell us whether their intention in so doing is to reduce the proportion of bus passengers eligible for bus and fuel grants. Is their intention that anyone setting up a contract arrangement for the provision of buses to take workers to work—or for any other regular service—should be denied the fuel grant that has been available to them until now? If so, they should say so, and explain why.

A much wider issue is raised by amendment No. 341. The Opposition feel that here is an opportunity to bring much more rhyme, reason and sense, as well as a degree of local democratic control, to the question of eligibility for bus grants and fuel duty rebate.

As the law stands, statutory duties are laid upon local authorities to promote good, efficient, co-ordinated passenger transport networks within their areas. The degree of obligation varies according to the area, but I shall not go into that as we discussed it earlier in our proceedings. In some areas, a very precise duty is laid upon the authority. In other areas, the law indicates that it should aim to achieve that.

In either case, the Opposition believe that if a local authority in pursuit of that duty decides that it is desirable to sustain a total network of services which, individually or as part of a network agreement, it is prepared to bring within the transport supplementary grant payment system, none of those services should be excluded either from the bus grant or from the fuel duty rebate. That is our underlying purpose.

At present, in a number of areas, a very delicate balance exists which could be seriously disturbed by any withdrawal of fuel duty rebate or bus grants. There are JPEG 2093 district councils currently running urban services without the aid of transport supplementary grant because the county will not provide it. However, it is not uncommon in such cases for the county authority to reach an agreement whereby transport supplementary grant is provided for services on the periphery of the urban area but within the area of the district council.

If we are respecters of local democracy, as Opposition Members certainly are, we must regard the elected local authority members as being in the best position to judge what is an adequate public transport network in their area. This argument affects rail as well as road services, although the amendment deals only with the road transport aspect. If that duty is laid upon the authorities and they decide that it is proper to use part of their ratepayers" money and that part of taxpayers' money that is made available for transport supplementary grant to support services, it would be an absolute nonsense if any one of those services were disqualified from the bus grant or the fuel duty rebate. It would become no more than a book keeping exercise, as the money would be taken away with one hand and paid back with the other, and it would devalue that element of transport supplementary grant.

I appreciate that this touches upon the question of works buses, which one would not expect to be directly affected by the transport planning and policy arrangement. However, if I am right in saying that in some areas the only reason for laying on works buses is that there is no public transport network to provide for the workers, there must be a relationship between the two. I remember discussing with workers at Millom, when the steelworks there was closed down, the possibility of their obtaining jobs in Barrow or Ulverston. The first question that they raised was how they could get there, because there was no adequate public transport service. I was forced to go to the transport authorities to ask whether they could lay on additional services for a specific employment requirement arising from the closing of the steelworks. They were not in a position immediately to respond. One result of that was that Lister's textile works in Barrow actually laid on a bus service from Millom to get unemployed steel workers to come and work in Barrow.

That could happen in other areas. We 2094 see possibilities, unfortunately, not just of steelworks closing down, but of other firms collapsing and demands being placed upon public transport services which they will be unable to meet unless the local authorities that operate TSG and have the responsibility for transport planning and policy can bring the advantages of TSG to bear without any fear of the service costs being raised by withdrawal of fuel duty rebate. It is a simple concept but, one that I believe embraces local democracy in a way which I hope members of the Committee will support. In that spirit, I commend the amendment.

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Transport

(Mr. Kenneth Clarke)
: The right hon. Member for Barrow-in-Furness (Mr. Booth) was correct in his initial comment that the clause arises out of the redefinition of stage carriage services that we debated on clause 3. Because stage carriage is the basis for eligibility both for new bus grant and fuel duty rebate, it is necessary to bring the eligibility in line with the new definition in the Bill. That is essentially what clause 3 seeks to do.

We had to decide which categories of service ought to be eligible for fuel duty rebate and new bus grants, assuming, as 2096 has always been the case, that the same definition is used for both, as proposed here, and how best to bring that category of service within the definitions that we are now using in the Bill. The best starting point, in order to consider the amendments and decide whether the criteria are too tight, might be to examine the policy that lies behind the new bus grant and fuel duty rebate.

The new bus grant was always intended to be a grant towards capital investment in new buses for a temporary period in order to facilitate, in particular, one-man operation of buses. Clearly, the intention and policy of the grant, on which the Government's approach is consistent with that of the previous Government, was to increase efficiency on regular stopping services where a bus conductor would in the past have been employed to collect fares, and where it is now desirable, in the interests of economy and efficiency, to go over to one man operation. That gives a clear indication of what kind of service we ought to have in mind as being eligible for new bus grant when considering the definitions in clause 51.

Fuel duty rebate is a non-controversial measure, and everyone agrees that it should be given to a particular category of passenger bus services. It is aimed at local stopping services where a regular public service exists. We all agree that it is in the public interest to give a reduction in its costs by rebating its fuel duty in full.

That is the present policy. It is more or less the policy that we have tried to reproduce in the Bill. But there are one or two anomalies. One is the position of works services.

There is no intention on the Government's part to discourage the provision of works services. I agree entirely with the hon. Member for Holborn and St. Pancras, South (Mr. Dobson) that many employers find it desirable to take employees to their place of work. They are usually bitterly opposed by the network passenger transport operators. Often I see contested cases which come in by way of appeal when road services licences have been refused to works operators. Where clearly employees have wanted such a service which could not otherwise have been provided, we have been able to encourage and facilitate the provision of one. There are many JPEG 2097 throughout the country and it is unwise to consider reducing them now.

The present position on fuel duty rebate is anomalous. Works services fall into two categories and will continue to do so under the Bill as it stands. Some are contract carriage and some are stage carriage. As the law stands, if they are regular services, they cannot be contract carriage if they charge the employees one-third—even if the fare is heavily subsidised by the employer.

So the present position is that, normally, where employees pay a fare, whether a full fare or a contribution towards the cost of providing the works bus, it usually operates under a road service licence. Where the employer bears the full cost and the employees are charged nothing, it operates as a contract carriage.

It is anomalous that, at present, some works services qualify for fuel rebate and new bus grant and others do not. I should like to be able to give the Committee an estimate of which is the more numerous, but we have not done a proper survey and are therefore not sure. Those more experienced than me in this field suspect that there are probably more contract carriage than stage carriage. But there is certainly an anomaly which is difficult to justify.

When considering which way to go. Reverting to what I said earlier about the underlying policy that led successive Governments to continue new bus grants on a temporary basis and contemplate fuel duty rebate as a permanent feature of passenger transport, I do not think that works buses that were closed to the general public were actually the sort of services that anyone had in mind when they advocated either new bus grant or fuel duty rebate. As regards new bus grant, the works bus is not the kind that usually has a conductor. The introduction of one man operation to works services was not the intention when bus grant was made available. Fuel duty rebate was aimed at the regular local service. It is anomalous that only some works services get the rebate at present.

We sought to avoid that by putting in the definition—it is the only part of the definition about which anyone was seriously concerned, and no one objected to the exclusion of excursions and tours—that in order to be eligible for the grant and the rebate, the service should be a stage carriage service available to the general public. That will make clear that 2098 it is the kind of local transport operation which ought properly to come within the definition.

Two further points are relevant. I accept that the results of what we are doing will mean that some works services which at the moment get fuel duty rebate, and could conceivably be eligible for new bus grant, will lose the rebate, which is the more important. Operators will then have to decide what to do.

I suggest that where a works service is being provided on a stage carriage basis, usually it is confined to being a works service only because of objections from other operators to its having any kind of general road service licence. Stage carriage services normally exist because of the impossibility of overriding the objections of other operators to unrestricted road service licences in cases that go before the traffic commissioners.

I think that when most of the operators of works services face up to the implications of what will be the Transport Act 1980, they will contemplate using the new provisions of the Bill to go before the commissioners and enlarge the service that they wish to provide, primarily for the benefit of their employees, in order to make it available to the general public. They might consider that as a way of getting over the difficulty.

Under the proposed criteria of the Bill, I think that they would find it much easier to obtain a licence, and where they get such a licence the effect will be to enlarge the service available to the general public, going to and from work particularly at peak travel hours.

A possible side-effect—albeit a minor one—of what we are doing in clause 51 is that some operators of works services on stage carriage licences will decide that they had better get a road service licence to enable them to take fare-paying members of the general public on their services, making them more widely available. The Government's reaction to that is that it may be highly beneficial and result in an enlargement of service to the public.

A second, and more subordinate point than the main policy lines that I have argued—and it will, of course, be argued that all we are doing here is saving money, which is true of some services—is that the effect of clause 51 will be rather neutral. One effect of our redefinition of stage carriage in this Bill, as compared with previous definitions, will be to enlarge to JPEG 2099 a modest extent the number of present services that will be defined as stage carriage compared with those defined as stage carriage under previous legislation. To that extent, there will be a somewhat increased liability on public funds arising out of that enlargement. It will be some small compensation, and will probably in the end result in a more or less neutral effect. Some works services which at the moment get into the definition of stage carriage and therefore get the rebate will, because of the anomalous effects of present legislation, be excluded.

The overall effect of clause 51 is not to save large amounts of money to the Treasury or anything like that. I do not have precise figures, but I think that, on balance, what the Treasury gains on the swings it will lose on the roundabouts as a result of our definitions in the Bill.

That is certainly the effect with new bus grant, where there are no financial implications in what we are doing and where there is a cash limit at present. As regards fuel duty rebate, the effect of the Bill as a whole, on services as a whole, will be neutral.
