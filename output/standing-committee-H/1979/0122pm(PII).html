Mr. Prescott
: I beg to move amendment No. 32, in page 3, line 20, at end insert

'but shall not include any elements of profit'.

The amendment is clear, and concerns a matter which the Committee has frequently debated. When considering what the aggregate amount of fares payment will cover, we note that the clause says:

"the running costs of a vehicle for a journey shall be taken to include an appropriate amount in respect of depreciation and general wear."

While many Opposition Members feel that there can be wide interpretation of what costs should be included in depreciation and general wear, the amendment seeks to make clear the Government's stated position, that is, the total amount of money given to the driver by car-sharing passengers must not allow for any profit.

During the course of our debates it has become clear that although a surplus over and above normal charges may mean a profit—that is, more money than the actual costs involved—it may not necessarily mean, as I understand the matter and as courts have decided in certain cases, that by making a profit one is involved in a business. Therefore we are in some difficulty. To my simple mind, "profit"—which is a surplus over what are, in this instance, legitimate costs —always means a financial gain.

The argument seems now to have been refined to say that one can make a profit so long as that is not done regularly. When there is a similar route, that may be considered as a contributing factor to the definition of gain. Not being a lawyer, I do not know a great deal about case law—except for a reference in an earlier 398 speech, and I am not sure where I picked that up.

However, I believe that courts, in defining hire and reward, have made a great many judgments that appear to have confirmed the point that the simple matter of profit—that is, a surplus over costs—does not necessarily mean that a business is being carried on. The amendment was drafted with the argument very much in mind that profit meant that a man was in business. Bearing that in mind, from the interpretations that have been made in our debates, the amendment is not entirely correct in seeking to strike out what we consider is the motive for conducting the business—namely, the securing of a profit.

The Government have told us that they still believe that anyone making charges—and we have been given indications of what may be reasonable charges—will be allowed to have them determined by magistrates' courts. They will decide whether a surplus that is made over and above what may be considered reasonable costs—that is for depreciation, general wear, petrol, and so on— constitute a profit and whether such profitable elements were sufficient to contribute to the definition of a business, which the Bill is designed to prevent occurring in respect of car sharing. We have the difficulty that, our amendment makes the principle clear in general but it does not state too clearly what is meant by the definition of profit. As I understand the matter, both sides of the Committee have agreed that we want to make clear to all those involved in car sharing that, while it may be possible to charge passengers an amount equal to the costs, it is not our intention that a profit should be made from the arrangement.

Since the Bill's introduction, a great deal of time has been spent on explaining its purposes. There was some difference between us over whether Part I could be amended to include the Bill's purposes. The point was made that it would serve as an example to those who were seeking to make a judgment about what was intended in the various clauses of the Bill. Part of the argument was that those involved in making such a judgment could look at Part I for guidance.

It might be interesting for the Government to consider—if not now, perhaps on Report—making a declaration in the Bill that there is no intention that those JPEG 399 involved in car sharing should make a profit. As there is flexibility in the definition of profit, it does not necessarily mean that someone will be committing an offence if he makes a surplus in certain circumstances. It should be made clear that the Government do not intend that those sharing their cars and carrying passengers, and those who charge separate fares, should not make a profit.

The courts would then have sufficient grounds to define whether such a surplus was sufficient for them to make a judgment about profit, and whether there existed other factors that would contribute to defining whether a business was being carried. I refer to such things as whether the activity was regular or irregular, or a one-off occurrence, and whether all those matters are involved that will determine what is legitimate hire and reward.

The amendment boils down to the Opposition giving the Government an opportunity, without tying matters down as firmly as we thought we had done when drafting it, to say that profit would mean a surplus. The Government were led to reconsider the matter of directions, which we discussed on earlier amendments. Therefore, we hope that the Government, even though they may not be able to adopt our amendment, will take an opportunity later to declare in the Bill that people should not make a profit as a result of offering to share their cars and by charging, as allowed in the Bill.

: This is an amendment which, in many ways, I should like to accept. What has been said by the hon. Member for Barrow-in-Furness (Mr. Booth) and the hon. We have to decide whether the inclusion of a specific reference in the terms to "elements of profit" would add anything to the Bill or whether it would introduce fresh complications. It appears that the addition of the phrase would add unnecessary complications without giving any advantage over the present wording. The difficulty arises because "profit" is an uncertain word. It means different things to different people. We all understand it JPEG 401 to mean the surplus of revenue of some kind over costs of some kind. But in different circumstances it could be regarded in different ways, and historically the courts have done so.

This can best be illustrated by reference to a volume entitled "Words and Phrases Legally Defined", to which lawyers occasionally resort as an aid to the interpretation of statutes. The book includes a section on profit. Pages 189 and 190 are entirely taken up with conflicting authorities about the meaning of the word profit when applied to a given legal situation. Someone complained about not having sufficient authorities, so I quote an 1895 authority. This is Bishop v. Smyrna and Cassaba Railway Company (1895) 2 Chancery Division, 265:

"the word 'profits' like many other words in the English language, and even some of a technical character, is capable of more than one meaning, and it is often, and properly, used in more than one sense".

It goes on from there. There is also a case from 1911 concerning the Spanish Prospecting Company—(1911) 1 Chancery Division, 92—during which Lord Justice Fletcher Moulton launched into the optimistic words:

"the word 'profits' has in my opinion a well-defined legal meaning, and this meaning coincides with the fundamental conception of profits in general parlance".

Unfortunately he goes on to give a definition of profits which is not the same as that given in any of the other authorities on the two pages. It does not seem that he successfully resolved the problem thereafter, because the last authority quoted here is dated 1946. The word has given rise to endless difficulty.

I do not wish to give rise to further debate, because one could start exchanging examples. However, quite apart from the history of difficulty in defining the word profit, it is conceivable that in the context of car sharing there could be real doubt about what is a profit. For instance, to return to the right hon. Gentleman's last amendment, if I have to make a journey at a certain time of day from my home to a certain destination, it could be argued that any contribution that I receive from a passenger is in a sense a profit. I shall incur all the expense of the journey in any event. Anything the passenger contributes therefore saves me money. Had we accepted the right hon. Gentleman's pre- 402 vious amendment, we should have been in considerable difficulty in deciding what profit was represented in that context.

So, introducing a reference to the word "profit" although, if we had a long enough debate the two sides of the Committee would probably decide on what they agreed was "profit"—will probably introduce confusion in the courts. I think we should ask ourselves "Do the words of the Bill adequately cover the situation that we want to cover"? What we have provided in the Bill is a measure, so far, that the total paid to the driver should not exceed the amount of the running costs of the vehicle for the journey, the running costs being taken to include:

"an appropriate amount in respect of depreciation and general wear."

That means that on a particular journey, whether a driver has to make it or not, he will not make a surplus, vis-a-vis the overall running costs.

It seems to me that that is reasonably clear. To introduce profit as well is merely to give an alternative argument for someone arguing before a magistrates' court to latch on to, to try to say that what he was making did not amount to profit, or for a prosecutor to argue that it did amount to a profit. What we have here is more certain. It will be easier for a court to work upon than would be the case if we introduced the word "profit", as suggested by the Opposition.

We are trying to exclude people running a business. We do not want anybody to make money out of it, and we are seeking to protect those who do not make money out of it. What we have in the Bill will achieve that object more successfully than the amendment.

Friends as to what is our philosophy on car-sharing. It is a fair question. I believe that it is necessary to define a philosophy of car-sharing in the context of this legislation.

I answer that question by saying that I believe that the purpose of a good law in the context of car sharing should be to recognise that the role of car sharing is to supplement rather than to substitute for public transport. If any law were to be based upon the proposition that car sharing should substitute for public transport, it would be detrimental to the transport interests of the people of this country.

I think that Labour Members realise possibly more than Conservative Members the wide range of demand for public transport in our community. What may fit one area of the country may not necessarily fit another, and some of the broad generalisations that are thrown about when one debates the advantages of car sharing may be an absolute nonsense when applied in some areas.

For example, the Minister's claims about the savings of public transport, the saving of frustration and the saving of public transport difficulties which will flow from wider car sharing makes nonsense in the context of the Metropolis. I am not particularly worried about the Metropolis. I would sacrifice some inconvenience to the Metropolis for a better public service facility throughout the country. In this I might be at odds with my hon. Friend the Member for Holborn and St. Pancras (Mr. Dobson), for understandable JPEG 447 reasons.

In the context of the Metropolis, where about 90 per cent of the people come to work by public transport, even if one doubled the number of people travelling by private cars because of sharing arrangements, one would still have a Metropolis which was overwhelmingly dependent upon efficient public transport services for its operation. In that context, car sharing cannot in any circumstances that we can conceive avoid the major problems involved in sustaining and improving the public transport system.

If I might abbreviate my arguments about this application because of the lateness of the hour, I should say simply this. The only circumstances in which I can conceive of a car sharing arrangement in any way easing a public transport system is in those few areas where it might be possible to organise car sharing for travel to work in peak hour times—or at any other times of peak demand on transport—in such a way that the public transport system can run with fewer vehicles. In many parts of the country the public transport service has to determine the number of vehicles it requires to run by the peak hour demand. Therefore, if one could have a carefully organised transport system which lifted off a bit of the peak, in those few places it might be possible to have a more economical public transport system. But that is not the aim of the car sharing provisions of the Bill, as was made very clear when our amendment to restrict journeys on an even wider basis was substantially rejected by the Government.

Therefore, it is a matter of philosophical difference in part. But underlying that difference there is a much more important argument, which we shall probably continue beyond the Bill and into many public transport debates in the House, the argument on what the approach of Government should be to public transport —whether it should be seen as the role of Government to sustain public transport on the basis of meeting not only majority but minority needs throughout the community.

Against all the expectations of myself and a number of others, it may become possible for more people to operate private cars in the immediate future. I do not think that it will. I think that the pointers are towards fewer people. The total cost to the community and to the individual of 448 running a private car may rise to the point where the true cost is more than half the average take-home pay, in which a new demographic trend will be towards people choosing to travel in other ways and not to have a private car, to spend their money in other ways. That is more likely to be the case.

Even if I am completely wrong and the time comes when the majority have access to a private car, there will still be a great advantage to our community if we can have a really efficient public transport service. If we can have that and develop our public transport service, we shall not only provide for the needs of those who do not have access to cars but we shall provide a valuable alternative to those who have cars but who choose at other times to use public transport services.

That is the view that I take and my philosophy in my approach to the relationship between car sharing and public transport. It leads me, I regret to say, to wish to oppose the clause as not measuring up in any way to the real problems of that relationship in the present age or in any foreseeable future.
