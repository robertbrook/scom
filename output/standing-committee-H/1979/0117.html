Mr. John Prescott: The clause is apparently designed, according to the Minister's statements on the Floor of the House and in Committee, to capture the potential of car sharing and, using the emotive phrase, set the motorist free.

This is a major issue and we intend to spend a little time developing the argument on this principle. Indeed, the Minister has made clear that this is something for which he has always campaigned and it may well become known as the Fowler clause— probably becoming as infamous as the name Beeching in relation to the railway system.

Therefore, we intend to deploy arguments that reflect not only the concern of the Opposition in this matter but, as we understand it, both in evidence given to the Department and correspondence that we have seen, of private hire car and taxi drivers.

It is clear from our debates on subsections (1) and (2), taken with schedule 1, that the motorist sharing his car will now be able to accept payment from separate fare paying passengers travelling with him and his car will not be regarded as a public service vehicle. Therefore, all the normal conditions, controls and safeguards associated with a vehicle so defined will no longer apply. Indeed, subsection (1) further reduces the coverage of the definition of a public service vehicle.

The amendment seeks to restrict the number of passengers who may travel in the car at any one time on one journey— namely, to delete "one or more" and insert "not more than five". That will change the figure of nine passengers— eight passengers and the driver—as the limit for a car involved in picking up passengers and charging a fee, to no more Col. 240 than five, which effectively will mean six— five passengers and the driver.

I now deal with the numbers argument which began to develop at the end of our previous sitting. The Minister said that it was no major step to go from seven to eight. Indeed, we have proposed the figure of five, not the figure of seven contained in the Labour Government's legislation on car sharing. There are good reasons for that and I hope to bring out the difference between the approach of the Opposition to the car sharing principle, as reflected in the numbers proposed in the amendment, and that of the Government.

It was said that the increase proposed in the Bill was no major leap. That may not appear to the Government to be a major leap for mankind, but we know that legislation often determines the design of cars, in some cases directly and in others indirectly. Legislation on the number of car seats is another example of where the design of cars can be affected.

I am more conversant with the shipping industry—where a similar problem has developed with legislation, leading to what is known in that industry as the "paragraph" ships. These ships are designed to avoid being covered by a subsection in a specific piece of legislation. Cars are not necessarily free from that either, although they may not necessarily be known as "paragraph" cars.

An obvious example was vans that were converted to shooting brakes simply by having windows put in their sides. That meant that less tax was paid by the purchaser, although eventually legislation was brought in to change the practice. That is only one example, and most of us know of others.
