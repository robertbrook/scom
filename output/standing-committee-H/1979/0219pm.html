Mr. Ken Weetch: I beg to move amendment No. 304, in page 66, line 27, at end add:

"The obligation of the Corporation transferred by virtue of section 36 include the obligation of the Corporation set out in section 1 of the Transport Act 1968

  1. to provide or secure or promote the provision of properly integrated services for the carriage of goods within Great Britain by road and rail;
  2. to secure that in the provisions of those services, goods are carried by rail whenever such carriage is efficient and economic; and together with the British Railways Board to co-operate with regard to the matters referred to in subsection (i)."

While you have been coming here, Mr. English, the Committee has been waiting with breathless expectation for me to move the amendment.

It is couched in narrow terms, but contains issues of principle that could be debated very widely. Specifically contained in the amendment is the principle of freight integration. Later in the Bill we shall debate that and refer to the Freight Integration Council—or to its demise. Therefore I do not think that it would be profitable at this stage to enter into a long debate on that subject. This is the wrong time and the wrong place in the Bill to do that.

Nevertheless, to the Opposition freight integration of road and rail is a very important principle, as is that section of the Transport Act 1968 which referred to it in the context of the National Freight Corporation. In trying to transfer that principle to the successor company, we should ensure that it should have regard to Col. 1702 road and rail integration of freight traffic.

"Integration" is probably the most vague, often the most abused and misunderstood word in the transport vocabulary. I have been struggling to discover what an integrated transport policy is, because interpretation differs with each person to whom I speak. Since 1976, when the Department of Transport was created—with my right hon. Friend the Member for Stockton (Mr. Rodgers) as the Secretary of State—I have listened to practically every speech in the House about an integrated transport system. I do not know how I have survived all those speeches, but I have done so and shall tell the Committee my tale.

The best approach to freight integration is the interpretation in the amendment. There is a functional definition of the principle of integration.

Integration is associated with the function of various modes of transport; with what they do at present and have done in the past. I should like to refer the Committee to paragraph 8 (3) (ii) of schedule 6 as proposed in our amendment, which refers to an obligation:

"to secure that in the provisions of those services, goods are carried by rail whenever such carriage is efficient and economic".

Those words "efficient and economic" are important.

Lest it be thought that the Opposition are trying to saddle the successor company with a social obligation that has no economic meaning, nor one in terms of cost and efficiency and the proper commercial running of the business, I should like to stress the words in the amendment—"efficient and economic".

The Opposition set great store by integration for its own sake. That is, in social terms, we wish to get a lot of heavy traffic off the roads and onto the railways. We do not want to do that if at the end of the day the policy is so grossly uneconomic that it means impairing the efficiency of the whole transport network. That is not what we seek.

In its narrow terms the amendment proposes to put an obligation on the successor company to consider rail freight as an important part of an integrated service that could be provided. We are completely confident that the use of rail for freight can be economic and efficient. Close examination of the amendment shows that it stresses commercial and Image Col. 1703 economic matters. The Opposition appreciate that if the new successor company is saddled with a social obligation difficult problems will arise. Such obligations must be funded by Government.

There is a strong line of argument that although a private company based on commercial principles should take social costs into account that is something which properly lies within the perspective of central Government, and not the narrow confines of a commercial company that is operating for profit. As my right hon. and hon. Friends will emphasise in the course of the debate on the amendment, it has been couched deliberately to emphasise the phrase "efficient and economic".

In our debate on amendment No. 303 the Parliamentary Secretary said that he found the prose rather turgid and pedestrian. Nevertheless, I commend the wording of this amendment, which is succinct, clear and states simply and exactly what we wish it to do.

It should be noted that we do not seek to create any administrative superstructure for the transfer of freight from road to rail in the context of the new company. I am certain that the amendment will be resisted by the Government on the ground that when the successor company comes into being it will be necessary to give the new board the power and flexibility to make its own commercial decisions.

I can anticipate the Government's arguing that a successor company cannot be tied down in respect of what it regards as a commercial decision between road or rail, or integrated services. I do not doubt that the Government will say, "We shall have a new board for the successor company which will take decisions that are commercially profitable and viable. If those include an integrated service, that will be adopted as part of the company's commercial policy."

I have some doubts about that line of argument. At present, it is too readily assumed by many transport operators that railway transport is uneconomic when they have not undertaken a full investigation of what railways can offer in carrying freight.

I commend the amendment to the Committee. We place a high priority on this principle.

Receipts from British Rail parcels traffic last year totalled £119 million, and of this £42.5 million, which is 35 per cent., came from collected and delivered traffic. Total British Rail receipts for freight and parcels in 1978 were £493.9 million.

To put British Rail's parcels business in perspective, about 69 per cent. of the total market of 1,700 million packages a year is handled by own-account operators, NFC's share is 5 per cent. and the proportion taken by British Rail is only 3 per cent. The railways board is pessimistic about retaining this share, a view not shared by the unions involved that tend to be less Image Col. 1705 pessimistic about the prospects of the transport industry in general. However, in addition to the contract for the parcels service British Rail is also reliant on National Carriers for the cartage of most of what it calls its "less than wagon-load" freight. The volume of collected and delivered traffic on British Rail is about 2 million tonnes annually which is less than 2 per cent. of the total freight activity of the board which amounted to 171 million tonnes in 1978. The existing contractual arrangements between National Carriers and British Rail operate within a framework of a code of practice agreed between the board and National Carriers. The arrangements provide for British Rail to advise National Carriers on an annual basis of its total vehicle requirement. Actual requirements at area level are negotiated locally on a four-monthly basis, and the contract price for this can be adjusted at the start of a new contract to meet special circumstances, such as, for example, an increase in the price of oil.

These arrangements can continue after the repeal of sections I to 5 of the 1968 Act. However, if British Rail is to maintain a national collection and delivery service, and presumably that is the Government's intention, it is essential that it is provided with the facilities for the necessary cartage at the end of journeys. It is feasible that British Rail could find contractors from the private sector to do that, but it is unlikely that any private hauliers can offer a service similar to that supplied to British Rail by National Carriers. On the other hand, there may be a temptation on the part of British Rail to put parts of this contract out to separate tender in specific towns and areas. At this point it must be recognised that there are certain areas of the country where National Carriers' operations are too thin on the ground for the work to be put to it even at present. That particularly applies in isolated areas of Scotland and Wales.

Given the cut-throat nature of the business it is almost certain that in these circumstances National Carriers will be undercut by some operators in particular areas anxious to get a foothold in a business with an annual turnover exceeding £25 million. Any proposal of this nature by British Rail would certainly be vigorously opposed by the NUR and the other unions involved, first, because that must result in a loss of jobs of the union mem- Col. 1706 bers of National Carriers, and second, because part of the commercial advantage gained by any private haulier will be built on the foundation of inferior conditions of service for staff.

In other words, there is a general undercutting in standards of pay and terms and conditions of operators in competition with National Carriers. In this respect trade union pressure has ensured that the basic rates of pay in the hire and reward sector of the road haulage industry are almost common throughout the country, but in terms of pensions and sickness and accident benefits, the NFC's conditions are far better than anything prevailing in the private sector.

Therefore, in a sense the only way in which British Rail can fund someone to undercut National Carriers for this service is by engaging an operator who is doling out to his staff worse terms and conditions.

Another reason for resisting a piecemeal approach to the British Rail contract is that this places in jeopardy the national parcels service operated by the railways. If the railways allocate part of the existing contract to private hauliers, National Carriers is likely to up the price on that part of the contract that remains with it. That would be a natural response and would obviously mean that no financial advantage would be gained by British Rail, although it has on occasions indulged in activities that led it to that.

In these circumstances the board may feel obliged to extend its involvement with private hauliers into more and more areas until National Carriers is squeezed out. The consequence would be the loss of hundreds of jobs of National Carriers' staff who are predominantly members of the NUR. The railway collection and delivery service—this is important for those using the railways for "less than wagon-load" loads—will vary significantly in standard from one part of the country to another. At present it may generally be described as being postalised and of a fairly universal nature. It is not likely to remain so in future. People who have been accustomed to having what I have described as a postalised service will not get it in future and they will not be very happy about that.
