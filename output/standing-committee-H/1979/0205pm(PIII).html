On resuming—

Mr. Fowler
: I should like to remind the Committee of the detail of clause 18. Clause 18 sets the scene for the new system of PSV operator licensing. There is no great change of policy here. We all agree that controls over the safety of public service vehicle operations are of fundamental importance. Those controls are basically unchanged. What we propose is a change in the licensing system which puts them into effect.

A brief look at the present system will reveal why a change is necessary. What we have at the moment is an unhappy marriage between the requirements of an EEC directive, aimed primarily at operators, and United Kingdom legislation dating from the 1930s, aimed at the vehicles. The marriage was inevitable at the time my predecessors had to implement the EEC access directive since primary legislation was not really available. It is available now, and the industry would think we had failed in our duty if we did not take this opportunity to try to produce a more logical, more sensible and less bureaucratic system than we have at the moment.

In essence, we are replacing the annual licensing of every public service vehicle by the licensing of operators for up to five years. We have considered the previous three clauses with great care. I hope we have convinced Labour Members that, in those clauses, controls on the safety of individual vehicles are being maintained at a high level. Clause 18 and the following clauses will ensure that only satisfactory operators are allowed to enter and remain in the industry, and that there is some surveillance of the way in which they look after their vehicles.

Clause 18 provides the basic framework. Public service vehicles may be used for hire and reward only under an operator's licence. Penalties are laid down for the 1106 contravention of this requirement. Licences are issued by the traffic commissioners for the traffic area in which the operating centre for the vehicles is situated, but a licence covers the use of vehicles by the licence-holder throughout Great Britain.

That is the background to what we are proposing. I think it fair to say that the change we are making is important. It has been welcomed by the industry. We should be much criticised were we to let pass this opportunity to legislate in this area.

The right hon. Member for Barrow-in-Furness (Mr. Booth) and my hon. Friend the Member for Faversham (Mr. Moate) raised points concerning the extension of the clause. They asked whether the system for operator licensing could go wider than it does at present. At the moment it covers stage, express and contract carriage. As the right hon. Member for Barrow-in-Furness said, works buses and school buses are not within the system at present. The position goes back to 1930; it has not suddenly appeared. It is true that we are not seeking to extend it.

I suggest that there is a distinct difference between hire and reward, in holding oneself open to the public and the kind of examples that the right hon. Gentleman gave. He gave the example of the works bus and the school bus. Particularly in the case of the school bus, the local education authority can be deemed to take care of the safety aspects of that service.

However, I can go further. We have absolutely no evidence that either works buses or school buses have presented a particular problem in recent years in relation to security and safety. But, in addition, the EEC road-worthiness directive will mean that for those vehicles, too, there will be the yearly inspection, and the annual check system will come into being in 1982. We already have The Motor Vehicles (Tests) Regulations 1976, and The Motor Vehicles (Construction and Use) Regulations 1978. So I hope that both the right hon. Gentleman and my hon. Friend will accept that, although the present situation is different, there is no question that we are in an unchecked situation.

Mr. Moate
: I find—unusually for me— some reassurance in what my right hon. Friend says about the EEC regulations in this respect. But he said that the EEC regulations would require that other vehicles—for example, works buses— JPEG 1107 should have annual inspection. Am I right in assuming that that will require further United Kingdom legislation? Clearly, it will not be necessary for public service vehicles, because they will be covered by this Bill.

Mr. Fowler
: It will require regulation, and that regulation will be introduced. We dealt with that matter at an earlier stage in the Committee's deliberations. The intent and guarantee that I can give the Committee is that annual checks will be introduced, and I hope that that information will be of considerable reassurance.

The right hon. Gentleman also mentioned separate licences in each area where the operator has an operating centre. The operating centre is defined as the place where the person ordinarily keeps his buses; it is a question of fact. We are concerned that a check is made on the maintenance facilities. That does not mean that the operator has to have his own maintenace facilities—the maintenance facilities may be under contract to him— but that he must satisfy the traffic commissioners that there is a basic maintenance facility available to him.

This system is welcomed by the industry. I believe that those working within the industry, the trade unions, will also welcome it. I hope that the Committee will allow clause 18 to stand part of the Bill.

That might easily be illustrated by the part of the clause that gives power to the commissioner to lay down the maximum number of vehicles that can be operated. Obviously the commissioner will look at the operator's ability to handle that number of vehicles, the maintenance facilities, and so on. He will have the power to say "You can operate so many of that type of vehicle and so many of that type; so 1138 many double-deckers so many single-deckers, so many 40-seaters and so many 20-seaters". That must relate to the operator's facilities and his proven competence. There is a relationship between the clause and the preceding one.

My only other point on the clause is that, like so many clauses that we have so far debated, one cannot really test its effect by reading it, because it depends on a statutory instrument yet to be made which, in this case, will prescribe the conditions that may be imposed. Therefore, we have debated a clause concerned with imposing conditions without knowing what conditions may be imposed.

That is one of the difficulties under which we labour as long as the Act is constructed in such a way as to leave so many important changes to statutory instruments. I should welcome the Minister's comments on that and I hope that in order to facilitate our having better debates when we deal with clauses the effects of which depend upon the laying of a statutory instrument, he can give us some guidance as to the broad lines of the statutory instrument which he would intend to introduce, given that he gets the clause in the from in which he proposes it and given that the Bill becomes an Act.

Mr. Fowler
: The clause provides for conditions to be attached to operators' licences. There must be a condition limiting the number of vehicles that may be used under a licence, because, as the hon. Gentleman said, the maintenance facilities of the operator would be suitable only for a finite number of vehicles. In addition, the nature of those facilities may limit the number of double-deckers or articulated vehicles, for example, that could be dealt with, so commissioners have discretion to specify the maximum number of different kinds of vehicles.

These are the conditions foreseen in subsections (1) and (2). Clearly these are consequential on a change to an operator-based system. Such conditions are not needed under the present system, because each licence covers only one specific vehicle.

We have also thought it prudent to provide for other sorts of conditions to be added relating to the use made of vehicles under the licence, but this is not to be a substitute for road service licensing itself. We do not intend conditions to be set, for JPEG 1139 example, limiting the operator to using only so many vehicles on express services.

The only conditions to be allowed will be those to be prescribed by regulations. We have in mind particularly those mentioned in subsection (4), which we have discussed. It will be clear from that discussion that we are thinking mainly of code-of-practice type conditions to apply to operators generally and that we have not made up our minds on the precise nature or timing of any regulations.
