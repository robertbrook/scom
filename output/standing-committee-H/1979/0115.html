TRANSPORT BILL

Clause 2
Definition of "public service vehicle"

10.30 a.m.

Mr. John Prescott
: I beg to move amendment No. 16, in page 2, line 33, leave out "(other than a tramcar)".

The Chairman
: With this we will consider amendment No. 17, in page 2, line 34, after "tramcar", insert "or trolley bus".

Mr. Prescott
: We turn now to the trolley bus exemption. Amendment No. 16 was one of the amendments tabled before we received the explanatory notes from the Minister and when we had difficulties over the timing of the Committee. Therefore, we do not intend to cause delay. A considerable number of amendments have already been selected by you, Mr. English, and a number have been tabled by the Minister himself, which, I feel, justifies our argument at the beginning of the Committee that a certain amount of time was necessary in which to consider the implications of the Bill. Certainly the Minister's awareness of that is reflected in the amendments he has tabled.

Amendment No. 16 was originally tabled as a probing amendment to elicit why the Minister wanted to exempt the trolley bus while leaving tramways in the clause defining a public service vehicle. We were helped to some extent by the Minister's notes which showed his thinking about certain parts of clause 2.

However, the only justification given for the exclusion of trolley buses from the exemption in respect of public service vehicles is that the exclusion is vacuous. That word is defined as meaning empty, without meaning and not reasonably justified.

It is fair to accept that, as I understand it, there are no trolley buses trundling around our transport system at present, but I have received a number of letters from people wanting a return of the trolley bus. Whether that would be justified, 192 bearing in mind the large number of rails and poles associated with trolley buses, is a matter for debate.

In excluding trolley buses from the exemption does the Minister consider that trolley buses may come back, and that, if they do, they will be treated as public service vehicles. He should not close his eyes to the fact that the development of the transportation system may well bring with it new types of trolley buses.

However, there is an implied assumption that it will come back as a public service vehicle. For example, it could be assumed to be a trolley bus that carried more than nine persons, not a trolley taxi or a trolley car or whatever. Developments in the transportation system are becoming rapid and, with the development of atomic energy a cheaper system may be developed to run on electricity, so all possibilities should be considered.

The 1960 Act mentions "a trolley vehicle". When that legislation was enacted trolley buses were still in existence. Therefore it was necessary to make clear that a trolley vehicle was exempted from the definition of a public service vehicle. However, if they existed today they might well be considered to be public service vehicles for the purposes of the Bill.

The definition of "trolley vehicle" in the Road Traffic Act 1960 is:

"a mechanically propelled vehicle adapted for use upon roads without rails and moved by power transmitted thereto from some external source;" "External source" is perhaps the operative phrase. One so readily assumes that the power of the trolley system was derived from rails or wires on poles carried above the road surface, the trolley bus merely using the roads as the means of travel but powered by an external source.

My right hon. Friend the Member for Barrow-in-Furness (Mr. Booth) and I visited the Tyne and Wear transport authority last week. It last used trolley buses in 1966. The major disadvantage of the system at the time, when most bus authorities were discontinuing trolley buses, was the costly maintenance of miles of wires and poles and the high and increasing cost of electricity.

In considering the distinction that the Minister has drawn between trains and trolley buses it is easy to distinguish between the kind of transport system that runs on rails and the trolley bus, which was a kind of motor vehicle but was powered from an external source. One can see why there should be separate legislation for railways, but it is perhaps not equally JPEG 193 clear why trolley vehicles should have the same exemption.

It is worth referring here to a debate which took place in 1930, when with the 1930 Act 50 years of Socialist legislation was born. In the main, that legislation has not changed. In fact, this Bill does not greatly change the thinking behind that Act on public service vehicles. In the debate on 1 May 1930, the Opposition moved an amendment to include trolley vehicles in the definition of the public service vehicle and in the definition to be covered by the commissioners in determining the fares—that is, to be the same as a motor vehicle. However, that amendment was rejected, and the reason given by Herbert Morrison, as he then was, made clear why the Government refused to include both trolley buses and tramways in the definition of a public service vehicle. Herbert Morrison said:

"There is a very good reason why tramcars and trolley vehicles should not be in this part of the Bill. They are both of them statutory undertakings run in direct pursuance of Parliamentary powers, and as a rule regulations can be made by the Minister of Transport governing them…Therefore, clearly…they cannot run except under direct statutory powers, and it is not proposed that they should run other than by statutory authority"— [Official Report, Standing Committee C; 1 May 1930, c.884.]

As I think the Minister will agree, there were of course separate powers for dealing with those matters and so that might have been correct at that time. The argument now presented to us is that as no trolley buses exist it seems a little irrelevant to include them in the exemption from this power.

We believe—as the Minister recognises in his notes—that trolley buses will develop. In fact, a number of hybrid systems are beginning to develop. For example the rather excellent transit system in the Tyne and Wear area could be considered a combination of a rail and bus system. At the Derby engineering works —which I know the Minister, too, has visited—work is proceeding on a kind of bus on rail. So a number of different transport systems are being developed which in the past have been dealt with by different pieces of legislation.

Such a development in the future may well be a trolley bus which was originally considered as a hybrid vehicle. I think that when it was originally brought in it was referred to as the trackless tram. So 194 the borderline between different types of transport is often crossed.

One of the first Committees on which my right hon. Friend and I sat related to legislation on the hovercraft. That was a combination of road, aircraft and maritime legislation applied to an entirely different means of transportation. We did not try to force it into any one of those. It carried across all of them.

We may see the development of a trolley bus system in the future, particularly as its main means of power would be electricity. As the Government are making clear, and not without some point, the development of electricity from nuclear energy will be a major source of power for future transport systems. It may come from battery sources, but may also come from an external application of power—perhaps a single wire—which would not involve massive maintenance costs as did the old application of power by poles to two sets of lines, and so on. Again one would not want to treat that kind of vehicle— because of its different means of external power—as a public service vehicle.

Clearly, a trolley bus system may come into being, but as there is none in evidence now, it is argued that we should take it out of the list of exemptions. But if trolley buses were introduced, we think that the definition of "public service vehicle" would be sufficient. Without the amendment fresh legislation might be needed at that time, but if the amendment were accepted it would be sufficient to leave things as they are and to conduct experiments under existing legislation.

I refer the Minister to the example of the flexi-bus in south Yorkshire. That was, by our definition, considered to be a trailer bus. The Government could not give permission for it to be used as a public service vehicle to carry fare paying passengers. It is being used in south Yorkshire today as a passenger carrying bus because exemptions can be made under existing legislation to enable passengers to be carried, but not to be charged. That bus is now trundling around successfully on the south Yorkshire system in Sheffield. It is an expanding system and well accepted by those driving and operating the buses.

This legislation will change that. It recognises that trailers can be used as public service vehicles and the anomaly will not continue. But it shows how we JPEG 195 must adjust existing legislation to meet new developments in transportation systems. If the only argument for not exempting trolley buses is that there are none presently, why not exempt them? It will create no difficulty for any other legislation and may give the Minister greater flexibility when the newly developing trolley bus is considered in future?

I am not at all sure that we shall have the broad legislation that will cover all the contingencies that may arise in public transport over the next decade. In particular, oil supply—irrespective of any other energy problems—will be a world difficulty, even though it will not be a major problem in this country for about half a decade.

If in the future we shall be concentrating predominantly on energy saving in transport, there is a fair possibility that the track vehicle taking up road space will come back.

I regret to say, although I do not make any apologies, that I have not had the time to look at the present legislation governing tramcars. I do not know whether there is general legislation, or whether it is simply private Acts of Parliament. But it seems to me that if we are discussing some form of lightweight tram-car—that seems to be the most likely form in which it may make a come-back—it could well be that the ancient legislation on which the old ones relied will no longer be appropriate.

I do not think that it is a party political point. My right hon. and hon. Friends and I are not raising it with a view to delaying the passage of the Bill. We genuinely feel that it would be worthwhile for the Minister to have his Department consider, very seriously, extending whatever definitions are necessary.

The Bill could then enable various innovations in public transport to be introduced, over the next decade without the need for legislation, or without the problem which practical people in the field may find in building new vehicles when their pursuit of efficiency, saving of materials, weight and energy leads them to produce things which do not fall within the existing tramcar or trolley bus definitions.

I hope that the Minister will see fit to reconsider this aspect so that we have a law which will enable people in the field to respond in an imaginative and flexible way to the problems that will face them in six, seven or 10 years' time without having to come back to this House or without being able to make excuses. This last matter is important, because public transport is absolutely ruined by the ability of the people operating it to make excuses about their failure to provide a decent service.

We should not leave the management of various public transport bodies in a position where they can say "We cannot introduce these innovations safely or cheaply within the law, because the law is stopping us, and we shall have to wait for legislation which will take another 18 months or two years". Of course, they would be dead right about that, because the ability of this place to respond to technological change is notoriously poor.

On all counts I believe that the Minister JPEG 199 should make sure that this piece of legislation enables this degree of flexibility in the future.

I should like to explain the purpose of the present wording of the clause. It is to get over the particular doubt and confusion to which the right hon. Gentleman referred—that is, whether a vehicle is a public service vehicle not only when it is actually being used for the carrying of passengers for hire or reward but when it is temporarily not being so used—for example, when it is in the garage at night when or when it is returning to the depot after a journey in service. Surprising though it may sound, I am told there is a genuine slight doubt about the 1960 Act. It is hoped that the wording in the Bill will clarify the matter and put it beyond doubt.

It is arguable that as the 1960 Act stands a vehicle is a public service vehicle only when it is carrying passengers. It would therefore also be arguable that if the word "being" were inserted, as the right hon. Gentleman has suggested, the result might be that it would still be a public service vehicle only while it actually had passengers in it. To take the matter to its extreme, it could be argued that a vehicle would not be a public service vehicle if it happened to have failed to pick up any passengers because nobody was at the stops. Certainly, there would be doubt about a vehicle in a garage making other sorts of journey.

I am not aware that anybody has successfully got away with that argument. However, it is plainly desirable that the matter should be sorted out and made absolutely clear in the Bill. The only practical effect is to establish the powers of vehicle examiners. It would be possible at the moment, or if we raised any doubt in the drafting, for a maverick operator to claim that the vehicle examiners had no power to examine his public service vehicles when they were standing in the garage at night, because they were not being used for the carrying of passengers for hire or reward. We do not want that sort of argument to be maintained. Therefore, we have proposed this form of words, to make clear that it does not matter at all whether a vehicle is in use at the time. It will affect only the position of vehicle examiners.
