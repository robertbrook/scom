I will deal first with the earlier amendments rather than the main substantive amendment moved by the hon. Member for Northampton, South (Mr. Morris). His idea that the Secretary of State casually comes to a decision on whether to give a grant to a district council is a little tease. I looked at the word "casually" with great envy. The basis we wish to secure is that there shall be no burden, no increased burden, no undue burden, upon the local authority. It is right for me to tell the Committee how I JPEG 227 envisage that we shall get to that desirable situation.

This is a reflection on this principle of flexibility which the Minister is always enunciating. Inevitably people will be worried in the new town areas at the relative vagueness of this legislation. A number of outside bodies have commented on this clause and expressed the same sort of anxiety as we have heard today. I will not quote at length, but one particular comment, which has a bearing on something the Minister said, came from the Town and County Planning Association, which said:

"Here is the danger of the financial burden falling very unequally among the local authorities concerned. Housing built by the early new towns stands in to those corporations and the Commission at a very low book value, and very low fixed interest rates—3 per cent. to 4 per cent.—are being paid on loans. Housing built more recently has been more expensive to build and the borrowing has also been much more expensive, with interest rates up to 15 per cent. Thus the transfer of housing built by a new town in recent years, mainly in the second and third generations new towns, will be a far greater financial burden to the local authorities taking it over."

This is what the Minister acknowledges. It is right to get it firmly on the record. There is a real fear of heavy burdens falling on some of the new towns. It can be illustrated by the fact that, in the new towns as they stand, the proportion of the burden of providing housing borne by the rent payers is much JPEG 231 lower than the proportion borne normally in the local authorities. Some 25 per cent. in the new towns comes out of rents. The remainder comes out of the subsidy. For housing overall the figure is, I think, somewhere between 43 per cent. and 55 per cent., depending on whether one includes rebates or not. Therefore the burden borne by central Government in the new towns is much greater, two or three times as great, as it is in other areas.

It is important to make sure that the assurances which this Bill is designed to contain are absolutely valid, and this is the reason for our set of amendments. On the other hand the Under-Secretary was right to say, when he discussed this, that there is somebody else who has an interest in this matter and that somebody else is the taxpayer. Some of my hon. "I do not think it is unfair to introduce this question of the consideration of the taxpayers" interest".

He went on to say:

"However, I think the Committee will agree that it would not be reasonable to ask the taxpayer to be financially responsible if the calculation of the size of the burden were to be based on the wholly unreasonable policies of a district council."—[ Official Report, Standing Committee H, 13th May 1976; c. 222–3.]

We have heard echoes of that comment this morning, and it is a point which we have to consider seriously. I have great sympathy with the arguments put forward by the representatives of the new towns. We have to consider carefully the exact implications of this set of amendments. It is one of the facts that there are areas in this country where the local authorities, normally Socialist, are pursuing a rent policy aimed at keeping rents to an extremely low level. Some of them are defying the implied wishes of the present Government. I have had quoted to me one example. Sedgefield District Council has apparently a substantial differential in the rents it raises as compared with those of the Aycliffe Development Corporation. This is an example which can be, and, no doubt, is being, repeated in different parts of the country. One is entitled to ask what would happen in those areas if housing were transferred from the development 232 corporations to local authorities who were hell bent on keeping rents at an excessively low level, and what, as a consequence of this, would be the burden on the taxpayer.

Would it be inherent and implicit in the amendments we have put forward that the cost of subsidising the low rents of the houses transferred would fall on the taxpayer? I am concerned that this might be the case. It would be intolerable that the taxpayer should be saddled with this kind of burden in this kind of situation. One of the difficulties about the business of transferring housing assets to local authorities will be the question how they will deal with the situation where there are different levels of rent between the development corporations houses and their own houses. This will pose a real managerial problem. I think that if local authorities take the view that their sole job is to keep rents down to an artificially low level this will be damaging. It seems to me one of the risks of the amendments we are putting forward that this could happen.

The question therefore is: what is the right course for the Committee to take at the present time? I have carefully thought about the arguments put forward. They are arguments to which it will be proper to return when on Report. Even though one accepts part of what the Minister has said, as I do, nevertheless there is still a degree of anxiety whether we can be sure that the councils will get a really fair deal out of the Governent. The Minister has given the Committee an assurance on this point and I do not dispute his intentions. I think he is quite genuinely keen to make sure that the local authorities get a fair deal. There are real problems about planning for every contingency, foreseen or unforeseen. This is one of the areas where there is some case for the Minister having a little bit of discretion up his sleeve, reluctant though I am normally to give him discretion because I am not sure what he would do with it. In this case there is some reason for allowing discretion. My advice to the Committee is not to press the amendment today, but to make clear that this is something we are likely to come back to on Report when the Government themselves will have had time to think further about it, JPEG 233 and may be we can then see whether we can take the thing a bit further and give more assurance to the local authorities who are concerned about this matter.

The Committee will have noticed that in the latter subsection a corporation is required to secure that all occupiers of the buildings or other land transferred or managed are informed of the way in which they are affected by the transfer scheme, while subsection (1) requires them to take steps to draw the attention of occupiers of buildings or other land, etc. So that the word "all" appears in one case and not in the other.

A deliberate distinction has been drawn in this clause. Whereas subsection (2) is concerned with the situation where a transfer has actually taken place, or land has actually been subject to management schemes, and is directed to securing notification of the occupiers of the buildings or other land involved, the steps required by subsection (1) have to be taken before a scheme for transfer and arrangements for management have been negotiated.

JPEG 237 At that time no one knows which properties will be included in the transfer or management arrangements. Therefore, it is appropriate to require the corporation only to secure that the possibility of the transfer scheme and its likely effects are generally known in the areas to which it is likely to relate. In all the circumstances, the inclusion of the word "all" in subsection (1) would impose a greater requirement on the corporation than is either necessary or appropriate. That is the reason for the distinction, and I hope that, in the circumstances, the hon. Gentleman will agree that it would not be appropriate to accept this amendment. I hope that what I have said explains why the clause is drafted in this way.

Clause 12
PROTECTION OF EMPLOYEES

Mr. Guy Barnett
: I beg to move, Amendment No. 38, in page 13, leave out 238 lines 1 to 6 and insert

"in its application to a person who as a result of a transfer scheme is, after the scheme comes into force, in new employment with a different employer".

This amendment has two purposes. First, it deletes the reference to paragraph 10 of Schedule 1 to the Trade Union and Labour Relations Act 1974. Secondly, it applies the adaptation of Schedule 1 to the Contracts of Employment Act 1972 which is set out in Clause 12(5) only to persons whose change in employment occurs as a result of a transfer scheme. The first part of the amendment corrects an error in drafting. There is no need to mention separately paragraph 10 of Schedule 1 to the Trade Union and Labour Relations Act 1974 because it is covered by the reference on page 12, line 45, to "any other enactment".

The second part of the amendment corrects lines 4 and 5 on page 13. The subsection provides that, for the purposes of the Contracts of Employment Act and various other statutes—for example, the statutory provisions concerning unfair dismissal—a break in employment due to a transfer scheme is to be ignored in computing a period of "continuous employment"; but the wording of lines 4 and 5 applies that protection to employees of a new town corporation or a district council who are in different employment following a transfer scheme, whether or not their transfer is as a result of such a scheme. The amended wording restricts the protection to the persons specified in subsection (2); that is, those who, as a result of a transfer scheme, are in new employment with the same employer or employed by the other party to the scheme.

Amendment agreed to.

Mr. Guy Barnett
: I beg to move, Amendment No. 39, in page 13, line 16, after "shall", insert

"(if it would not otherwise be so treated)".

The effect of the amendment is to make it clear that the provision of subsection (6) may not need to apply in respect of all transfer schemes. This is a drafting amendment to subsection (6) to deal with a possible overlap between the provisions of that subsection and subsection (5).

The purpose of the two subsections is to secure that a change of employment as the direct result of a transfer scheme does not prejudice an employee's rights in respect of JPEG 239 unfair dismissal, redundancy payments and certain other matters.

Those rights in each case depend upon the employee's having been "continuously employed" for a certain period. Subsection (5) deals with those statutory provisions to which Schedule 1 to the Contracts of Employment Act 1972 applies; and subsection (6) applies, with adaptations, Section 13 of the Redundancy Payments Act 1965. That section of the 1965 Act, taken together with section 3 of the same Act, provides that where there is a change in the ownership of business and an employee is employed by the new owner of the business the cessation of his first employment does not constitute a "dismissal" for the purpose of obtaining a redundancy payment. It was originally thought that it would be necessary for this clause 12 to apply Section 13 of the 1965 Act in respect of all transfer schemes; but we now understand that where the whole of a new town corporation's housing undertaking is transferred by the same scheme to one district council the provisions of paragraph 9 of Schedule 1 to the Contracts of Employment Act 1972 will apply to employees who are in "new employment" as defined in subsection (2) of this clause as a result of the scheme. Paragraph 9 of that schedule has a similar effect to that of Section 13 of the Redundancy Payments Act 1965. Accordingly, it is not necessary to apply that latter provision to transfer schemes where the whole of the new town corporation's housing undertaking is transferred by the same scheme to one district council.

Amendment agreed to.

Clause 17
SHORT TITLE, EXTENT AND CONSEQUENTIAL AMENDMENT

Mr. Guy Barnett
: I beg to move, Amendment No. 40, in page 15, line 7, after "Act" insert "except section 13(3)."

The effect of this amendment is to apply Section 13(3) to Scotland and Northern Ireland as well as England and Wales. Section 13(3) provides for the New Towns Staff Commission to be included in Part II of Schedule 1 to the House of Commons Disqualification Act 1975, so that while persons are members of the commission they will be disqualified for membership of the House of Commons. This drafting amendment is necessary because although the Bill itself does not apply in Scotland and Northern Ireland, the House of Commons Disqualification Act 1975 does apply in these countries.

Amendment agreed to.
