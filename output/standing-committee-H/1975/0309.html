Mr. Powell: In the amendments to which the Committee has just returned, we have Image Col. 97 been considering one only of the difficulties that the endeavour at definition, which is indispensable, has the effect of highlighting. I want to refer to something which may present a wider difficulty and which goes even closer to the root of the matter and to our objections to this whole approach to discrimination and equality of opportunity.

I should like to focus the attention of the Committee on the words in subsection (2) (b) of this clause:

"… due allowance being made for any material difference in their suitability."

I do so, not in order to query the precise wording, which would be improper at this stage, but to raise what, I concede, is a matter of principle which the necessity of finding some such wording as that serves to emphasise. It is of the nature of such words as "due allowance" and "material difference" that they are highly subjective and that the difference between two candidates for a post, which to one person might appear unimportant, may, to another, appear to be decisive. Again, there may be a difference between the two candidates which might be a difference in an area which one employer would regard as irrelevant to the interests of a job, and which another, in all good faith and without any religious discrimination, might regard as highly relevant.

I do not think that it is a flight of imagination to invite the Committee to consider what must be a normal situation for a prospective employer or personnel manager who may be interviewing two or three people for a job. We all know what I mean by saying that in that situation, one can have the impression that the candidate selected is the man who fits. The paper qualifications of A, B and C, for all practical purposes, are the same. Certainly they are all above the minimum limits which may have been fixed for the job. A, B and C are all men who, in the opinion of the interviewer, would do the job adequately and satisfactory. Yet there is something about C which causes the personnel manager to say "That is a born sales representative. There is something about that chap that tells me that he will fit this job in a way that the other applicants will not."

I have chosen the example of a sales representative because we all know, though we would be hard put to define it, what we mean by "a born salesman", Col. 98 and I defy anybody, unless there is a question of a previous record, in an abstract list of qualifications—a numbered proforma to be filled out by the applicants—to include in it characteristics which fit together, to define what we all mean by "a born salesman". As I have said, I have taken the case of a vacancy for a salesman, but what I have suggested applies, to a greater or less degree, to almost any appointment which requires to be made.

I concede that in the hiring and firing—and I hope that it will be more hiring than firing in Northern Ireland—of unskilled labour and, to a considerable extent, of skilled labour, this difficulty to which I refer can hardly arise. Indeed, I am prepared to concede that if an employer or a personnel manager sought to apply this kind of subtlety in taking on unskilled labour, one might deduce that he was looking for differences which were not material and that he was trying to make allowances which were not due allowances.

But this Bill is not confined to unskilled labour. It is not even confined to tradesmen, the supply of whom, even with the regrettably high overall level of unemployment in Northern Ireland, is, as the Minister of State knows well, extremely tight in some areas and for some purposes. But the Bill covers a whole range of employments and, without hesitation, I submit that taking the whole range of employments which it covers, there are numerous circumstances in which it is part of the duty and responsibility of an employer—if he is directly responsible—and the personnel manager, to be sensitive to such differences which, from his experience, he has found will fit one man for a post and not another.

The consequence of both this and subsequent parts of the Bill is that such a person making such a decision may well find himself called to account to say what differences he found in a number of candidates' suitability and what allowances he made. I wonder whether that is an onus —at any rate, in the form in which it stands in this definition clause—which it is realistic to place upon employers and personnel managers.

We are, as this clause once again reminds us, endeavouring to make matters justiciable which hitherto they have not been, and we are intending to make the difference between the way in which Image Col. 99 various firms handle those matters, the difference of success and failure for those firms. Let us not put too fine a point on it, but that is what Part III is all about: We shall penalise firms if the agency, subject to appeal, believes that firms are not furnishing equality of opportunity.

Therefore, it is no light matter to attend to the precise terms in which the equality of opportunity is to be envisaged, because the Appeals Board, to which we shall come in the next clause, must direct its mind, and must direct its legal mind, to due allowance and material difference in suitability.

I ask the Committee to consider, whatever may be their point of view on the general approach in this Bill, the danger of the Bill, as drafted, leading to the position which I have envisaged of a personnel manager being hauled before the agency, in effect, or the Appeals Board and being invited to say, "Well, there were these three candidates A, B and C, Mr. X. We found that A was a Roman Catholic. This is what will happen. If it is not going to happen, we do not need this part of the Bill and, arguably, we do not need the Bill. But this is what we are asking for, by the manner in which the Bill is drawn.
