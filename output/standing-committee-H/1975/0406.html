Clause 49
MEANING OF "EMPLOYMENT IN NORTHERN IRELAND" AND "OCCUPATION IN NORTHERN IRELAND"

Question proposed—(1st April)— That the clause stand part of the Bill.

10.30 a.m.

Question again proposed.

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Stanley Orme)
: Having re-read the words of the right hon. Member for Down, South (Mr. Powell) when he returned to the point which he had raised earlier, as to why the rest of the United Kingdom should be treated differently from Northern Ireland, I think that there is a difference of view here. We believe that the Bill and this clause are aimed at the problems that exist at the moment in Northern Ireland.

It is quite right, as the right hon. Gentleman says, that a British or foreign firm could discriminate at the moment in advertising for employment and sent to Northern Ireland people who would not be covered by the Bill if the firms were not resident in Northern Ireland. My answer is that this is highly unlikely and we have no examples of this type of blatant discrimination being carried out. If it were, I am sure that the Government would look at it.

The right hon. Gentleman asked us to have another look at this point and I am prepared to do that. I am not taking a dogmatic stance, but, having looked at it, I see no reason for any change at this stage and, rather than extend this type of legislation to the whole of the United Kingdom, I should like to see the necessity for such legislation lapse in Northern Ireland. We are introducing it because we are forced to do so at this time. I hope that the Committee will accept that explanation.

Mr. J. Enoch Powell
: I am grateful to the right hon. Gentleman for renewing as, of course, I had no doubt that he would, 482 his assurance about further consideration of this point. When he does so, I should like to suggest that he views it upon the narrower rather than upon the wider scale. Whatever one's preferences might have been and whatever are one's prejudices in favour of United Kingdom legislation full stop, we must accept the fact that this is a Northern Ireland Bill. It is not the intention of my hon. Friends or myself to extend it in any natural sense of the term to the rest of the United Kingdom. We are disturbed by the apparent possibility under the Bill that exactly the practices at which it was aimed, in exactly the same places in which it is aimed at them, might be excluded in the sort of case I was describing when the Committee rose last time.

Therefore, if I may, I should like to direct the right hon. Gentleman's attention to two specific areas, both of which I would claim are Northern Ireland areas. One is the case implied in the wording of this subsection where there will be employment in Northern Ireland in the course of the relevant occupation. The Committee will recall that it was the words "or mainly" in this subsection which sparked off this discussion. That is the first point that I should like the right hon. Gentleman to consider. We have here employment which on the face of it will, at any rate in part, be employment in Northern Ireland which is being exempted, subject to the other circumstances, from the ambit of the Bill.

My second point is recruitment in Northern Ireland. It seems to me that the substance of the Bill deals with recruitment in Northern Ireland, the selection in Northern Ireland of A rather than B, for reasons which Parliament condemns. Therefore the locus delicti—the place of the offence—at which we are aiming is Northern Ireland and my hon. Friends and I are not trying to shift it.

While not wishing to press the Minister further at this stage I ask him, when he carries out his promise, to address himself not to the larger question which has gone by, whether this should be a United Kingdom Bill or not, but to the two specific and relatively narrow questions of Northern Ireland application which I have endeavoured to define.

Question put and agreed to.
