In Clause 7 we come for the first time to the manner in which the register of declarants will be used for the purposes of pressure, persuasion or compulsion at whatever point in the scale one might assess it. I want to draw attention to what, in our view, is a faulty approach in the Bill as it now stands.

Perhaps I could make this most clear by directing the Committee's attention to subsection (2)( b), which says that the agency shall

"remove from the register the name of any declarant who … has declared that he no longer adheres to the Declaration"

I should have thought that if anything was plain-sailing, that provision was. In subsection (2)( b)(iii) the clause goes on to say that if the agency concludes that the person is, in its view, breaking the declaration, the agency has the power to follow a procedure laid down in Clauses 12 and 24, and Clause 8, in the matter of appeal.

200 This again is perfectly straightforward. It is a perfectly intelligible and controllable piece of machinery that, having got the register of those who have made the declaration, the agency should use its power to remove from that register the trigger which detonates its investigation and its policing—if I may use that rough expression—activities; and those policing activities are, in the terms of the Bill, properly subject to both statutory provisions set out in Clauses 12 and 24 with the appropriate Schedules, and also the right of appeal in the Clause to which we are presently coming.

I submit to the Committee that there is no need to institute another procedure, side by side with that, subject to no sort of appeal and liable to the suspicion—of which I am sure that the agency would not be guilty—of harassment and unreasonable behaviour. That is the ground set out in subsection (2)( b)(i).

As I read this Clause in conjunction with Clause 8, there is no appeal procedure, and none of the statutory machinery of the Bill applies under sub-paragraph (i), so that the power of the agency stated in paragraph ( a)—to require a reaffirmation—is unrestricted. So far as I can see, Clause 8 is deliberately so drawn that action by the agency under subsection (2)( b)(i) of this Clause would not attract the safeguards or the appeal. There may be some way in which they are attracted, and no doubt the Minister will draw the Committee's attention to it, but the broad proposition that I put to the Committee is that having got an appeals procedure and being about to institute a statutory-controlled form of investigation by the agency, we should not short-circuit it and allow the agency to use its power under paragraph ( a) as a direct means of bringing duress to bear on declarants.

There should be no misunderstanding in the Committee that my hon. Friends and I naturally consider that if a person makes a declaration, he should be held to its terms, and there should be means of proceeding to deal with him if he is shown not to have conformed to its terms. Whether a register is the best method of doing so, I would not, at this stage, argue because we have added Clause 6 to the Bill. I would argue, however, that enforcement, however gentle it is and however subtle the persuasion, should be subject to statutory safeguards JPEG 201 and to the right of appeal.

As the Clause stands, if one looks at paragraph ( a), one can see the power of the agency to require reaffirmation

"… at such intervals and in such manner as the Agency may determine …"

Read in the light of paragraph ( b), this appears to give the agency an unrestricted power without giving reasons, and without cause shown, to come down on a declarant suddenly on a Monday morning and say that to remain on the register, he must reaffirm his declaration. In those circumstances, the declarant should have all the rights and the agency should have all the duties which are attracted by subsection (2)( b)(iii) and Clause 8. Therefore it is wrong that the reaffirmation procedure should be separated from the investigatory and appeal procedure.

Indeed, what I am saying to the Minister is that he is losing nothing—nothing, certainly, that he ought to want—if he only allows the agency to remove from the register under the terms of paragraphs ( b)( ii) and ( b)( iii). I am sure the hon. Gentleman would agree that it is quite unreasonable for the agency to come down upon a declarant with the clear implication that he is in breach of the declaration, otherwise why ask him to reaffirm it? That is the clear implication, that the agency should be able to come down on the declarant without having investigated the matter and consequently without his having the grounds of appeal provided in Clause 8.

While I am on this point, perhaps I might invite whichever of the Ministers is to reply to clarify subsection (2). There is no question here of reopening matters discussed on a previous Amendment, but on "Clause 7 stand part" the Committee is entitled to more clarification than it has yet had as to the meaning of subsection (2). I am not now arguing, as I was previously, that subsection (2) ( a) should be removed but I am asking that its meaning should be made clearer to the Committee than it has hitherto.

In this context, I refer to the remarks of the Under-Secretary at our last sitting. His attention was drawn to the words in line 39, page 4 "in such manner", when it was pointed out that those words are quite at large, or appear to be quite at large, and that, whereas the Bill prescribes—or used to prescribe, at any rate—a particular form of declaration, it is left to the agency to devise for itself any form it likes of 202 reaffirmation, however onerous or however undesirable it might seem. Having gone to the trouble to debate and discuss what ought to be the proper form of an initial declaration, it seems quite wrong that the form of a reaffirmation should be left at large. If that is so, we were wasting our time and shall perhaps in future be wasting our time in giving a statutory form to the declaration.
