Clause 1
TRANSFER OF HOUSING AND ASSOCIATED PROPERTY, ETC., TO DISTRICT COUNCILS

We understand the Government's thinking about flexibility, and we acknowledge that it is necessary on occasions, but we are dealing with a fairly finite matter. On the ground, it is clear what should be transferred and our list is pretty exhautive. Nothing much could get through its net. A degree of fishing might be required to put in some odd items, though there is nothing I can think of, and the working party could not think of anything else either. The working party said that if something obviously had to be put into the list, the logic of the case on the ground would mean that it could be included.

We are a little hesitant about giving the Minister the sweeping powers in this clause because hon. Members who have served in local government will know of cases where local authorities believe the 50 law is quite clear, but then the Secretary of State sends out a contrary directive. We have great hesitation in approving this flexibile approach. We believe the list should be spelled out. We rest our case very much on the working party report. Its members were experts, and spent a great deal of time considering the matter. They produced a good report which we should not lightly reject.

Individuals on the ground will wish to decide on the assets to be transferred. That discretion is much JPEG 51 more for the parties concerned than for the Secretary of State. The new town corporations and the district councils will have to agree.

I wish to correct an impression I may have given to the Committee on Tuesday. I have not yet studied the Hansard of our proceedings, but I may have suggested that rent offices will have to be transferred. This is not the case. It is a matter for agreement between the parties. Our flexibility is designed to meet a variety of circumstances and to suit the individual needs of new towns. We want to enable new town corporations and district councils to come to amicable and sensible agreements about what should be transferred and how it should be done.

52 The aim of Amendments No. 15 and 16 is to substitute the affirmative resolution procedure for the negative procedure for the orders under the clause. The business of substituting the affirmative procedure for the negative procedure has probably taken up more Committee hours than any other subject. It is a general rule of parliamentary life that Oppositions prefer the affirmative procedure and Governments prefer the negative procedure. The Government and previous Leader of the House caused genuine alarm by the way in which they talked about encouraging enabling legislation. It is not just a matter of an Opposition grumbling when I say this approach is profoundly wrong.

I know we are living in a world with a different philosophy to law-making from our own. While not doubting the many advantages of joining the EEC, we shall have to ask ourselves a number of questions about our parliamentary procedure. The British approach to lawmaking over the centuries has been pragmatic rather than theoretical. We wait till a problem arises and then deal with it as competently as possible. In consequence, nearly every item in our legislation, however boring and unintelligible it may be to some people, is of very great importance. The philosophy that more matters should be decided by orders rather than in the Acts is one which should be watched very carefully. I was recently reading a famous book written by Lord Hewart on this subject in the 1920s. No doubt my hon. Friend the Member for Ludlow (Mr. More) was nurtured on this book, which sets out the dangers of a move towards delegated legislation.

I am not, with these amendments, just reacting in the normal Opposition manner. There are genuine reasons why they should be adopted. Essentially, this matter boils down to the importance of the topic. The general convention is that if something is not so important and contentious, the negative procedure is tolerable, but on a matter of major importance or real significance within the structure of an Act, the affirmative procedure is right.

We have in previous debates clarified the nature of the order referred to in subsection (3)( g). This was helpful, because I did not understand exactly what the JPEG 53 order was about. It seemed it could be read as either specific or general and could relate to the circumstances of a particular transfer scheme rather than to something of general application to all subsequent schemes. My understanding of what the Minister said on Tuesday is that once an order has been made it is of general application to all transfer schemes and cannot be specific to a particular one. Under subsection 4 it is possible for this order to be varied or revoked by subsequent order. Anyway we have cleared that matter up. That is important.

10.45 a.m.

I want to argue that these orders which may or may not be made subsequently are clearly an inherent part of the Bill itself. They are not, in my view, something which can, therefore, legitimately be regarded solely in the terms of delegated legislation. When we talk of delegated legislation we talk of legislation the basis of which, and the power for the making of which, are established in the Act. Perhaps there are details with which we did not want to clutter the Act; perhaps events, it was thought, might develop requiring Ministers to take actions in particular circumstances.

I do not think that applies in this case. This order-making power is giving the Government the right or power to introduce an order which will not be implementing what is in the Bill already but which, in effect, will be an additional subsection of this clause.

I do not know whether I make myself clear but there is a real difference between the two. Normally an order is based on something which appears in an Act, which says that, for the purpose of working out how the scheme will be operated, the Minister may bring to the House an order to implement the scheme. What this clause is essentially saying is that we have thought of the number of contingencies which are expressed in paragraphs ( a), ( b), ( c), ( d), ( e), ( f). We have in humility or prudence or common sense to admit that there may well be things we have not thought out. The Minister was saying on Tuesday that there may well be things we have not thought about and that they will crop up at subsequent times and we want 54 to be in a position to incorporate those things when they come up at a later date.

I accept the need for being able to deal with contingencies which have not been dreamed up, possibilities not covered by the Stevenage working party, for example, or things which the 80,000 civil servants at the Department of the Environment have not been able to imagine. We accept that the Government have to find ways of dealing with those contingencies or possibilities, but what essentially will happen, when one of those possibilities arises, is that an extra bit of the Bill will be required.

The ideal place for that extra bit of the Bill would be in an addition to the Bill. If one were being purist, one would say that the Government should pass amending legislation, but in view of the fact that the Government have already got more than enough legislation on their plate I am prepared to forgo insisting that there should be that further legislation.

As a corollary of that we are entitled to ask that when they do have to make an order it should at least have a chance to be properly debated. Everybody knows that an order which is subject only to the negative procedure does not normally have a chance of being properly debated. It is one of the great problems of our parliamentary life that we have these orders, but that if we are able to talk about them, that is looked upon as being almost miraculous. We know perfectly well that the negative procedure is haphazard, and it is a matter of chance whether we have an order debated on the Floor of the House. By and large, when the official Opposition put down a Prayer they will have some chance of getting it taken on the Floor of the House. However, that is not a certainty. There is no guarantee that if a Prayer is put down by leading figures of the Opposition the Government will allow time for it. Debates on Prayers are subject to strict limitation on time. I understand one does not get two hours, but that the debate lapses at 11.30 pm automatically. We have had occasions when important Prayers have been allowed the minimal period of debate, and this is unsatisfactory. This applies also to official Opposition Prayers, and if the official Opposition have that difficulty in getting something debated, how much worse it is for JPEG 55 Back Bench Prayers. Governments are markedly unenthusiastic about allowing Back Benchers' Prayers against Government orders. It is recognised that this is an unsatisfactory procedure, but nobody seems to be clear how it can be remedied.

In this case we are talking about something which is an extension of the Bill rather than a legitimate piece of delegated legislation. In view of this there is a strong case for insisting that this Order should be taken under the affirmative procedure and for saying that the Government approach, as expressed in subsections (4) and (5) is inadequate.
