The Chairman
: I remind the Committee that Government new Clause 1— Grants and loans—has been read a Second time. Certain amendments to it have already been disposed of, and the hon. Member for Chingford (Mr. Tebbit) is in course of moving his Amendment (f), in line 29, leave out

' and to include amongst its members one or more representatives of trade unions '.

Mr. Norman Tebbit
: This is the amendment which would leave out the requirement to include one or more representatives of trade unions on the body which would advise the Secretary of State on matters concerning common-owned firms. As I said last week, there is a case eventually for reasonable-minded men to say that there is no need for us to legislate further to place the trade union movement in a privileged position above all others.

It is curious that the requirement is not that there should be trade unionists on this body but that there should be representatives of trade unions, which is rather different. What is the rôle of these people? Is it to advance the cause of common-owned businesses, or is to represent the interests of trade unions? Presumably, as they would be representatives of trade unions, it is to advance the interests of trade unions. Those two aims may conflict from time to time. It seems to me that then the representatives of trade unions would be personally in an invidious position—indeed, in an immoral position—in that they would be able to block or damage the advance of the interests of common-owned firms in order to advance the interests of the trade union movement.

In the Press on Monday, or perhaps Tuesday, there was a story about the degree of concern which exists among leading members of trade unions about the ways in which the power of patronage 72 is used already in this country. That related to the particular case of a certain gentleman who was retiring from his post as a full-time trade union official and how he was becoming surprisingly well endowed with State jobs He had received two well paid appointments from a Minister who was a Member of Parliament sponsored by his trade union, and he was in the process of receiving a third such appointment. It was causing a good deal of pain to the secretary of another trade union who felt that this was getting a bit much.

We should consider carefully whether it is in the best interest of the trade union movement that its members should be constantly placed in such a privileged position. I know that the hon. Member for Nuneaton (Mr. Huckfield) is a sponsored Member of the same union as that which had received such benefits for one of its former members recently. I know that the hon. Member for Consett (Mr. Watkins) is sponsored by another union, and so is the hon. Member for Chorley (Mr. Rodgers). It seems to me that they are taking their rôle as sponsored Members too far. They are seeing their sponsorship now as a means by which they put the trade unions which put them here into privileged positions above everybody else.

Why do we need representatives of trade unions on this body at all? What are they there for? What is their rôle in relation to common-owned firms? If everybody is the boss, if the firm is owned by all those there concerned, what is it that these trade union representatives will fight for? What sort of advice could they give to the Minister through this body which would be so different from that of people who are actually running or who have had experience in running common-owned firms? Is it for any good purpose that we should have these people on the body, or is it merely that it has now become the custom that the trade union movement, through the TUC, demands a seat on everything as the price of its acquiescence in a statutory incomes policy? Is that what it is? Do we have to buy them off all the time by putting them on the boards of pension funds, putting them in the chair of industrial training boards, and now even putting them into this little body? Surely, it is not such a commanding height of the JPEG 73 economy that the TUC must be represented there.

What does "representatives of trade unions" mean? Does it mean just a chap who happens to be a member of a trade union, or someone who is sent by a trade union to represent its interest?, Which does it mean? How will the Minister discover whether the person concerned is representing the trade union as opposed to merely representing himself? Is he supposed to have a brief about any important matters from his masters, or is he supposed to be a free agent?

We should be well advised to delete this subsection of the clause, not necessarily out of any feeling that even the Trades Union Congress, the trade union movement at the top, is now unduly powerful and should not have its power extended but out of respect for the common ownership movement in this country, which does not have to have tied to it a representative—a delegate—someone who turns back all the time to the trade union movement before he decides what he can do and say.

I hope that my hon. Friends will support me when we vote to delete this odious provision from the Minister's Bill, especially as is was not a provision of the original Bill when it was first laid before the House.

It is not often that we have to decide whether we can debate amendments before we can debate new clauses and then find out afterwards that we have to debate new clauses first. Many of us, if we have not learned all that much about the industrial common ownership movement have certainly learned a fair amount about the procedural techniques of getting a private Member's Bill through.

In thanking you, Mr. Jones, for the wisdom and guidance that you have bestowed upon the Committee, I not only want to thank my hon. Friends and hon. Members opposite for the co-operation and enthusiasm they have shown to get this Bill through the Committee, but to congratulate my hon. Friend the Member for Consett (Mr. Watkins). It is the second time that he has piloted a private Member's Bill through this House.

It is a monumental task to undertake when one starts from scratch, having found that someone has a good idea which one wants to translate into legislation. There are the consultations with the interests involved and consultations with the parliamentary draftsman and with the various Bill offices of the House of Commons. Then there is the trauma of trying to get it through and having to make certain changes on the way, especially when one realises that certain provisions which one would like to make should not be in the Bill but in another piece of legislation. I congratulate my hon. Friend for his perspicacity and tenacity. He has done it well, and I hope that the Bill will now complete its remaining stages very quickly.

I also want to say a word of congratulation and encouragement to many of my hon. Friends who have devoted a political career and a political lifetime in many cases to the co-operative movement in various ways. Although this may be a small piece of legislation, in terms of what it does and what it can do it is a very great step forward along the road which many of my colleagues had been treading long before I came into the House. I hope that this will be a great JPEG 105 step forward in their endeavours along the road they have been treading for a long time.

Finally, I am glad that we have made quick progress and I hope that we shall make just as speedy progress on 16th July; that is when I hope we shall have further discussions on the Bill. Although the various reminiscences on selection conferences were becoming interesting, and I was looking forward to other contributions from both sides of the Committee on that topic, I think you were quite right, Mr. Jones, to say on that occasions like this it is advantageous that the technicalities of the Bill should have precedence over the technicalities of selection conferences.

I hope that we can now report this Bill to the House, and that we can make 106 the speediest possible progress to Royal Assent.

The Chairman
: I am grateful for the kind thanks which have been expressed to the Chair. Members will have seen the very difficult points of order in the proceedings that we have had to contend with during the Committee stage of the Bill, and I should like to add my thanks to those that have already been expressed to John Sweetman.

This is a subject in which I am particularly interested, and it has been an awful temptation to make contributions to the proceedings of the Committee. I have applied the rules of order much more strictly to myself than to the Committee.
