I now return to the remarks made by the hon. Member for Belfast, West (Mr. Fitt) before we rose on Tuesday. The hon. Member for Belfast, West made clear that to him the idea of any continuing registration, recording or numerical taking into account of the religion of employees, was repugnant. I should have thought that in saying that, the hon. Gentleman was saying what must be true for all members of the Committee. Whatever may be the extent, described in the van Straubenzee Report as "some extent", of religious discrimination in Northern Ireland, what none of us wants is that it should be exacerbated by in- Col. 244 vestigations into religious persuasion which actually serve to highlight real or apparent discrepancies of which otherwise no one might have been conscious.

That is the reason my hon. Friends and I take exception to, or doubt the wisdom of—perhaps I might put it that way—including the clause in the Bill. It is a very different thing for an agency like this to carry out on its own responsibility various investigations which it thinks appropriate and which it regards as conducive to the purposes for which it is established, and, on the other hand, for that agency to be specifically charged by statute with doing certain things. It is our contention that the terms of Clause 11 are such as virtually force the agency, and put it under an obligation even if it believes that to discharge it would be counter-productive, to undertake investigations which we believe will on balance be of an irritant and undesirable character.

I draw the Committee's attention to the first two words of the clause:

"It shall be the duty of the Agency".

There is no question of "may" as opposed to "shall". The agency, reading its terms of reference in the Bill, whatever view it takes, whether it shares the views of the hon. Member for Belfast, West and myself, about the undesirability of lists of people of one religion and those of another religion, will find that it has a duty. What is that duty? It is to identify—I leave out for the moment the words "and keep under review", to which I shall presently return—patterns of employment in Northern Ireland for the purposes of

"… considering whether they reveal the existence or absence of equality of opportunity;"

which, as defined in Clause 3, relates to equality of opportunity as between those of different or no religious persuasions.

Taking it no further than that, I seriously ask how it is possible for the agency to perform that function of establishing the initial pattern unless it will actually find out what are the religions of the persons employed and the persons obtaining employment in the various circumstances as set out in Clause 3. It surely could not be regarded as discharging the duty of identifying—and that is a precise word—patterns of employment unless it actually conducts, in some form or another, a census. Indeed, if it did not do that, it would be open to challenge: "Well you have identified patterns but where is your evidence? Are you going on hearsay?"

So much for identifying the patterns of employment. If that were all, we might be prepared to say "We do not like this, but the agency has got to have a starting point" and, perhaps, when we have looked at the pattern which it thus identifies, as we have all told the world, it will appear that the degree of real discrimination is relatively limited. So we may be willing to let pass the proposition that at the outset the agency should identify the pattern of employment in the way I have indicated.

But that is not all. The agency is given a duty—and now I come to the other words—to

"keep under review … trends"

It cannot remain satisfied by saying "This was our starting point. This was the pattern that we established when we set up in business. We have worked within the terms of the statute; we have dealt with a number of cases of discrimination; we have used the Declaration of Intent; we have approached employers when we felt that all was not well, and we have to report year by year not only the detailed results of our work but our strong impression that, as a result, our work is reducing to some extent the religious discrimination in employment which existed". The agency is not to be allowed to do that.

The agency is given a statutory duty to keep under review the trends. Again, if that means anything, it means that, having once done a survey, it is to be the duty of the agency to repeat that survey—and it must be repeated by the same method; it must be statistically comparable; otherwise it will be totally misleading—at regular intervals.

I am reminded of another context of Image Col. 247 which one might say that while one might envisage having a plebiscite or a referendum just once to establish a proposition, the notion of having periodic plebiscites or referenda is the recipe for chaos. Similarly, I put it to the Government that they are here asking for a periodical repetition of a form of survey which in itself is highly sensitive and open to grave objections. I do not know at what intervals the Government think it would be appropriate to pull up by the roots the plant of non-discrimination, the equal opportunity sapling, to see how it is getting on—not a very effective process of arboriculture. Perhaps five years?

The trouble about a statutory duty to review is that the intervals tend to get shorter, that people tend to say "It is not good enough just to leave the thing for five years from the initial date. You have been given a statutory duty to review the trends, and you ought to provide us with a running commentary, as it were, of how equality of opportunity and the prevention of discrimination is proceeding".

Therefore, this is an invitation—I withdraw that word, it is more than an "invitation"—this is a direction to the agency to carry out a survey of religion in employment and to carry out that survey at regular and possibly frequent intervals.

What is more—and I am now referring to a point to which we shall come again in Clause 12—in paragraph (b)(ii) the agency is to carry out this survey with a view to proving a negative as well as an affirmative; to prove the absence of equality of opportunity as well as its existence. This is stated in paragraph (a) also. It is inconceivable that such an onus of proof could be discharged unless first of all there were a statistical layout of the religious affiliations of the employees in all but the smallest firms. Over and above that, there would be a searching investigation in each case to discover how far whatever might turn out to be the balance was due to nondiscriminatory factors—if I may so describe it—or due to the absence of equality of opportunity.

Therefore, we are here imposing upon the agency, over and above the duty of conducting this statistical periodical survey, the duty of comment in detail upon it. It will be no use for the agency to establish the statistical pattern and say "We recognise, of course, that there are geographical fac- Col. 248 tors. There are craft, trade and other factors which come into it." Gentleman to address his mind to is the simple proposition that there is nothing that this clause will oblige the agency to do that it could not under the rest of the Bill do perfectly well, albeit much more tactfully, conveniently, flexibly, and more in accordance with its own judgment and knowledge of the situation than is obligatory under this clause.

We are adding nothing to the powers of the agency by this clause. We are, in fact, placing an obligation upon it. I remember one or two sittings ago when the Under-Secretary, lauding the independence and judgment of the agency, said that we do not need to be fearful of the good judgment which the members who will be appointed Image Col. 249 to the agency will bring to their task. I shall pray that in aid. I do not see why we should suppose that, having been set up for the purposes stated in the Bill, the agency will be deliberately blind either to the situation as it finds it, or to the progress which is being made as a result of its operations. We can therefore safely trust the agency to use its judgment in establishing what facts it thinks necessary. One of the great advantages, if we leave it to the judgment of the agency, is that it will not be called upon—as it will inevitably be called upon as a result of its duties—to publish such statistics as it may accumulate.
