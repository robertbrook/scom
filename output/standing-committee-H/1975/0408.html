: When we suspended our proceedings on Tuesday I was saying that I thought Clause 57, which is a long and detailed clause relating to certain interpretations of critical importance to the Bill, was defective in certain respects. In addition, I think that certain interpretations contained in the clause call for an explanation from the Minister.

When we suspended I was about to refer to the interpretation of "unlawful discrimination" on page 41 of the Bill. 530 The Minister may care to know that there is quite a history about the ending of the financial year. Apparently, originally, the financial year used to end on 31st March and the change to 6th April for the commencement of the new financial year dates back to 1753 when Britain adopted the Gregorian Calendar, named after Pope Gregory XIII, in which there was an 11-day difference. I do not suppose that any of those considerations have influenced the Minister in selecting 31st March as the specific date given in the third line on page 40 of the Bill, but I should be grateful if he would comment on it when he replies.

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Stanley Orme)
: We have had a long speech from the hon. Member for Harborough (Mr. Farr) and I shall certainly reply to the salient points he raised.

This Committee stage has been very thorough and there has been no attempt by any Member to delay the Committee or to engage in filibustering. I hope that the rumour that it is the intention of some hon. Gentlemen opposite to keep the Committee going until Tuesday is not true, because I think our work is far too important for that and everybody in the Committee has played an important part in it.

At the previous sitting the hon. Member for Harborough objected to the word "definition" of contract. Clause 58
AMENDMENTS AND EXTENSION OF ENACTMENTS, AND REPEAL

Mr. J. Enoch Powell
: I beg to move Amendment No. 170, in page 43, line 24, leave out "( b)".

Small though it is, this amendment raises a very important point of administration and legislation. If we refer to the 1973 Act we find that section 20(1) defines the duties of the Standing Advisory Commission on Human Rights. Paragraph ( b), which is to be repealed by subsection (3) of this clause, relates to the duty of that Commission to keep the Secretary of State informed as to the extent to which persons or bodies have prevented discrimination by persons or bodies not prohibited from discriminating by that law.

I quite understand why the enactment of the Bill renders that duty of the Commission superfluous. But my hon. Friends and I do not, as at present advised, understand why the other part of the function of the Advisory Commission as set out in Section 20(1)( a) of the 1973 Act is not also, in effect, rendered superfluous by the machinery which we are setting up by means of this Bill.

The function set out in subsection ( a) is to advise the Secretary of State on the adequacy and effectiveness of the law for the time being in force in preventing discrimination on the ground of religious belief or political opinion, and it provides redress for persons who are aggrieved by discrimination on either ground. I cannot imagine anybody more suitable for doing that task than the agency created by the Bill for the purpose of preventing discrimination on those grounds, and helping to obtain redress for persons who are aggrieved. Surely, the agency in its formal reports, and those tendered by other bodies under the Bill, both to the Secretary of State and to Parliament, and in its informal activity will be in the best possible position for carrying out the function in paragraph ( a).

I would hardly expect, minuscule though the amendment is, that it could be accepted in its present form. As I read the 1973 Act we should by making this amendment deprive the Human Rights Commission of any function whatsoever. That is something which could be argued in another context. But it is certainly not the intention 532 of my hon. Friends and myself. Our intention is to draw the attention of the Minister and the Committee to what appears to be a very serious overlap in the functions of the two bodies. We all know that the Human Rights Commission is going about its work on a different plane and in a different setting from that in which the agency will operate. Whether the ultimate result of its deliberations and activities will be useful or not, it is premature to attempt to predict.

However, we are still worried by the fact that there is a complete overlap, as I see it, between the duty of the Commission under Section 20(1)( a) and the duty of the agency under this Bill.

I hope, therefore, that the Under-Secretary will be able to show how it is intended that, in future, the functions of these two bodies shall be kept apart because, quite clearly, if the agency is set up it will have a wealth of information and experience in the working of

"the law for the time being in force in preventing discrimination",

which no other body could possibly have.

It would be stupid of the Commission to say to the agency, "Tell us how you are getting on. What are the weaknesses of the Act?", and for the agency simply to say "This is the report that we have sent to the Minister and here is a carbon copy of it". This clearly cannot be the intention.

On the other hand, it is undesirable that we should part from the clause without a clear understanding of how the two bodies in future are intended respectively to function. The more distinct those functions can be in future, the more useful, as well as more economic, will the operations of each body turn out to be.

The Under-Secretary of State for Northern Ireland (Mr. J. D. Concannon)
: In moving his amendment, the right hon. Member for Down, South (Mr. Powell) gave the answer to it as well. As he said, the amendment has the effect of repealing the whole of Section 20(1) of the Northern Ireland Constitution Act 1973.

As it stands, the clause repeals only subsection 20(1)( b) of the Act and is necessary since the functions given to the Standing Advisory Commission on Human Rights under that subsection are, effectively, transferred to the Fair Employment Agency.

JPEG 533 Subsection 20(1)( a) of the Northern Ireland Constitution Act, which the amendment would also repeal, provides that the Standing Advisory Commission on Human Rights should advise the Secretary of State on the adequacy and effectiveness of the law in preventing discrimination no matter where it occurs, so paragraph ( a) confers general functions outside the scope of the Bill, outside the field of employment as well. So it is not only in employment, but on the grounds of religious belief or political opinion and in providing redress.

The amendment would, therefore, remove the whole raison d'être behind the Standing Advisory Commission on Human Rights. Even if, as the right hon. Gentleman said, this were to be the right policy, it would be wholly wrong so to provide in a Bill dealing with Fair Employment.

I hope that explanation satisfies the right hon. Gentleman, who said that his intention was not to do as the amendment provides but to probe around and see what could be done.

Mr. Powell
: I am obliged to the Under-Secretary for drawing attention to the important fact that Section 20(1)( a) of the 1973 Act is comprehensive, whereas this Bill deals only with employment.

The overlap, therefore, is limited to the field, important though it is, of employment. It would, perhaps, be right for me to give notice informally that I think that overlap deserves attention, and that that could conveniently be considered at a subsequent stage of the Bill, perhaps in terms of an amendment, to exclude employment, when referring to Northern Ireland, or whatever words might be appropriate, from the ambit of Section 20(1)( a) in the future.

I therefore hope that we shall have a further opportunity of considering the point, as narrowed quite rightly by the Under-Secretary. I only add that our brief debate is an illustration of the way in which the evolving constitutional situation influences our attitude towards Northern Ireland registration.

Probably, in practice, if this Bill had come first, then the feeling that one had to set up the Standing Advisory Commission might well have been postponed. We are in danger—this is a point worth briefly making—as a result of the successive bites that have been taken of the cherry—which is a very inadequate metaphor for 534 that of which bites are taken—of getting rather a clutter of different points of view superimposed on one another in successive strata of legislation.

New Clause 1
DISCRIMINATION BY EMPLOYEES

"It shall be unlawful for an employee to discriminate against a person, in relation to employment in Northern Ireland, where he seeks to prevent that person from obtaining employment on grounds of religious belief or political opinion by means of threat, obstruction, or by any other means".—[ Mr. Farr.]

Mr. Powell
: There lies behind all these amendments the single and simple point that there will come into the possession of the agency a vast amount of information. Provided that it is divested of any means of identifying particular persons or firms, the agency and its officers ought reasonably to be allowed to use that information freely. Therefore, it seemed to my hon. Friends and myself that the clause was drawn more restrictively than necessary, provided that the reservation were made for information relating to an identifiable firm or person.

The purpose behind these amendments, therefore, is not merely a simplification but is to ensure that, provided we can set on one side and fully protect the identi- 562 fiable information, there is no let or hindrance upon the agency or its officers making use of information which they have obtained in the course of their duties.

May I give one example of the sort of case we have in mind? Let us suppose that the agency is engaged in one of its conciliation procedures. It is trying to bring about a settlement upon certain terms. I see no reason why the agency or its officers should have any inhibition in saying to the employer and the employee, "As a matter of fact, we have a case where such-and-such was alleged, and we found that such-and-such was the outcome, which is why we are making this recommendation to you now with considerable confidence."

Provided that the agency or its officers in those circumstances does not go on to say that it was such-and-such a firm, or does not disclose information which points unambiguously to a particular firm, we ought not to hang restrictions around the use which the agency makes of unidentifiable material which comes into its possession. That is the purpose of the amendments.

Mr. Orme
