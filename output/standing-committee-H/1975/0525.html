10.30 a.m.

Resolved,

That during the proceedings on the Parliamentary and other Pensions and Salaries Bill the Committee do meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past Ten o'clock.—[Mr. Charles R. Morris.]

The Chairman: Before calling the first amendment I should remind the Committee that there is a Financial Resolution in connection with this Bill. Copies are in the Room.

Adequate notice should be given of amendments. As a general rule, I do not intend to call manuscript amendments. As hon. Members will have seen from the Notice Paper, all the amendments are starred, but there was little time for Members to put down amendments. They have all received careful consideration.

clause Clause 1
title MEMBER'S PENSIONABLE SALARY

Mr. Tim Rathbone: I beg to move, Amendment No. 2, in page 2, line 5, at end insert

'except that no such higher rate shall be specified in respect of an actual salary arrived at by a salary increase in excess of any Government wage restraint scheme (statutory or voluntary) then being applied'.

The Chairman: With this we can discuss the following:Amendment No. 5, page 2, line 18, at end add—

'(5) This section shall only apply to Members who retire or die on or before the dissolution of the present Parliament'.

New Clause 1 (Act not to apply to Members' salaries after increase)

'Notwithstanding any other provisions of this Act, if at any time the salaries of Members of Parliament are, in conformity with any wages and salaries policy of Her Majesty's Government then in force, increased to a higher vote than those paid on the posting of this Act, then the new rate of salary shall be a Member's pensionable salary for all purposes.'

Col. 4

Mr. Rathbone: The reason for this amendment harks back to the moment when the announcement was made of the increase in parliamentary salaries, which was four days before the announcement of the Government's extended wage restriction scheme which is now in operation or which is about to come into operation. It was at about that time that the Government saw fit to raise Members' salaries to £5,750, and that was in contrast both ways. Firstly it was in contrast to the £8,000 which was recommended by the Boyle Committee, and secondly it was in contrast to the £6 a week maximum wage increase which was to be announced four days subsequently. It is for this reason that I have suggested this amendment so that the rate presently in the clause,

"a Member's yearly salary at the rate specified in the resolution"

will be limited by any guidelines established at this moment or in the future by the Government for wage and salary increases throughout the country.

The reasons for this are that we—in the House of Commons—would be seen to be doing to ourselves what we are asking the rest of the people—voluntarily at the moment but with the threat of statutory limitations hanging in the wings—to do to themselves. It is unfortunate that running through this whole Bill there is an unspecified, albeit omnipresent, self-servingness about establishing a method of pensions which is unique to the Members and officers who are mentioned in the Bill. It is so that we can ask people to do as we do rather than do as we say that I move this amendment.

Mr. Paul Dean: I am grateful to you, Sir Stephen, for selecting with the amendment which has been moved by my hon. Friend the Member for Lewes (Mr. Rathbone) Amendment No. 5, which is in my name. This is an attempt at a compromise following the House's acceptance of this Bill on Second Reading.

We have to be extremely careful about how we proceed in this area for two very good reasons. The first is that we have the power, denied to pretty well any other section of the community, of fixing our own pay and pension arrangements. Having this power, surely it is an obligation upon us to see that we exercise it with great restraint, and show an example to the rest of the community. The other thing which is important is that, although it may be said that our pension arrangements are still nothing like as good as they should be, the fact is that they have been substantially improved over recent years and we now have the benefit of inflation-proof provisions along with the public services generally. I recognise that during pay restrictions the hardest case is nearly always that of those who retire while restrictions are on. If the final salary is held down, then in any scheme based on the final salary the pension is held down, too, and this can last for the rest of the pensioner's life. He can suffer for evermore, whereas those still in work have at least an opportunity to catch up when the pay restrictions are removed. I have, therefore, tried in this amendment to identify this group and have suggested that it would be appropriate for hon. Members who die—and whose widows or other dependants would be entitled to some benefit under the pension schemes—or who retire before or on the dissolution of Parliament to be in a special position and that it should be possible for their pensions to be calculated on a notional pay basis.

In other words, we would be establishing a privileged position which, I believe, would be reflected in all future pension arrangements for hon. Members.

I was saying that the hardest cases will come among those who die in the immediate future or who retire while the restrictions are still on. For this reason I have suggested, and I hope that I am being helpful, a compromise—that we should allow these privileged arrangements for hon. Members in that category. For the rest, those who will be here thereafter, an objectionable principle enshrined in this Bill, and it is, above all, one which we deny to the rest of the community.
