Mr. David Watkins
: This is the Question we were discussing when the Committee rose last week, and it would be appropriate if I explained some of the reasons for the proposed deletion of the clause.

It is an important clause, and the only alteration of any real substance which is proposed in the Bill arises from the 18 deletion of the clause. I recall that on Second Reading the hon. Member for Chingford (Mr. Tebbit) rightly said that it was a clause which required rigorous examination, and at our last sitting the hon. Member for Eastleigh (Mr. Price) felt that an explanation should be given of why it was proposed that the clause should not stand part of the Bill. I agree with that.

The omission of the clause does not in any way preclude the conversion of companies into common ownership enterprises, which can take place under existing legislation. Indeed, a number of them have so taken place. I remind the Committee that Scott Bader Commonwealth was a conversion made no less than a quarter of a century ago under existing law, and more recently, the Airflow Community at High Wycombe has also been converted under existing law. The main point I make is that the withdrawal of the clause, which specifically refers to conversions, does not in any way preclude conversions from taking place.

The clause was intended to simplify the procedure for conversions but, regrettably, investigation showed that eliminating certain problems would create others which could well be worse than those we were seeking to eliminate. First, I refer to the creation of loan stock, which is proposed in the clause. I am advised that the total extinction of share capital would damage creditors. Hon. Members will be aware that in the event of a failure creditors take priority over shareholders, so, clearly, as was stated on Second Reading, there could be difficulties there. There is also the fact that such loan stock would have to be serviced and repaid at the end of the loan period, which could conceivably be a difficult and insupportable burden on a firm.

On a much wider scale, there is no existing machinery within company law for the conversion of shares into loan stock. To create such machinery would be a complex matter beyond the scope of a Private Member's Bill. It may be said that one of the virtues of the Bill is that it has highlighted the need for a general revision of company law in the light of developments which are taking place. It is a problem Governments of all complexions have thought about, and which needs to be tackled by the Government on a long-term basis.

JPEG 19 I feel some regret at the withdrawal of the clause, but it would create difficulties greater than those which it was intended to resolve, and its proposals are more complex than was expected when we originally drafted the Bill. For those 20 reasons, I have to advise the Committee that the clause should not stand part of the Bill.

New Clause 2
COMMON OWNERSHIP ENTERPRISES AND CO-OPERATIVE ENTERPRISES

' .—(1) For the purposes of this Act a common ownership enterprise is a body as to which the registrar has given, and has not revoked, a certificate stating that he is satisfied—

  1. that the body is—
    1. a company which has no share capital, is limited by guarantee and is a bona fide co-operative society; or
    2. a society registered or deemed to be registered under the Industrial and Provident Societies Acts 1965 to 1975; and
  2. that the memorandum or articles of association or rules of the body include provisions which secure—
    1. that only persons who are employed by, or by a subsidiary of, the body may be members of it, that (subject to any provision about qualifications for membership which is from time to time made by the members of the body by reference to age, length of service or other factors of any description) all such persons may be members of the body and that members have equal voting rights at meetings of the body,
    2. that the assets of the body are applied only for the purposes of objects of the body which do not include the making over of assets to any member of the body except for value and except in pursuance of arrangements for sharing the profits of the body among its members, and
    3. that, if on the winding-up or dissolution of the body any of its assets remain to be disposed of after its liabilities are satisfied, the assets are not distributed among its members but are transferred to such a common ownership enterprise or such a central fund maintained for the benefit of common ownership enterprises as may be determined by the members at or before the time of the winding-up or dissolution or, in so far as the assets are not so transferred, are held for charitable purposes.

(2) For the purposes of this Act a co-operative enterprise is a body as to which the Secretary of State has given, and has not revoked, a certificate stating that he is satisffed that—

  1. having regard to the provision which is made by the written constitution of the body as to the manner in which the income of the body is to be applied for the benefit of its members and all other relevant provisions of the constitution, the body is in substance a co-operative association; and
  2. the body is controlled by a majority of the people working for the body and of the people working for the subsidiaries, if any, of the body.

(3) The registrar may charge such a fee as the Treasury may determine for any certificate which he proposes to give in pursuance of this section, and any sum received by him in respect of such a fee shall be paid into the Consolidated Fund.

(4) If it appears to the registrar or the Secretary of State that a body in respect of which he has given a certificate in pursuance of this section has ceased to be a common ownership enterprise or a co-operative enterprise, he may revoke the certificate.

(5) In this section—

1948 c. 38. "company" means a company as defined by section 455(1) of the Companies Act 1948 or a company registered under that 45 Act; and
1974 c. 46. "the registrar" means the Chief Registrar within the meaning of the Friendly Societies Act 1974;
and for the purposes of this section a body is a subsidiary of another body if it is a subsidiary of the other body for the purposes of the Companies Act 1948 or the Friendly and Industrial and Provident Societies Act 1968'.—[ Mr. Les Huckfield.]

I hope that the Committee will forgive me if I speak at length this morning. This is a rather complicated new clause designed to replace the original Clause 1 of the Bill. Basically, the new clause seeks to define for the purposes of the Bill what is meant by a common ownership enterprise and a co-operative enterprise. I know that those who have been campaigning for these improvements for many years, including my hon. The new clause seeks to define a common ownership enterprise as a body to which the Chief Registrar of Friendly Societies has given a certificate stating that he is satisfied about certain criteria relating to the structure of the body and the rules under which it functions. The Chief Registrar will be entitled to charge a fee for the issue of the certificate and, of course, the certificate may be revoked if it appears to the Chief Registrar that the body in question has ceased to be a common ownership enterprise.

I suppose that my hon. Friends and hon. Gentlemen opposite may be tempted to ask why it was necessary to amend the original Clause 1. One important difference is that in new Clause 2 the Chief Registrar of Friendly Societies is limited to certifying on the basis of facts ascertainable by checking the relevant documents. My Department takes the view that it would not be right to expect the chief registrar to take on a very burdensome and, I think, unnecessary task of forming value judgments on the ownership and control of an enterprise which might necessitate substantial inquiries. Under the original Clause 1 that kind of inquiry would have been necessary.

Subsection (2) of new Clause 1 would widen the scope of the Bill to include co-operative enterprises. The subsection defines a co-operative enterprise as a body to which the Secretary of State has given a certificate stating that he is satisfied about certain criteria relating to the body's constitution and the way in which it is controlled. Again, the certificate may be revoked if it appears to the Secretary of State that the body in question has ceased to be a co-operative enterprise.

The provisions of subsection (3) of the old Clause 1 are not included in new Clause 2 because there seems to be no need to specify a minimum number of members for a common ownership enter-price or that the membership should comprise a specified proportion—for example, 65 per cent.—of those employed in the enterprise. The important points are that only employees should be eligible for membership of the enterprise, JPEG 23 and that all employees should be entitled to be members, subject to provisions of qualification in appropriate cases.

I move now to subsection (4) of the old Clause 1. There were two rather serious objections to that subsection. First, the limitation on the distribution of an enterprise's residual assets was confined in the old subsection (4) only to the circumstances of winding up or dissolution so that such assets might have been shared among the members prior to the business being formally dissolved. The new Clause 2 provisions will apply a restriction of this sort throughout the existence of the enterprise, so we are seeking to extend the provision in that respect.

Secondly, the old Clause 1(4) imposed a function on the High Court to select a suitable distribution for the assets of a dissolved or wound-up enterprise in the event of the members failing to decide the matter. This, we feel, is not a proper function for the courts, and consequently it is provided in new Clause 2( b)(iii) that such residual assets, in the absence of determination by the members, should be held for charitable purposes.

I shall now sum up my remarks at this stage. Basically, we seek to change the duties which will be imposed upon the registrar, particularly with regard to certification, as we do not think that he should have to deal with quite the burden of work originally proposed. Second, we seek to amend the definition of common ownership or co-operative enterprise, and we believe that the definition in the new clause is more fitting than that given in the original Clause 1. Then we seek to change certain provisions in the old clause on the distribution of funds.
