NEW TOWNS (AMENDMENT) BILL

Clause 2
CONSULTATIONS WITH A VIEW TO TRANSFER SCHEMES

Further amendment proposed [29th April] No. 19, in page 3, line 27, after "district", insert "or county".— [Mr. Raison].

10.30 a.m.

Question again proposed.

Mr. Joseph Dean
: At our last sitting, the hon. Member for Bromsgrove and Redditch (Mr. Miller) said that when county councils were involved it should be mandatory for them to be part of the consultative process and that they should be able to initiate consultations. I do not believe we can put into the Bill a clause to suit every contingency. In the metropolitan areas formed two years ago as a result of the last local government reorganisation the responsibility for housing was correctly left with district authorities. There has been constant friction between councils and district councils in metropolitan areas over who does what in relation to certain duties. The Bill will have to be very specific on this matter.

Last week I said that new towns in the old Lancashire area would be affected. The area of friction, if county councils had a mandatory right of consultation, could be even larger. For instance, it might cover the whole of the North-West, where there are at least two new town areas which would be controlled by a district authority which is a housing authority. If, without a great deal of thought, we include county councils in the consultative process, it will be another issue which could divide county and district authorities in local government, and there are far too many things already dividing them to the disadvantage of the people they represent. I know from my own experience that this has happened.

94 People shudder when there is talk of another reform of local government which must be made to work, but the sooner we grasp the nettle and make unitary authorities a totality, the sooner local government will function properly.

There would be biter resentment in some metropolitan districts, many of which have large housing authorities, if county councils were to initiate consultations. The situation in some regions might be different. County authorities may have had an historic role in these matters, and it might be acceptable for them to be included in the consultative process. However, giving them that right in metropolitan areas is a recipe for more friction, which can do no good to the people involved in the very things this Bill is supposed to be about.

The Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Mr. Guy Barnett)
: This has been a useful debate because it has enabled us to consider whether county councils should be involved in consultations about the transfer of assets.

My hon. Friend the Member for Leeds, West (Mr. Dean) pointed out the confusion in functions which sometimes exists between county and district councils as a result of the reorganisation of local government. He was right when he said it would not be proper for county councils to be able to initiate consultations in what is a matter of complete concern to district councils. I am sure that, on reflection, the Opposition will agree.

The hon. Members for Aylesbury (Mr. Raison) and Bromsgrove and Redditch (Mr. Miller) made powerful cases on the ground that county councils were responsible for education and roads and were, therefore, inevitably involved in new towns. However, we are talking about the transfer of virtually complete estates or towns.

I agree with both hon. Members on the importance of ensuring, as far as possible, close consultation and agreement between county and, district councils where there is a split of responsibility between, say, housing and education—in the past there have been too many occasions when housing estates have been built and the local schools have not been big enough—but I do not think we need to regard the county council's rôle in JPEG 95 consultation quite as highly as the Opposition have made out. We are talking about the transfer of completed estates where, presumably, schools are in operation and roads have been completed.

Mr. Timothy Raison
: I am prepared to accept that our amendment may give greater power to county councils than is justified, but before this useful debate ends could the Minister say whether he is prepared to consider the possibility of providing a formal right for county councils to be consulted? Would he be prepared to amend the Bill to ensure that county councils' views are sought during the preparation of a scheme?

The county is the principal planning authority and has a right to be involved in matters which involve substantial chunks of housing. Though county councils should not necessarily have a right to initiate consultations or a right of veto, they should have a right to be consulted.

Mr. Guy Barnett
: It has been considered that the interests of county councils can be protected through administrative guidance, but I am prepared to look at the matter to see whether there is some way in which county councils' interests can be protected and to see whether we can write in a guarantee of consultation. Important points have been made in the debate. I hope the Opposition will be prepared to accept the assurance I have just given.

Mr. Raison
: In view of that assurance, for which I am grateful, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

On Second Reading we made clear that we accepted the principle of the Bill, though I explained that some of my hon. Friends had some reservations. We feel that the Bill should have provided an opportunity to stimulate the sale of new town houses, to sitting tenants in particular. That is what the amendment seeks to achieve.

I accept immediately that it is possible that the amendment is technically imperfect. Opposition amendments are sometimes, to put it mildly, technically imperfect. If the Government say that they accept the spirit of our amendment and will come up with a better drafted scheme at a later stage, there will be rejoicing on our side, and that will be that.

The amendment gives us a chance to consider something which we believe profoundly is the right approach to be taken to housing in new town areas. Why do we advocate so strongly that there should be the sale of new town houses primarily to sitting tenants? Why do we believe it is right that there should be extensive home ownership in the new towns, and that one of the most useful ways of stimulating such ownership is by the sale of existing stock?

There are a number of reasons for our advocating the sale of new town houses, and to a great extent they are the same reasons that lead us to believe that ordinary local authorities should be able 108 to sell council houses to sitting tenants. As it happens, we have had one or two opportunities recently in the House to debate these matters. We were able to have such a debate on Second Reading, and we spent some time last week in the debate on local government considering the same matters.

The first reason for our believing in the sale of council houses to tenants or new town houses—I suppose that in many ways it is the best of all reasons—is that it satisfies human aspirations. In my view, it adds to the stock of human happiness, and in many cases it is something which tenants welcome. However, if they do not like the idea they do not have to buy their house. There is no problem about that.

Such evidence as exists demonstrates that the sale of new town houses and council houses is genuinely popular. In this context I refer to the Cullingworth Report on "The Ownership and Management of Housing in the New Towns". Admittedly it was produced a decade or so ago, but it contains some quite solid evidence on the desires of those who live in new towns. Chapter 7, in page 66, reads:

"However, though we did not ask for opinions on the relative advantages of public and individual ownership, we did ask for views on the sale of development corporation houses to tenants, and the results were startling."

Those results are given in subsequent tables, which I shall summarise. I must explain that the Cullingworth Report did some serious research. To dismiss it, as one Labour Member did recently, as some minor form of opinion poll is misleading. It was a serious piece of research that appeared in serious Government-sponsored publications.

The startling results to which the report refers relates to inquiries which took place in four of the new towns, namely, Crawley, Stevenage, Aycliffe and East Kilbride. In answer to the question whether houses should be offered for sale to tenants, it appears that—I shall not read out the full details, I shall merely add together the results of the question in the four towns—12 per cent. said "No", 71 per cent. said "Yes", and 17 per cent. gave a qualified "Yes". There could not have been a much more overwhelming vote than that. The figures relate to whole town samples. The Cullingworth JPEG 109 Committee took sample opinions from everyone who lived in the areas rather than specifically questioning new town tenants. However, the Cullingworth Committee asked development corporation commission tenants for their views. It asked whether houses should be offered for sale to tenants.
