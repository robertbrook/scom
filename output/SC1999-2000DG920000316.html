Ms Jowell
: I want to make progress. I shall be happy to allow interventions later.

The regulations support the plans to introduce a single point of entry or gateway into the benefits system. That gateway will focus both on work and on supporting people who can work in returning to the labour market. That is why we are bringing together the Employment Service, the Benefits Agency and local authorities to provide a streamlined service that gives claimants one point of contact for all their benefits needs. That is known as ONE and the model is currently being piloted in the 12 areas covered by the regulations.

The prime purpose of the regulations is to make it a requirement for the receipt of benefit for all working-age claimants living in the pilot areas who make new claims to benefits to take part in a work-focused interview. Interviews will usually take place as part of the claims process, although there will be discretion to waive or defer the requirement if that is justified by the circumstances of an individual case. That point was well aired during the passage of the Welfare Reform and Pensions Act 1999. There will also be provision for repeat interviews, which will be triggered by prescribed events such as the receipt of a personal capacity assessment or the continuous receipt of benefit.

The ONE pilots have been running in four areas since June. A further eight pilots, testing call-centre technology and four are led by private and voluntary sector consortiums, started in November. They are already beginning to show possible benefits in helping people to plan a route from benefit to work. They have been well received by people who claim benefit, who have welcomed the streamlined service, the personal approach and the single point of access. For example, a woman attended a start-up at Leamington Spa to inquire about claiming income support because her husband had just left her. She was going to give up her part-time job at a local school and move on to benefit. However, at the critical time, she saw a personal adviser who calculated that she could claim family credit and be much better off than she would be on income support. Fast family credit was applied for, and she remained in work rather than claiming benefit. That would not have happened under the old system. Her claim would have been accepted, and she might have remained on benefit for years. That is a personal example of why we strongly support this approach.

Participation in interviews has not been obligatory until now, but the number of people who have volunteered has been encouraging. Some 64 per cent. of lone parents and 54 per cent. of sick and disabled people have agreed to take part in work-focused interviews. Although the figures are encouraging, they emphasise why we need the regulations. More than 44 per cent. of those offered an interview are not taking up the option. We are therefore not able to advise 8 them of the benefits of participation, and they do not have access to the help and support that is available. They might remain on benefit when, with appropriate help, they could get into work.

The provisions will not require lone parents or disabled people to take a job or undergo training. Steps that they take beyond attendance at an interview are voluntary. They have a choice. However, the mandatory interview ensures that that is an informed choice.

There are 17 regulations and I am sure the Committee does not want me to go through each in great detail, but it may be of assistance to highlight some of the key provisions.

Regulation 4 provides for someone of working age who lives in a pilot area and wishes to claim one or more of the specified benefits to take part in a work-focused interview. The benefits to which this provision applies are those specified in section 2A of the Social Security Administration Act 1992: income support, incapacity benefit, severe disablement allowance, invalid care allowance, widows' and bereavement benefit, housing benefit and council tax benefit. A personal advisor will be appointed and the interview will take place in a ONE office or elsewhere as appropriate. Claimants under the age of 18 will have a personal advisor, but the requirement to take part in a work-focused interview will be modified for them. Their interviews will be with an officer of the careers service and will focus principally on training and educational opportunities.

Regulation 5 identifies those who claim a prescribed benefit, but who will be exempt from the requirement to take part in a work-focused interview. Some of the prescribed benefits—for example, widows' benefit and housing benefit—can be claimed by people who are already in work. For them, a work-focused interview would not be necessary, so they will be exempt. People who are claiming or are entitled to a jobseeker's allowance are required to undertake work-related activity as one of the conditions for receiving that JPEG 9 benefit. The provisions therefore are not appropriate for them.

Finally, people who are not habitually resident in Great Britain are excluded for obvious practical reasons.

Mr. Fabricant

Ms Jowell
: It is important to be clear. If they are not habitually resident in Great Britain, they are not eligible for benefit and therefore the issue of fraud would not arise.

Regulation 6 deals with repeat interviews. It provides that, in order to continue receiving full benefit, claimants may be required to take part in a work-focused interview at specified points throughout the lifetime of their benefit claim.

Regulations 7 and 8 are important provisions in that they allow for the requirement to take part in an interview to be waived or deferred. The requirement may be waived if an interview would be of no assistance to the claimant or would not be appropriate in the circumstances of the case. It may be deferred if it would be neither appropriate nor of assistance at that particular time.

Mr. Pickles
: On that specific point, who determines whether or not to defer the interview?

Ms Jowell
: That decision would be made by the personal adviser. The hon. Gentleman is no doubt aware that personal advisers have received, are receiving and will continue to receive careful training in making these decisions.

Regulation 11 sets out what a client must do to satisfy the requirement that he take part in the interview. He must attend at the appointed time and place and must answer questions related to his employment prospects when asked. These questions will cover the following areas: educational qualifications, employment history, any vocational training undertaken, skills acquired, any paid or unpaid work he has engaged in and any medical condition that he or she considers puts him or her at a disadvantage in gaining employment; and any caring or child care responsibilities. As I said earlier, that does not put any requirement on the claimant to take work following the interview.

Regulation 12 sets out the consequences of failing to take part without good cause. In a new claim in which the requirement to take part in an interview has not been deferred, the client will be treated as not having made a claim, so benefit will not be awarded. In a new claim in which the interview has been deferred and benefit has been paid, benefit will be terminated. In cases in which a claimant fails to take part in a repeat interview under regulation, benefit will be reduced by an amount equivalent to 20 per cent. of the income support personal allowance for someone over 25.

10 Claimants will be encouraged to take part at all stages and will have numerous opportunities to do so. If, during an interview, claimants appear not to be taking part, the personal adviser will make it clear to them that their inaction might prejudice their entitlement to benefit.

Regulation 14 provides a non-exhaustive list of matters to be taken into account in considering good cause for failure to take part in an interview, including misunderstanding the requirement to take part as a result of language, literacy, learning difficulties or official misdirection; a pre-arranged medical or dental appointment that could not be rearranged; difficulties with normal transport; established customs and practices of someone's religion preventing them from attending interview; seeking work; an accident; sudden illness or relapse; attendance at the funeral of a close friend or relative; or a disability preventing someone from attending interview.

JPEG 11 Regulation 15 provides a right of appeal against a decision that a person has failed to take part without good cause, whether as originally made or revised. All decisions taken by delegated authorities will be treated as decisions of the Secretary of State and will be heard by the independent Appeals Service.

Let me briefly move on to the contracting out order. Private and voluntary sector organisations are leading the delivery of the ONE service in four of the 12 pilot areas. They are Deloitte Consulting in Suffolk and Leeds, Reed/Shaw Trust in, north Nottinghamshire and Action for Employment in North Cheshire. This order is designed to enable employees of those organisations to perform certain functions in relation to work-focused interviews. The order relates only to functions connected with the ONE service: it does not cover any other social security functions, such as those related to the determination of claims to benefits.

However, the Committee should note that the provisions are not restricted to the pilot areas and could be used more generally if private and voluntary sector providers are to be involved in any expansion of the ONE service to other parts of the country—a point that I dealt with earlier.

Paragraph 2 of the order allows for specified functions of the Secretary of State to be exercised by those whom he authorises in that behalf. Those authorised to exercise those functions and the extent to which they may be exercised will be detailed in contracts between the Secretary of State and the providers. The functions that may be contracted out are those relating to work-focused interviews, the revision of decisions related to work-focused interviews and certain functions relating to claims for housing benefit and council tax benefit.

That means that the contractor or his employee may decide, for example, whether a work-focused interview should be waived or deferred, whether a client has taken part in such an interview or whether there was good cause for failure to comply with the requirement. They may also revise or supersede such decisions, for example, where fresh information comes to light. Paragraph 3 makes similar provision in relation to local authorities.

We have had a full and illuminating exchange about the regulations and the contracting out order, which are an important building block in the Government's programme of welfare reform, moving people who can work from benefit into work. I commend the regulations and the order to the Committee.
