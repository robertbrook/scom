Food Law and Consumer Health Safety

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Ministry of Agiculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Jeff Rooker)
: I welcome on behalf of the Government the opportunity to debate two important documents that were published by the European Commission this year. They were presented by the Commission as representing a new approach to consumer health and food safety, which will benefit both consumers and the industries involved in producing and supplying food.

The first document, the communication on consumer health and food safety, describes the reorganisation of the Commission's departments dealing with consumer health. The scientific committees that give advice have been grouped together in one directorate-general under Commissioner Bonino, and the system has been strengthened by the establishment of a steering committee, which will consider issues that affect more than one policy area. Appointments to the committee will follow advertisements rather than nominations from member states. Commission officials responsible for monitoring member states' enforcement and inspection activities have also been transferred to the directorate-general and their working methods have been reviewed.

The report of the Select Committee on European Legislation states that the three principles set out in the document, of scientific excellence, independence and transparency, are ones that we can endorse—a view that I hope members of this Committee share. However, the Select Committee is concerned about how the new arrangements will operate and how they will fit in with national arrangements.

The developments have the potential to strengthen the process of obtaining independent scientific advice in the European Union and improving the openness of the process. That is consistent with some of the changes that we are making in the United Kingdom to establish the food standards agency, under which there will be greater independence and transparency based on reliance on the best available scientific advice.

The Commission's second document, the Green Paper on the general principles of food law in the European Union, is a detailed survey of European Union food law and poses many questions about the future direction of policy. It sets out six fundamental goals of Community law; it considers the scope for repeal of existing law or extensions into new areas and it describes the means adopted to ensure effective implementation of agreed rules.

2 The Government are the first to admit that the Green Paper poses questions rather than offers solutions, but we agree with the Select Committe that it must be seen as an influential text for the future, because it has started a debate among all interested parties, not just member states. The Government have set out to ensure that United Kingdom interests take a full part in that debate.

We widely publicised the Green Paper and provided a copy of it on request. We also arranged meetings with key organisations representing consumers, retailers, manufacturers and producers. Some 24 organisations have shown us their response to the Commission, although others may have sent it in direct. While all the organisations broadly welcome the opportunity of a debate on the future of European Union food law, consumer groups by and large seek more harmonisation of the rules, for example, on claims, nutrition and labelling. On the other hand, industry has argued for consolidation and simplification of the existing rules, and for better efforts to be made on implementation and enforcement. The Government have responded publicly to the Commission and copied their response to other member states and interested organisations.

In Brussels, the Luxembourg presidency is having open debates on the Green Paper in four councils—the Agriculture, Consumer, Internal Market and Health Councils. The European Parliament and the Commission held a joint meeting of invited representatives of member states and interest groups earlier this month. Following those debates, I hope that the Commission will feel able to synthesise the comments received and draw the appropriate practical conclusions as soon as possible. In our view, a more rational and systematic approach to food law within the Community is long overdue. The next step should be agreement within the Council on a medium-term plan for staged implementation of the necessary legislative reforms.

In conclusion, the Government would like to see the European Union food law move from a producer-orientated to a consumer-orientated perspective. However, it must be made crystal clear that we are talking of the genuine and legitimate needs of consumers and not of giving legislators, wherever they are, carte blanche to introduce new forms of protectionism based on spurious subjective criteria or to react to every food scare with draconian new restrictions. Consumers want to be able to rely on controls that guarantee the safety of food, but they also want diversity and choice.

Provided that safety regulations are met, the Government will continue to oppose rules that narrow the range of foods available and prohibit foods or processes that do not conform to a traditional recipe or process from one part or another of the Community. We believe that more information must be made available to consumers, including that provided on food labels, to allow them to make an informed choice. Indeed, we made it clear in our JPEG 3 written response that a widening of consumer choice should be seen as a seventh fundamental objective of European Union food law.

The Community's food law has, for many reasons, developed in an unco-ordinated way; most members of the Committee will understand why. Applying the principles of subsidiarity in such a situation is difficult. The time has come to devise a common policy—perhaps in the form of a general food directive—to establish common principles and definitions; it should be tied coherently to more detailed horizontal directives on labelling, additives, nutrition and so on, and expanded to vertical controls only when necessary on particular foods. It is crucial that enforcement officers should have simple, clear legislation to follow, legislation that ordinary people can understand, and which can be enforced uniformly across the EU.

As I said, the paper offers more questions than solutions. I can therefore be asked a wide range of questions this morning. I shall do my best to answer hon. Members. If I cannot, I shall give them a written response.

We must be guided by science as well as by common sense and if science points to areas of greater risk, that is where we must shift resources.

We do not intend to send food police into every nook and cranny of shops, cutting plants and abattoirs—far from it. We need to check to ensure that a product is safe, healthy and wholesome, so that consumers can have confidence, and to spend money in the areas of highest perceived risk.

Regulation and direction depend on the context. In some areas, regulations are better when specific controls are imposed. The additives directives, for example, are extremely detailed and would probably 38 be better implemented by regulation. We must use common sense about that. Nevertheless—let us be honest—directives and regulations are sometimes the subject of a turf war in Brussels. The issue cannot necessarily be laid down in tablets of stone.

On the sloppy use of language, we are not dealing with internal documents. It is a matter of diplomacy. We all occasionally hear foreign affairs Ministers and ambassadors putting the most outrageous points of principle into the most delicate language when they are trying to put a country's case. Whether that is done at the United Nations or in Brussels, we have to understand it. We are also trying to use delicate language, with the aim of achieving success. We do not wish to antagonise our European partners. We want the Green Paper to be a success and we want to give a lead on it. We seek to improve it by adding to its basic principles. We cannot do that by megaphone diplomacy. It is much better to use moderate, common-sense language, but to be hard when making decisions and that is what we shall do, as we have already shown. We are not subservient and we want to be good members of the European Community, but, where we have to, we shall take action in British interests. When the time for decision comes, the hard edge will show. In the mean time, we want to reach an acceptable position and we shall not do that by being bellicose and using wild language.

I have covered some aspects of the scientific community and I have no doubt that the issue will arise again this year. The right hon. Member for Fylde asked a key question about the UK presidency. We shall indeed, during January to June next year, seek to publish the list of EC rules that we want repealed and the reasons why. That gives us a target date. I hope that that is an acceptable time scale.

I cannot deal with the definition of "wholesomeness" or of athletes' food, but I can say that there will be no extension of the nine, limited categories of the two types of infant milks, weaning foods, slimming foods, medical foods, gluten-free foods, diabetic foods, low-salt foods and athletes' foods.
