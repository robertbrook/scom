Mr. Galbraith
: It is interesting to note that there are probably more amendments to this clause than to the rest of the Bill put together. There must be a lesson in that, but I'm hanged if I know what it is.

Three themes run through the amendments. First, why should there be a change? Amendment No. 190 will allow us to discuss that. Secondly, will the persons to whom those powers are entrusted be suitable and adequate, and what safeguards do we have? Thirdly, to whom, other than the Secretary of State and the service, will the new service be answerable for standards and quality? I want to introduce those three themes. I hope that my hon. Friend the Member for Delyn (Mr. Hanson) will catch your eye, Mr. Knox, and develop them.

It is clear to the Opposition that the purpose of the clause is to privatise the careers service—to jeopardise training, education and careers advice for a vulnerable group. That is partly to pave the way for the abolition of local education authorities with the progress of the grant-maintained schools. That is the not-too-hidden agenda which the Minister, when he tells us that we have had an interesting debate and that many points have been raised, will dress up with excuses about efficiency, flexibility and local accountability. Every time that I hear those words I know that the reality will be the opposite.

The Scottish Office, in a consultation exercise in 1992, acknowledged from the start that the careers system, as constituted, functions extremely well and that there are few problems with it. The Department of Employment's internal review—the Bruce review—generally supported the careers service. It is regrettable that the results of that review were never published.

Some partnerships are developing in careers services along lines suggested by various individuals and they are beneficial. It is not necessary to legislate to develop that part of the careers service.

But this clause is draconian and is intended to mess up the service from top to bottom.

Local education authorities in England and education authorities in Scotland, as constituted, provide a valuable and useful function. The clause is not necessary. As constituted, the careers service is an essential client-orientated service. We fear that the clause would lead to the loss of that purely client-orientated service. The careers service is not a citizens advice bureau—somewhere people go when they have problems—but the first place people go to get good careers advice which should prevent future problems. It gives advice and guidance at the start of their careers. Therefore, the responsible body must be able to gain access to school curricula at an early stage and to plan them jointly with teachers of a range of subjects.

It is important to remember that the careers service might be an aid to recruitment for employers but that it is not a recruitment agency per se. That distinction is important. The problem with some of the Bill's provisions is that there will be a conflict of opinion, particularly if local enterprise companies and training and enterprise JPEG 689 councils become involved and the careers advice service becomes a method for filling the requisite number of places in the LEC or TEC provisions. That must not happen. That is not the function of a careers service. The function is to supply careers advice, not to act as a recruitment agency.

Without that conflict of interest, the careers service offers impartial advice with no vested interest in favour of a certain educational establishment, training organisation or employer. It can assess a comprehensive range of information provided by the school or college on the ability, character and ambitions of each client, and it develops close links with schools and staff and with employers in various other organisations. The careers service provides a service that is tailored to individuals' needs and provides them with a client-orientated service, which is in the best interests of the individual. There is no conflict of interest. The careers service is not an employment agency. That is the most important theme in the amendments.

The amendments are all on the same basis. They delete reference to "other persons" or introduce qualifying adjectives for those other persons so that they are able to provide simple vocational and educational guidance. We want to ensure that the careers service is not ripped off in the TECs and the LECs by inexperienced cowboys who are handed taxpayers' money which goes down a black hole and provides nothing. We do not want that to happen. We want to ensure that, when the Secretary of State takes the powers, they are given to qualified, experienced people who are accepted in the community and who have the confidence of all involved.

To ensure that that happens, monitoring and assessment will be necessary. That is dealt with in amendments Nos. 252 and 208, which mention a statutory body to implement those provisions. A person or body, other than the Secretary of State, must be answerable for what happens. That person or body cannot be a cowboy who has set up as a careers service.

Those are the main aims of the amendments. The Bill provides for the privatisation of the careers service and a paving clause to make easier the abolition of LEAs. We are opposed to the clause and want to amend it.

Ms. Angela Eagle
(Wallasey)
: I wish to concentrate on amendments Nos. 245 and 248, which are crucial to achieving an amelioration of the more draconian aspects of the clause. As my hon. Friend the Member for Strathkelvin and Bearsden (Mr. Galbraith) said, the clause centralises power and gives total discretion to the Secretary of State in a way that is shocking to behold. We are trying, through the amendments, to surround the clause with checks and balances to insure that the outcome causes as little damage as possible to careers structures which already exist and which are begining to do a good job.

I want to spend a little time considering what good careers guidance is about, how it has been supplied in the past, and the implications of the clause. Local education authorities currently have a statutory duty to provide a careers service for pupils and all those in higher education in their areas. That is set out in sections 8 to 10 of the Employment and Training Act 1973. Until now, therefore, the careers service has been run as an integral 690 part of local education authority provision. However, as discussions are taking place in other Committees about changing structures of local authorities and education provision, one suspects that changes in the structure of local education authorities are being anticipated and that the Government are attempting to create a system that will fit into a new model of opted-out schools and the fracturing of our education system. There are problems with that approach and some dangers, which I shall discuss later.

The careers service has run reasonably successfully as part of the integrated local authority provision, although, in 1990, the Government announced a review and, in 1991, published a consultation document which suggested three possible ways forward. Completely out of the blue, they said that the status quo was no option because "times have changed." Clearly, the Government think that the status quo is no option, and three options were proposed as possible models on which to develop future careers guidance. The first option was that TECs should have full responsibility. My hon. Friend the Member for Strathkelvin and Bearsden highlighted one of the dangers of following that course; that TECs would act as recruiting agencies to fill jobs rather than pursue the real aims of careers guidance.

The second option was education business partnerships between local education authorities and TECs. In fact, since that consultation document was issued, several successful partnerships have begun to develop. The people who have put much work and commitment into those partnerships were commended by a Government document evaluating the initiative on careers service partnerships. Those same people have been shocked to discover the contents of clause 33, which appears to throw away all that work and take the third option mentioned in the original paper—contracting out to competitive tender. We share their anxiety that that is the Government's intention.

The clause is so short and provides so few safeguards that we are worried about the system that will result from privatisation. Amendments Nos. 245,247,248,250 and 251 would help to ensure local accountability of the new service-providers to teachers, to educational establishments in which they may have to spend some time operating, to clients and to the local community. Other amendments would ensure that only fit persons were allowed to offer such advice and would establish monitoring mechanisms aimed at keeping quality high. It is important that we have those monitoring systems because careers guidance should play an important part in the development of children and young adults. Those systems should ensure that they not only get the most out of the choices that are offered to them but go to places where they will be happiest and most effective. It is a complex issue.

The purpose of careers guidance is to help individuals to make wise decisions when they plan or develop a career. The labour market is increasingly complex, with a plethora of qualifications and training institutions. An individual who has to make such decisions should have some professional careers advice.

Careers guidance also enables individuals to choose and plan their path through life. An important phrase that JPEG 691 I have come across in my consideration of this matter is that careers guidance enables young people to unlock their potential. That is very different from filling empty jobs or acting as a recruitment agency.

When properly done, careers guidance serves as an enabling process that allows individuals to have their eyes lifted to wider horizons. They can have a path plotted for them so that they can obtain what they want. Unprofessional, bad careers advice narrows people's horizons, lowers their expectations and merely slots people into convenient places. The careers guidance that the Opposition support would have to raise people's expectations, increase their awareness of what is available to them, and open their eyes to wider horizons.

It is vital that careers advice is professional and impartial, so that when people sit opposite careers advisers they are not pushed into doing one thing or categorised as factory people or people who can fit into a certain slot. People should be given an appropriate chance to have a proper dialogue with an impartial adviser, who can open their eyes to careers that they may not have considered and ensure that they get the most out of the process. It should be a two-way process. It should not involve the adviser saying, "Well, there are vacancies down the road. Why don't you apply there?" The process should involve much more than that.

When careers advice is good, it is very good and when it is bad it is horrid. The Opposition want to ensure that the service that is provided—unless the Government have had a sudden change of heart and want to withdraw the clause due to the strength of our arguments—can guarantee quality.

There are other aspects in which careers advice is particularly important. I feel particularly strongly that there should be equal opportunities for women, black people and those with disabilities. Careers advisers must have the right training to overcome stereotyped views of any groups with particular problems in society. We have had discussions with the Royal Association for Disability and Rehabilitation and the Royal National Institute for the Deaf, which have expressed particular concerns about quality and access to the post-privatised careers guidance system, partly because they are acutely aware that, unless proper training is given, professional advisers at the coal face may be labouring under out-of-date or unjustified assumptions about the capabilities of the people in front of them. The same could be said of a woman who might want to go into a non-traditional area of work as of someone with profound deafness, who may have a certain aspiration that a badly trained or untrained adviser might consider beyond his or her capacity.

It is essential to ensure equal opportunities, and such an aspiration is part of good careers guidance. Those who are face to face with children and young people must be aware of those issues and be properly trained to give correct, impartial advice.

Amendment No. 245 aims to ensure local acceptability and accountability of the new careers services. It is essential that any new service is acceptable to the local community. If they are to operate effectively, the careers services must be trusted as impartial and professional and as offering advice of a high standard by the local people in educational establishments such as TECs, schools or colleges, by parents and by others who may use the service. Without that trust, there will be creeping worries that 692 people are being directed to jobs for the convenience of a local employer. Such problems must be erased. That trust, local acceptability and accountability impinge on amendment No. 248.

It is important that the new owners or suppliers of careers services should be accountable to local interests for the work that they do. We must ensure that a reasonable monitoring service accompanies the work so that if a careers service begins to run off the rails or to behave in a way with which local stake holders—for want of a better word—disagree, it can be pulled back and called to account. In other words, the show should be kept on the road and must not run off at a tangent, which might not meet local people's requirements.

Amendment No. 245 also states that the Secretary of State must specify in advance the criteria for being a fit person to run a careers service. The clause allows any Tom, Dick or Harry to set up a careers service.

A completely new system would have to grow up.

In Gloucestershire, a successful partnership has developed that comprises a small number of representatives who have been nominated by the local TEC and the local education authority. The board has a broad membership: three representatives from the TEC, three from the local education authority, a secondary head teacher and the principal of a college of further education. That is a good mix from the institutions that are responsible for providing education and work experience, which is increasingly becoming part of careers guidance. Work experience enables pupils to have experience of working in a particular job before leaving school. It would be sad in the extreme were this type of work to be disregarded.

Can the Minister tell us about the Government's intentions regarding those partnerships? Will they survive, or will they have to turn in to commercial operations so that they can bid for the right to continue to provide their advice?

An important aspect of such a partnership is that it involves at board level, and in all its decision-making processes, the local authority and the LEA. The opposition believe that that local involvement and JPEG 693 accountability are essential if the careers guidance service is to be respected by the clients, their parents and the educational establishments that provide the opportunity for it to flourish.

Amendment No. 250 would make it obligatory to employ staff who are competent to perform the necessary duties. It ties in with some of the concerns that organisations have about the protection of people with special needs of various kinds—people with disabilities for example. It seems to me to be incontestable that those who are to be asked to provide such an important service should be competent to perform the necessary duties. Young people at school have access only once to effective careers advice. If the advice is wrong at that stage, it is economically inefficient. Individuals realise that they have made the wrong choice, they drop out of courses or training and they have to pick up the pieces and start again. The advice should maximise their chances of getting a job and a role in life with which they feel happy. Proper advice can be given within a crucial period of only a few years. The Opposition would not like the chance of careers advice to be fluffed by the Government's ill-thought-out reforms, which as currently worded, do not guarantee quality. It is hard for me to believe that the Government do not expect competence from those charged with performing this duty. It should not be assumed that quality, especially in a competitive tendering situation, will result from the Government's reforms. We have to legislate to ensure quality. We must ensure that the monitoring system is sufficiently adequate to pick up any decline in quality and to act quickly to remedy it.

Finally, the aim of amendment No. 251 is to ensure that careers guidance is recognised as a process that involves work over several years, following an individual from early on in their time at secondary school all the way through to when they leave school at the age of 16 or 17, or go on to higher or further education. To be most effective, the careers advice service needs continuity, in just the same way as companies that are awarded television franchises should be allowed time to develop quality broadcasting and their way of working. We believe that a rapid turnover of careers services provision would be detrimental. It would be sensible to allow three years for the providers of careers services to settle in and give coherent, independent advice to people as they go through school.

I am happy to support the amendments that attempt to add accountability, independence, quality and monitoring provisions to the clause. The Opposition believe that the clause, unamended, is unacceptable. It would centralise power in the hands of the Secretary of State and there would be no means of checking what was going on. It could lead to a free-for-all. We certainly support the amendments. If they are not accepted, we shall oppose the clause.
