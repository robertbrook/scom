DISTRIBUTION OF FOOTWEAR (PRICES) ORDER 1978

10.30 a.m.

The Under-Secretary of State for Prices and Consumer Protection (Mr. Robert Maclennan)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the Distribution of Footwear (Prices) Order 1978 (S.I. No. 1307).

The order gives effect to recommendation by the Price Commission in its report into the prices, costs and margins in the distribution of footwear in the United Kingdom. The report was made pursuant to a direction issued under section 10 (1) of the Price Commission Act 1977 by the Secretary of State for Prices and Consumer Protection on 12th September 1977, following widespread public concern at costs and services within the sector, requiring the Commission to examine the prices, costs and margins in the distribution of footwear. The report was published on 9th June 1978.

The Committee may find it helpful if I detail some of the Commission's findings to explain and give a perspective to the order. The Commission found that although the period since 1970 had seen a growth in real terms of 20 per cent, by value, of footwear expenditure, with two-thirds of all sales accounted for by multiple specialists and an increase in the share by variety stores, this growth was misleading. It disguised the power in the market place of one major multiple outlet—the British Shoe Corporation—and its consequences for competitive forces and the gross profit percentage margins enjoyed by retailers as a whole.

Thus, gross profit percentage margins in footwear distribution grew rapidly in the period between 1961 and 1971 with only two of 20 defined retail trades in the 1971 census of distribution showing higher gross margins in the latter years. Similarly, although the number of footwear multiples outlets showed a higher growth in numbers of outlets than retail trades generally, there was no increase in their share of the trade and little increase in the average sales 4 per employee. The broad picture was one of low labour productivity and a wide disparity between individual shops in terms of efficiency, with many prepared to tolerate inefficient practices—although that picture should not be allowed to mask the real improvements that had been achieved by some retailers.

The Commission's conclusion from the examination was that the relatively static share of the market enjoyed by the largest multiple outlet—the British Shoe Corporation—had its impact on the gross margins of footwear distributors as a whole. The absence of a fully competitive market situation had allowed those margins to rise to a higher level than might otherwise have been, with gross profit margins ranging in the three-year period between 1975 and 1977 between 40 and 53 per cent, for the multiple specialists, with average gross profit percentage margins at a relatively high level of 42.8 per cent, for 1977, and an increase in margins of 37 per cent, between 1961 and 1971—higher than that achieved in retailing generally. This average margin for multiple specialist compares with that of 34.6 per cent, achieved in the same year by the independents.

The Commission accordingly recommended that:

"the gross profit percentage margin of each multiple footwear specialist company"—being multiples with 10 or more outlets—

"(a) should not in any case be allowed to exceed the gross margin in 1977 and


where the 1977 gross margin exceeded that achieved in 1975 should not exceed

  1. the gross margin achieved in 1975 or
  2. a level of 2 percentage points below the gross margin achieved in 1977

whichever is the greater, but in all cases subject to the safeguards for basic profits provided for in section 9 of the Price Commission Act 1977. These percentage margins should not be exceeded in any period of six months commencing on or after 1st October 1978. They should be achieved by obviating or restricting increases in retail selling prices, as and when they may otherwise have been justified by cost increases or other relevant considerations or by making price reductions. For this purpose it will be permissible to take into account only 'arms-length' values for cost incurred. When an enterprise has an accounting year that does not correspond with the calendar years 1975 and 1977, the gross margins attributable to these years shall be construed as being the overall level of gross margin achieved in the most recent accounting year of the enterprise ending before 1st July 1976 in respect of 1975 or 1st July 1978 in respect of 1977."

Following representations made to him, my right hon. Friend the Secretary of JPEG 5 State for Prices and Consumer Protection considered that it would be appropriate for the recommendation to be implemented but with certain very significant exceptions with which I shall deal shoitly. The order gives effect to my right hon. Friend's decision and since the margin control will extend beyond 12 months, it is subject to affirmative rather than negative, resolution of both Houses.

The order specifies in article 2 the persons to whom it applies and implements in article 3 the recommendation of the Commission that I have detailed. Article 4 provides for its enforcement by the Price Commission and the schedule details those to whom the order does not apply.

The order, therefore, applies to all footwear distributors, or in case of companies in a group the members of the group taken together, having 10 or more retail outlets other than those specified in the, schedule including their respective subsidiaries. It will be in operation for a period of two years from 1st October 1978 until 30th September 1980.

The Committee will understand, however, that perhaps more than most sectors of retailing, the consumer interest in footwear is not solely about prices. The consumer's concern is for the quality of the product, health considerations, a satisfactory complaints procedure and the existence of a strong and efficient domestic manufacturing base.

The representations which my right hon. Friend received during his consideration of the report from manufacturers and retailers and consumer and trade union organisations suggested this to be a widely held view and one which was shared on all sides. My right hon. Friend therefore took the opportunity, in considering the Price Commission's report, to take account of all these wider interests as well as the price considerations.

A number of multiple footwear retailers, representing a substantial majority of that sector of the trade, expressed a willingness to offer a package of measures of benefit to the consumer and the industry, which would, at the same time, build on existing work and further the Government's wider objectives in a number of areas of public interest.

These offers involved a number of undertakings. First, there was an undertaking to freeze gross profit percentage margins in their 1978 and 1979 accounting 6 years to 1977 levels and assutances on the footwear code of practice, the footwear code monitoring report, "The Retail Commitment" and retail employment.

It would be right for me to explain briefly the implications of the various assurances been given by the trade. The voluntary code of practice for footwear was launched in August 1976 with the principal aim of improving the quality of service offered to the public by distributors and retailers. It was intended to provide customers with the proper, necessary information, to encourage staff training and to provide a speedy and efficient complaints procedure. The assurances that the multiple specialists were willing to offer further strengthened their commitment to the code and gave assurance of their intention to establish cooperative schemes to cater for extreme sizes of footwear, the establishment of an industry-funded research programme and the development of educational programmes for those most at risk in respect of footwear, such as children and elderly purchasers.

The footwear code monitoring report, which comments on the first two years' operation of the code, identified a number of areas where further attention was required, including pre-testing of new ranges, labelling and classification, and fees for the use of the footwear test centre. The retailers indicated to my right hon. Friend their intention to work towards implementing these recommendations.

As part of the Government's industrial strategy objectives, the footwear industry steering group—a body representing manufacturers, distributors and unions in the footwear industry—recommended in April 1977 the forging of closer links between manufacturers, suppliers of materials and distributors and that the implementation of this recommendation should be the responsibility of a newly created footwear industry working party, a body on which, again, the various sectors of the industry are represented. These recommendations are known as "The Retail Commitment". As a result of the discussions which followed the Price Commission report, the multiple retailers confirmed their adherence to the commitment, agreed to the establishment to monitor it, and said that they would consider seriously the extension of its coverage.

Finally, discussions with the multiple retailers showed that, as part of a package JPEG 7 of measures, they would be willing to go beyond the provisions of the Employment Protection Act in the protection of their employees and embody this in assurances to my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State.

Thirty-nine companies offered such undertakings and assurances, and the Secretary of State accordingly decided to exempt them from the provisions of the order. These companies are detailed in the schedule to the order. Because of the importance which the Government attach to the provisions of the package of undertakings and assurances, by right hon. Friend would be willing to offer exemption from the order to those companies subject to its provisions, but whose consideration leads them to offer such undertakings and assurances as have been given by the scheduled companies. The Secretary of State would therefore propose to give effect to any such further undertakings by way of amendment to the order.

I am sure that the Committee will agree that the standards of British footwear and of retailing are generally good and that our industry has a high reputation. But there is no room for any complacency. The multiple retailers have recognised that and their constructive response to my right hon. Friend's consideration of the Price Commission's report has further strengthened efforts already underway to secure improvements across the whole sector. The acceptance of those undertakings and assurances by my right hon. Friend has been widely welcomed by both sides of the industry and I think that they give the opportunity of long-term benefit to the consumer. I therefore recommend the order to the Committee.

Retailing is generally more profit- JPEG 9 able than manufacturing, but I do not think that profitability is notoriously one of our worst problems in this country, certainly not in the last few years.

As somebody who has the misfortune to have size 12 feet I would be the last to approve anything that did away with a wide range of choice in shoe shops.

I was delighted that the Under-Secretary of State mentioned extreme sizes in the context of the voluntary code of practice, because it is a real difficulty for people in my position to get any choice of shoes or to get any shoes at all without having them hand made.

The report said that there was a wide disparity of efficiency within the retailing industry and low labour productivity. On those grounds it was recommended to the Secretary of state that there should be some control of the profit margins.

It is interesting to note that there was scant reference—in fact, on my examination, none—to the questions of investment and employment.

The Price Commission, as its name indicates, is primarily concerned with price. Price is the trigger which allows it to activate its inquiries and, although it has a general remit on competition, price is undoubtedly the most important element of its work. But here we have undertakings which relate not just to price, but to many other matters affecting the industry. Again, I should like confirmation of where this authority lies. Presumably it is section 12(3)( a), because it was anticipated when the Bill was passing through the House that undertakings would be mainly on the question of price. To be faced with these undertakings over a much wider area puts a new complexion on the workings of the Act.

One needs to be assured that all these undertakings are consistent with competition and efficiency in industry, which is the Price Commission's prime purpose. The Minister has mentioned that there are consumer considerations other than price—the quality of the product, health, complaints procedure and a strong domestic manufacturing base—and he has outlined in detail the undertakings given by the industry in return for exemptions from the order. I do not quarrel with the general 10 undertakings that the industry has given to adhere to the voluntary commitments already entered into in the voluntary code of practice of August 1976 and "The Retail Commitment" of April last year, but I ask the Minister to indicate what he meant by his cursory reference to the undertaking on employment. He said that the industry had undertaken to go beyond the Employment Protection Act in relation to its employees.

That is a genuine matter of interest to consumers, because I—and perhaps the consumers would too if they were aware of it—find it a little difficult to reconcile such a provision with the ground cited for the order being made in the first place, namely low labour productivity. How will it be possible to reconcile going beyond the provisions of current legislation and, at the same time, achieving better productivity in the industry? It may be possible in the longer term, but I believe that it would be helpful to the Committee if the Minister were to outline in greater detail what is at issue in this respect.

I ought to draw the Committee's attention to the distinction between the statutory undertakings over which my right hon. Friend has powers of enforcement through the notification of the Price Commission that they may have been breached, which are in a sense not voluntary, and which relate to the control of gross profit margins contained in the order, and the assurances which relate to the other matters—quality, the adherence to the retail commitment and the firm intention to abide by, and indeed develop, the work of the working group—which do not carry statutory force and for which we are obliged to accept that the good faith of the companies which have given these assurances is involved and that it is their firm intention to carry out the assurances.

Therefore, it does not flow from the order that the assurances will be implemented, but we have no reason to believe that they were not given in good faith. In his opening remarks, the hon. Member for Romford asked whether there was a question of hybridity here. I am advised that there, is not and, as he said, the Bill specifically permits the Secretary of State to do what would otherwise give rise to a question of hybridity. The hon. Gentleman also referred to what he regarded as the scant attention given by the Price Commission to questions of investment and employment. I believe that it is fair-to say that all these matters undoubtedly require consideration by the Price Commission under the criteria set out in section 2 of the Price Commission Act and that it is appropriate for the Commission in issuing its reports to highlight the considerations to which it attaches the greatest importance. But in his response to the Price Commission's report my right hon. Friend has as must be clear from what I have said—attached importance to the representations about the somewhat difficult conditions faced by the industry, particularly in the manufacturing sector, and the possible consequences that would flow for that sector from the full implementation of the Price Commission's recommendations.

The hon. Member for Romford raised the question of the Secretary of State's authority to accept undertakings and assurances. I hope that with the distinction I have drawn between the undertaking, the authority for the acceptance of which is clear from the Act, and the assurances, for which there is no statutory authority, I have dealt adequately with his point.

The hon. Gentleman asked also what I meant when I spoke of the assurances given by the industry in respect of employment which went beyond the provisions of the Employment Protection Act. Specifically, one of the things which has been agreed is that in the event of a threatened redundancy, where the Employment Protection Act requires consultation with the unions affected if there are more than 10 people involved, consultations will take place if there are only five people likely to be affected. The assurances go somewhat wider than that, but that is illustrative of the point I was making.

Mr hon. Friend the Member for Chorley (Mr. Rodgers) spoke of the fact that the 20 years referred to were bumper years for the trade. He was right, and that is what gave rise to the recommendations of the Price Commission. Indeed, my right hon. Friend in deciding the extent to which he was prepared to give effect to the recommendations of the Price Commission in regard to controlling gross profit margins, had to have, and was statutorily required to have, attention paid to the years recommended by the Price Commission. It is not open to him to depart from those recommendations and to introduce more burdensome controls that are now proposed. But the reasons for his having limited the undertakings on gross profit margins to those that I have described flow from other considerations, particularly those affecting the manufacturing industry, and also the substantial undertakings and assurances that have been given in respect of the consumer interest generally.

More than one of my hon. Friends asked whether it would not have been a better response to the Price Commission's report to seek a planning agreement with the industry. I am sure that they will know that the difficulties of obtaining planning agreements are real. But in this case, although in principle there may be a great deal to be said for what they urge—I have a good deal of sympathy with what they have said about the desirability of obtaining a planning agreement for the industry—the practical difficulty that lays in our way is that we have a requirement to give effect to the Price Commission's finding within a statutorily prescribed period of three months. Therefore, to bring in all the companies affected, as we have tried to do in this order, within the framework of a planning agreement would simply not have been feasible within that statutorily limited time scale.

My hon. Friend the Member for Sowerby (Mr. Madden) spoke both of the excessive profits and the excessive imports of the industry, but in a sense these two facets of the problem of the shoe industry do not exactly square with each other. But they are both problems and are not susceptible to treatment within the limited powers that my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State enjoys. It is not wise to seek to build too much upon what can be done by way of extraction of assurances relating to the trade in response to a recommendation which is limited to profit margins.

It is for my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Industry and those in the working group to make sure that the provisions of "The Retail Commitment", which my hon. Friend the Member for Rossendale (Mr. Noble) spelt out in some detail, are followed up and built upon. I can assure my hon. Friend and other 22 hon. Members who are clearly interested in this trade that my right hon. Friend will keep his eagle eye on the affairs of the industry, because there is no doubt that the shoe industry—particularly the retail trade—has given rise to more consumer complaints over a longer period of time than almost any other section of the retailing trade, and this cannot be regarded as the end of the affair. I am very grateful to my hon. Friends and the hon. Member for Romford for participating in this useful debate.
