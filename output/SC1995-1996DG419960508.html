Social Security (Industrial Injuries and Diseases) (Miscellaneous Amendments) Regulations 1996

4.30 pm

Mr. Keith Bradley
(Manchester, Withington)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Social Security (Industrial Injuries and Diseases) (Miscellaneous Amendments) Regulations 1996 (S.I. 1996, No. 425).

Welcome to the Chair, Mr. Anderson.

The statutory instrument amends the list of prescribed diseases for industrial injury purposes. Most importantly, it makes fundamental changes to the reduced earnings allowance for people reaching pensionable age who have been injured or contracted a disease while at work. I will concentrate my comments on the changes to the reduced earnings allowance.

The changes were initially flagged up by the Secretary of State in a leaked letter to the press in October 1995, and were subsequently announced in the uprating statement made to the House. It must be stressed that no consultation has taken place with anyone beyond the Government's advisory body, the Industrial Injuries Advisory Council. We feel strongly that there should have been much wider consultation.

It is important to put on record precisely what reduced earnings allowance is. It was intended as compensation for loss of earnings due to incapacity as a result of industrial accident or disease. It is payable to someone who is unable to return to his regular employment or undertake work of equivalent standard because of the effects of disablement caused by the accident or disease. The allowance is, therefore, payable if a claimant is incapable of carrying out his regular occupation. It is paid not because of a specific job, but because of loss of ability to earn money.

The allowance is based broadly on the difference between a person's earning capacity before and after the accident or disease, although it is still subject—at April 1996 figures—to a maximum payment of £39.60. It was estimated on 24 November 1995 in column 360 of Hansard that 142,796 people received reduced earnings allowance at 2 April 1994. It must be stressed at the outset that many people will potentially be affected by the changes.

Reduced earnings allowance is no longer payable in respect of industrial injury or disease occurring since October 1990. This decision was made despite opposition from the Government's own watchdog, the Industrial Injuries Advisory Council, which argued that reduced earnings allowance could enable a person 4 to move to a safer or less well-paid job if they were disabled in the course of their employment. Perhaps the Minister will tell us why, in the face of opposition from their own advisory council, the Government proceeded with these changes. It was estimated that the abolition of reduced earnings allowance would save £67 million between 1990 and 1993, which would rise to £140 million by the year 2001. However, these savings were intended to fund a package of new benefits for disabled people. The Government apparently took the view that the means-tested disability working allowance would act as a replacement for reduced earnings allowance. In fact, in its 1993 period report, the Industrial Injuries Advisory Council expressed "dismay" at the decision to abolish the allowance and concern that the DWA would not adequately replace it.

Disabled working allowance was not introduced until almost two years after the abolition of reduced earnings allowance. The take-up has run at a very poor 17 per cent. There appears to be no evidence that people injured at work are in the group now claiming disabled- working allowance instead of reduced earnings allowance. Will the Minister tell us whether the Government's position remains that DWA is the alternative to REA? How has money been spent as a result of the changes made? What is the situation regarding take-up and who is receiving the allowance?

Even before 1990, reduced earnings allowance had been cut. The Social Security Act 1986 reduced the amount of reduced earnings allowance by the amount of the earnings-related additional pension payable with invalidity benefit. Entitlement to reduced earnings allowance was ended for those who reached retirement age after 9 April 1989. Those who had already retired by then had preserved entitlement, but the amount was frozen and its worth was about 30 per cent. less than before reduced earnings allowance.

Today, we have received a letter from the Secretary of State which tries to justify the changes before the Committee. It is a mark of the Government's discomfort over the considerable public outcry about the changes that the Secretary of State has been forced to write to Members about this debate. That shows how important the debate is and how the Government's intentions have been received by the public. We shall continue to stress the anxieties felt by particular groups and the losses that they will suffer.

The only people who can now receive reduced earnings allowance are those who were in receipt of it before October 1990 who have not had a single day's break in entitlement since then—reduced earnings allowance is not payable for diseases that have been prescribed since October 1994—or those who have claimed since October 1990 for an accident or disease that occurred before that date. The number of people eligible to claim the full reduced earnings allowance has been very much reduced.

That is the background to the proposals, the changes already made and how those changes affect people. Recipients of reduced earnings allowance who are over state retirement age has been shifted to a much JPEG 5 lower-paying retirement allowance, Crucially, backdating of claims for reduced earnings allowance has been prevented from going back further than one year before the date of the claim.

Three crucial issues need to be considered. First, there is the damage that the reduction in the amount payable will cause to specific groups, Secondly, there is the fact that the changes were introduced almost overnight without transitional protection for people who may have planned their retirement incomes on what they thought they would receive through reduced earnings allowance. Thirdly, the changes in the backdating rules have specific consequences for the benefit because of the nature of the injuries and diseases suffered by those who may be eligible.

Regulation 6 changes the definition of regular employment for the purposes of reduced earnings allowance to be a contract of service for at least 10 hours a week. It also introduces an absolute cut-off for receipt of reduced earnings allowance at pensionable age—either 60 or 65. I will return to the discrimination on that cut-off point later.

The change also applies during the week in which the regulations come into force, that is, 24 March 1996. In contrast to many other social security provisions, there is no transitional protection for existing reduced earnings allowance claimants at the point of change; many of them may have been on reduced earnings allowance for many years prior to 1990. The Government have the power under section 175(4) of the Social Security Contributions and Benefits Act 1992 to make transitional provisions. Their failure to do so for reduced earnings allowance claimants means that the principle of protecting existing claimants from the impact of benefit changes has finally been abandoned. Many claimants will suffer immediate cash losses upon reaching pension age.

I urge the Minister to explain why the Government have not been prepared to introduce transitional arrangements for the group of people who are so badly affected by this change in policy. The Daily Telegraph reported on 30 March 1996 that the Benefits Agency was dispatching letters to claimants asking for the return of their order books. An estimated 20,000 pensioners will lose up to £30 a week as a result of these changes, saving the Government an estimated £25 million in 1996-97 and £30 million a year thereafter. Thus there are immediate cuts in benefit with no transitional protection in order to save the Government money. It is part of their salami-slicing of the benefits system.

We should contrast that with the old rules under which reduced allowance replaced reduced earnings allowance if someone was both over pension age and was giving up regular employment. On the day before giving up the work, the reduced earnings allowance award was at least £2 a week. At least some contribution was made immediately. According to a commissioner's decision, No. R(I)2/93, "regular employment" implied the intention to remain in the labour market. If a person was off sick, made redundant or on holiday, there was no intention to give up work. So long as there was one or more day's 6 interruption of employment—that is unemployment or incapacity—the person was regarded as not having given up work and therefore could be entitled to reduced earnings allowance. The effect of the new regulation is that even when there is an intention to stay in regular employment, with just one day's sickness or unemployment, a person over pension age will lose all his reduced earnings allowance entitlement and be forced to transfer to retirement allowance. Again, I would be grateful if the Minister could explain exactly why there is this change in position and an instant cut-off of this crucial benefit.

People will really suffer. The allowance under reduced earnings allowance is £39.60 a week at current benefit rates. The change to retirement allowance pays merely £10 a week which is a dramatic reduction in the amount to which families will be entitled. This proposal could cut up to £1,500 a year from the injured person's income. My hon. Friends will mention specific cases where such changes have a dramatic effect on the family income.

Mr. Bradley
: We are meant to be having a serious debate. I shall not respond to the hon. Gentleman's inanity. If he wants to make a serious point about cuts in benefit, of which we are considering only the latest, I shall be happy to give way. If he is not interested in the subject of the debate, perhaps it would be better if he left now.

I shall return to the point about potential discrimination. The cut-off age is 60 for a woman and 65 for a man. Several recent European Court cases have challenged that. One case dealt with severe disablement allowance, which now ends at 65 for men and women. I should be grateful if the Minister commented on that, and at least considered delaying the introduction of the regulations until a definitive legal judgment establishes whether discrimination between age groups can be challenged through the courts.

I want to deal with backdating the benefit. Regulations 2 and 3 limit the arrears to 12 months after a late claim or application for review. Under previous legislation, reduced earnings allowance could be fully backdated when there was good cause for a late claim. Otherwise, only three months' arrears were payable. The Government claim that the change to reduced earnings allowance simply aligns backdating industrial injury benefits with other benefits. However, the reason for unlimited backdating under previous rules is the special nature of industrial injury schemes.

JPEG 7 Many conditions have a long latency period, and may only emerge years after exposure to occupational hazards such as asbestos, coal dust, silica, radiation and excessive noise. Many industrial diseases may not come to light quickly. Therefore, late claims and backdated benefits are crucial. The number of deaths that are attributed to diseases caused by asbestos has steadily increased in the past 20 years. It is expected to peak in the next 15 to 25 years. Backdating should be more generous because of exposure to occupational hazards, and delay in recognising the disease. It should not simply be aligned with other benefits.

New reduced earnings allowance claimants would have to establish that they had become disabled as a result of an industrial accident or disease more than five years ago, and that they might have suffered from the effects of such a disability for some years. Industrial injury benefits are among the most complex and least-known benefits. Advisers, solicitors and even Benefits Agency staff can misunderstand the rules.

Backdating should be consistent with all benefits conferred by industrial injury schemes, for example, disablement benefit. Other benefits do not constitute an adequate comparison with those for industrial injuries. I hope that the Minister will comment on the special nature of the diseases from which people who apply for reduced earnings allowance may suffer.

The reduced earnings allowance was abolished in 1990. Therefore, only a limited number of claimants will need to take advantage of the unlimited backdating that the Opposition believe to be appropriate. Only 2,953 new awards of reduced earnings allowance were made between April 1993 and April 1994. Those figures are taken from a written answer on 19 January 1996 at column 826 of Hansard. They show that less than 2 per cent. of the case load sought backdating. A further restriction is at the least mean-minded—and, more importantly, it is draconian—in relation to diseases and injuries for which people might claim reduced earnings allowance.

The detail is clear. The Government want to use the statutory instrument further to reduce the benefit for disabled people. The stark detail does not give the full colour and flavour of the anguish and problems that many people who fall foul of the benefit cut will suffer. Case after case is being presented in which people are losing significant amounts of money. Many organisations—the Disability Alliance, citizens advice bureaux, trade union councils representing many trade unions and their workers throughout the country—are coming up with cases concerning people who had planned in the belief that they would be entitled to the money through their retirement, and who are suffering significant loss of money. It is not an exaggeration to say that instances are emerging all the time in which the changes will mean an entitlement of £30 a week less than previously. That is a significant impact on family finances that have been planned in the expectation of receiving the money.

8 The Opposition do not accept the rationale behind the Government's statements on the matter. We do not buy the Secretary of State's attempt to justify the change through the letter that was sent to hon. Members today. We urge the Minister to reconsider the proposals and to ensure that people who have suffered—often drastically—because of industrial disease and injury are not further disadvantaged by the Government's mean-mindedness.

In essence, the regulations will deprive retired disabled people of up to £30 a week which they were told they would receive for life when they first applied for the benefit. Introducing the regulations is a miserly and cold-hearted exercise.

The Secretary of State's explanation for the measure, in his letter to hon. Members dated 7 May, was that

"technical deficiencies in the 1988 legislation meant that some people continued to receive Reduced Earnings Allowance after they had retired."

My view is that there were no technical deficiencies in the 1988 legislation. Parliament approved the measures then without full knowledge of what they contained. Many Members of this Parliament were not in Parliament in 1988. The House did not want a benefit that was given to people for life to be taken away from them. The Social Security Act 1988 prevented new applicants from being accepted on to the reduced earnings allowance scheme after October 1990. It did not provide—nor, I believe, was it intended to—that current recipients of REA would have their benefit stopped when they retired. I do not believe that Parliament agreed to that. If the Minister disagrees, I hope that he will point out the evidence for his view.

In a reply to my hon. Friend the Member for Delyn (Mr. Hanson), the Leader of the House said, during business questions on 25 April at column 604, that Parliament "agreed … the principle" of the legislation. It is a nice way with words: "We agree the principle, now all we have to do is slide it under the door, with no chance of amendment. We will have a one-and-a-half hour debate upstairs in a Committee Room after we have enacted the regulations."

That is a shabby way to treat thousands of our constituents and their Members of Parliament. I wonder what our constituents think of us. We are supposed to legislate, yet the Executive treat us in this roughshod way. We should understand that we are discussing people who are unable to work to their full potential because of industrial injuries or diseases. Their earnings potential has been reduced through no fault of their own. Parliament rightly took the view that people who became partially disabled in that way JPEG 9 should be eligible for support for the rest of their lives and that was the basis of the reduced earnings allowance—or hardship allowance as it was called when I worked in industry. It was the basis on which people receiving REA planned their spending and savings. They thought that they were permanently entitled to the payment as part of their compensation for being injured at work.

One of those affected is my constituent Mr. Hughie Craig, with whom I worked in industry many years ago. He was born in 1928 and started work at the age of 14 in coal mining, where he stayed for many years until he had an accident which left him entitled to receive the reduced earnings allowance. He told me in a letter that he was

"unable to continue on [his] pre-accident job which means a loss of earnings for the rest of [his] life".

My constituent was told after his accident, many years ago, that the reduced earnings allowance would be payable for the rest of his life. But he was unlucky enough to reach retirement age after 9 April 1989. The Government have reduced his £38.12 a week to £9.90 without any Act or Parliament.

Mr. Craig told me that the first he knew of a change in the law was when he received a letter dated 28 March asking him to return his REA benefit book, which he did. The letter from the Benefits Agency told him only that

"due to a change in the law … we may need to adjust your order book".

The letter to Hughie Craig did not mention the fact that a possible reduction was planned. No warning was given and when he asked a member of the local Benefits Agency staff why he had to return his book, she told him that she did not know.

Three days later, the Benefits Agency wrote again to Mr. Craig telling him that, with immediate effect, he would lose almost £30 a week. Following an emergency telephone call to my home that evening, my constituent wrote to me stating:

"If this new law is carried through, I will find it very difficult to live and to manage".

He told me that he had planned his finances including the REA as it was a lifetime payment, agreed by the state. He has a mortgage, bills and regular commitments and losing £30 a week without notice and without proper transitional arrangements, which my hon. Friend the Member for Withington mentioned, will severely damage his ability to make ends meet. Mr. Craig added in his letter:

"Surely, by law, we should have received notice of any change in our benefits. Normally we can accept or appeal against the change."

He continued:

"I do not accept this change. By the way it is being carried out, it is not fair or just. The people receiving REA cannot afford to lose this kind of money."

My constituent is not unique. In a parliamentary Answer in January, the Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State, the hon. Member for Hertfordshire, North (Mr. Heald) told the House that in April 1994 more than 140,000 people received the reduced earnings allowance, nearly 55,000 of whom were over 10 pensionable age. All those who retired after April 1989 will lose under the terms of the regulations and they will only be the first. Those already identified by the Minister will be followed by anyone who has since been disabled at work and transferred to a job on reduced earnings.

My constituent, Mr. Craig, is not happy. He rightly summed up the Minister's attitude, saying:

"This Government have no respect for the elderly. They are attacking the weak and vulnerable. They seem to think that senior citizens have no rights. We have been attacked and mugged."

That is how he feels. Just under £30 a week being taken from someone such as him at the whim of the Government means that he has every right to feel that he has been mugged by the Secretary of State for Social Security. I agree entirely with those sentiments.

These are mean-minded and mean-spirited regulations. They will cut spending for the needy, presumably so that money can be found for Budget day handouts later this year. Yet just £20 million will be squeezed from elderly disabled people by the measure. Can that sort of money be better spent on tax bribes? I do not think that anyone with any feelings of fairness and justice would believe that it could.

The regulations are unfair. Their detail has never been agreed, but they have already been enacted—the will of Parliament has been circumvented. I ask hon. Members on both sides of the Committee, what justice there is in Members of Parliament, on the basis of some sort of vote in 1988 taking from people who suffered industrial accidents 20 years ago a benefit that those people thought would go with them for the rest of their lives—to their graves. It is outrageous.

The Government have undermined Parliament's judgment by retrospectively changing the law. If they were saying that people who were injured in future would receive the benefit only until retirement I might not agree, but there would be more justice in that than in taking a benefit from a person who has been receiving it for decades, thinking that it would continue for life.

The change has been carried out without express parliamentary approval. Sorting this out in a Committee Room at this hour is no way to deal with the matter. The change has been carried out without consultation and in a way that undermines Members of Parliament. There has been very little advance warning to our constituents—the people who are most affected by the regulations.

I urge hon. Members to reject the regulations, if for no other reason than to show Ministers that they cannot ride roughshod over Members of Parliament and our responsibilities to our constituents.

Ms Judith Church
(Dagenham)
: I shall start by referring Members of the Committee, particularly Conservative Members, to regulation 5. I know that often, when we deal with statutory Instruments, not everyone has had time to read the regulations and consider them in any detail. I should like to draw the Committee's attention to some of the additions made by the regulations to the schedules for industrial injuries and prescribed diseases. Carpal tunnel syndrome, for example, is extraordinarily unpleasant. I do not know whether any Members of the Committee has encountered a constituent who has it. It is the sort of occupational disease to which those outside who are working on the Jubilee line extension and the new Parliament building are exposed. The sort of construction work that a modern society needs the building industry to do requires people to use handheld power tools. Carpal tunnel syndrome is an extremely debilitating disease—people who suffer from it are unable to hold or lift things, and are in constant pain for a large part of their life.

Allergic rhinitis, referred to in regulation 5(7), arises in a number of industries; for example from isocyanates in the paint industry, platinum salts in the electronics industry and epoxy resins from a number of industries. Those are welcome additions because the old regulations did not reflect the modern nature of manufacturing. The regulations are a catching-up exercise. Many people were suffering from these conditions and struggling for years to have them recognised as prescribed industrial diseases, but Governments did not see fit to do so. Other examples include the enzymes in detergent manufacture, the antibiotics in the pharmaceutical industry and reactive dyes, textiles and materials.

I do not draw hon. Members' attention to this list and go through it in great detail to be pedantic. It may seem to some hon. Members that many of these conditions are rather exotic. They are not the sort of things than many of us come across in our working lives, particularly in Parliament. However, we know that many of our constituents work day in, day out, throughout their lives with many of those conditions. We cannot avoid people working with such agents if we want to live in a modern society. If we want plastic JPEG 13 objects and the benefits of modern society, we have to have these agents as part of our industrial manufacturing base.

It would be nice if such hazardous substances which are being used in the workplace were properly controlled, as they should be under the Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 1988. In a previous occupation, I had some part in drafting those. The sad fact is that many of those substances are poorly controlled in the workplace. We know that many of the people who are getting prescribed industrial diseases and who are suffering from ill-health in the workplace are doing so because of poor management and poor control. It is not their fault that they end up with these diseases; they are doing jobs which we, perhaps, do not want do do. I do not know about Conservative Members, but a number of Opposition Members have experienced industrial diseases from work that they have done in previous occupations.

The problem is that the Government do not understand the burden carried by such people, which is the legacy of our advanced, modern, industrial society. People who suffer these diseases often have been debilitated early in life. People in their 20s have been exposed to some of the products that cause allergic rhinitis. They are unable to work because they have tremendous respiratory difficulties. They cannot get up and down stairs and they are young people with young families. In some of the more hazardous industries such as the building industry and the chemicals and plastic industry, of which I have personal experience, people have been paralysed after falls and suffer from huge disabilities as a result of the work that we as a society want them to do.

No one will say that we can cast adrift those people because of the sacrifices that they have made in their working lives, but I suggest to the Government that, in a sense, the regulations largely will cast them adrift. The Government are saying to them, "You made the sacrifice, but suddenly, we are going to take away your money. We will not give you any warning, and we are not even saying that we are sorry for not warning you." As the hon. Member for Rochdale (Ms Lynne) said, people were turning up to claim their benefits and then finding out that they had been cut.

Opposition Members have considerable experience of these matters, which is why it is important that we are having this debate. I sometimes wonder whether Conservative Members know enough about these matters to understand what is going on. My hon. Friend the Member for Withington previously worked in the health service, which picks up a substantial part of the bill for people who are injured at work. My hon. Friends the Members for Rother Valley and for Barnsley, West and Penistone (Mr. Clapham) had experience in the mining industry and in trade unions before they came into the House. I worked in industry for a number of years as one of Her Majesty's inspectors of factories, investigating occupational ill-health, disease and industrial action and, on the criminal law side, trying to pick up the pieces of devastated lives. I recognised that those people could 14 no longer expect to lead a normal working life and to live with their families in the way that we all hope and expect to. Their industrial disease or accident meant that the rest of their lives would be substantially less pleasant.
