New Clause 24
NOTICE BY TENANTS


"After section 15 of the Housing Act 1988 (limited prohibition on assignment etc, without consent) there shall be inserted—


'Notice by tenants'


15(A) A tenant may end an assured or assured tenants' shorthold tenancy by giving the landlord reasonable notice in writing".—[ Mr. Raynsford ]

Brought up, and read the First time.

10.30 am

Mr. Nick Raynsford
(Greenwich)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

We now come to a minor technical new clause, which is designed to remedy an anomaly that has been created under clauses 75 and 76. I have read the Hansard report of the previous sitting, so I am conversant with the arguments that were advanced then. As the Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, the hon. Member for Hertsmere (Mr. Clappison), said on Tuesday, the clauses will allow a private sector landlord to create a periodic tenancy from the outset in the case of an assured shorthold tenancy. That will give no contractual fixed term for that tenancy.

Under normal landlord and tenant law, a tenant may determine a periodic tenancy at any time upon giving reasonable notice. When there is a contractual fixed term, many standard tenancy agreements include in their terms a break clause, whereby a tenant can determine the tenancy upon four weeks' notice. The new clause would regularise the position.

If the new clause is not included in the Bill, it will be possible in theory for private sector tenants to be unable to move because there is no provision to allow the tenant to determine the tenancy. I hope that the Undersecretary, when he has reflected on the matter, will realise that the new clause is sensible and that it is designed to improve the procedure and to avoid a potential anomaly. If he does not agree with the wording because it is not as simple and straightforward as 14 days, but is more complicated than that, I hope that he will accept its principle.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Mr. James Clappison)
: I have listened carefully to the hon. Gentleman's argument, but I am not sure that the new clause is as technical as he would have us believe. He made the fair point that the landlord and tenant may agree to include a break clause in the tenancy agreement from the outset, but that is a matter for them to conclude freely at the outset of the tenancy.

524 The new clause would insert into every agreement a break clause for the tenant only. It would, therefore, inhibit the parties' freedom to conclude an agreement between themselves into which they were both freely prepared to enter. The insertion of such an amendment into every fixed-term tenancy, especially every fixed-term assured shorthold tenancy, would undermine the position of the landlord. The incentive for a landlord to grant a fixed-term assured shorthold tenancy is the assurance that the tenant is prepared to pay the rent throughout the period of that fixed-term tenancy. If such a clause were inserted into each agreement, the landlord would lose that assurance.

Circumstances may arise in which a landlord feels that there is little in it for him to grant a fixed-term assured tenancy. He might consider it to be of no advantage to grant a term for longer than a minimum because the tenant could, in any event, bring the tenancy to an end by giving reasonable notice. The hon. Gentleman may think that it is reasonable that landlords should be subject to that lack of assurance. However, as is often the case with regulation of tenancies by the state, if such regulations were inserted into each contract, thus undermining the freedom of contract, they would have an adverse effect for the private rented sector as a whole.

Landlords might be unwilling to grant fixed-term tenancies, especially fixed-term assured shorthold tenancies. That may result in a shortage of such tenancies. The new clause would undermine the freedom of contract between the two sides. It would undermine the position of landlords. It is not desirable. It is up to the landlord and tenant at the outset to conclude whatever agreement they wish.

Mr. Raynsford
: That is not an adequate answer. In two important ways, the Bill will reduce tenants' rights. It will allow landlords to create shorthold tenancies as default tenancies and will reduce the amount of time that tenants can be in arrears before becoming subject to mandatory eviction. The new clause is a small measure designed to provide an element of relief for tenants. As the Minister stated clearly on Tuesday, tenants could find themselves in a periodic tenancy with no provision for termination, and if they chose to end the tenancy they could be sued by the landlord for the income that he might otherwise have received.

To reject the new clause on the ground that it will undermine the market is nonsense. Landlords will be allowed, in every instance if they wish, to let on assured shorthold tenancies, the only obligation on the landlords being to let for a six-month period. To cavil at the new clause because it would somehow damage the market does not carry any credibility. I am astonished that the Minister should take such a negative view about such a simple, straightforward and reasonable proposal. I hope that he will be more accommodating in future. However, I wish to make progress so that we can deal with other business—particularly with housing benefit so I beg to ask leave to withdraw the motion.

Motion and clause, by leave, withdrawn.

New clause 25 is meant to prevent landlords evicting tenants simply because the tenants have used their right under the law to have repairs made to the property, to have unreasonably high rents reduced, or to have put right any of the other things that tenants should be able to have put right. At present, whether such complaints are made directly to the landlord or through an environmental health officer, the landlord's reaction may be not to respond to the complaint, right the wrong, or make the necessary repairs, but to evict the tenant. That problem is particularly bad for assured shorthold tenancies because that limited period of security has weakened the tenants' bargaining power.

We believe that the Government have too often supported the landlord to the detriment of the tenant. The new clause would redress that imbalance. Many tenants attempt to enforce their statutory or contractual rights, but their landlords either fail to renew the tenancy or seek possession at the end of the shorthold tenancy. The result is that not only the tenants who complain or who try to enforce their contractual rights 526 have to pay excessive rents, or live in dangerous or unsafe conditions, but subsequent tenants also suffer the same problems.

As the Committee knows, many of the examples that I cite sadly, are, from my constituency. Today I shall give another example from Lewisham. A large number of students live in the borough, who study at the excellent Goldsmiths' college. Many of them live in the private rented sector. One group of students was given a standard six-month tenancy starting in October, at the beginning of the academic year. They discovered in January that the landlord had been running the meters at a higher level and was overcharging them for the resale of the electricity and gas. They spoke to the landlord about that and asked him to reset the meters and make a refund, taking the beneficial attitude that the landlord might simply have made a mistake. The landlord's response was to say that if the students made a fuss they could get out. Indeed, in the middle of January they received notice of possession and they were out by the beginning of April. They were expecting to take their exams in May, so they were in a particularly difficult position. The landlord re-let the property immediately.

There are more serious cases, involving unsafe conditions. A woman in Kent with two young children rented a flat for the six-month shorthold tenancy. She was a mature student. Such cases particularly affect the student population. She discovered that the gas fires in her flat were dangerous because of the escape of carbon monoxide gas. The landlord refused to do anything about it so, with the help of a student welfare officer, she contacted the Health and Safety Executive, which asked the landlord to sort the fires out. The woman was studying for a degree to give her young family security and herself a decent job. She had paid her rent on time, had been a good tenant and had done nothing wrong, but the landlord took eviction proceedings against her. He made it clear that he wanted possession because she had complained. After she left, he immediately re-let the property and did nothing about the gas fires.

The new clause is about not only giving tenants better bargaining powers but ensuring that they live in safe properties. It would also ensure that landlords did not behave irresponsibly, leaving their flats in a second-rate, substandard fashion. As my hon. Friends have said, allowing landlords to behave in such a way harms the good landlords who want to let decent property.

The Under-Secretary might respond by saying that there are already procedures to deal with such matters and that there are environmental health officers. However, environmental health officers often have to respond to tenants discreetly and clandestinely, as they realise that if the landlord finds out that tenants have complained, the tenants are likely to be evicted. We want procedures to be open, straightforward and clear, so that landlords obey the law, provide safe property and do not evict tenants who simply try to enforce their contractual or statutory rights.

Mr. Raynsford
: I beg to move amendment No. 360, in page 52, line 10, at end add—

"(3) In section 5 of the Social Security Administration Act 1992 (regulations about claims for and payment of benefit) at the end of subsection (l)(h) insert 'and, as regards the administration of housing benefit, for requiring a request for information or evidence by the determining authority to be made in such a manner and within such a period as may be prescribed, not being more than seven days from the date on which the claim is received at the appropriate office of the Department of Social Security or at the designated office, whichever is the earlier'.".

We now come to part IV of the Bill, which deals with housing benefit. It is a curious benefit, and the subject of split responsibility, wherein lie many problems. The responsibility for shaping the benefit, determining the regulations, the amount of entitlement and so on, all lie with the Department of Social Security, whereas the administrative responsibility lies with local authorities.

That is why we are considering the administrative clauses relating to housing benefit. Because of the split, we cannot table amendments to tackle some of the very serious problems in the benefits system, such as the deep poverty traps, the steep withdrawal of benefit as income rises, disincentives and so on that are widely recognised to be intrinsic problems of means-testing. Nor can we attempt to reverse the unfair and discriminatory cuts in entitlement to benefit affecting young single people that will come into effect later this year or the withdrawal of entitlement from many asylum seekers that became effective last month and which has caused widespread concern and hardship. Unfortunately, the remit of the Bill and your chairmanship, Mrs. Winterton, ensures that we cannot debate such amendments today.

Instead we must confine ourselves to the administration of housing benefit, which also concerns us and has caused many problems since the scheme was first introduced in 1982. The rushed—many would say bungled—implementation of the scheme, resulted in chaos, involving many thousands, if not millions of claimants waiting an inordinate length of time for their benefit entitlement. That led The Times to describe the scheme as follows:

"The worst administrative fiasco in the history of the welfare state".

That forced a review of the system and further changes in 1987 and 1988, but problems still continue. The Audit Commission produced a report two and a half years ago entitled:

"Remote Control: The National Administration of Housing Benefit"

which highlights many of those problems. I shall refer briefly to some of the issues identified by the Audit Commission. It said that the performance of some authorities had been criticised by volunteer bodies, academics, landlords and individual claimants. Some academics and others argued that problems with housing benefit were a major factor preventing homeless, young, single people from obtaining permanent rented accommodation in the private sector.

The National Association of Citizens Advice Bureaux, which has provided us with much useful evidence is reported by the Audit Commission as saying: 530

"The quality of service being provided by many local authorities throughout the country in administering housing benefit is inadequate at almost every level".

Research was published by the Housing Corporation—I am almost reluctant to mention this as it was conducted by the company that I used to run before being elected to my present position. I declared an interest, but of course, it is all in the past. That research found that

"whilst the overall picture is not uniformly bad, the scale and range of problems [with HB administration] found by the study strongly indicates a major barrier towards [housing] associations achieving the expectations placed upon them by the Government's current and future housing policies".

The local government ombudsman found serious shortcomings in some authorities' operations. For example, investigations revealed

"a deplorable catalogue of failure … and at times nothing short of administrative chaos … serious failures in the way Housing and Community Charge Benefit are administered"

and that failures caused Mr. X

"gross injustice in that he lost his home and possessions".

There we have a clear statement from the Audit Commission that the administrative problems continue.

The amendment focuses on the length of time that it takes a local authority to process a housing benefit claim. In theory, the local authority is required to process the claim within 14 days. Responding to a debate on another subject on Tuesday, the Undersecretary made the valid comment that, according to official statistics, 80 per cent, of claims were determined within 14 days. That, of course, is an admission of failure because, by definition, if only 80 per cent, are processed within 14 days, one in five are not. That is unsatisfactory, given that we are dealing with people who desperately need that money to pay their rent.

There is, however, a more serious issue. The 14-day rule bites only from the time that the local authority has all the information necessary to process the claim. In many cases it takes a great deal longer than 14 days—in some cases it may take months—for the local authority to gather that information together. In some cases that may be because of the claimant's failure to provide it. More often, it is because of the need to co-ordinate information between the local authority and the Department of Social Security, or to get verification from employers about income data supplied by the claimant and to carry out a serious of outside checks. Those checks are perfectly proper, but the amendment addresses the crucial point that no time scale is imposed on the local authority to ensure that the process is conducted expeditiously. There is no requirement for it to be completed within a specified time.

There is, as I mentioned, a requirement that once the authority has all the information, it should process the claim within 14 days, but as I said, it could be months before that information is gathered, so the 14-day period is overruled by the fact that the authority does not have the necessary information. The Audit Commission confirms that and points out that in many cases it is a real cause of delays, which result in people waiting a disproportionate length of time before they receive their benefit.

The National Association of Citizens Advice Bureaux highlighted the problem and one particular JPEG 531 case, which I think the Committee should hear. It illustrates how grievous the problem can be. A citizens advice bureaux in the south-east described a housing benefit claim fiasco that one of its clients experienced. It stated:

"Following CAB assistance with claiming Income Support, housing benefit and council tax, the client returned to the bureau seven weeks later to say that he still had not received any housing benefit"—

Seven weeks later. The account continues:

"The bureau phoned the housing benefit department and was told that the benefit forms were now out of date. Following a series of phone calls the client was asked to go to the housing benefit department in order to make a claim. Three weeks later, the unfortunate man returned to the bureau stating that he had still not received any housing benefit. The CAB rang the department requesting that the payment be made urgently. Three weeks later the client had still not received any money."

That was 13 weeks beyond the initial point. The case goes on:

"On the last occasion, the CAB again rang the housing benefit department and was told that they were awaiting information from the DSS concerning the man's income support claim."—

That is the classic problem, when the local authority must wait for information from a Government Department—

"The CAB reminded the department that they had promised to treat the case urgently and that the man had now not received housing benefit for over two months."

By my calculation, that should read "over three months", but perhaps there is a glitch there that I have not spotted. The report continues:

"They were assured that money would be sent out by the end of the week. The CAB advised the man of this and told him to come back to the bureau if he had not received his money. In the words of the CAB: 'the client returned, unbelievably, he had still not been sent any money,' All in all the client had to wait four months whilst the local authority processed his claim, without the intervention of the CAB he may well have had to wait much longer."

Such administrative delays, while the local authority and the Department of Social Security fail to get their act together, are totally unacceptable.
