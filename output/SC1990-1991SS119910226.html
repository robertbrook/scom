The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton)
: I am glad to respond to the hon. Member for Linlithgow (Mr. Dalyell), who raised several issues concerning the staffing and resources of the Nature Conservancy Council for Scotland. His worry stemmed from a letter from the secretary of the Nature Conservancy Council's trade union side to the Minister for the Environment and Countryside in the Department of the Environment. The hon. Gentleman read out several extracts from that letter. With your agreement, Mr. Martin, I should like to respond to the hon. Gentleman on those points that are relevant to our consideration.

The hon. Gentleman's worries relate to three main factors. The first is the level of staffing and funding for the Nature Conservancy Council for Scotland, as a predecessor of Scottish Natural Heritage. The second factor is the number of extra scientists and the extra benefits in scientific terms that will accrue. The third concerns a number of issues about setting budgets for the new conservation agencies.

Subject to your views, Mr. Martin, I suggest that the third matter is not of direct relevance to the schedule. However, I accept that the establishment of the staffing levels for the NCC is of some relevance to SNH, and in giving background on that, I hope that I may allay some of the hon. Gentleman's anxieties.

All attempts, whether by the Scottish Office or the NCC, to calculate a reasonable basis for the staffing and funding levels of the three new bodies were hindered by the simple fact that the NCC accounting system cannot attribute indirect spending, such as on central services such as scientists, to different regions.

The Committee may recall that the letter quoted by the hon. Member for Linlithgow referred to a consultancy by PE Inbucon. That consultancy was carried out for and on behalf of the NCC to estimate 78 the level of funding necessary for the three new bodies. As noted in the letter and confirmed by the hon. Gentleman on 21 February,

"there is a difference of opinion about the matter."— Official Report, First Scottish Standing Committee, 21 February 1991, c.72.]

The scope for interpretation of the analysis is considerable given that the NCC could not attribute costs directly and has several elements of expenditure that can vary considerably from year to year and from area to area—examples include casual and temporary staff, and management agreements.

As for Scotland, we can be a little more definite. The best estimate for the final year of the NCC operation is that £7.4 million will be spent directly and that between £2 million and £3 million could be attributed to Scotland. Those rough estimates give a total spend of about £10 million for 1990–91. The number of the NCC staff based in Scotland is about 245. For 1991–92 the NCC will have a budget of nearly £19 million and a staff complement of 385. The NCC expects to have 70 per cent of its staff in post by 1 April and to have reached 90 per cent by June. By any standards, that represents a significant increase in both resources and staffing in Scotland.

The analysis to assess the required level of staffing and resources in Scotland was carried out jointly by officials of the Scottish Office Environmental Department and the Nature Conservancy Council Scottish directorate. Both the complement and the budget have been accepted by Mr. Magnusson and his board as sufficient for the tasks they face in the NCC's proposed one year of operation. I emphasise that our aim has always been that the NCCS should do more than the present NCC in Scotland, especially in relation to improving understanding of and changing attitudes about nature conservation.

I hope that I have helped to clarify the position on the staffing and funding levels for the NCCS and, more important, the way in which those important factors were determined. I have not gone into detail on the calculations of resources for the other conservation bodies as that is not appropriate to the consideration of schedule 1. : I beg to move amendment No. 2, in page 2, line 5, after "Scotland", insert

"including sites to be designated as National Parks.".

The amendment would invest SNH with the power to bring forward areas for designation as national parks. When the Bill was first mooted in the heady days of the late 1980s, it was considered that it might include the necessary provisions to introduce some form of management for Scottish upland areas. The Opposition and many other people are disappointed that the Bill does not do that.

A generous interpretation is that the matter is still out for consultation and that the final policy has not yet been formulated. As the Countryside Commission did not report until September 1990, the Minister may have received only recently the result of its consultations, which are still being considered.

The less generous interpretation is that there is a lack of political will to get the Bill onto the statute book. It is a controversial issue and it will require some bold political decisions. Given the current state of the Tory party in Scotland and the effects of previous Scottish legislation, the Government are not prepared to create controversy by making those difficult decisions.

The Opposition accept in principle, but with some reservations, the need for some arrangement to manage upland areas, including national parks. We give that our qualified support. The Countryside Commission report deals not only with national parks, but with the management of all upland areas and it considers indicative strategies as methods of management. It also deals with land management forums for the various land management areas. It selects four areas, which we shall consider when appropriate, for designation as national parks that will require special attention and special management arrangements.

The name "national park" is not the most appropriate. Unfortunately, it is good shorthand. Everyone talks about national parks, but the vibes are wrong. What is in a name? It seems that everything is wrong in this case. In many people's minds the name is a token of bureaucracy—turnstiles, gates, impaired access, swings, roundabouts, picnic places, ma, pa and the wains. Everyone and everything is wrong with it. We should seriously consider changing the name. The only good thing I have to say about the proposed natural heritage areas is that they have a better name.

There should be no blanket solution for national parks. What is needed for Loch Lomond and the Trossachs is obviously not appropriate for Wester Ross, the Fannichs and the surrounding areas. The number of people, their forms of recreation, the form of the land and its fauna are different. The whole culture is different. To impose a blanket solution JPEG 81 would be improper. Indeed, the Countryside Commission makes separate arrangements for Wester Ross. I do not want to comment unnecessarily on the rights and wrongs of such arrangements at this stage, but we should not permit a blanket solution.

Whatever the method of management, it must involve the local people and their elected representatives. Not under any circumstances should one take an area away from its local community and try to impose management from outside. Unless the local people living in local housing, the shopkeepers, the landowners, the fish-farmers and whoever else are intimately involved with a powerful voice—not a perfunctory talking-shop voice, but a real voice— such arrangements will never work.

Those areas must also be adequately funded. That is a problem with the natural heritage areas that the Minister intends to introduce in a later amendment. It is a pity that we cannot discuss it now. For example, the Peak district receives £2.4 million in Government grants and the Lake district receives £1.7 million. Unless we are prepared to invest money, natural heritage areas will not work. For example, including Loch Lomond and the Trossachs in the regional parks system has been a good effort, but it has fallen down because of inadequate funding to protect and control the various areas within it. Finally, as well as ensuring the conservation of an area and its natural heritage, the national parks must ensure access.

We support the concept of national parks for many of the reasons outlined in the Countryside Commission report. I do not want to go into all of them, but they relate to mountain areas with tremendous scenery, great recreational opportunities, natural history and cultural history. Those areas are worth preserving, but we have failed to protect them. The planning to deal with changes resulting from forestry and fish-farming has been poor. When one listens to complaints about those areas, forestry is mentioned constantly. I am aware of the new indicative strategies that have been formed, but they remain a bone of contention.
