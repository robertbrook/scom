Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mrs. Margaret Beckett
(Derby, South)
: I do not intend to detain the Committee long, but it would be wrong to let the clause pass through the Committee without making a few observations about its general effect and where it stands in the pattern of the Government's legislation on these matters.

If the Government proposed to abolish unemployment benefit they would undoubtedly make themselves somewhat unpopular, but they have been too clever for that. They do not intend to do any such thing. But, year after year, they have reduced the likelihood of someone being able to obtain unemployment benefit—one of the few benefits still paid as of right and not means tested—by successively tightening the conditions under which such a benefit may be claimed.

The Opposition do not believe that the state of the employment market justifies such action by the Government. Rather, the reverse is true. As unemployment is higher than the Government anticipated before the general election, it is wrong for them to continue to make it more difficult for people to claim unemployment benefit. There has been a series of technical changes, most of which I would defy even the hon. Member for Derbyshire, South (Mrs. Currie) to publicise and make interesting. Perhaps I exaggerate, but it would be hard even for her. The changes do not strike people as being of any great significance until they affect them directly: they may discover that, although their record in employment entitled them to unemployment benefit in the past, it will not entitle them to it in future.

One of the features of this successive reduction or removal or rights is the way on each occasion it has affected women in particular and those who work part time. They are also the group of people most likely to be affected by the clause. Many will probably be married women who are unlikely to sign on if they cannot claim unemployment benefit, as they will not normally qualify for income support. Furthermore, the Government have tightened up other provisions in employment legislation of late and introduced restart interviews in a way that seems to have been specifically designed to drive married women off the 352 register. They have to fill in lengthy forms and are questioned in detail about their childcare arrangements and whether they are really available and looking for work.

A young woman came to my advice bureau on Friday with precisely that problem. She had been in work and had left to have a child. She now wishes to go back to work. She has grown up in the belief that in this country there are reasonable rights and justice for someone in work, but she is shortly to be disabused of that. She no longer wishes to work for her previous employer for the simple reason that her wages would no longer be sufficient to pay a childminder. She is therefore looking for a different pattern of work for more pay.

She first fell foul of the system on the question whether she was available for work. Secondly, when she had dealt with that problem, the question arose of how quickly she could obtain the services of her childminder. Like most people, she is not closely familar with past let alone present procedures, so she said what she thought was fair to her childminder and not what the Department required her to say. She thought it fair to give her childminder a couple of weeks notice because it was unlikely that she would have to start work at shorter notice than that. That resulted in her being told that she was not really available for work, whereupon she consulted her childminder who agreed to take her child at 24 hours notice. She then has to go back to the Department and say that, after all, her childminder would take her child at 24 hours notice.

I cannot recall what reason was given for ruling that she is not looking for work. Undoubtedly, she is looking for work, but that to and fro process would have put off a less determined woman. If she were not, as she is, genuinely, determinedly and actively looking for work, I am convinced that she would have given up long ago. That is precisely what will happen with many women who will not sign on if they are told that they cannot claim unemployment benefit.

: Order. I have allowed the hon. Gentleman a long preamble to these rather specific amendments, but I remind the committee that if hon. Mr. Battle
: I am grateful for your reminder, Mr. Winterton, and will endeavour to obey.

My argument was that, although we are told that the Bill will catch cheats, that is not the purpose of the clause, which simply changes the rules so as to deny benefit to people who are not cheats. I shall now deal with exactly how that is to be done.

How do the Government intend to ensure that people take active steps to find work? The main lever is to disqualify people by changing the time brackets. Amendments Nos. 86 and 92 are aimed at that JPEG 359 provision. Altering the time brackets is the easiest way to get the figures down, taking large numbers of people off the unemployment benefit register. But that has nothing to do with catching cheats. Suspicion sometimes works its way round so that people who are treated in that way are accused of being cheats. The tone of the measure has caused resentment. It is covered up by doublethink about catching cheats, but, in practice, people will be forced off the benefit registers.

The present maximum period of disqualification for voluntary unemployment was fixed at six weeks in 1911, and remained for 75 years without being challenged. For some of that time there was high unemployment. The same arguments about pricing people into work, easing the rigidities in the labour market and so on were heard throughout the 1930s and 1940s, and even into the 1950s. Yet there was no suggestion that the disqualification period be changed. That period was extended to 13 weeks in 1986 and to 26 weeks in April 1988.

During the Committee stage of the 1986 Bill, the excuse offered by the then Minister for the extension of the period was that, although the number of people leaving jobs voluntarily had fallen steeply between 1979 and 1981 from 416,000 to 245,000, the numbers had risen in 1985 to nearly 300,000. Although that figure was still far below the 1979 level, the Minister said that it was a reason for more than doubling the period of disqualification.

Whatever the reasons were for the changes in the figures, they have nothing to do with changing the maximum period of disqualification. What is the connection? Where is the Minister's evidence? Why has there been no research or consultation? Why was the proposal not put forward in advance? I hope that you will not rule me out of order, Mr. Winterton, if I say that, although the Bill gives the impression of being brand new and tackling a matter that has not been thought about before, we must remind ourselves that if appears in the context of the biggest shake-up of the welfare state since Beveridge—the Fowler review. Yet there was no mention of the unemployed, their levels of benefit or their entitlement to it in the Green Paper or the White Paper that followed it. At the time, we asked questions about what would happen, particularly to the long-term unemployed, because the White and Green Papers were silent on the subject.

The proposal to extend the maximum period was tacked on to the 1986 Bill at the last moment, and it was done in that way for one purpose—to save money at the expense of the unemployed. It may have been easy for Ministers to pass over the unemployed as feckless and irresponsible, but they certainly were not benefit cheats, and the Government could not pretend that they were. The Government should not have given that as an excuse for altering the maximum period.

The increases, first to 13 and then to 26 weeks, show the Government's attitude and reasons for imposing the disqualification period. The change in period should not be imposed as a punishment for behaviour that the Government disapprove of when 360 the claimants have done nothing wrong. It is an attempt to protect the fund from claims by people who are thought to have become, or remained, unemployed as a result of their own actions.

The Secretary of State's subjective judgment is being built into legislation, without evidence. Is voluntary unemployment an insurable risk? In the past, all Governments took the view that voluntary unemployment was not an insurable risk and that someone in that category cannot expect to draw unemployment insurance benefit from the first day of unemployment. But it has also been agreed that a risk that is uninsurable from day one may become insurable if the person remains without a job some days or weeks later. He may decide for reasons of his own that he does not wish to stay in his present job, and instead of waiting until he has found another one, leaves in the reasonable expectation that something will come up or that he has an interview in prospect. If the person is disappointed and does not get another job, he may have to wait several weeks before the state accepts that his predicament could have been reasonably foreseen and that the risk has become insurable.

The Secretary of State may tell me that no one should leave a job on spec without the prospect of another job. I suggest that people have all sorts of reasons for leaving jobs. They may not be accepted by adjudication officers as just causes for leaving, but to the person concerned—the result of family circumstances of or whatever—they are perfectly reasonable. That applies particularly if the person thought that he might well find another job. It is not always a person's fault if he leaves a job on spec, and he should not be penalised for doing so.

What, then, is the maximum period for which a person should be expected to manage on his own resources before unemployment becomes insurable? If between 1911 and 1976 the answer to that question was six weeks, why has it now been extended to six months? Why does someone have to live on the resources of his former employment for six months when in the past it was accepted that he would exhaust whatever resources he might have saved in six weeks?

The real reason is that every increase in the disqualification period reduces the cost of unemployment benefit to the Secretary of State, the social security budget and the Treasury.

In the debate on the 1986 change, the former Minister gave another reason. He said:

"That suggests … that any discouraging effect that the legal deductions have been having has diminished or is ineffective."— [Official Report, Standing Committee B, 30 April 1986; c. 1870]

In other words, the Minister's predecessor considered that too many people were exercising the right to leave their jobs, so the national insurance scheme is now being used as a means of punishing them. That is the purpose of the clause and we seek to amend it. There should be no means of punishing people and discouraging future offenders because the offence was created by the Government and is not innate to people who leave their jobs.

JPEG 361 The same argument was used to defend the increase from 13 to 26 weeks. In a written answer on 10 November 1987 the Secretary of State said that a main purpose of the sanctions was to discourage people from leaving jobs voluntarily without just cause. He said that the numbers had again risen and that the penalty had to be doubled.

If the Government believe that people should be fined for leaving their jobs, they should legislate accordingly and leave the courts to decide on proper penalties. It is difficult to imagine that any court would impose a £900 fine for a first offence under such a law, but that is precisely the amount that a single person stands to lose from April 1989 if she or he is disqualified for 26 weeks and cannot claim income support. Even if income support can be claimed, the penalty is still over £360. Such is justice in clause 9.

Amendment No. 86 aims to restore unemployment benefit to its original level and reaffirm its original purpose. It does not aim to fine people for exercising their legal rights, but to protect the national insurance fund without causing needless hardship.

Adjudicating officers decide on the disqualification period. The maximum 26-week period is used as a blanket figure. Citizens advice bureaux and our own case work have found that the maximum 26-week period is used in the majority of cases. When alerted to that fact the Minister reassured us in a letter to the National Association of Citizens Advice Bureaux on 18 November 1988 that the Commissioner's decision would prevent indiscriminate implementation against claimants of the maximum 26-week period. He argued that adjudication officers

"should abandon their practice of automatically seeking to impose the maximum period of disqualification".

Sadly, despite that reassurance the full 26-weeks is used in almost all cases. In the meantime, adjudication officers report that they have no clear guidelines to follow when considering whether to impose less than the blanket coverage. Does the Minister consider that the maximum figure should be the figure used? If the Minister means that 26 weeks should be the time allowed, he should omit the word "maximum" because it suggests that people will get away with less and practice that does not happen. Adjudication officers should be given clear guidelines.

Timing also affects claimants. They suffer stress and financial hardship because of delays in the many stages of claiming unemployment benefit. The proposals can only exacerbate that. Before they make their decision, adjudication officers are still obliged to wait 28 days for the employer to respond to their investigative letter. The Employment Bill proposes further to reduce the employer's obligation to provide written reasons for dismissal. Therefore, we can only project that further delays will occur. As a result, more problems will be engendered by claimants. I should like the Minister to comment on the delays and assure us that that will not be so.
