TOWN AND COUNTRY PLANNING BILL,
[OFFICIAL REPORT.]
CLAUSE 17.
—(Power to make orders for preservation of certain buildings.)

Viscount CRANBORNE
: I beg to move, in page 20, line 6, to leave out from the word "has" to the word "may," in line 9, and to insert instead thereof the words "been submitted to the Minister, he."

The aim of this Amendment is to take all initiatory power to schedule historic houses out of the hands of the local authorities. I do not mean by this any criticism of local authorities. We all know that they are one of the glories of England, and that no other nation can produce such a body of voluntary workers, so efficient and honest. At the same time, the world is not perfect, and nearly everybody has his failings. One of the chief failings of local authorities is a complete lack of artistic sensibility. They look upon these questions entirely from the point of view of the economic prosperity of their towns and the convenience of their inhabitants, and the fact must be faced that very often economic and artistic interests clash. They are almost bound to clash. You get such a state of things as a very ancient house standing out into the street and interfering slightly with the traffic, or you get a narrow street of old houses which might be pulled down and the street widened in order to make a shopping centre. My experience is that in such a situation the local authority will be on the side of economic prosperity and not on the side of historic and artistic interest.

There has been a particular case of it lately—a glaring case—with regard to the 526 old town house at Dundee. That, I understand, is by far the most important building in Dundee. It was built by the father of Robert Adam and is a building of great artistic importance. Yet the Dundee Town Council, because the building was interfering with the development of the town, proposed to pull it down. A number of other instances of the same type could be given. I have had a production sent to me by the Society for the Preservation of Rural Amenities called "The Threat to the Peak," and in it, among other things, are a large number of criticisms of the Sheffield Town Council showing that they do not use their existing powers to produce by-laws to avert atrocities of various kinds. I do not think any of us blame city councils, town councils, and district councils—they really do not know or care about such things; but it seems a most fantastic thing, that, when we are bringing forward this Bill in order to improve the situation, these very people who have shown themselves particularly incapable of dealing with this aspect of planning be made the arbiters of taste in the matter. It seems a ridiculous thing.

That is not the only reason why they are not suitable authorities for this purpose. I think it is true to say that many members of local authorities themselves own land in the districts which are to be town-planned, and though I am certain that their decisions will be come to as far as possible in an unbiased way, it puts them in an impossible position. Take the example, which is not an unfair one, of a town councillor who has bought a small bit of land in the outskirts of his town with a view-to development later on. The town planning scheme is brought in, and this land contains certain old houses which interfere with the scheme of development. I am sure that this councillor will do his best to be unbiased, but it puts him in an intolerable position, and it is contrary to the whole position of this country that people who are interested should be made the judges in a matter of this kind. There is a further objection of a most fundamental kind. There is a provision in the Bill to schedule land in private ownership, but there is no provision to schedule land in public ownership. Very often the only important building in the town is in public ownership, as in Dundee. It may be an JPEG 527 old town hall, or a market house, or some Georgian buildings used as local government offices. If the local authority schedule these, well and good, but if they refuse to do it, the Minister has no power to make them. He can modify the schedule, which means that he can take things out if there are protests against it, but there is no initiatory power on the part of the Minister.

The right hon. Gentleman may say that the new provision which he foreshadowed last time we met will meet this case. He said that he would introduce a new Clause to allow private 'persons to get into communication with the Minister about the preservation of certain ancient buildings in their areas where the local authority has not acted. That was a concession to the hon. and gallant Member for Salisbury (Major Despencer-Robertson). That does make a certain difference, but it is a very haphazard method, because it will depend entirely on there being somebody else in the district who has better taste than the local authority, and such a person may not always exist. If we are going to deal with this thing, let us do it properly. Therefore it is proposed under this Amendment that the initiatory power should be in the hands of the Minister. I do not propose that it should be in his hands alone, for two reasons. The first is that I do not think the Minister would accept the whole onus of deciding, and the second is because I do not think the Committee would give the Minister such autocratic powers.

Therefore, it is proposed in a later Amendment in the name of the hon. Member for Salisbury to set up an advisory committee of experts. I understand that the hon. and gallant Member does not stand by the wording of his Amendment but only by the principle, and if the Minister were willing to accept the principle, my hon. and gallant Friend and those associated with him would be ready to accept any other plan if the right hon. Gentleman thought it more practicable. It is very important, however, that the principle of the advisory committee should be accepted. There is no lack of experts in this country. There are the Office of Works, the National Trust, the Fine Arts Commission, and innumerable people who have not very much to do, and plenty of taste, who would be able 528 to do this work perfectly well. The work they would have to do is not at all heavy. They would deal only with the outside of buildings. All they would have to do would be to take a large scale Ordnance map and mark the buildings which ought to be preserved. There would not be a day's work in any one scheme for the advisory committee, and there will not be so very many schemes.

I have had some figures sent to me, but as I understand that figures are regarded as misleading, I will not quote them, but even if the figures were multiplied by 10, the advisory committee would not be over-worked. Under the present scheme as it is put forward, the whole arbitrament of taste would be put into the hands of people whom we know are not really fitted to exercise it. They are very good for other jobs, but not for this. It is as though the Committee of Selection were to choose Hobbs to represent England in lawn tennis because he made 100 at Lords against Australia at cricket. They are fitted to do local government, but not this work. As an alternative, we should have, under the proposed Amendment, a committee of the greatest experts, who would be specially fitted to deal with the subject. I cannot believe that the Committee will take any other decision but in favour of this Amendment.

The MINISTER of HEALTH (Sir Hilton Young)
: I confess I thought, in view of some of the Amendments put down, that my Noble Friend would have attached less importance to this Amendment than he appears to do, but since he still attaches importance to it, let me deal with it and show that it would not really make a useful addition to the machinery of the Bill. The effect of the Amendment would be to prevent the powers which it is useful to give in respect of the preservation of interesting ancient buildings from a completion which they could receive alone from the Clause in its present form. Of course, I am under none of those illusions which the Noble Lord attributes to some of the advocates of the Bill as to the perfection of local authorities. On the other hand, I do not take such a gloomy view of it as some of my hon. Friends opposite. I know that admirable work has been done by many local authorities in the preservation of the beautiful monuments and ancient JPEG 529 buildings which are to be found in their districts, and such exceptions as that to which he has referred, about which I have nothing to say, are undoubtedly exceptions to the common run of the very careful handling of this question by local authorities.

The Noble Lord wants to secure to the central authority the responsibility of this power of preservation. That is already in existence under the existing provisions of the law relating to ancient monuments as administered by the Commissioners in touch with the Office of Works. There you have the machinery for central initiative. As the Noble Lord rightly says, in the new addition which I propose to Clause 13 you will have the machinery for the initiative on the part of private persons for the preservation of ancient buildings. There is something which you still want, and that is the initiative on the part of local authorities themselves to preserve their ancient buildings.

The Noble Lord takes a gloomy view of what the local authorities will do. But you must not deprive them of all means of grace. You must not prevent them doing what they can if they want to do it. The Clause gives an opportunity to local authorities to take action for preservation if they wish to do so, under the reasonable control and checks which the Bill provides. The Commissioners of Works desire to see their powers for preservation supplemented by a reasonable extension of the powers of local authorities, and I think that the further Amendment which I am putting down to the Clause will do a great deal to appease the reasonable anxiety of some Members on the subject of ancient buildings. I must not anticipate the question of advisory committees and I will only say at this point that the Minister will always have, not only the general assistance of expert and skilled persons of high taste but also organised assistance, through advisory councils and committees, and voluntary assistance of that sort. In the circumstances I think that this is a useful extension of the powers of the good local authorities and that there is nothing to be feared from the bad authorities, if there be such, and I trust that the Noble Lord will not press his Amendment.

His Amendment would still enshrine the principle on which the whole Bill is based, that it is necessary to employ a staff of bureaucrats to preserve owners of property from their own folly. Much of this Bill is based on the remarkable belief that economic efficiency can only be found in district councils, and this Clause rests on the assumption that beauty can only safely be entrusted to bureaucracy. Let us consider for one moment to whom we owe the existence of these buildings which the Clause seeks to preserve. These buildings were not created by Ministers of Health, or district councils, or Offices of Works. They were created by individuals, by free Englishmen, many of them building without architects and according to what seemed to them to be the best plan. These buildings have been preserved to us not by Acts of Parliament but by the loving care of generation after generation of free Englishmen who did not know what a Minister of Health was and who did not, in many cases, know what a district council was. It seems folly to assume that the continued existence of these buildings can best be ensured by entrusting further powers to any form of government.

It is true that in many cases these buildings are threatened by modern economic conditions. Those conditions have been produced, to a large extent, precisely by Bills of this kind. If the people who put up these buildings had had to pay £19,000,000 per year for a Ministry of Health, it is unlikely that many of these buildings would ever have come into existence at all. I object very strongly to the whole principle of the Clause. It may be true, as I say, that there is a threat to existing buildings, and the Minister's Amendment undoubtedly would go some way to meet our objection but not the whole way. There is a case of which the Minister, I JPEG 531 think, has some knowledge which may he mentioned in this connection. A neighbour of mine in the country is the owner of a very large building of historical interest. Modern conditions, he expects, will make it impossible for him to keep a roof on it, and accordingly he is building himself a small house in his park. It would be intolerable, of course, to live right against a mouldering ruin, and he contemplates destroying the building. The state of affairs which makes that necessary is deplorable, but you will only make conditions of that kind worse by saddling local authorities with duties which they are unfitted to perform and creating the large additional machinery contemplated in the Clause which will have to be paid for.

My Noble Friend who moved the Amendment said that the task of scheduling ancient buildings would be a very simple one and would be hardly more than a day's work for a local advisory committee. I am a member of the Royal Commission on Ancient and Historical Monuments, a Commission containing, besides myself, many persons of great eminence and judgment and employing a trained and expert staff. We estimate that the completion of our survey of ancient and historical buildings in England will take us 86 years. That hardly agrees with my noble relative's idea of the simplicity of the task. It is not and cannot be a simple task. The idea that the Ministry should be responsible for all these buildings, and that there should be the machinery contemplated in the Clause for action by local authorities and appeal to the Minister and consultation with the Commissioners of Works and so forth seems to me to be bureaucracy run mad. There would be enough in each case to keep scores of clerks busy for months, and, even with the Minister's Amendment, many thousands of buildings would still be affected. I am glad that the Minister has not accepted the Amendment, but I hope that the Committee will do the drastic thing and reject the whole Clause, because it contains a principle to which a House of Commons pledged to economy ought not to give its consent.

Sir H. YOUNG
: I beg to move, in page 20, line 11, to leave out from the word "district" to the word "and," in line 13, and to insert instead thereof the words:

"directing that, without their consent, the building, if it is a dwelling-house, shall not be demolished or, if it is not a dwelling-house, shall neither be altered nor demolished."

This is a very substantial alteration which I am proposing in the Clause in order to meet a body of opinion, which has been very clearly and very forcibly expressed, as to what the proper and legitimate content of this Clause should be. I think the first in the field was my Noble Friend who moved the first Amendment to-day and since then there have been opportunities for gauging public opinion on this question, and I have received the impression that the alteration which I am now proposing is necessary in order to bring the scope of the Bill into harmony both with what is right and with general public opinion on the question. As the Bill stood originally, it gave what I call the power for preservation or power to prevent anybody in the case of a building of architectural or historic interest from demolishing or altering that building.

It is very forcibly said that that goes too far in respect of inhabited and inhabitable houses, for two reasons; first of all, because it is too much of an interference with the rights of the individual to prevent him from altering a house 538 in which he is living, and, secondly, because it is not necessary in the interests of the community to prevent even the most ancient and historic buildings from being altered. Some of the most interesting historic buildings have been freely altered in the past, and accretions and alterations may have given them so much of their interest and beauty. Both in the interests of beauty and in the interests of the individual, we think there should be a limitation of the power; and the limitation which commends itself to common sense is that you should not be able to step in and demolish a lovely old house, but that you should be able to alter it. The reason is that once you have demolished a thing, it is gone for ever, but alterations can always be put back. Nothing irremediable happens there. I trust that this Amendment will commend itself to the Committee, and that it will be found to cover a number of other criticisms which may be advanced for other Amendments in the future.
