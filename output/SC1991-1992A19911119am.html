We shall particularly watch out for some of the luminaries of the campaign in support of the poll tax. For example, we shall watch with special interest for the moment that the dread words "poll tax" pass the lips of the hon. Member for Thurrock (Mr. Janman). There will be a special moment when the Minister of State, the last defender of the poll tax, uses that phrase to describe the measure. He has already sold the pass because, if I recall correctly, he was with the Secretary of State when he consented to sit under the billboard which I mentioned already.

As someone once memorably asked, what is in a name? We have the answer in the extreme lengths to which the Conservative party went to try to dissociate itself from the term "poll tax". One cannot blame it for that because the poll tax was redolent with history's judgment of what is regarded universally as a repressive and unacceptable measure. We need look back only to the peasants' revolt of the 14th century and to the perhaps less noticed, but equally unsuccessful, attempt in 1641 to introduce a poll tax. We know now from the most recent attempt to introduce a poll tax—which exists still and which will continue to apply for at least another year or two—that the term "poll tax" is reviled on the lips of virtually everyone.

That brings us to the council tax, the new poll tax, the new label attached to the Government's latest efforts to disentangle themselves from their difficulties. As I suggested earlier, the term "council tax" seems to have the fingerprints of the Secretary of State on it. In the absence of evidence to the contrary, we can ascribe to him the responsibility for dreaming up that fanciful title. The term is unusual for a tax. It describes not what is being taxed— income tax, property tax or value added tax—but the government agency that is, in principle, levying the tax. That is virtually without precedent, and I should be interested to learn if hon. Members can think of other examples.

I conclude that the measure is an attempt by the Secretary of State to lay any complaints about the tax at the doors of the authorities that are forced to levy it. That is why it is called the council tax. It is part of that great, sustained, all-embracing and comprehensive effort by the Government throughout their lifetime to denigrate local government. It is designed to put councils in the dock; if people have a complaint about the council tax, they must take it to the council. The Government are trying to say that the tax has nothing to do with them.

That attempt is part of the Government's long-standing and, I am glad to say, unsuccessful effort regarding the poll tax. In the latter days of the poll tax, when the true magnitude of the disaster became apparent, the Government always tried to say that it was nothing to do with them and that their hands were clean because the poll tax was levied and collected by local authorities. It seems that the same stupid attempt is being made again and, in some senses, with even less justification.

It goes without saying that the council tax is a Government creation, otherwise the Committee would not be debating the Bill. There would be no council tax were it not for the feverish imaginations of the Ministers and officials who dreamed it up.

8 The tax is inaccurately described as a council tax. Even according to its justification, it is not set and determined by local authorities. That is because, amid the structure of local government finance, the council tax—or whatever other form of revenue is raised by local government—is very much a residual factor. It is the one thing that is not, at least in principle, set and decided elsewhere. It is a balancing act. It is the element that responds to the decisions of others. The amount of revenue raised by the council tax is determined by decisions about revenue support grant and the uniform business rate. Even that element is beyond the control of local authorities.

With the powers taken on an earlier occasion and reaffirmed in the Bill, even that residual element, even the figure put to it— [Interruption.] Let me conclude my point because I see that the hon. Member for Battersea is wearing a puzzled expression. He sometimes has difficulty following such points, so it may help him if I conclude and then he can decide whether he has understood.

In accordance with the powers confirmed in the Bill to cap all local authorities, the mere threat—let alone exercise—of those powers means that every budget, every residual element, every balancing item and every poll tax or council tax bill will be decided by central Government. That is why I made the point earlier that every council tax bill will have on it the fingerprints of the Secretary of State, either because he has set the budget and applied the cap or because, by threatening to apply the cap, he has compelled the budget to be set in a way that he approves. Either way, every budget and bill will be the Secretary of State's budget and bill. That is why calling this tax the council tax, in a vain attempt to pin the blame on local authorities, is not only vain, but stupid.
