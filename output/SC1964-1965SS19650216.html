JPEG 5 What in fact do "standards of education" mean? They obviously can vary considerably from one part of the country to another, from one school to another, and so on. 12 To take the arguments in turn. First, "standards of education". It is interesting to note that the Wheatley Report—which we have followed in the drafting of the Bill wherever possible—in paragraph 7, dealing with the powers and functions of the Council, uses a number of phrases—

"the determination of standards of qualifications for entrants to training … the standards to be required … standards of admission … entrance standards".

The Wheatley Committee showed no particular inclination to restrict itself to "educational qualifications", or to anything which could not be best summarised in these words, "standards of education". That, of course, is a wider definition than "educational qualifications", but it is to be preferred because it is an inclusive term.

I must make it clear that it will be for the Council to decide how to determine the standards of education which it regards as necessary for various purposes. In other words, it will have to determine the educational qualifications which measure up to the standards of education which it regards as necessary. We are giving the Council the responsibility of deciding what educational qualifications measure up to the standards which it selects as suitable.

This is important, because in our discussion of these Amendments we are dealing with fundamental attitudes to the whole Bill and the whole concept behind it. We are discussing the relationship between the responsibilities of the Secretary of State for Scotland and the powers and the duties of the Council itself. What the Bill proposes to do is to arrive at a correct apportionment of functions between the two. In using this wider definition of "standard of education" we are clearly imposing on the Council a responsibility which we believe that it will take seriously, while recognising the realities of the situation which will face it.
