Mr. R. A. McCrindle
(Brentwood and Ongar)
: When the Committee rose on Tuesday, I was in the process of calling attention to the level of air fares and relating that to the effect on the revenues of British Airways. I was trying to elicit a reaction from my hon. Friend the Minister to some of the points that I had made.

I had just pointed out that the Government had understandable pride in low transatlantic fares, but I expressed a fear that unless large fare increases came along soon we could face the failure of an airline. I do not imply that that airline would be British Airways, but they could not escape and have not been able to escape from the consequences of the dilution of revenue that has been the result of the lowering of fares across the Atlantic.

I had turned to Europe, and I had conceded that there was scope for reduction in European air fares. Massive across-the-board reductions not only are not desirable if the service that passengers have come to expect is to be maintained, but it is not possible unless the airlines' finances are to be strained almost to breaking point. Nor is it any good to engage in glib talk about the breaking cartels as though 76 the achievement of that desirable objective meant that air fares would come tumbling down without economic consequences for the airlines. It is instructive that the greatest resistance to an across-the-board reduction in European air fares comes not from IATA but from Lufthansa, which the Committee will not have failed to notice is about the most profitable airline in Europe.

I wonder sometimes whether the movement to reduce air fares in Europe during the past 18 months is entirely appreciated. The fares are often compared unfavourably with air fares in the United States, forgetting altogether the entirely different circumstances of lower aviation fuel cost and lower charges. One can consider two comparable routes, such as Amsterdam to London and San Francisco to Santa Barbara. In July 1980, the ratio of the European fare to the American fare was 2·9, whereas in September 1981 that had come down to 1·5. So without the breaking of the cartel to which some people continue to turn their attention, considerable movement was achieved in that case. If the Committee believes that that is an isolated example perhaps I could give a more pointed one by contrasting the fares from Stockholm to London and Los Angeles to Seattle. In July 1980 the ratio was 4·6, and in September 1981 it had come down to 1·5.

I repeat that we should look for some reductions in European air fares. We should expect some further innovation by the British airlines, but massive dilution of revenue would be unhelpful to British Airways and ultimately not in the best interests of the consumer.

I wish to quote from a respected travel writer, Mr. Roger Bray, who writes for the London Standard. He says:

" But people cannot travel at prices which do not allow the maintenance of a viable industry unless somebody somewhere, subsidises them."

I assume that the Minister will confirm this morning that whoever may be prepared to subsidise the airlines, it will not be the Government. Therefore, I hope that he will take on board my points about the role that the Government must play in resisting the undue dilution of British Airways revenue. It is all a matter of the profitability of our national airlines.

I wish to relate those remarks to privatisation and to suggest that the profitability of British Airways is crucial if the Government's policy of privatisation is to proceed. I start by reminding the Minister that I am in favour of the privatisation of British Airways. I have asked some questions about it in the past, but I am in agreement with the policy. I wish to see it carried out successfully. There is no clash on a basic point of principle, but I found myself in some slight disagreement with my hon. Friend the Member for Ruislip-Northwood (Mr. Wilkinson). We face a real dilemma because there is an economic timing and a political timing for the sale of British Airways. My hon. Friend is prepared to press ahead with all conceivable speed because,, among other reasons, he feels that it is essential JPEG 77 to privatise British Airways so that the airline will be encouraged to revamp their management and inject a more entrepreneurial approach into their affairs.

I am not persuaded that British Airways are necessarily waiting for privatisation before showing considerable signs of marketing expertise and an upgrading of their management, which I have always considered to be among the better management of nationalised corporations.

I have some real questions about the date of privatisation for my hon. Friend the Minister. Can he tell us today on which date the Government wish to offer the shares to the public? Secondly, am I correct to assume that included in the list of considerations is whether, by that time, there will be a track record of profitability by British Airways?

I wish to put the matter to the Minister as gently as I can. If the timing is wrong, is it possible that not only shall we be left with some of the stock on our hands, but we shall be left with political egg on our joint faces because it will be seen that a bold experiment has not come off? In all those circumstances, I hope that my hon. Friend will take the point that we must offer to the public a going concern with a growing track record of profitability.

I suggest several ways in which the Government, without direct interference, can be of assistance in returning the airline to that track of profitability. The charges to which we referred during an earlier sitting of the Committee could be lightened if there were a modest change in Government policy. There could be a realism about air fares. Finally, there should be a recognition by the Minister that competition from independent airlines is a policy to which both he and I subscribe.

However, I suspect that he, like me, will concede that at the moment the pursuit of the introduction of massive independent competition to British Airways may once again be seen as reducing the viability and profitability of British Airways as an airline, the assets of which we wish to dispose.

I suggest that the two sides of the Government's policy on the matter might be seen to be in conflict. Either we build up British Airways ready for the sale that surely is pre-eminent in Government policy, or we encourage maximum competition from the independent airlines. The Minister must turn his attention again to whether we can continue with both sides simultaneously in the way that we have been trying to do since the passage of the Civil Aviation Act 1980.

I have a question for both British Airways and the Government. I have been talking about the dilution of revenue of British Airways, and I come now the vexed question of discounting. We have all become aware of what is called the bucket shop syndrome—the tendency of airlines, unable to attract custom at full fares, being prepared to dump large numbers of tickets on to the market at discount prices, again to the advantage of the consumer. Who would begrudge the opportunity of people to travel at a lower price than would otherwise be available? Again, not only the revenues of British Airways but the revenues of most international airlines have been diluted as a result.

In the past, British Airways have been critical of the bucket shops' development, although one has not always been totally persuaded that they have not been dumping more than a few tickets on to the market. Be that as it may, they have now decided that they will enter the discounting game openly and sell discounted tickets through all official travel agency outlets. I endorse that, but I am a little concerned that this is a further example of the dilution of revenue.

What is the Government's policy on discounting? There is little doubt in my mind that the announcement by British Airways is an indication that they are breaking the rules, but they and other airlines have been breaking the rules for years. I was instrumental in forcing the setting up of a working party on the subject in 1977, under the chairmanship of the hon. Member for Hackney, Central (Mr. Davis), which got us nowhere. The discussion has been continuing for a long time. Would it not be honest for the Government now to say that discounting is a permanent feature of the aviation scene and that as a result we should stop all the hypocrisy of saying that airlines should not sell their wares at a discounted price if, in their commercial judgment, that is what they wanted to do?

It is irreconcilable that we should have a statement from the IATA airlines at Cannes a few weeks ago grandly announcing a new joint effort to remove discount tickets from the market, followed three weeks later by a British Airways announcement that they will flood the market with discounted tickets. That is some evidence of the present state of psychology of the aviation industry. The Government have a responsibility to say that of course they wish the public to continue to enjoy discounted tickets, as far as they can be made available commercially by the airline, and that there will be no question in future of any prosecution.

My wish is to bolster British Airways without detriment to the independent airlines. I suggest to my hon. Friend the Minister that the Government's role is to maximise the revenue of British Airways by encouraging them to continue the imaginative marketing that they have shown a flair for in the recent past, but that the Government should give positive assistance with fare policy and charging. I hope that the Minister will recognise that these 80 are pertinent points relative to the whole purpose of the Bill. For whatever reason, we all have an interest in a viable and profitable British Airways. I have suggested that some of the actions that have been taken by the Government, and some that are in prospect, could lead in an opposite direction. That would not be in the Minister's interest. I should not want it to happen, and it would not be the most successful way to lead to the privatisation of British Airways that both the Minister and I wish to see achieved.

Clause 3
APPLICATION OF FINANCIAL LIMIT OF BRITISH AIRWAYS BOARD TO FOREIGN CURRENCY BORROWING

Mr. Sproat
: I beg to move amendment No. 15, in page 2, line 5, leave out ' subsection (3)' and insert ' subsections (3) and (4)'.

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to take Government amendment No. 16.

Mr. Sproat
: May I first express my appreciation to the hon. Member for Batley and Morley (Mr. Woolmer) for his earlier remarks? I quite understand his reservations about certain aspects, to which we shall no doubt return.

This is a purely technical amendment, and I hope not to detain the Committee too long. Nevertheless it is only proper that I should at least put on record why we feel that it is necessary. I shall outline briefly the purpose of clause 3(1).

Subsection (1) provides that the sterling value of British Airways foreign currency borrowings should normally be calculated, for the purposes of assessing British Airways' position against the financial limit, by reference to the exchange rates prevailing on the last working day before the loans were received by British Airways. It thus fixes the sterling value of foreign currency loans against the financial limit, in contrast to existing legislation under which British Airways foreign currency loans have to be valued according to current exchange rates and have fluctuated widely in value as a result, as I pointed out on the previous amendment.

The reason why I am now proposing that foreign currency overdrafts should be excluded from subsection (1) is that the new method of valuation would not be easily applicable to them. As many of my hon. Friends will know from bitter personal experience, overdrafts involve a constantly moving balance and are subject to a far greater number of transactions than the medium and long-term borrowings for which subsection (1) was intended. It would be unnecessarily complex and expensive for British Airways to value each drawing on their overdraft according to subsection (1) and then to keep track of how much of it had been repaid under subsection (2) in order to calculate the sterling value of the outstanding overdraft against the financial limit at any given time. For those reasons, I believe that it is better to exclude foreign currency overdrafts altogether from subsection (1), which means that they will continue to be valued as they would be now— that is, at current exchange rates. This will not reintroduce a significant element of uncertainty into the assessment of British Airways' position against the financial limit, because British Airways' foreign 102 currency overdrafts will always be very small—certainly not more than £10 million initially—in comparison with their medium and long-term currency loans, which as the Committee knows, already amount to many hundreds of millions of pounds.

In the light of that technical explanation, I hope that the Committee will accept the amendment.

Amendment agreed to.

Clause 3, as amended, ordered to stand part of the Bill.
