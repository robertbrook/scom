In the 1957 Bill, and in the White Paper, it was made clear that though the Government were not in favour of compulsory amalgamation, they would give every encouragement to either co-operation or amalgamation of smaller units that could take advantage of any grant that might be available. That statement has been repeated many times over, and I think that we say almost the same thing in " Prosper the Flowers ". How many holdings that will exclude is very difficult for the Minister, the Parliamentary Secretary or myself to estimate. In a document sent to every hon. Member of the Committee, the National Farmers' Union states that as at June, 1957, there were 161,522 holdings of under 20 acres in England and Wales.

In paragraph 18 of the White Paper the Minister gives some figures for the whole of the United Kingdom. He tells us that there are approximately 500,000 holdings of from one acre to the maximum acreage, but he goes on to say that a large number of those ½ million holdings are supposed to be part-time holdings. He estimates that the number of JPEG 99 full-time farm businesses in the United Kingdom to be slightly in excess of 300,000, of which more than two-thirds will fail to qualify for grant because of their size or because of their standard man-days; they are either too big to qualify or too efficient.

Then he says that most of the remainder, or something like 65,000, will qualify either for the small farms grant or for the supplementary scheme. In addition, it is suggested that

"… about 25,000 former recipients of marginal assistance are expected to be eligible only for the Supplementary Scheme …"

That makes 90,000 in all. If, therefore, we take the slightly over two-thirds of the 300,000 that cannot qualify because of size or efficiency, plus the 90,000—the 65,000 and the 25,000—it leaves, approximately, 200,000 holdings within the United Kingdom that can qualify neither for the one thing nor the other.

I think that my figures are correct, but for the benefit of all hon. Members I will repeat them. These are the Minister's figures, as shown in paragraph 18 of the White Paper. According to that, there are 500,000 holdings of all sizes. There are over 300,000 full-time businesses, of which two-thirds—approximately 210,000—will not qualify because they are too large or too efficient. There are 65,000 that may qualify for a small farm grant, and 25,000 that may qualify under the supplementary scheme. That leaves 200,000 holdings—most of them, I suppose very small; between one acre and 19 acres—in the United Kingdom that, for one reason or another, cannot qualify for any sort of grant.

In those 200,000 holdings there will, of course, be every size, kind and gradation between one acre and 20 acres. Some will be associated with the village pub. In the old days, the pub had an odd field or two associated with it because the sales of beer alone were never sufficient to maintain the landlord. Some smallholdings will be created by the making of new roads or by other forms of industrial development.

Others will be associated with forestry, and many of them will have no possible chance of ever becoming viable. That, we generally accept. On the other hand, out of the 200,000—particularly where two, three or more very small units have 100 joined, there must be many that could, if they co-operated and/or amalgamated, make themselves viable with the appropriate Government assistance.

It is clear that there will be many of the smaller holdings in, say, Suffolk or Norfolk, where there are only one, two, three or four acres, that will qualify because of standard man-days—intensive horticulture; and others where they will qualify because of intensive livestock production with purchased feedingstuffs. But there will still be a large number of smaller units that could, by cooperation or amalgamation, ultimately make themselves viable.

The only purpose of the Amendment is to persuade the Minister to make possible the co-operation or amalgamation of two or more units. We think that that sort of encouragement should be given. Indeed, it is an opportunity that the right hon. Gentleman just cannot afford to miss. It is very interesting to note that hon. Members opposite are utterly unconcerned about those 200,000 small units. They have not put down a solitary Amendment of a constructive character. Of the only two Amendments that have been voted on, they have voted against us—when we were trying to help them to help these small farmers. However, they are apparently not concerned about two-fifths of the total number of holdings.

For 30-odd years in the Chamber I have heard hon. Members who ought to be sitting opposite but who have mostly passed away, wax almost poetical when talking about farming being a way of life, and the enormous value of these small farmers to our social life. Today, hon. Members opposite have no time for the smaller units. By their actions they are demonstrating beyond any doubt that they are concerned only with the bigger farmers.

The Amendment is a very simple one. We say—or, at least, we mean—that if there are adjoining farms it is practical and sensible for them to amalgamate. The small farmers concerned are anxious to amalgamate and they should be given the encouragement and the opportunity to do so. Finally, they should be given the grant or grants that would make it worth their while to carry out such a scheme. We think that the Amendment is of some importance. We hope that the JPEG 101 Minister will see the wisdom of grasping this lifeline, without which he may live to see the departure of many of his hon. Friends behind him as a result of their having failed in their duty to the smaller farms.
