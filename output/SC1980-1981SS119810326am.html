I am glad that the Bill refers to children who have special educational needs, and not to the disabled child or the mentally handicapped child, because it goes much wider than those two groups. One small example of what I mean by that is the child who in the first two or three years of his life—perhaps because of premature birth, an accident or illness— does not develop normally. There is nothing wrong with the child; he is neither physically disabled nor mentally handicapped, but his development has been retarded. In the first years of his life, or even at some point once he has started school, the child may have been in a car accident and left unconscious, or incapable of taking part in education for six months or a year. In terms of his age, that child has special educational needs, because he requires to be taken through the work that he has missed more rapidly than other children, so that he catches up with his peer group.

That is a special educational need, as is that of those whose development has been retarded at an early age because of an illness or something else that has happened to them. Sometimes the cause may be emotional. There are children who, for some reason, have been disturbed at a very early age. They have to be taken through that by psychological means. The retardation of their development is not mental disability in the sense that is normally meant, but the children have to be force fed a little in order to push them through and bring them up to the same level as their peers.

When such children start school, or during their first two or three years at school, they can be given more care and attention at a special school, or through remedial education or attendance at special units for some of their educational time. They can be brought up to the same level, so that when they reach, perhaps, the end of the infants' stage of the primary school, at the age of 7 or 8, they are on the same level.
