Mr. Peter Ainsworth

The local authority would have to prove that a hedge on which damaging work had been carried out was protected by the Bill, that approval had not been given through the notification procedure and that the work was not permitted under clause 5, which covers, for example, the resiting of a gate.

The Bill requires a landowner to establish in his defence that work was permitted or that a hedge was not protected. By dispensing with that as a defence and requiring the local authority to show that the work was not permitted, the burden of proof would be shifted from the defendant to the local authority. I see that my honourable Friend is nodding, so he is in agreement so far. The prosecution—the local authority—would be doubly burdened. In criminal proceedings the burden of proof rests initially with the prosecution. Once the facts have been established, the burden shifts to the defendant. The role of the prosecution—the local authority—is to show that an offence has been committed: that there was a hedgerow, that it was on land within the scope of the BUI and that the person accused of removing it did so. The defendant must establish that, because of facts particular to him, he did not commit the offence. That is the general nature of a defence inlaw.

40 It is wrong in principle for a local authority to have to establish that facts exist that are better known to the accused. The defendant, who carried out the work, is in a better position to establish the facts of his defence than the local authority—for example, evidence of removal and fact that it had not extended for more than 20m. It is surely right that the landowner should be required to consider carefully any work that he undertakes. The incentive to do so would be considerably and unacceptably relaxed by the amendment.

That approach is recognised in other legislation—for example, the tree preservation order system under which the burden of establishing that a tree that was felled without consent was dead, dying or dangerous and that its removal was therefore permitted rests with the defendant.

The clause does not contradict existing and well-established legal practice and the amendment is unnecessary.

Amendment No. 53 provides that hedgerows adjacent to, as well as on, common land, protected land, a golf-course or a racecourse should be covered. That is an important provision because hedgerows of some antiquity often grow at the boundaries of such land. Those hedgerows would not be protected by the Bill's provisions. Amendment No. 53 would provide that protection.

Amendment No. 54 provides that a hedgerow on an open space, as defined in the Town and Country Planning Act 1990, should be protected. That relates to our discussions last week on the need to protect urban and suburban hedgerows on open spaces. 42 The same issue is tackled by amendment No. 56, which gives an alternative definition of public open space in an urban area.
