When all those Tory high-tax Budgets are taken into account, those increases add up to £805.31 more being imposed on a typical family.

The Labour party and the British people believe that all of those increases have blown apart any Conservative claim to be a low-tax party and the statistics bear that out. In 1978–79 total tax as a proportion of GDP stood at 34.3 per cent. In 1995–96 the ratio will be 37 per cent. In 1978–79, 32.2 per cent, of the gross income of a family with an average income went in direct and indirect tax. This year that figure will rise to 38.2 per cent. In 1978–79 income tax and national insurance took 26.4 per cent, of a typical family's gross income. From April that figure will be 27.4 per cent.

Whichever way we look at it, people are facing a heavier tax burden as a consequence of 16 years of a Conservative Government. That tax burden has not just become heavier, it has become more and more unfair as its relative burden has shifted from the richest in society to middle and lower-income Britain.

The comparison between the richest fifth and the poorest fifth of the population is revealing. In 1979 the poorest fifth paid 31 per cent, of their gross income in tax and the richest fifth paid 38 per cent. By 1992, the most recent year for which I have the figures, the poorest fifth paid 39 per cent, of their income in tax and the richest fifth paid just 34 per cent. That is a total inversion of the progressive principle in taxation which used to command agreement across the political spectrum.

Sir George Young
: As with the previous debate, the Government's philosophy is to try to maintain a low rate of corporation tax for smaller companies. We are proposing a rate of 25 per cent., which compares well with the 1978 rate of 42 per cent. That is a worthwhile reduction for this country's smaller companies and it is one of the lowest rates in the world.

I understand what the hon. Lady said about simplification. For those small businessmen who are not companies, but who trade in their own right, self-assessment will bring worthwhile simplification and, 158 hopefully, savings. But clause 32 deals with corporation tax for small companies and there will always be a difference between the regime governing smaller companies and that governing those who trade in their own right. It will not always be possible to marry them up, which I think was the thrust of one of the hon. Lady's remarks.

We have tried in the Budget to ease the burden on smaller firms. Such measures include transitional rate relief, capping increases in the uniform business rate at 10 per cent, for large businesses and 7.5 per cent, for small ones, and cutting employer national insurance contributions by 0.6 per cent, for employees earning less than £205 a week, which will particularly benefit smaller employers. Smaller employers are now allowed to pay their national insurance and PAYE quarterly, and we are consulting on annual accounting periods for VAT. The impact of those measures on small firms can be seen in the encouraging signs of recovery after a difficult recession. A European business survey conducted last month for Grant Thornton showed 59 per cent, of United Kingdom companies enjoying rising profitability and there has been a dramatic increase in self-employment—200,000 more people are self-employed than two years ago.

Other measures in the Budget help small companies to access funds such as venture capital trusts and enterprise investment clauses. We shall come on to that subject shortly.

We believe that we have got the regime right. We are keeping one of the lowest rates that we have, we are simplifying and streamlining when we can, and there are other improvements in the pipeline. Of course, we recognise that the representatives of small firms and those who are self-employed would like to see further progress. I hope that we can maintain our dialogue with them, and make that progress.
