Natural Heritage (Scotland) Bill [Lords]
New Clause 3
GEESE ON ISLAY

".—(1) It shall be the duty of Scottish Natural Heritage to establish on conservation and environmental grounds a desirable maximum to the numbers of Barnacle and White Fronted geese feeding on Islay.

(2) If it appears to Scottish Natural Heritage at anytime that the maximum under subsection (1) above has been exceeded, they shall introduce such measures as seem to them to be appropriate to control the population and to maintain it below that maximum.

(3) Scottish Natural Heritage may from time to time vary the maximum numbers established under subsection (1) above.".— [ Mrs Ray Michie.]

In the 1960s and 1970s, people had to obtain a licence from the then Department of Agriculture and Fisheries for Scotland to shoot geese, because they were a protected species. About 750 to 900 geese were shot each year, mainly by visiting sportsmen who provided a useful income to the islanders because of their need for bed-and-breakfast accommodation. They assisted the farmers to protect the crops. At that time, the total number of barnacle geese amounted to 12,000 as opposed to 30,000 as it is now. However, under the birds directive, the shooting of barnacle geese for sporting purposes was outlawed, but given the present position, that decision was disappointing.

The Nature Conservancy Council suggested bird scaring as a method of solving the problem. However, that meant that the geese were simply transferred from one field to the next and back again. They are wily birds and are not fooled by flag waving—undertaken as part of a youth training scheme—and other methods by which they can be moved from the best grazing land. An 80 per cent grant is provided for the bird-scaring scheme and I am worried that it costs £80,000 a year to move geese from one field to another, and back again. Given that about 800 geese 264 were shot a year in the pre-scaring days, that means that £100 is provided for each goose that is not shot.

Naturally, I am delighted that we have increased the number of barnacle geese in Islay. We must not forget that that is closely related to the nesting season in Greenland. We must be flexible and as the numbers of geese increase and decline, we must react accordingly, so that such a valuable stock is maintained on Islay. With the best will in the world, we must find a compromise between retaining the geese that we all admire and love, and rectifying the undoubted damage that has been done to the agricultural grazing land, which has had such a bad impact on farming. We, in Scotland, know that not many areas are more difficult to farm than Islay.

We should still maintain or increase the number of white-fronted geese. If we do not do anything about the barnacle geese, however, their numbers will increase to the point of no return when there will be insufficient grazing to maintain barnacle flocks. We do not want the birds to go hungry, or to move to other parts of Scotland that they have not frequented before.

Islay is not a bird sanctuary—it is an island on which many people live and work in difficult circumstances. We must ensure that they are given a fair opportunity to continue making their livelihoods. At the same time, we must maintain a magnificent flock of birds that give so much pleasure to so many people. However, a limit must be set and, because the birds now number 30,000 or more, the problem is becoming serious.

I do not wish to wait until Scottish Natural Heritage is in operation. I want the Nature Conservancy Council for Scotland to discuss the matter with the Royal Society for the Protection of Birds, the British Association for Shooting and Conservation and others who have a practical knowledge of the subject. If nothing else, our constructive debate has shown that there is a major problem to which it will be difficult to find a solution. We must not ignore that problem nor is it enough to implement, at enormous expense, a bird-scaring flag-waving scheme, which has achieved little success. The grant of £80,000 could be used much more usefully for the conservation purposes of conservation.
