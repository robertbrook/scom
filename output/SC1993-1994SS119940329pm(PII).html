Mr. George Robertson
: Before we were rudely interrupted, I wanted to quote the hon. Member for Swindon, one of the silent members of the Committee. He has not spoken here but last week he spoke on another crucially important embarrassment for the Government. He said:

"I have to say in the way of an apology that I and the majority of my colleagues in Parliament did not appreciate the way it would work. We got it wrong."

Had the Prime Minister said that this afternoon he might not have had such a reception. The hon. Member for Swindon was talking about the Child Support Agency. He was trying to extend the culpability for the mistake across more than just the parliamentary Conservative party. That was the point he was making but he might just as well have spoken about the Local Government etc. (Scotland) Bill. Had he been listening during all the long hours he has spent here he should be beginning to realise precisely what sort of difficulty the Government are getting themselves into, particularly in respect of water and sewerage services which we are now beginning to discuss.

Perhaps the contrition that the hon. Member for Swindon showed about the Child Support Agency, rightly and properly given what has now come to light, should be applied to the Bill. His boss is absent, but the hon. Gentleman remains silent. Inside himself I am sure that he is beginning to have the same nagging doubt about water and sewerage in Scotland that he should have had earlier about the Child Support Agency. Those who watch our proceedings should be aware of that background information.

We have heard the Government's new explanations for their case. I call them new because we have not had much by way of explanation from them up to now. Their first argument is that there is no alternative to their proposal: the old authorities have gone and joint boards are not good enough. But there is an alternative. It is before the Committee in this very amendment, which calls for 12 boards, mirroring the existing local authority structure. It is a structure that works and delivers and has produced good quality water for the people of Scotland. The Government should vote for that amendment.

If the Government want verification they should not listen to me because I would be seen to be partisan, they 1618 should turn to page 21 of their consultative document. A rereading of it would reward any hon. Member, because it states:

"A 15 authority model could arguably work as well as the current 12 authority structure, although some joint working arrangements might be needed."

The Government's own document contains the admission that the Opposition's amendment would work effectively.

We have exposed the Government's case as the poverty-stricken case that it is. Our 12 board structure would maintain the effectiveness of the delivery of water and sewerage services and would provide boards that were both close and accountable to the people they served. Conservative Members have told us that only quangos could have the expertise to run the water and sewerage industry. No one in this Committee or the wider world really believes that. No one believes it is necessary to have quangos uniquely made up of the Secretary of State's appointees to do the job better than local councillors who have done it for the past 150 years during which the industry has been in the public sector. We have been told. that it is necessary to have the quangos to avoid the constraints on the public sector borrowing requirement.

The hon. Member for Tayside, North, displaying that financial acumen that has made him a legend in his lifetime, tells us that we cannot possibly continue down the present path because we would have neither the expertise or the scale to attract money and that, in any event, it would fall foul of the PSBR regime. My hon. Friend the Member for Linlithgow, in an incisive contribution, pointed out the fallacy involved in the way in which this country calculates the PSBR.

Hon. Members should turn to the consultative document again. It is a veritable treasure trove for those wishing to pick holes in the Government's case. The proposal for the creation of new water authorities is outlined on page 22. That is the option chosen by the Government, the one that we are seeking to amend to today. The document states:

"As public sector bodies, however, investment would remain subject to the constraints of public expenditure."

Conservative Members' crucial argument is demolished by the Government's consultative document. The effect would be exactly the same as for joint boards run by local authorities.

The hon. Member for Tayside, North of course knows better than any consultative document—after all, he is sitting beside the man who, presumably, wrote it and who certainly persuaded the Government to pursue the course that they are on. The hon. Member for Ayr may well be somewhere imagining a great new scheme to save the Prime Minister's skin. I ask Conservative Members who have not read the consultative document to read it carefully and to reach the only possible conclusion—that the Government's case is threadbare.

Conservative Members, and, indeed, the Minister, have spoken of new private sector finance to pay for the investment that has been sadly missing from the public sector, but the reality of the new water structure will be just as it was under privatisation in England and Wales, where such promises were made, but where the facts proved different. Investment did not arrive and the customers will therefore have to pay. In a rare, frank, moment the hon. Member for Tayside, North admitted JPEG 1619 that. He said that he did not know how much the customer will be willing to pay, and that the market will need to be tested, but his implication with regard to charges was, "As much as we can get away with." Not that there will be much choice. The consumer will not be able to choose between Strathclyde water, Central water, northern or eastern or even East Anglia water. Consumers take from the tap what they can get, so prices will be very inelastic. The authorities will charge what they want, as they do in England and the charges will go up and up inexorably.

Last night I spoke to an hon. Member in the Tea Room, who told me that in his area water rates had increased from £30 for a household to £300. The local water charge is virtually the same as the council tax. The same thing will happen in Scotland. The magical idea that the consumer will be protected by the injection of private finance is completely bogus and idiotic.

The Government claim that we have conjured a spectre of privatisation with which we have scared all the innocent voters of Eastwood and Ayr and 1.25 million voters in Strathclyde. The Under-Secretary, the hon Member for Dumfries has said, "Unfair! I have told them that it will not be privatised". The Minister may be a nice and decent guy, of whom, in Committee, one might say, "I would buy a used car from him." One would not, however, buy a used water industry from him. I do not know whether this Parliament is to be his last or his second last. He is a Minister now, but he may not be always. Few people hang on long as Ministers for the Government—they do not hang on long as Prime Minister either. The Under-Secretary's assurances will be listened to, but if they are not believed it is nothing to do with the Opposition. It is because of the healthy scepticism of the Scottish population, who have listened to promises from the Government before, and have found that they have been betrayed time upon time. If the Minister says, on behalf of the Government, "There will be no privatisation," the source is the same as the assurance that "We have no plans to extend VAT".

What will happen on Friday? The Government who claimed to be the party of low taxation will put the equivalent of 7p in the pound on taxes from the end of the week. Because of the broken promises from the party that said that it would reduce taxes year on year, families will, as the Chief Secretary of the Treasury admitted on the "Today" radio programme this morning, pay an extra £10 a week.

The Government's promises do not stand up. The suspicions will linger because they are justified. The Government cannot run from that nor avoid people's suspicion, hostility or refusal to believe that the precious water that they regard as so fundamental will remain in the public sector.

We shall soon vote on amendment No. 553, which provides our alternative to the Government's plans for the three super-quangos. We propose what the Government earlier defended as absolutely essential for the police and fire services—large and efficient joint boards for the water industry which are also close enough and accountable enough to continue to deliver the service to the Scottish people.

Conservative Members assume that the decision to be made is that the costs of the water industry must be borne out of current charges, as distinct from borrowing which is deferred taxation. That is a political judgment. It is like the question of buying a house. Should the house be bought right away in the first year, even though it is an assset that will be used over a long period? Mr. McLeish
: I beg to move amendment No. 588, in page 129, line 4, at end insert—

"3. The property of an authority shall not be disposed of whether by disposal or lease for more than 21 years, unless the authority has received the consent of all of the councils constituted under section 2 of this Act whose areas form part of the area of the authority, provided always that such consent shall not be required in respect of disposals with a value of less than £50,000 in the case of a single disposal but shall nevertheless be required if the aggregate value of disposals (for less than £50,000) in any financial year has already exceeded £500,000.".

The debate will, in a sense, be a re-run of the arguments advanced in the previous two sittings. This probing amendment relates to property and reveals a new aspect of the quango deals with which the Government are besotted. It is designed to protect some of the assets that will be passed to the three new super-quangos.

It is important that we consider the English and Welsh examples in our debate because their 10 water and sewerage companies are a scandal. The privatisation of water and sewerage services was supposed to be a cost-effective, value-for-money operation, designed to provide consumers with a higher quality of water at a reasonable cost. Nothing could be further from the truth. The phrase that sums up what has happened is rip off.

We heard earlier that English water companies had amassed massive profits and paid massive dividends. Often, that is part and parcel of the asset-stripping by water companies of the property and assets distributed to them. It is important that we bear that in mind in the Scottish context.

Let us consider Strathclyde. There is an abstract notion that eventually pipes, sewerage works, treatment works and staff will be sold—or, in the case of Strathclyde, passed on. But some of the most valuable property in terms of recreation, amenity and countryside will also be passed to the three super-quangos.

In that respect, accountability is crucial. The Government do not have a clue what accountability means in a modern democracy. They are operating JPEG 1627 outwith democracy so why should they have any regard for my arguments? Who in their right mind would want to pass over, without batting an eyelid, the facilities surrounding Loch Katrine or those surrounding the reservoirs that serve Fife, to three super quangos, to be run by the placemen and women of the Conservative party? That is a frightening prospect for any Committee and is clearly one of the reasons for last week's momentous achievement in the Strathclyde referendum.

People view water as something to be drunk and sewage treatment as an essential for modern communities, but they are also concerned about the environment. The first question that we need to establish is what has been built into the mess for which the Government have just voted, by way of safeguards for the valuable property, recreational and other assets.

Asset-stripping is a very germane topic in Scotland now. The establishment of quangos leads to the important considerations of secrecy and sleaze. Schedule 7 contains nothing about the public disclosure of items that are discussed behind closed doors. Why on earth should we give valuable property and land to quangos, which will meet in private and, behind closed doors, take multi-million pound decisions, that could leave the public worse off and, on eventual privatisation, leave a few individuals much better off? The secrecy factor is vital. Nothing in schedule 7 promotes openness in Government. It is all about the cosy clique which we have discussed before, destroying and selling off public assets—all behind closed doors. What safeguard will the Minister provide this evening for the future of the items that I have mentioned?

What we could euphemistically call the sleaze factor had been apparent in the Greater Glasgow health board with the Peterken fiasco and in local enterprise companies all over the country. The closed-door syndrome that arises with quangos often leads to deals being struck that are in the interests not of the public, but of a few people who are hand-picked to dominate the institutions. The sleaze factor must be taken seriously. It is a sad indictment of modern democracy that a Government care so little that they are willing to transfer multi-million pound assets to organisations in the guise of quangos, which will, we are told, be closer to the community by virtue of the Secretary of State's picking those who sit on them.

Scandal, sleaze and secrecy tell us what quangos are about.
