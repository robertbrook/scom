TRANSPORT BILL
Clause 44
Meaning of "the relevant pension obligations"

10.30 am

Mr. Albert Booth
: I beg to move amendment No. 318, in page 39, line 36, after "scheme", insert—

"taking account of the amendments which were introduced with effect from the date when employments in respect of which pension rights are earned under the scheme were contracted out under the Social Security Pensions Act 1975".

The purpose of the amendment, stated at its broadest, is to ensure that the provision made under the Bill for the railway-based historic rights pensions is not so limited as to exclude those changes in the schemes which have to take place in order to meet the requirements of the Social Security Pensions Act 1975. The reason the Bill contains a limitation so excluding them lies in the date in subsection (1), which states that

"'the relevant pension obligations', in relation to a B.R. pension scheme, means subject to subsection (3):—

  1. ( a) any obligations of the Board which were owed on 1st January 1975".

The effect of the Social Security Pensions Act 1975 was to require changes in schemes subsequent to that date. Therefore, a number of obligations have arisen which are not covered by this clause.

I take some heart from the Minister's reaction to my contention that the Bill put at risk a number of pension rights. During a recent sitting he said that that was an alarmist view, and he made a number of other suggestions about the contribution I had made. This morning the Parliamentary Secretary, in the absence of his Minister, has the opportunity to justify the Minister's statement and show that in drafting the Bill the Government did not intend to produce that result, and that they are now prepared to show that they are not putting at risk or diminishing pension rights with this legislation.

The main railway pension schemes were 1924 contracted out under the Social Security Pensions Act 1975. This took place with effect from 6 April 1978. Therefore, the pension scheme rule changes made in order to meet the statutory contracting-out conditions occurred after 1 January 1975—the date mentioned in subsection (1)( a). This means that the changes do not count as relevant pension obligations, although they relate to the same periods of pensionable service as other relevant pension obligations. Hence, they would not qualify for Government support.

It is necessary to show some of the changes in this category. The first group that I should like to mention is guaranteed minimum pensions, which have to be provided on a basis that would make them equal to pensions that could have been provided under the State earnings-related scheme if it had not been for contracting out. Guaranteed minimum pensions are usually less than fund pensions, although that is not always so. It would appear that, when a pension is built up to a minimum in such a way, the amount of that buildup does not qualify for Government support as the Bill is drafted.

Another example is widows' pensions and death benefits that are provided at a minimum level. In so far as provision has been made for them subsequent to the qualifying date, they do not appear to be covered for Government support in this part of the Bill.

There has been a series of complicated amendments to the rules governing benefits for those leaving service before retirement age. I do not claim to have mastered all the complexities or to understand every change that has taken place in the rules in respect of those leaving service, but I put it to the Parliamentary Secretary that it would be a task of almost impossible complexity to sort out for each individual what would have been paid under the old rules and to take the difference as not being eligible for Government support under the terms of the Bill. One of the reasons for that complexity is that, under the old rules, the benefit might have been taken as a lump sum and under the new rules it could be available as a pension.

Another change which falls within this category is that of contributions payable by members whose pensions have been increased. It is questionable whether that may be taken into account in assessing Government support. Perhaps the Parlia- JPEG 1925 mentary Secretary will advise us about that. Under contracting-out legislation, pensions must now be payable from the age of 70 for men and 65 for women, even if employees remain in service after those ages. Those pensions would not qualify for Government support until the employee retired from service if they had to be operated under the rules prior to the contracting-out taking place.

To obtain a contracting-out certificate, it is necessary to satisfy the preservation of pension requirements under the Social Security Act 1973. One change was that if a member had a deferred pension payable from a fixed age and forgot to claim it, he became entitled to it retrospectively, whereas previously he would have been entitled to the pension only from the date when he claimed it. It seems that such retrospective payments would not qualify for Government support as the Bill is drafted.

I should like to give one more example of the changes that have taken place affecting pension rights which may be debarred from qualification under the Bill. Let us consider the preservation conditions on pensions that have been introduced. Under those conditions if a member leaves and takes a refund of his contributions, and a transfer value has previously been paid into the railway pension fund on his behalf from a non-contributory pension scheme, that fund now has to preserve a benefit in respect of the scheme. This element of the preservation pension would appear not to qualify for support.

There are other examples that I could give, but I do not intend to do so because I believe that those I have given are sufficient to show that there is a significant number of changes to pension rules which at present definitely give an entitlement to benefit and which would be excluded.

One of the most important effects of contracting out is that the national insurance scheme is responsible for paying post-retirement pension increases on part of the fund pension. That means that the railway pension funds no longer have to meet the whole of the liability for pension increase, resulting in a saving in the Government support from that source. Having made that saving, it is not unreasonable that the Government should meet the relatively small additional cost involved in supporting the additional liabilities from 1926 contracting-out changes. That is really what the amendment is designed to achieve.

There is a further consideration. If the amendment is not made, there will be a substantial administrative burden over the next 50 years or so because each person's pension will have to be subdivided into the part which would be supported under the Bill, which I hope will be the major part, and that which would not be catered for by the support and which in many cases would be a relatively small amount. This would not necessarily be a once-and-for-all calculation for each individual. It would have to be reviewed annually.

I am told that the actuary to the funds has advised that that problem should be avoided if at all possible, not just because of the considerable administrative work involved, but also because of the technical difficulties of determining the unfunded proportion if part of the benefits is to be excluded. That follows because of the nature of the exclusions, which in themselves are extremely complicated and often depend on contingencies upon which it is very difficult to base an actuarial value.

There are therefore considerable practical advantages in accepting the amendment. The cost would be relatively small and would be far outweighed by the savings on inflation-proofing of pensions which are a direct result of the contracting-out requirements. I therefore hope that the Parliamentary Secretary will accept the amendment, not just as a way of showing the pensioners and those who administer their funds that the Bill is not intended to exclude them from what they now properly regard as pension rights which follow directly from a piece of Government legislation requiring changes in their pension rules, but also to reduce the horrifyingly complex task of having to calculate almost year by year what part of the pension is covered by the Bill and what part is not.

That is why obligations upto 1 January 1975 are all that the 1978 Act is concerned with. Let me deal now with particular improvements to the Social Security Pen- 1928 sions Act 1975. That piece of legislation is longer and more complex than this Bill, but I am reasonably familiar with it—indeed, I led for the Opposition on its Committee stage in 1975. It does not oblige anybody to contract out of the State system. It offers to occupational pension funds the opportunity to contract out if they decide that it is in their interests to do so. By 1 April 1978 the managers of each occupational fund had to decide whether they proposed to contract out of the State arrangements under the 1975 Act. It follows from that that they had to decide, if they contracted out, what arrangements they would make to finance and fund any additional obligations that they had to undertake in order to contract out.

I am sure—I do not query its judgment—that British Rail was right, in its own interests and those of its pensioners, to contract out of the 1975 Act system on the due date, 1 April 1978. If it had not contracted out, it would have had to go fully into the earnings-related State system, and it would have had to pay greatly increased national insurance contributions both for itself and for its employees. It is true that it still has post-retirement inflation-proofing from the State, as provided under the 1975 Act. But it contracted out because the balance was wholly in its favour, just as every other private fund tried to contract out.

I suggest that the cost to the State in terms of post-retirement inflation-proofing is, almost certainly, substantially less than the loss to the national insurance fund by way of abated contributions, which are available to schemes that contract out. That is what it was for, and it was a sound and sensible judgment by British Rail to contract out. In order to contract out, it had to comply with the minimum obligations put by the 1975 Act upon contracted-out schemes. If a fund wished to contract out, it had to adjust the rules of its scheme to make sure that it could provide a guaranteed minimum pension. That is what the board did.

As I understand it from what the right hon. Gentleman is saying, it was discovered that certain widows' pensions had to be introduced, and that in one or two schemes the benefits were below the guaranteed minimum pension which is roughly equivalent to what the State scheme would have provided, and some improvements to benefits had to be made. That is JPEG 1929 all right. British Rail took on that new obligation on 1 April 1978. If it had been a private occupational pension scheme, I should normally have expected some increase in contributions to be made in order to finance the additional obligations. As I understand it, the board did not make an increase in contributions at that stage to pay for the increased obligations. The funds are perfectly solvent but the board made a decision that was entirely its own—by "board" I mean at all times the pension fund managers—that it did not need to fund these improvements. So I gather that it has not done so. That is nothing to do with the Government.

The Government are still standing behind the obligations that existed in January 1975. The amendment would add to the historic rail-based obligations Government support for improvements that were made to those pension rights on 1 April 1978. I am sure that members of the board decided with their eyes open that it needed no additional contributions in order to finance those improvements. Therefore it cannot expect to be able, under this Bill, to seek to add yet further improvements to the historic pension rights.

I do not criticise the decision to contract out. It was a sound decision which involved improvements to the historic schemes and I am sure that the board realised that. I understand that they were of a modest order because many pension schemes already provided a guaranteed minimum pension. But they are not covered by these arrangements and I can see no case for enlarging the protection in the Bill to cover them.
