Mr. Raynsford
: Following this morning's lengthy debate on the principles of a licensing scheme as opposed to a registration scheme, we move to more detailed consideration of the contents of such a scheme. Amendments Nos. 212 and 213 are in a sense a halfway house between that which the Government espoused and we advocated this morning.

The Government argued that there should not be a mandatory national licensing scheme. We believe that they are wrong but if that is what they think, and if they seriously accept that effective action must be taken to improve standards and tackle problems in unsatisfactory properties, we hope that they will appreciate the logic of the amendments.

Amendment No. 212 would require the registration scheme to be mandatory. Amendment No. 213 would allow a local housing authority to apply to the Secretary of State for exemption from that requirement if it could demonstrate, from the number and condition of houses in multiple occupation in its area, and from the action that it is taking to deal with such problems, that licensing would not be justified. That would achieve the benefits of a mandatory national framework, for which we argued, while allowing exemptions to authorities. It 238 would allow the Secretary of State to take a view on the performance of local authorities. That may help the Minister of State, who this morning expressed concern about the Government's ability to enforce legislation. They would be able to take an overview of authorities' performance and either agree exemptions if they believe authorities are in control and do not require a national scheme, or require them to adopt a national scheme if Ministers are not satisfied. That might be regarded as a compromise between our positions this morning, when the Government rejected our amendment. I hope that they will have second thoughts this afternoon and realise the merits of the amendments.

Mrs. Diane Maddock
(Christchurch)
: The Committee will not have missed the fact that one of my amendments is similar to amendment No. 212. My second amendment goes a little further than that tabled by the hon. Member for Greenwich (Mr. Raynsford).

Amendment No. 245 would make mandatory a registration scheme for local authorities for houses in multiple occupation. Amendment No. 246 goes with it, setting out the procedure through which authorities apply for exemption. Where I differ from the Government is that while they prefer that local authorities opt in, I think that it is much better to make the scheme mandatory and allow them to opt out.

We support a compulsory national registration scheme. We have consulted local authorities throughout the country and it is clear that they want the scheme to be mandatory. We are worried that if the schemes are not mandatory, varying policies and enforcement practices will exist in different local authorities.

Differences and inconsistencies, which sometimes seem arbitrary, make it difficult for landlords to plan expenditure on their properties. Later, we shall discuss opt-outs of specific properties from various registration schemes—that is worrying. Amendment No. 245 would remove local authorities' discretion to do that, but it would give them the power to apply for an exemption, for which there should be a clear procedure. Amendment No. 246 sets out such a procedure.

New subsection (7), which the amendment would incorporate in the clause, sets out the information that local authorities would need to provide to support their applications, which would include the total number and type of houses in multiple occupation in a local authority's area. It might seek exemption because it had few properties and was able to control them. Many members of the Committee are concerned about control of houses in multiple occupation. Some local authorities might want to be exempt because they provided services to tenants and landlords without needing to be part of a scheme.

In an application for exemption, we would also like a local authority to state how many houses in multiple occupation meet the standards set in section 352(1)(a) of the Housing Act 1985 for amenities such as toilets, baths, showers, cooking facilities, means of escape from fire and other fire precautions. That information is crucial. A local authority would have to show that it had accurate information about the extent of substandard conditions and poor management in its area. Even if the information showed that a high proportion of houses in JPEG 239 multiple occupation were unsatisfactory, the local authority might be exempted on the next criterion, which is a written strategy document to show that it has an active, forward-looking programme for tackling substandard houses in multiple occupation. That would be a thorough test for local authorities that sought exemption. They would have to show that their strategy for tackling problems was proactive, that their programme was comprehensive and made the best use of their enforcement powers, and that they encouraged and supported responsible landlords to maintain their properties in good order.

The Campaign for Bedsit Rights carried out extensive research, in which it was supported by the Joseph Rowntree Foundation. Out of the 169 local authorities it considered in England and Wales, 51 per cent. had a written strategy that detailed policies and practices for houses in multiple occupation. The Department of the Environment commissioned research in 1995. On the basis of that study, nearly two thirds of local authorities have a structured approach to houses in multiple occupation and some written strategy.

Our proposal is prescriptive in its requirements of local authorities' applications for exemption, but it is a good way of dealing with an important issue. We must ensure that local authorities are given the tools to do their job. They have asked for schemes to be mandatory, but we appreciate that some local authorities may have a good case for exemption. It would have to be a good case, as the amendment makes clear. If they have a good case, perhaps they should be allowed to be exempt, but it would be the exception for them to be allowed to opt out. This morning the Government were saying that they would set up a scheme into which they hoped that all local authorities would opt. My party—and, I think, many members of the Committee—believes that the amendment provides a much better way to move forward and ensure better conditions in houses in multiple occupation. This morning, the conditions in which some people lived were graphically described. We all want to tackle that; we must find the best way to do so.
