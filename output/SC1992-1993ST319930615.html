Draft Castle Vale Housing Action Trust (Area and Constitution) Order 1993

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Mr. Tony Baldry)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Castle Vale Housing Action Trust (Area and Constitution) Order 1993.

The Chairman
: With the agreement of the Committee, it will be convenient to discuss at the same time the other order before us, namely, the draft Tower Hamlets Housing Action Trust (Area and Constitution) Order 1993.

Mr. Baldry
: It might be for the convenience of the Committee if I briefly introduced the two instruments. Designation orders for other housing action trusts have recently been made and the principles of housing action trusts have been well set out. I shall be happy to respond to any questions that members of the Committee would like to ask.

The Committee is considering the designation of two further housing action trusts at Castle Vale and in Tower Hamlets. Both designation maps are straightforward, unlike the one that we considered in relation to the Liverpool housing action trust, which included most of the tower blocks in Liverpool. The Castle Vale housing action trust covers the area immediately surrounding the Castle Vale estate, with the exception of one privately owned warehouse. Apart from that, the whole of the Castle Vale estate, which was built on a runway during the 1960s, is part of the housing action trust.

Mr. Robin Corbett
(Birmingham, Erdington)
: I have some good news for the Minister. The former hangars, which he described as a warehouse, have been razed to the ground and the headquarters of Betterware now sit on that site. Those headquarters will be occupied from next January and will offer the prospect of local jobs.

Mr. Baldry
: Yes, that is excellent news. The site is clearly shown on the map as being outside the designated area, to which I referred, and it will not form part of the housing action trust.

As I am sure members of the Committee have seen, the trust area in Tower Hamlets includes several tower blocks. The three estates in Bow—Lefevre Walk, Monteith and Tredegar Road—contain 1,600 dwellings in a combination of high and medium-rise blocks. There are no houses.

Today, we are agreeing the designation of the two areas, which will, in due course, be transferred from the local authority to the housing action trust once the homing action trust boards have made their proposals.

Hon. Members have asked me to give assurances that sufficient funding will be available to the housing action trusts throughout their lifetime. I shall certainly give that assurance. Housing action trusts have been extremely 4 successful. They produce corporate plans annually, covering the forthcoming three years, and the finance limits for housing action trusts are set on the basis of those plans. An external finance limit is set for the first year and indicative finance limits are set for the subsequent two years, and then confirmed. The funding for housing action trusts is not top-sliced. It comes from a separate vote and there is no question of a housing action trust not receiving grant aid for any year. We also appreciate that the profile for funding housing action trusts will not necessarily be the same during the trust's lifetime. In the early years, not so much might be spent; then there will be a peak in spending that will tail off towards the end of the trust's life.

Another cause for concern is what happens at the end of the trust's life—when it has completed its work and the estates have been refurbished and rejuvenated. As members of the Committee will know, at that stage, tenants on an estate will have a choice about whether to return to the local authority or to have an alternative landlord, such as a housing association. Many may have bought their homes under the right-to-buy scheme or the rent-to-mortgage scheme in the interim. A range of options are available to tenants.

HAT have commanded substantial support from local residents, so I hope that the Committee can make speedy progress today.

Tenants in Castle Vale and Tower Hamlets voted for their HAT to be established by clear majorities of 92 per cent. and 65 per cent. respectively. From my visits to Castle Vale, I know that, now that the HAT has been set up and the tenants have chosen their board the tenants are keen to get on with the job. They are enthusiastic about the opportunity that has been provided by the Government through HAT. The order, which makes the designation, is a further step on that route. I hope that the Committee will agree to it speedily, so the work can be got on with and be done with as quickly as possible.

I am interested to know whether the Government are moving towards contract compliance for the HAT proposals. At the end of the day, we shall look hard not only at tenants' participation and involvement but at the arithmetic. In recent years many local authorities have developed dynamic, imaginative and participatory models to involve tenants in the management of their estates. I hope that the Government are not suggesting that all the eggs must be crammed into the HAT basket, as for many tenants, there are no other options because the Government have cut off the resources. That is why HATs are proving to be a Hobson's choice—they are all that is on offer and, at the moment, they seem to tenants to be the only way in which they can get repairs done in their lifetime.

I hope that the Government will understand that HATs are not the answer. I hope that they reconsider their approach and their attitude to local authority housing, and that, rather than marginalising residualised local authority housing by cutting back on housing investment programmes, they will realise that we need to get away from the phrase used by the right hon. Member for Kingston upon Thames (Mr. Lamont)—short-term thinking. We need proper, programmed renewal and repair, but that would mean a major shake-up in the way in which the Government budget for housing.

Mr. Robin Corbet
(Birmingham, Erdington)
: My hon. Friend the Member for Leeds, West (Mr. Battle) is perfectly right to put the orders in the context of the Government's housing policy. My hon. Friends and I—and, I hope, Conservative Members—know from the experience of advice bureaux that the preponderance of housing cases during the past decade has risen dramatically. They account for approximately 90 per cent. of my constituency case load, and the problem represents the most enormous misery for growing numbers of people—young families trapped in one-bed flats at the top of tower blocks and families expected to bring up small children on the top deck of maisonette blocks with four flights of concrete steps and related problems.

Whatever the virtues of the HAT programme, the Minister and his colleagues should acknowledge that we have a major housing crisis which, sooner or later, must be dealt with. I am not arguing for a moment that all, or even the main, responsibility for solving the crisis should rest with local authorities—I believe that there should be a mix of provision. But the problem must be solved because it contains social dynamite: denying people what I consider to be one of the basic rights of life—the right to a roof over a person's or a family's head.

The people of the Vale gave a thundering welcome to the housing action trust. In a ballot in which 74 of every 100 people used their vote—about the turnout for a general election—92 of every 100 said yes and eight in every 100 said no. That means that about 3,400 people voted yes, with 290 against. That vote was about more than the prospect of getting a better place to live, although that is important in an island state where 55 per cent. of tenants live in tower blocks, 10 per cent. live in low-rise blocks and 7 per cent. live in maisonettes. Many of those residents are expected to live like vertical battery hens in sky blocks, where the lifts are unreliable and foul, where damp causes ill health because of the way in which the blocks were thrown up and where a few people find it easier to behave as bad neighbours than as good neighbours. Of course they want and deserve better housing. But that is only the beginning.

The hallmarks of the Vale are its poverty, poor housing, poor environmental conditions and the joblessness of 40 of every 100 people of working age. At local primary schools, 36 of every 100 children come from families so poor that they qualify for free school meals. At secondary schools, 29 per cent. qualify. Almost seven in 10 tenants have incomes so low that they qualify for housing benefit. I hope that the Minister does not expect people of the Vale to feel any gratitude to the Government, which have helped to put and keep them in that condition.

The housing action trust is the tenants' decision; their demand and determination to work together to build a better life with better housing, better transport, better facilities, a better environment and, hopefully, training to give them a better chance of taking up local jobs when developments in the neighbouring Birmingham Heartlands urban development corporation get going. I hope that the Minister will tell us—my hon. Friend the Member for Leeds, West referred to this—that the Birmingham TEC and the Government's training and other agencies will get alongside the HAT, Birmingham Heartlands and local JPEG 9 employers to ensure that training is available so that people of the Vale can obtain the training they need in order to secure local jobs.

I want to pay tribute to Derek Waddington, the city of Birmingham's director of housing, and to his team, to councillor Stan Austin, who chairs the city council housing committee, to John Northover and local Department of the Environment officials, who have all worked so hard to persuade the Government and enable the HAT to be offered. Several of us had anxious moments in the days before the Budget when it seemed that public spending cuts might at least delay, if not extinguish, the go-ahead for the HAT. I want to pay special tribute to Mrs. Carol Rafferty, the determined person who chairs the community action team, which helped to organise the tenants and residents in the run-up to the HAT ballot. It is all too easy to expect there to be little local input in run-down areas, such as the Vale, where understandable apathy and acceptance is the rule rather than the exception; not so on the Vale.

Like all other communities, the people of the Vale have among them those with great, unused and mainly unknown strengths just waiting to be tapped. That is not to say that there have not been rows, arguments, walk-outs and fall-outs as people try to work together—and there will be more, although hopefully fewer, as people learn more about the skills of working together for a shared and common purpose. I congratulate Mrs. Rafferty, Mrs. Joan Lawrie, Mrs. Joyce Curtis and Mr. John Newton on their election as the four resident representatives of the HAT board.

I find it perverse that the Department would not assist in enabling the 3,500 residents of owner-occupied houses and flats on the estate also to have a say in a separate, parallel ballot. That gave the impression, which I know was not meant, that those residents were nothing to do with the HAT. That is, of course, true in terms of transfer of ownership from the city council to the HAT, but the experience, skills and help of owner-occupiers is needed just as much in the development of the new Vale as those of tenants, as I have tried to make clear to them. Building the new Vale is a job for everyone who lives there.

The Minister referred to funding HATs. I understand, and perhaps the Minister will confirm, that £130 million has been mentioned as the likely spend during the five to 10 years of the Castle Vale HAT's life. Does that allocated sum have to be spent in equal amounts over the years of the redevelopment? I heard what the Minister said about the need to understand that, in such matters, it is not possible to achieve level spends. The Minister will recall that on 11 June, in his reply to a question about city challenge funding, he made it clear that spending was subject to the annuality rules. I assume that the same will apply to HATs.

10 The Minister went on to say that underspends in one city challenge area are offset by speeding up programmes in other city challenge areas. Would that also apply to HATs? If so, will the Minister reconsider that matter? On 11 June he told me:
