Mr. Grant
: Opening the discussion of part V, I will make a few general comments.

The Widdicombe committee highlighted the use of companies by local authorities to carry out certain activities. The committee recommended that the law relating to local "authority-controlled companies should be amended to make it clear that they should be set up only when there is specific enabling legislation and that there should be safeguards relating to their articles of association, membership, audit and reporting arrangements.

The Widdicombe committee recommended in paragraph 8.115 of its report that the case for companies needed to be considered in the

"context of a detailed appraisal of the requirements of the service in question".

However, the committee did not consider it within its remit to do that. Morever, the commitee saw the examination as being appropriately considered within the framework of an examination of

"the proper role of local authorities in economic development".

The Government, while carrying out a survey of the use of companies, failed to consider this in the context of the delivery of service—that is, economic development. The two were dealt with separately. Thus, there was a failure to recognise the importance of a company structure in the undertaking of local economic development. Further, the time allowed for consultation, and the Government's willingness to understand the importance of the issue, have been less than satisfactory.

The Government decided that the first Widdicombe recommendation relating to the view that local authority companies could be set up only where there was specific enabling legislation would prove restrictive, so they did not accept it.

In the 1988 consultation paper, the Government, noted the contribution made by company structures, and stated: 748

"It is clear that there are important operational advances for a local authority in a variety of cases in using a company to conduct an activity in which the authority has an involvement."

They added that they

"do not wish to restrict this flexibility, which has particular use in joint ventures".

The Government have not pursued a frontal attack on local authority companies. Instead, they have attempted to restrict and constrain local authority interests in companies through regulations that will restrict substantially the involvement of local authorities in organisations that use a company structure.

The Government's proposed requirements go beyond the concerns raised by Widdicombe. The Government intend to pursue the matter throughout local government services and external relationships with the private and voluntary sectors.

The Widdicombe recommendation related only to local authority-controlled companies. The proposals in the Bill extend that, and are designed to constrain local authorities' interests in companies per se, chiefly by bringing many such companies within the local authority capital controls system which is set out in part IV.

The proposed restrictions on companies in part V were set out in a consultation document published by the Department of the Environment in June 1988. There was a required response date of 30 July 1988. That document provoked extensive criticism and argument from a wide range of local authorities and their associations, from commercial organisations such as chambers of commerce, and from the voluntary sector. The changes accepted by the Government after consultation are identified in the DoE paper entitled:

"Local Authorities' Interests in Companies in England Wales; Summary Points Made in Response to Consultation Documents and Government Comments".

The changes are relatively minor and the main thrust of the proposals remains. As a result, local government is faced with complex and vague legislation which will adversely affect worthwhile initiatives undertaken by local authority companies.

The wide variety of interests in which local authority companies are involved reflects diverse local circumstances and is a strength of local government. The DoE survey discovered that more than one third of local authority companies are involved in economic development. Of the remainder, nearly one fifth involve information technology centres or training.

The arguments for local authorities using companies to provide urban regeneration, economic development and training can be summarised simply. First, these matters have new needs but require different solutions. By using the company approach, local authorities can respond with greater flexibility and speed to meet the needs of their local economies.

Secondly, the company structure enables economic development and other aspects of urban regeneration to take place in close partnership with the private sector. The Government have promoted such partnerships and the maximising of private sector JPEG 749 investment in inner-city areas through joint ventures is a constant theme of Government policy. In my constituency, the private sector and the London borough of Haringey got together to convert the former Sainsbury's store in Tottenham High road into retail units for the benefit of small business. The venture was welcomed by the Government and by the people of Tottenham, who were pleased to get rid of an eyesore. The units have been taken by ethnic minority businesses and reflect the people of the area. Haringey and other councils have taken on board the Government's request that local authorities enter into joint ventures with the private sector.

Thirdly, in many areas, public sector intervention is needed to tackle poor infrastructure, high unemployment, inadequate skills and the absence of private sector investment. A major reason for local authorities' use of companies, solely or in joint ventures, is their attempt to raise additional funds to match or to exceed those provided by the public sector. Often, developments could not go ahead without public or local authority funding. In other cases, a public sector contribution has attracted more than four times as much in private funds.

Local authorities have been led to believe that the Government want joint ventures because they want private investment rather than public sector investment. The Government say that they want private sector business acumen to be placed at the disposal of local authorities. Local authorities have taken the Government at their word, but yet again they are being restricted in their attempts to carry out the Government's wishes.
