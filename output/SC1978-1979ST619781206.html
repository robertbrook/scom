SEA FISH INDUSTRY ACT 1970 (RELAXATION OF TIME LIMITS) ORDER 1978

10.30 a.m.

The Minister of State, Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. E. S. Bishop)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the Sea Fish Industry Act 1970 (Relaxation of Time Limits) Order (1978.

The Chairman
: I think that it would be for the convenience of the Committee if with this we were to take the following:

The White Fish Authority (Research and Development Grants) Order 1978.

The Fishing Vessels (Acquisition and Improvement) (Grants) (Variation) Scheme 1978.

There will be the one and a half hour time limit on the debate.

Mr. Bishop
: I am glad that it has been agreed that the Committee should consider these instruments together. The Sea Fish Industry Act 1970 (Relaxation of Time Limits) Order 1978 and the White Fish Authority (Research and Development Grants) Order 1978 complement one another. They provide for continued Exchequer assistance for the fishing industry and the funding of what are perhaps the most important activities of the White Fish Authority and Herring Industry Board. Although similar measures have been laid before the House in recent years a word about each may be helpful.

The Fishing Vessels (Acquisition and Improvement) (Grants) (Variation) Scheme 1978 extends the current arrangements by a year, up to the end of 1979. These arrangements, which have remained substantially unchanged since 1967, provide for grants from Exchequer funds towards the cost of construction of new fishing vessels and the improvement of existing ones. Since March 1975 the rate of grant has been standardised at 4 25 per cent, of approved costs of all projects. We propose no change for the coming year.

I turn now to the Sea Fish Industry Act 1970 (Relaxation of Time Limits) Order 1978. Hon. Members will be aware that the Act provides for Exchequer funding of the borrowing requirements of the statutory bodies for the purpose of giving loans for acquiring, reconditioning or improving inshore fishing vessels and their gear and for fish processing and ice-making plants. Unless renewed before then, the enabling power will expire at the end of this year. The order before us today extends these powers for a further year. It also extends for a similar period powers whereby Ministers may contribute, by way of grant, towards the valuable research and experimental work undertaken by the statutory bodies.

This leads me to the third order, the White Fish Authority (Research and Development Grants) Order 1978. It has only one substantive provision which I can explain very briefly. The intention of this provision is to raise the sum which can be made available from central funds to the White Fish Authority for its research and development work by £1 million. If the Committee approves the order, as I anticipate that it will, this will mean that the aggregate amount provided for the White Fish Authority since 1952 will total £6 million. The additional sum of £1 million would be made available to the authority for its research and development work to match the contribution which it makes from its levy on first hand sales of white fish in this country.

Paragraph 2 of the order refers to the limit of £2 million imposed by the Sea Fish Industry Act, 1970. Section 23(2) of the Act provided for this sum to be increased if necessary by an amount not exceeding £1 million at any one time. Similar orders to that now before the Committee increased the limit of £2 million to £3 million in 1972, to £4 million in 1975, and £5 million in 1976. At the current rate of expenditure on research and development this £5 million will, in total, have been reached in 1979. The order would, on present calculations be sufficient to enable the Government to support the White Fish Authority's programme for a further two to three years if necessary. There can be no doubt that the authority's research and development JPEG 5 work is one of its more essential functions and much valued by the fishing industry. The Government's financial contribution to it is expected to total £508,000 in the current financial year 1978–79.

The White Fish Authority's research and development programmes are kept under continual review both by its own research and development policy advisory committee, on which the fishing industry is well represented, and also by the Fisheries Research and Development Board which was set up by fisheries Ministers in 1973 to ensure a proper balance in the objectives and priorities of all Government-aided fisheries research and development.

The precise sum granted each year by the Government depends primarily on the fisheries department's approval of the programme and on the amount which the authority, in consultation with the industry, decides to devote from its levy income to research and development.

Two of these three instruments under consideration today have as their purpose the provision of assistance towards investment in new or improved vessels. Members of the Committee will be well aware that the terms to be negotiated for a common fisheries policy satisfactory to the interests of the United Kingdom will have an important bearing on the shape and the size of fleet which will be required for the future, and on future grants and loans provisions. It might be argued that, given present uncertainties, prudence would demand a stop on assistance for investment in vessels. However, past experience has shown that an arbitrary stop of that kind can cause real social and economic hardship, particularly where there are many self-employed people involved. Moreover, there would be an unfortunate impact on shipbuilding yards.

In practice the fishing industry is showing no tendency to invest in a grossly expansionist fashion. New building has been at a relatively modest level in recent years, primarily to capitalise on opportunities available in inshore and near and middle waters. The statutory authorities are also required as part of the scheme before us today, to examine every grant application in terms of economic viability and in relation to the overall needs of the industry. At the moment, both authorities are restricting aid to modernisation and such construction as will replace vessels lost to 6 fishing for various reasons. There is therefore, little risk of imprudent investment being encouraged by this scheme.

Expenditure under the scheme during the current financial year is expected to amount to about £6 million and the Government are making provision for comparable expenditure in 1979–80. Hon. Members will appreciate that such level of expenditure means that, despite the many uncertainties which exist, the industry's contribution towards its future efficiency is running at nearly £18 million per annum. Clearly, the availability of grant is an important investment consideration for the industry. However, until the future structural requirements become clearer we think it inappropriate to enter into any long-term contracts. For this reason we propose to continue recent practices and extent the arrangement for a single year only, that is until the end of 1979.

Those are all the points I wish to make this morning. I believe that the latter ones regarding factors which have an influence on the size and shape of the fleet are highly relevant, when we are devoting public money to the building of new vessels. We know that there is uncertainty. We do not expect a definitive answer on all these points this morning. : We have had an interesting debate. I shall try to answer some of the points which have been made. Having dealt with such measures for a number of years, and anticipating that I may have the privilege of continuing to do so for some years. I am constantly repeating myself. I believe that much, of the future shape of the industry will be determined only when we have the outcome of the common fisheries policy. 14 In my opening speech I said:

"Hon. Members will appreciate that such level of expenditure means that, despite the many uncertainties which exist, the industry's own contribution towards its future efficiency is running at nearly £18 million per annum."

This ties up with what was said by the hon. Member for North Angus and Mearns (Mr. Buchanan-Smith) and shows a cautious optimism in the future. But we shall not get the answers until the uncertainties of the common fisheries policy have been removed.

I shall deal with some of the points made by the hon. Member for North Angus and Mearns. He paid a powerful tribute to the work of the WFA and the HIB. With regard to the entry of foreign-owned fishing vessels to the United Kingdom register, we keep this under review. Member for North Angus and Mearns about the re-structuring of the fishing industry, he will be aware that the EEC JPEG 15 has tabled proposals to assist re-structuring.
