Mr. Bradley
: I thank the Minister for agreeing to debate the two instruments separately. I do not think that the Committee will be long detained on the first, although there may be considerable discussion and debate on the second.

The instrument makes a series of amendments to the regulations, but I want to concentrate on paragraph 6, which deals with the revocation of regulation 49. Much concern has already been expressed about that revocation. I refer the Minister to an early-day motion tabled on 10 November, which expressed great anxiety about the Government's proposals. To date, 165 hon. Members have supported that early-day motion, which clearly demonstrates the widespread anxiety.

The measure deals with late claims for supplementary benefit. Because that was abolished a while ago, it may be thought that there is no great need to keep the measure on the statute book. There are major anxieties about such an attitude. I am grateful to the various pressure groups and lobby groups that have advised us on the difficulties that may be experienced by the most vulnerable people if the Government proceed with revocation of the regulation.

Regulation 49 maintained the possibility of a late claim for supplementary benefit, even though the supplementary benefit scheme had been replaced by the income support system. However, in order to be able to make a late claim for supplementary benefit a person still had to prove that he or she had good cause for not having made a claim previously. There was, therefore, no automatic right to supplementary benefit if a late claim was made. It is important to stress that we are not discussing whether a claimant would be entitled to supplementary benefit if a late claim were made. It would be considered in the usual way, but the claimant would have to prove that there was a good cause for not having made a claim at the correct time.

4 Ignorance of entitlement was not good enough to establish good cause. A person had to show that he or she had difficulties or disabilities that made it reasonable for him or her not to make inquiries, or that he or she had been misled by wrong advice or information. The entitlement would be assessed and the claimant would then have to show good cause for not making the claim at the right time. Having shown good cause, the claimant may then be entitled to the benefit.

The regulation is especially relevant to people with disabilities and perhaps particularly to those with a mental handicap. Many groups have said that they are worried that those vulnerable groups would be discriminated against yet again by the revocation of regulation 49, especially people with educational difficulties, who may not have been in a position at the time of their entitlement to make a correct claim.

I understand that the Government want to bring forward the revocation because when the regulation was introduced, it was intended to be temporary, but it is difficult to understand why that is justified. If people fail to recognise their proper entitlement, perhaps because of incorrect advice or because their circumstances do not allow them to inquire about it why should they be prevented from claiming it when they become aware of their right to it?

Why was the provision brought forward so quickly without due consultation and without advertising that it would be withdrawn, thereby denying the opportunity for a trawl of people who may be entitled to benefit to ensure that they made their claims before the regulation was withdrawn? The Minister will confirm that the Government's right to reclaim overpaid benefit or benefit paid to people who were not entitled to it will remain on the statute book. The right for people to claim is being withdrawn, but the Government's right to take money back will remain. That is not even-handed. As the Child Poverty Action Group said, what is sauce for the goose is not sauce for the gander.

It is also important to remember that if someone establishes good cause for the delay in claiming and is awarded supplementary benefit, that person could be entitled to more transitional protection, which may increase the amount of income support to which he will become entitled.

As the early-day motion makes clear, the provision affects those on the lowest incomes and those who ae most vulnerable in society. We should take all measures at all times to ensure that when they have established a right to benefit, even if that is supplementary benefit, they should not be disadvantaged and lose the right to that entitlement.

We do not oppose all changes in the regulations, some of which are sensible, but I urge the Under-Secretary to reconsider regulation 49 and to leave it on the statute book was to ensure that no-one is denied the right to benefit if that right has been clearly established and good cause has been shown for not claiming it at the relevant time.

Mr. Burt
: I am grateful for the opportunity to discuss the regulations and I am also grateful for the contributions of Opposition Members. I shall cover the points made by hon. Gentlemen, but first I shall answer the question about why we have introduced the regulation. The hon. Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire (Mr. Kirkwood) will know that we constantly watch the social security system. From time to time anomalies appear and we try to correct them. It is not a question of doing things piecemeal, but of responding to need and putting matters before the House to tidy up regulations. It is a constant process that all Departments go through. There is a need, and a reason, for each of the provisions and I shall highlight some of them. We have thought about these changes—they have not been made casually.

I shall deal with the points made by the hon. Member for Withington. The provision that revokes regulation 49 of the claims and payments regulations has attracted considerable attention, which is exemplified by the early-day motion of 10 November tabled by the hon. Member for Hartlepool (Mr. Mandelson) and some of his colleagues. The level of concern is out of all proportion to the effects of the revocation. The terms of the early-day motion highlighted a fundamental misconception about the regulations. I should like to emphasise that there is no question of people losing benefit through official error as a result of the change.

Regulation 49 was a transitional provision to enable claims to supplementary benefit and family income supplement to be dealt with after those benefits were abolished in 1988. It is true that its revocation now would preclude any new claims for supplementary benefit for periods before April 1988. However, it does not make sense for people to be able to claim benefit intended for current needs four and a half years after its abolition, particularly when claims for its successor, income support, cannot be backdated more than 12 months. When income support was introduced, it was right to provide some leeway for late claims to supplementary benefit. All those who might have claimed have had ample opportunity to do so and the continued existence of regulation 49 is genuinely anomalous and unnecessary.

Of course, cases may come to light in which the failure to claim supplementary benefit was the result of departmental error, for example, where an official gave inaccurate or JPEG 7 misleading information. That is the central point conveyed by the early-day motion and the main point of many who have been worried about the regulation. However, there are well-established procedures for dealing with such cases and for making ex-gratia payments where appropriate.

I know that that is not the same as a statutory remedy, but it is important to recognise the difficulty of dealing with claims that are long overdue, the facts of which are difficult to establish accurately. The Social Security Advisory Committee carried out a thorough study of the whole subject of time limits in the 1980s. Its firm conclusion was that the interests of good administration and fairness to all claimants required absolute time limits and that it was right to deal with cases of official error outside those time limits extra-statutorily. I should add that that has always been the position for the majority of benefits. In having no absolute time limit, supplementary benefit was an exception.

I should also emphasise that the fact that people can no longer make late claims for supplementary benefit will not affect the position of people who claimed supplementary benefit on time, but who did not receive their correct entitlement. The regulations covering review of incorrect adjudication decisions already apply to supplementary benefit and will continue to do so. They provide for unlimited arrears in cases of official error. The Opposition's fears that the revocation of regulation 49 will prevent the correction of official errors are, therefore, wholly unfounded.

The hon. Member for Withington expressed concern about mentally handicapped claimants who may not have taken up their entitlement to supplementary benefit. I can only repeat that supplementary benefit, like income support, was intended to meet current, day-to-day needs. It is reasonable to treat mentally handicapped claimants in the same way whether their entitlement arose before or after 1988. However, it is highly unlikely that there are many, if any, cases of severe need, in which entitlement existed before 1988, but which have not been taken up four and a half years later. If any case of official misdirection comes to light, it will be covered. Therefore, such fears are unfounded.

It has been suggested that the regulations have been sprung upon an unsuspecting world. That is not the case. The proposals have been in the public domain since August and the Social Security Advisory Committee was content that the regulations need not be referred for formal consultation. That suggests that the Social Security Advisory Committee appreciated that no sinister motive lay behind the regulations.

The hon. Member for Withington raised the sauce for goose and gander argument; that there is no time limit on overpayment recovery. It would be illogical to place a time limit on the recovery of overpayment. That would advantage those who could conceal fraud the longest, and would be wrong. In addition, overpayments are recovered only in cases of claimant error. Underpayments are made good in full in cases of official error.

The hon. Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire raised three other points, the first of which related to regulation 4. The Benefits Agency wants to deliver a more efficient and responsive service and hopes to deal with more transactions by telephone. That will include the notification of minor changes in circumstances. Therefore, the amendment enables the Secretary of State to accept such notification. 8 Where necessary, the claimant will be asked to supply written confirmation of the change later. The new procedures will be piloted first to test their effectiveness, but the intention is to reduce bureaucracy when it is sensible to do so. In effect, a person who wishes to notify the Benefits Agency of a change of circumstances can do so by telephone rather than having the hassle of writing it down.

The hon. Gentleman's second point related to the natural or unnatural person. The regulation would enable benefit to be paid to a relative or close friend of the claimant in cases where the claimant would misuse the benefit because of some sort of incapacity, such as gambling the money away, being affected by alcohol or whatever.

The amendment would prevent payments being directed to a corporate body, such as a public utility or a housing authority, under that regulation. While regulation 35 provides for direct payment of income support to meet debts to corporate bodies, some creditors are thought to invoke regulation 34 to obtain direct payment of contributory benefits for housing or fuel costs. That was never the intention of the policy and the amendment would restore the original policy intention.

The hon. Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire made a point about the reference to Scotland. Regulation 8 makes some technical changes to the criteria for direct payment of water charges in England and Wales. Water charges in Scotland are levied as supplement to the community charge and are subject to the usual payment enforcement procedures, which might involve deductions from income support after a local authority has obtained a summary warrant. Those changes apply only to England and Wales because different arrangements apply in Scotland.

I hope that the Committee feels that I have answered some of the questions raised. The revocation of regulation 49 is long overdue. It serves no useful purpose and its continuing existence has lead to some strange abuses. Some welfare rights organisations have argued that its continued existence means that the supplementary benefits scheme is still current in all its manifestations. I am pleased to say that a social security commissioner has already disagreed with such a wide interpretation. Revoking the regulations will remove the opportunity for any future manipulation that wastes valuable staff time, which could be more usefully deployed elsewhere in the Benefits Agency dealing with need.

I hope that my remarks satisfy the Committee and that it agrees the motion.

I want to consider the administrative changes in terms of warning people about whether they are actively seeking work. Under the current arrangements, warning letters are sent to people stating clearly what they must do to ensure that they do not lose entitlement because of lack of proof that they are actively seeking work. The warning letters which have been sent out since the changes were made have been very successful. After having the procedures clearly explained and being given the opportunity to provide evidence that they were actively seeking work, the number 12 of people who were denied benefit was very small. It was also noticeable how many people subsequently claimed hardship allowance. People were able to satisfy the employment service that they were actively seeking work and were therefore, entitled to benefit, but very few eventually received hardship allowances. We must question whether people really knew of their entitlement to such hardship payments once they satisfied the adjudication officer that they had actively sought work.

We must look carefully at the administrative changes. Vulnerable groups of people not having received the warning letter, will be immediately denied benefit. We should consider those with literacy problems, health problems or those who are so worn down by the system that they find it very difficult to furnish proof of actively seeking work. That failure will prove them guilty before they can prove their innocence. People will be denied benefit at the very time when they are in greatest need of some payment. The sums of money are small but the claimants will be caught by the administrative changes.

Much research is being done into the effect of long-term unemployment and the way in which this has undermined people's confidence and their ability to claim their just entitlements. In the debate in the House yesterday, the shadow Secretary of State for Employment made telling points about the effects of unemployment. He said that in the current situation,

It is believed that the opportunities for employment are so limited, with unemployment rising, that to make the test more draconian is quite out of proportion to the problem that the Government are addressing. The Minister should look much more carefully at the advice of his own advisory committee on the way in which the rules will operate at a time of recession and high unemployment.

The changes also contradict the advice that was given in the Social Security Act 1989 when the availability to work rule was first introduced. I quote from the original note given to hon. Members at that time which stated that a claimant

"will be given a clear warning and will thus be specifically reminded of what constituted actively seeking work and will have been given an opportunity to take appropriate action, before the question of whether he fails to satisfy the test arises to put to the adjudication officer."

At that time, the Minister stressed,

"we do not want benefits to be withdrawn, but we want to encourage people to take steps to get back into employment."

The changes relating to the withdrawal of the warning letters mean that a person will lose benefit and will have no right of appeal against the decision until the adjudication officer has considerd the case. There will be a clear penalty against someone who may subsequently be able to prove that they were actively seeking work.

Another issue that immediately springs to mind is that because of the number of people seeking every job vacancy, many employers are not prepared, administratively, to furnish information to applicants to prove that they sought a job with that company. It is difficult for people to receive written notification that they applied for a job and were not successful, because it does not make economic sense for a company to reply to each applicant when so many people JPEG 13 are chasing every job. At a time of lengthening dole queues, the Government are making it harder for the long-term unemployed to satisfy the actively seeking work rule.

The regulations have a fundamental flaw. To be eligible for hardship payments the claimant has to satisfy tests under either regulations 10A(ii) or 8(iii), which concern availability for work. If a person is found to be available for but not actively seeking work, that person is not entitled to hardship payments. However, if a person satisfies neither the availability-for-work rule nor the actively seeking work rule, he may be entitled to hardship payment. That might mean that the people who are meant to be attacked by these regulations will, because of their lifestyle, street-wiseness and other factors, be able to claim hardship payments. However, other more vunerable, people will not be entitled to claim benefit because they will satisfy the availability-to-work rule but not the actively seeking work rule. Significant numbers of people may fall into that category.

That problem was recognised by the Social Security Advisory Committee, which was particularly concerned about women who may be fleeing from domestic violence or other abuse. The difficulty of those women was highlighted in the excellent debate in the other place. There is concern that such women, who may be in refuges, may be considered to be available for work. Because of their particular circumstances however, they cannot go out to find work. I am sure that the Minister understands their predicament.

I have highlighted the special circumstances of women fleeing from violence, but homeless people, people with literacy problems, people with disabilities that are not so severe that they are entitled to benefits which would give them a passport to a decent level of income, also have special circumstances. The figure of two per year per office throughout the country does not suggest that we are looking actively for people to come within the regulations.

Mr. Bradley
: In statistical terms, the Minister is right, but we must ensure that we are not making changes that will increase that statistical level and that more people do not suffer hardship because of those changes. The monitoring will prove the point one way or the other.

The Minister referred to the Government's proposed range of options to deal with the problem. I wish that they had been introduced as quickly as the changes in the social security regulations. We seem to be tackling the problem the wrong way round. We should consider the law and order and public order measures before making hasty changes in social security legislation, which may not hit the target.

The wide-ranging regulations may hit many vulnerable groups and that is not the most sensible way in which to proceed. I wish that the panoply of alternative legislation had been introduced first so that the regulations could be seen in the context of that other legislation rather than in isolation. If the proposed alternative legislation had been 26 introduced first, it might not have been necessary to change the regulations because the regulations and the administrative procedures based on them may have been sufficient. We might not have had to attack other people who are totally innocent of charges that the Government may wish to levy against new age travellers.

Although the Minister tried to reassure us, I leave the Committee with great anxieties about the changes in the regulations. The Opposition will monitor the changes carefully and I welcome the Government's assurance they will do that as well. If there are widespread difficulties of interpretation and vulnerable groups of people fall foul not only of the rule on actively seeking work but of the availability of work rule and the inter-relationship of the two regulations, I hope that the Government will return to the House with subsequent amendments to protect those vulnerable people.

We have made our position absolutely clear. We strongly support the advisory committee recommendation that the changes should not be proceeded with and that remains our position. Having put our views on the record, we feel it unnecessary to divide the House.
