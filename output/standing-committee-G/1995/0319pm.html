Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are discussing amendment No. 534, in page 73, leave out lines 13 to 15.

The Minister for Local Government, Housing and Urban Regeneration (Mr. David Curry): I was replying to the hon. Member for Christchurch (Mrs. Maddock). I believe that the hon. Lady was objecting to the fact that the Government were taking an order making power. I was about to explain that we needed an order making power to update legislation and to bring it into line with court judgments. In the past, that has taken some time. Such a power would enable us to do so more rapidly and more effectively.

An order making power will enable the Secretary of State to clarify the law if a court decision calls into question the meaning of a particular provision and the general understanding of how the system is supposed to operate. The amendments to the homelessness legislation introduced in the Housing and Planning Act 1986, which ensured that someone in temporary accommodation could still be homeless, did just that. However, it was several years after the initial court decision before such clarification was provided. The power will enable us to move more quickly. In general, we intend to use it to safeguard the proper rights of homeless households.

Amendment No. 539 seeks to ensure that the order making power can be used only in that way. I understand that; but there have been instances when the decisions of the courts have involved an unexpectedly broad interpretation of the law, which imposed a consequent burden on local authorities before being overturned on appeal. We need to be able to take the proper action in such cases. However, I assure the hon. Lady that we have no intention of restricting entitlement under the homelessness legislation to a position less favourable than is at present commonly understood.

Amendment No. 534 is based on a misunderstanding of the nature of the powers that we seek. Subsection (1) makes a clear reference to the circumstances under which it is not unreasonable to continue to occupy property—namely, where there is violence or a threat of Col. 722 violence. Subsection (3)(a)—which the amendment would retain—would allow an order to specify other such circumstances—again, in an absolute sense.

Subsection (2) sets out other factors that an authority should take into account in reaching decisions on individual cases; it cites the housing situation prevailing in the area. Subsection (3)(b) would allow an order to specify other matters that an authority should take into account in forming a view. For example, an order might refer to factors such as access to facilities or services essential to an individual's well being. To make such an order, we should need to rely on the provision contained in subsection (3)(b). Both limbs of subsection (3) are needed, so I invite the hon. Lady to withdraw the amendment.

Mrs. Diana Maddock (Christchurch): The intention was to ensure that regulations could be made only to keep legislation in step with positive developments in case law. I think that the Minister gave me some reassurance on that. Will he double check with lawyers that the wording of subsection (3) will achieve that? With such an assurance, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.
