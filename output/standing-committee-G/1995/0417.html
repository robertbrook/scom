'because of the public interest, a court would not restrain (apart from this Act)'.

 Forgive me, Mr. Mills, if a trespassed on your role as our authority in the Chair. I shall, of course, be guided by you in all matters. I think that this is the first time that I have participated in a Standing Committee under your chairmanship. You and I have been friends for a long time—since 1979, I believe—and it is a pleasure for me and, I am sure, for all hon. Members, to have the Committee convened under your chairmanship.

Government Amendment No. 1 would improve and clarify the definition of "public interest disclosures" by making it clear that the term refers to existing case law dealing with cases in which the courts would not restrain a disclosure of confidential information. It would make the test an objective one and would cover cases that were not, formally, actions for breach of confidence—for example, breach of contract cases.

As I said on Second Reading, the Government have considerable sympathy with the aims and motives that underlie the Bill. However, we have serious concerns about its provisions and are by no means convinced that such broad and wide-ranging legislation is appropriate to deal with the problems that have been 4 identified. One of our main concerns is that many of the Bill's provisions are unclear in intention, uncertain in effect and unworkable in practice.

Clause 1(a) is a prime example. It refers to a court finding a disclosure of information to be justified

"in an action for breach of confidence".

 That is simply not consistent with the way that established case law works. At present, there are cases in which the courts will not exercise their discretion to restrain a disclosure of information because they think that the disclosure is in the public interest—even though it will breach a duty of confidence that is in some way enforceable. However, the issue of whether a disclosure is in the public interest will not always or necessarily arise in an action for breach of confidence. The disclosure or proposed disclosure may give rise to some other action against the discloser, where the courts must consider whether it was made or should be made in the public interest.

The phrase

"a court has found, or would be likely to find"

 is also highly unsatisfactory. Surely the Bill is aimed at protecting employees who disclose information that is not yet in the public domain—not information where a court has already found disclosure to be in the public interest. To base the test on what a court would be likely to decide makes it a highly speculative and subjective proposition.

I hope that hon. Members will accept that it will help neither disclosers nor the organisations involved to enact a Bill that is a recipe for uncertainty and that, I am afraid, is what it is at present.

Mr. John M. Taylor
: The effect of amendments Nos. 4, 6, 12, 14 and 23 would be to narrow the coverage of the Bill to the disclosure of information acquired in the course of employment. The amendments would remove from the Bill's protection those in the professions, self-employed workers and—except when they are employees—voluntary workers, members of organisations and office holders.

Amendment No. 11 clarifies what the Bill means by a person being penalised for making a protected disclosure. It establishes that the protected disclosure may be the whole or part of the cause of the penalisation, rather than the principal cause, as in the Bill as drafted. It clarifies the definition of penalisation and brings it into line with similar provisions elsewhere in employment legislation. It makes it clear that the failure to carry out an action—for example, failure to promote someone—can count as penalisation, again in line with other provisions.

Amendments Nos. 15 and 16 would delete from the Bill the provisions that enable individuals to obtain respectively a special award of compensation and interim relief from an industrial tribunal if they are unfairly dismissed or, in the case of interim relief, claim that they have been unfairly dismissed, for making a protected disclosure of information. The compensation would thus be limited to that available in ordinary unfair dismissal cases and interim relief—a temporary reappointment until the hearing of the case—would not be available.

8 The amendments have three distinct purposes and I shall deal with each in turn. Amendments Nos. 4, 9, 12, 14 and 23 are designed to go some way towards addressing one of the main difficulties that we have with the Bill—specifically, that its coverage is far too broad and ill-defined. That would not only cause confusion and uncertainty, but would place real and undesirable burdens on business. The five amendments are designed to limit the Bill to disclosures of information acquired during the course of employment. I hope to discuss that matter in more detail in the clause 4 stand part debate. Those amendments would, if accepted, remove from the Bill's coverage those in the professions and other self-employed workers, those engaged in voluntary work, members of organisations and office holders.

I acknowledge the exclusion already provided in clause 2(5) in respect of legal advisers—an exclusion that I am sure it is right to make—but the Bill appears none the less to cover all other professional advisers and to deal with them all in the same way. In fact, as I am sure all members of the Committee will accept, the responsibilities of different professional advisers very considerably. Different professions have different rules designed to fit their particular circumstances—for example, when and to whom a member of the profession may disclose information in breach of the duty of confidence that he owes to his client.

As I said on Second Reading, the Bill will cut across many of those professional rules. Its effect will be to impose blanket provisions on, for example, a matter that we shall deal with in later amendments—the steps that a whistleblower must take to raise a matter internally before coming within the Bill's protection. Most of the professions have their own well-developed rules on those matters.

I also remain concerned that the Bill may commit professional advisers to disclose confidential information, not just to a proper authority but more widely. It is unacceptable for the Bill to cut across professional rules that apply to the disclosure of confidential information. Those rules have been carefully worked out and refined over many years to suit the circumstances of particular professions and the Bill will impose different, and almost certainly unsuitable, standards across the board.

Different considerations apply in respect of voluntary workers, members of organisations and office holders. When such individuals are employees, my amendment would not affect their protection under the Bill. However, when they are not employees, it would not be right to extend such protection to them. To leave the Bill as it stands would make its coverage virtually limitless; everyone from a boy scout to a member of the Women's Institute would have the potential to take court action to obtain redress for perceived penalisation.

I would not deny that problems may sometimes arise for voluntary workers, members of organisations and others who are not employees, but who acquire information that they think should be made public. However, if there are such problems, they should be dealt with on a case-by-case basis—with legislation, if JPEG 9 appropriate, tailored particularly to their circumstances—and not by a broad and unspecific measure such as this Bill. The amendments would confine the Bill to the clearly denned and well-recognised ambit of employment protection legislation and I hope that the Committee will accept them.

I hope that I need not detain the Committee long on amendment No. 11, which is intended to be helpful in clarifying the Bill's provisions and making them more effective. The amendment substitutes new wording for part of clause 3 and in doing so would achieve three things. First, it would establish that an individual is penalised within the meaning of the Bill if detrimental action were taken against him or her, wholly or partly because of a protected disclosure. That is in line with similar provisions elsewhere in employment legislation, for example, in the Employment Protection (Consolidation) Act 1978 which protects people against detrimental treatment for taking certain action on health and safety grounds. It contrasts with the position under the Bill, in which the protection applies only to penalisation that arises principally because of a protected disclosure.

My second claim for the amendment is that it would clarify the definition of penalisation and establish that it covers any form of detrimental action, not just the specific forms listed in clause 3(2). Again, that would bring it into line with the wording of established legislation with which practitioners, industrial tribunals and others are already familiar and well used to dealing. Thirdly, it would make it clear that penalisation can include not only a deliberate act by an employer—dismissal or demotion, for example—but a deliberate failure to act, such as the failure to promote or to provide training. That would bring the Bill into line with similar provisions in existing legislation. All three of the effects that I have described would widen the Bill's coverage and make it more comprehensive and effective. I hope that in the light of my explanation, hon. Members will accept the amendment.

Amendments Nos. 15 and 16 relate to the remedies available to individuals from an industrial tribunal in unfair dismissal cases brought under the provisions of the Bill. They would have the effect of deleting from clause 5 the provisions that enable individuals in such cases to obtain respectively a special award of compensation and interim relief. The Bill seeks to build on the existing remedies provided for in the Employment Protection (Consolidation) Act 1978 for health and safety representatives and committee members dismissed for taking certain types of action on health and safety grounds.

These special remedies were introduced in recognition of the fact that victimisation is particularly serious where it is directed towards an individual who has been elected or recognised as a representative carrying out a specific role in relation to health and safety matters. They are not available to ordinary employees with no representative role who are victimised for taking action on health and safety 10 grounds. The remedies available to ordinary employees in such cases are the ordinary unfair dismissal remedies.

I do not think that it would be right to extend the availability of special awards and interim relief—that is temporary reappointment until the hearing of the tribunal case—to employees victimised under the provisions of this Bill. They will have no special representative function or role in relation to whistleblowing and should not, therefore, have special remedies.

I do not seek to understate the seriousness of unfair dismissal in those circumstances. However, there are many other types of unfair dismissal that are equally serious and no special provisions are made with regard to remedies in those cases. In the Government's view the ordinary unfair dismissal remedies are perfectly adequate and acceptable.

Mr. John M. Taylor

What is wrong with clause 6, among other things, is that it is a reversal of the burden of proof for public bodies. The clause is drafted in terms of proving that it is in the public interest that information should be confidential. However, in the structure of the Bill, the clause comes into play only when a disclosure has been made and the person seeks a declaration or remedy. In practice, the effect of the clause would be that when someone disclosed information obtained in confidence from a public body, the burden would be on that body to prove that it was in the public interest for the information to be kept confidential.

The clause is unfair and unjustifiably discriminates against public bodies. When a private sector body is involved, the person who has made a disclosure must show that it was justified in the public interest. In the case of a public body, the primary requirement—the first provision in clause 1—is reversed and it is for the body to prove that the public interest is in the information being confidential. Moreover, singling 40 out public bodies for such unfavourable treatment runs counter to the general principle in clause 1 that the Bill should rely on existing common law that relates to the disclosure of confidential information in the public interest.

The Government can see no reason for treating public and private bodies in identical circumstances in diametrically opposed ways. It must be for the person who breaches a confidence, contrary to the obligation that he owes under his contract of employment or legislation, to show that there are genuine and adequate grounds for that breach. That is where the burden of proof must lie.
