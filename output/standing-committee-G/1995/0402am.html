Mr. Raynsford
: With new clause 52 we come to the low-rent test, which is one of the key obstacles to leaseholders enfranchising under existing arrangements. The low-rent test is defined in section 8 of the Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993.

There are three separate low-rent tests, the first of which applies to leases that were granted prior to April 1963 when, to qualify on the low-rent test, the ground rent must be less than two thirds of the letting value. The second test applies to properties on which the lease was granted between April 1963 and April 1990. In such cases, the test is that the ground rent must be less than two thirds of the rateable value. The third test applies to post-1990 properties when the ground rent is less than £1,000 in London and £250 elsewhere.

There is a series of problems with the arrangements. The figures are arbitrary and, in some respects, capricious. They provide considerable scope for landlord evasion. At present, it is easy for a landlord to 966 set the ground rent above the threshold to take the properties outside the scope for enfranchisement. Furthermore, there is a problem with properties when the lease was granted before 1963, where extraordinary difficulties arise in defining the letting value at the time and, thus, what two thirds of the letting value should be.

During the debates on the 1993 Act, strong opposition was voiced about the low-rent test, not just from the Opposition but from several Conservative Members, such as the hon. Members for Plymouth, Sutton (Mr. Streeter), for Croydon, Central (Sir P. Beresford), for Eastbourne (Mr. Waterson) and for Kensington (Mr. Fishburn) who spoke strongly against it. Sadly, they did not join us in the Division Lobby. Hopefully, today, some Conservative Members will put their principles ahead of the party Whip's blandishments and join us on this issue. We must do the right thing and I shall explain why the failure to do so in 1993 led to serious injustices to leaseholders.

A week ago, I received a letter from a leaseholder living in 68 Cadogan place in London. It states:

"I wrote to the Prime Minister in 1992 when his Government's Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Bill was being considered by Parliament. At that time leaseholders such as myself were assured that we would be able to buy our freeholds. This has proved well nigh impossible.

I acquired my long leasehold house on the Cadogan Estate in 1949. it was requisitioned during the war and was derelict. To restore, improve and maintain it over many years has been a great personal and financial sacrifice, particularly since my husband died.

The Cadogan Estate refused to renew my lease. Their solicitors wrote it 'would be the appropriate course for them to institute possession proceedings' against me at the end of the lease which would force me out of my only home. They know I have no statutory security of tenure because I'm disadvantaged by London's high rateable values.

As soon as the Leasehold Reform Bill became law in 1993, I had to exercise the only statutory right open to me by giving notice to my freeholders of my intention to acquire the freehold of my home. They would not admit my claim, obstructing me calculatingly on the sole point that my annual ground rent of £100 was not a low rent. They knew, in the case of leases granted between 1939 and 1963, I would have to prove that the ground rent is not more than two thirds of the letting value at the start of the lease. This has been the most tortuous and time consuming task for me and my experienced advisers.

I have been forced to go to the County Court on legal aid to establish the letting value. Despite having one of the acknowledged experts in such matters, the Judge completely failed to understand the complexities of how to ascertain the letting value of a lease granted over forty years ago. I now have no other recourse but to seek justice in the Court of Appeal. In a further attempt to obstruct me my freeholders are seeking to raise arguments not previously relied on by them.

The huge financial costs and the distressing personal effect upon me has severely impaired my health. My lease is a long lease at a low rent. It should fully qualify under the legislation. I am still no nearer achieving my vital legal rights. I most earnestly seek your help that steps will be taken to remove this disastrous anomaly."

 We could not ask for a clearer illustration of the injustice implicit in the present arrangements.

However, lest any doubts remain, it was confirmed in a letter to me from an extremely experienced chartered surveyor, Mr. Marr-Johnson, a partner in Marr-Johnson and Stevens. He wrote: JPEG 967

"I am a fellow of the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors and a partner in Marr-Johnson and Stevens…I have been in practice for 38 years, having trained in estate management with Chesterstons".

 Mr. Marr-Johnson wrote that throughout his career he has been concerned with valuations and general urban practice, having a large number of extremely well known clients, including most of the big estates. He has an impeccable track record. On the low rent test and the pre-1963 arrangements, he wrote:

"I have no hesitation in saying that section 4(1) of the Leasehold Reform Act 1967 is extremely difficult to interpret in any particular case. It affects only leases starting before April 1963, so the valuer is put to proof as to the letting value of a property more than 33 years ago. Frequently I find that the County Court has serious problems in understanding the valuation aspects of a case, and more often than not, the matter goes to appeal."

 Mr. Marr-Johnson cites a series of cases. He concludes that

"Although most of these mistakes were put right on appeal, many lessees fall by the wayside. I have just settled two cases where the claims were lodged 18 years ago in February 1978, and the lessees have lived under the threat of Court action and uncertainty throughout the interim…From time to time I am instructed to advise on doubtful cases under section 4(1); only too frequently I am obliged to include words in my report such as 'I am certain that the rent at the start of the lease was indeed low within the meaning of the Act, but I am far from certain that I would be able to prove it in Court.' The section works in a capricious manner, and in my opinion it often does prevent proper claims under the Act from getting through the qualifying process."

 That is devastating evidence that the current machinery does not work, that it prompts injustice.

Why is there a low-rent test? The only plausible justification is the one argued by the Government in 1993—that the absence of a low-rent test might undermine the rented market by allowing those paying a rack rent a right to enfranchise, but that is not so. Limiting enfranchisement to those with leases of 21 years or more, which is clearly part of the legislation, differentiates leasehold properties from rental properties, and the majority of lettings are now shorthold tenancies lasting for only six months, or other shorter periods. The matter was considered by the Lord Chancellor's Department when it consulted on commonhold in November 1990. The Department took the view, quite rightly, that there was no justification for a low-rent test for commonhold properties.

In conclusion, it is an unsatisfactory provision; it has an arbitrary and capricious impact; it is open to abuse; it is used as a device by freeholders to frustrate enfranchisement; it has been rejected by professionals as unworkable; and it was rejected by the Lord Chancellor's Department as unnecesary in respect of commonhold. The proposed change would be a long-overdue remedy for an injustice. It gives me great pleasure to move new clause 52. New clause 53 seeks to change the proportion of a property in non-residential use needed to allow leaseholders to enfranchise. The proportion of the property that is in non-residential usage is currently defined as subject to a maximum of 10 per cent. of the internal floor area. 968 The new clause will increase that to 25 per cent. There are many leaseholders living in blocks that have shops on the ground floor, and if more than 10 per cent. of the total floor area in non-residential usage, they do not have the right to enfranchise. That 10 per cent. rule can cause a serious injustice to people living in blocks over shops.

When we debated the matter in 1993, a series of amendments were moved. I tabled an amendment seeking a 50 per cent. limit, in line with the Landlord and Tenant act 1987. The Liberal Democracts spokesman suggested a 25 per cent. limit, and the hon. Member for Kensington (Mr Fishburn)—a Conservative Member—suggested a 20 per cent. limit. We all argued that 10 per cent. was unduly restrictive.

The Government said that they would consider the matter. The then Minister, the right Hon. Member for Ealing, Acton (Sir G. Young), undertook to return to the matter on Report. He also argued that the Government already had powers to amend the percentage by regulation. In practice, we had the worst possible outcome. There was no increase in the percentage, and the Government removed their discretion to amend it by regulation. We are stuck with a 10 per cent. limit, which is arbitrary. It does not allow people in blocks of flats the opportunity to enfranchise unless there are, literally, nine storeys above the shops on the ground floor—the 10 per cent.—and there are very few such blocks. That has been highlighted by many bodies—including the Leasehold enfranchisement Advisory Service—as an obstacle to enfranchisement.

: As the hon. member for Greenwich rightly said, these proposals were considered during the passage of the 1993 Act. It is important that the Committee should recall that the 1993 Act represented a considerable step forward for leaseholders by granting the right of enfranchisement. The Committee should also bear in mind that the right to enfranchisement involves compelling a freeholder to sell property. Therefore, at the time of the 1993 Act, it was felt that that right should be guided by principle and by striking the appropriate balance where necessary.

I shall deal with the detail of the new clauses, and that should assist the Committee. There are two tests to establish whether a flat owner can be regarded as an "owner occupier". First, there should be a long-term interest—that is more than 21 years—and, secondly, the leaseholder should pay only a nominal rent. Hence the low-rent test.

JPEG 971 The comparison of the annual ground rent to the rateable value is a simple test to distinguish between renting tenancies and "ownership" tenancies. As the hon. Member for Greenwich is no doubt aware, that test has a respectable history. It appears in roughly the same form in several pieces of important landlord and tenant legislation, including the Landlord and Tennant Act 1954, the Leasehold Reform Act 1967, which included a low-rent test that was the precedent for the 1993 Act, and the Rent Act 1977. It is, therefore, an established test, which has had an impact on the residential property market. It should not be changed lightly.

The 1993 Act also amended the Leasehold Reform Act 1967 to give leaseholders in houses the choice between the original low rent test and the test for flats. The alternative test takes into account ground rent during the first year of the lease and widens the scope for enfranchisement to those in houses with rent review clauses. Therefore, the 1993 Act materially improved upon the provisions in the 1967 Act for the application of the low-rent test on those owning homes under a lease.

That arrangement separates the managerial process from obligations to pay. Put simply, it gives the landlord the opportunity to spend other people's money on services.     "I said in response to that debate that I believed that, in principle, the guidance should deal with that. I undertook to examine the matter to see whether the guidance needed to be supplemented. To be consistent, I make the same promise in relation to the amendment."—[ Official Report, Standing Committee G, 27 February 1996; c. 365.]

     Of course, guidance cannot extend to the private sector. The Minister has no statutory power to give guidance in the private sector. Private landlords would not normally consider such guidance.

    The new clause would apply to all possession actions. It would require notice to be given to the social services where the landlord believes that the defendant might have mental health problems. The landlord would have to do that before he could issue his proceedings. That would not be an onerous task. I hope that the Government will consider new clause 63 favourably.

    The purpose of new clauses 85 and 86 is similar, but they deal with different matters. New clause 85 seeks to ensure that social landlords who obtain a suspended possession order cannot enforce it by means of a warrant for possession without first obtaining the court's leave.
