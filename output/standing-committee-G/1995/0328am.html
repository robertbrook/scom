Mr. Clappison: New clause 45 concerns the right of long leasehold owners of flats to acquire the freehold interest of the premises in which they live, which is contained in chapter 1 of part 1 of the Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993. Section 3 of that Act specifies the premises to which the Act applies. It stipulates that for the right of enfranchisement to arise the premises must be a self-contained building or a self-contained part of a building which must be in the freehold ownership of the same person—a significant stipulation. It has become apparent that, in order to deny leaseholders that right, some freeholders have been selling-off the freehold of one or a number of flats within premises. That sale is usually to a company which they control, but it has the effect of creating more than one freehold interest in the relevant premises, and therefore the enfranchisement rights simply fail, because of the stipulation.

The new clause and amendments will deal with this practice by allowing long leaseholders to enfranchise where one or more flying freeholds have been created, whether or not deliberately. They are extremely long and somewhat complicated, and I am sure that the Committee would not want me to go through the necessary changes line by line. Instead, I propose to confine my remarks to a simple explanation of the effects of new clause 45 and then to refer to one or two of the principal consequential amendments set out in the proposed new schedule contained in amendment No. 577.

Col. 872 In order to prevent the creation of a flying freehold from defeating an attempt at enfranchisement, the first step is to delete the reference in section 3 of the 1993 Act to the requirement that the relevant premises should be owned by the same person. This is achieved by new clause 45(1).

That may give rise to a further difficulty. Leaseholders can purchase the freehold of a self-contained part of a building as a minimum unit of enfranchisement. Once the reference to a single person owning the freehold has been deleted, in cases where there are several self-contained parts of a building owned by different freeholders, one group of leaseholders could buy up all the freehold interests in the whole building. In effect, they could take over other parts of the building with which they had no previous connection, providing that they made up the necessary majority of qualifying leaseholders. This could mean that in a row of terraced houses converted into flats, for example, a large house could take over a smaller one within the same terrace.

That would clearly be unacceptable, which is the reason for new clause 45(2) which provides that if a building is made up of several self-contained parts and there is more than one freehold interest within the building, the right of enfranchisement will not apply to the building as a whole. It will, however, apply to an individual self-contained part, even if there is a flying freehold within it.

New clause 45(3) refers to additional property, which is not part of the residential premises being acquired, but which may be acquired as part of the enfranchisement process. This must be property that is demised by the lease or property which the tenant is entitled to use under the terms of the lease. For example, it could be a garden or a separate block of garages used by tenants. As presently drafted, the Act allows the freehold of such property to be acquired by the leaseholders only if it is in the same freehold ownership as the flats. There is therefore a further potential device available to the freeholder of selling off this additional property to another person. The leaseholders would not then be able to purchase it, and the enfranchisement package may look less attractive to them. New clause 45(3) would remove that potential device for frustrating enfranchisement by deleting the requirement that the additional property must be in the same freeholder ownership as the flats for it to be included.

The proposed new schedule contained in amendment No. 577 will make consequential changes to related sections and schedules of the 1993 Act. The Committee may be pleased to hear that I will not describe them all in detail, but I should like to speak briefly to some of them.

In the usual enfranchisement process, where there is a single freehold interest, the freeholder becomes the reversioner—the person with whom the leaseholders deal, and on whom is served all the notices. Where there are two or more freehold interests, it may be less clear who the reversioner Image Col. 873 should be. Therefore, paragraph 15 of the schedule will insert a new part IA into schedule 1 of the 1993 Act to the effect that the reversioner shall be the freeholder specified in the leaseholders' initial notice. If the freeholders then agree among themselves to a change of reversioner, he can be appointed with the agreement of the court. That will mean that once the initial notice has been served by the leaseholders, the time limits will have begun, which will reduce the scope for the freeholders to delay the enfranchisement process.

Paragraph 18 of the schedule sets out the basis for calculating the price to be paid for the freehold and intermediate leasehold interests in a multiple freeholder case. The essential point is that the nominee purchaser acting for the tenants will pay a separate price for each of the freehold interests. Each amount will be calculated in the usual way as for any other enfranchisement purchase. Therefore, it will comprise three elements: the open market value of the freeholder's interest, the freeholder's share of any marriage value and any amount of compensation due.

Paragraph 18 also contains detailed provisions as to how the amount payable to any intermediate leasehold interest should be calculated.

In summary, the new clause and the related amendments deal with the flying freehold device, which has been used to attempt to frustrate the purpose of the 1993 Act and the wishes of Parliament. Therefore, it is important that the necessary steps are taken to rectify the matter and give leaseholders the right to enfranchise in such cases, both of which the new clause and the related schedule attempt to do.

Under the leasehold system, the freeholder or landlord has the responsibility for the repair and maintenance of the property, and recovers his costs of doing so through service charges paid by individual tenants. Because there is a potential imbalance of power, Parliament has from time to time legislated to protect leaseholders from landlords who might be tempted to exploit their power.

The Government's proposals are designed to tackle bad landlords—those who seek to exploit the system. It is appropriate to point out that such bad landlords are a minority. Many landlords are good, of course, but the minority of bad landlords can cause problems for leaseholders. We have proposed new rights that will limit landlords' recourse to forfeiture; provide easier means of challenging unreasonable service charges; enlarge the grounds for going to court; allow a manager to be appointed when there is proven bad management; and allow surveyors appointed by tenants associations better rights of access to landlords' records relating to specification and buildings.

We do not want to impose new obligations and duties on the good landlords—the majority—who act honourably.
