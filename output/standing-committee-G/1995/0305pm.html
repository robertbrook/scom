Clause 77
APPLICATIONS FOR DETERMINATION OF RENT: TIME LIMIT

4.30 pm

Mrs. Bridget Prentice
(Lewisham, East)
: I beg to move amendment No. 336, in page 45, leave out lines 21 to 30 and insert—

"(2) In subsection (1) after 'may' insert 'at any time during the original tenancy or any replacement tenancy until such time as the landlord serves a notice under section 13(2)'


(3) In sub-section (4)(a) after 'application' insert 'and, provided there is no change of circumstances, the determined rent shall remain for such period between one and two years as the rent assessment committee shall direct, regardless of any change of tenant or landlord.'.".

The amendment is designed to allow tenants more flexibility when referring their levels of rent to the rent assessment committee. It would fix the determined rent at the maximum payable for a minimum period of one year and for a maximum period of two years, whether or not there is a change in tenancy during that time.

The reasons behind the amendment are simple. The Department of the Environment's statistics show a considerable increase in the number of referrals to the rent assessment committee for determination of assured shorthold rents. For example, 166 referrals were made in 1989 and, in 1995, that number rose to 1,520. One reason for the rise is that local authorities are now more likely to encourage tenants to make referrals to the rent assessment committee when they are informed of a rent restriction on their housing benefit assessment. That is good practice and I image that all members of the Committee would want to encourage councils to continue it. It gives tenants on low income the opportunity to narrow the gap between their housing benefit and their rents.

Another reason behind the amendment might encourage the Under-Secretary of State to support it. It would save an enormous amount of money that is paid out in housing benefit. Let us consider the Department of the Environment's estimate showing that 300,000 shorthold tenancies are claiming housing benefit. About 40 per cent. of them have their rent restricted and the average rent restriction for housing benefit is about £900 a year.

If only half of the rent-restricted claimants applied to rent assessment committees and the rent was reduced by the average amount, the savings in housing benefit would be about £20 million. I am sure that the Under-Secretary's colleagues in the Treasury would be more than delighted if such a saving could 490 be made. It would mean that tenants would not have to fill in the gap of £900 that should be used for food and other essentials.

At present, tenants have the right to make referrals within six months of the tenancies, but they do not necessarily need to make such referrals within those first six months. They may be in work or they might not be particularly concerned about the level of rents. They may not realise that their rents are substantially more than other rents in the area. In some areas, market rents go up and down over time. Therefore, a rent that was only a little in excess of similar market rents might rise significantly. Alternatively, tenants may have moved to an area that was not particularly well known to them and it may take them some time to realise that rent levels in the locality were considerably lower than their rents.

It is obvious that, if there were fewer lettings of tenancies for rents that were significantly higher than market levels, fewer tenants would have rent arrears because of housing benefit restrictions or a change in financial circumstances. That in turn, would reduce the number of possession actions on rent arrears, which is an important factor if the mandatory grounds are widened as proposed in clause 78.

Despite all the rule changes for the assessment of housing benefit and the role of the rent officer in that process, in the vast majority of cases there is no scope for the quality of individual lettings to be taken into account. Therefore, poor quality, substandard, even dangerous accommodation sometimes attracts a better rent than it deserves and there is no incentive for landlords to bring their properties up to an acceptable standard. However, rent assessment committees can invite detailed submissions from both landlords and tenants about the merits of the property and can carry out inspections before determining the rent. Landlords would be penalised for providing substandard lettings and good landlords, who run well-managed homes would see that reflected in the level of the rent. It would encourage landlords to carry out proper repairs and improvements.

Encouraging the wider use of the rent assessment committee would be a strong disincentive for landlords who are tempted to get rid of tenants whose rents have been determined by the committee in order to re-let the property at the previous excessive rent. That is why we believe that giving the maximum rent payable for a fixed period of between one and two years, even if the tenant changes, would be appropriate.

This is a reasonable amendment that would help the tenant who may not initially have a view about the level of the rent, or who may after six months find that his finances have changed quite markedly. It would also help good landlords who have decent properties to charge a proper rent and would encourage rogue landlords to improve their properties. If we can achieve that, we would save the Treasury a considerable amount of money whch could be better spent elsewhere.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Mr. James Clappison)
: The hon. JPEG 491 Member for Lewisham, East (Mrs. Prentice) moved the amendment in an attractive way and was clearly anxious to strike a reasonable note. Notwithstanding that, the Committee should beware of the amendment because it would extend the scope of regulations on assured shorthold tenancies beyond what we propose in the Bill and, indeed, beyond what was established by the Housing Act 1988, when we were anxious to strike a balance and to keep the scope of regulation at a level that would encourage landlords to let their properties and increase the supply of rented property on the market. The Committee knows that that has been a success and that the balance that was struck in 1988 has secured those objectives. I know that there are some ardent deregulationists in the Committee who should note that the amendment would be re-regulationary.

Turning to the detail of the amendment, the best way in which to view the six-month period over which tenants are entitled to refer their rent to the rent assessment committee is to regard that period as an initial safeguard for tenants. Shorthold tenants were given the right in 1988 to refer to the rent assessment committee, thereby providing a safeguard against unreasonably high rents as shorthold tenancies and market rents were introduced. As the Committee is probably aware, in the housing White Paper, we proposed to remove that right on the ground that it was little used. I listened with interest to the figures mentioned by the hon. Member for Lewisham, East. I do not know the exact figures but I imagine that she would agree that since 1988 there has been a substantial increase in the number of assured shorthold tenancies. We should set the increase in referrals to which the hon. Lady referred against that background. Even now, 1,500 referrals is a small proportion of assured tenancies, of which there are about 800,000. However, in the light of responses to the White Paper, we decided to maintain the shorthold tenants' right of referral during the first six months of the original tenancy. We believe that six months provides a reasonable opportunity for the tenant to make an application, while giving the landlord the certainty that an appeal cannot be made after the deadline has passed. We would not advise going beyond that initial safeguard of six months by extending to a replacement tenancy the right of referral to a rent assessment committee.

I turn now to the new subsection (3), which is proposed in amendment No. 336. I cannot accept that the rent assessment committee should be able to set a rent for more than a year ahead. That would clearly be additional regulation. I suggest that the landlord should be certain that he can increase the rent in line with market movements; nor is it reasonable to expect the committee to predict movements in the rental market over a longer period, as envisaged in the amendment. The tenant will, of course, have the opportunity to negotiate with the landlord the rent for a replacement tenancy. Both landlord and tenant will be able to take account of any determination made by the committee during the original tenancy. If the 492 original tenancy lapses into a statutory periodic tenancy, the tenant will have another opportunity to apply to the Committee for a determination if the landlord serves a notice of rent increase under section 13 of the Housing Act 1988.

The safeguards already in place are adequate. To go any further, as the amendment envisages, would establish further regulation. That would be undesirable.

Clause 78
MANDATORY POSSESSION FOR NON-PAYMENT OF RENT: REDUCTION IN ARREARS REQUIRED

Mrs. Diana Maddock
(Christchurch)
: I beg to move amendment No. 332, in page 45, line 33, leave out from "possession" to end of line 37 and insert "leave out Ground 8".

The Bill proposes to reduce the mandatory ground for possession for assured tenants from three months to two months. Many believe that that will cause untold hardship for many private tenants, and that there are good reasons for removing the mandatory element entirely, which is the aim of the amendment.

Unlike the case of owner-occupiers and council tenants, under ground 8 the court has no discretion if it discovers that the tenant is at a certain level of arrears. Under the Housing Act 1988 the discretionary ground 10 allows the court to grant possession if it considers that appropriate. Given the fact that there are proposals to change housing benefit regulations so that housing benefit will be paid four weeks in arrears, there is even more cause for concern.

I should like the Under-Secretary to say why he thinks private tenants should be treated differently from home owners or council tenants. In the case of mortgage lenders, the courts have discretion to make suggestions about payment orders to deal with arrears and to defer, suspend or even refuse possession. In such cases, judges are allowed to take into account the circumstances of both the borrower and the lender.

If councils want to evict tenants, they must go through numerous hoops before they can do so. Even housing associations, by virtue of their tenants' guarantee, do not use ground 8 to evict tenants in arrears; they use discretionary ground 10. The Government view private sector lets as a business; they are a business, but we must remember that people live in such properties—they are their homes. That is why this matter must be considered carefully.

If ground 8 were removed from the Housing Act 1988, landlords could still use the discretionary ground. Frequently, it is probably more in their interest to use the discretionary ground because the court can arrange for the repayment of arrears. Landlords are far more likely to be paid arrears if the tenant is still living in their properties.

Rent arrears occur for many different reasons, but they are often caused by benefit delays, benefit restrictions and, in some cases, poverty. Because ground 8 is mandatory, the courts cannot take into account extenuating circumstances, such as ill health, ability to pay and whether a tenant has children or other dependants. There is an anomaly: the Government continue to cut housing benefit in the private sector even though private sector rents are increasing, yet they want to make it easier for landlords to evict tenants in rent arrears.

JPEG 495 In many areas, tenants face long delays in housing benefit. It has been estimated that 70 per cent. of authorities manage to pay only 80 per cent. of housing benefit claims within 14 days. Often the areas with the most delays are those where there is most pressure on housing benefit departments and where most people live in private sector accommodation.

The Government's proposals to pay housing benefit in arrears will put people under increased pressure. If ground 8 were to be deleted, rent arrears would remain a discretionary ground under ground 10, which would mean that the circumstances to which I have referred could be taken into account.

I shall give an example of the sort of difficulties that can arise, especially when councils are investigating fraud. Mr. X claimed benefit on the bedsit into which he had just moved. He then received a letter saying that his claim for benefit was being suspended because a possible case of fraud was being investigated. Mr. X's landlord was receiving benefit direct from the council, but failed to inform the council that a tenant had moved away. Mr. X was without benefit for eight months, and his landlord evicted him, citing the mandatory ground of being more than 13 weeks in arrears. If the discretionary ground had been used and the full circumstances had come out in court, the court could have judged that the landlord should not have been granted mandatory possession because Mr. X would have eventually received benefit to pay the arrears.

Tenants face another problem. If landlords want to get tenants out, they fail to collect rent and, sometimes, even refuse to take rent. Landlords use such methods to gain possession. If the power was discretionary, not mandatory, such matters could be sorted out.

I hope that the Under-Secretary will consider the matter; there is plenty of evidence that ground 8 is unnecessary and unfair. It is high time that we treated private tenants in the same way as owner-occupiers and social sector housing tenants. Often, private tenants pay the highest rents for properties in the worst conditions—that is not always true, but sometimes it is. It would be much better if individual circumstances were taken into account by judges in the courts when deciding whether repossession should be granted on the ground of rent not having been paid. There are cases of genuine hardship, benefit delays and many other good reasons for rent not having been paid.

We want a thriving .private rented sector, and the measure would give security to many people who live in such properties.
