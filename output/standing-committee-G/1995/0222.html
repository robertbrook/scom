Mr. Curry: We heard a great deal on Second Reading and in Committee about the problems faced by some towns—seaside towns are often mentioned, but the problem is not confined to them—caused by the proliferation of what have become known as benefit hostels. My right hon. friend the Secretary of state made a commitment on Second Reading to introduce measures that would enhance the powers of Col. 282 local authorities to tackle such abuses. Our amendments fulfil that commitment, and I shall invite the Committee to accept them.

I shall not rehearse the substantive arguments about the nature of the problem; my hon. Friends have done that persuasively and eloquently. There is virtual consensus in the Committee that such problems need to be dealt with. I shall confine my remarks to our proposed remedy. We propose making special control provisions available to local authorities. I shall outline what is special about them.

If a local authority adopts a registration scheme that includes special control provisions, it will have four main powers. First, it will be able to refuse the registration of an existing HMO, or refuse to renew its registration, if there has been a management failure. Management failure is defined in new section 348F. It is the failure by the manager of the HMO to take whatever steps are

"reasonably practicable to prevent the existence of the house or the behaviour of its residents adversely affecting the amenity or character of the area".

When I speak of management failure, it is to that definition that I refer. In such a case, the number of residents in an HMO will have to be reduced by natural wastage, to the point at which the property no longer has to be registered. It will be a criminal offence to allow new people to move into such an HMO.

Secondly, a local authority will be able to refuse to register a new HMO after a registration scheme has been introduced if the authority believes that there are already enough HMOs in the area and that the presence of another will have an adverse effect.

Thirdly, it will be able to revoke a registration if there has been a breach of conditions, or over-occupation due to a management failure.

Fourthly, it will be able to impose an occupancy direction when the registration of an HMO is revoked. An occupancy direction would enable a local authority to speed up the reduction in the number of residents; the reduction would be faster than would have occurred by natural wastage. It will provide that the number of people living in the house should be reduced to a level that was not registrable, giving at least 28 days' notice. That is tougher than simply revoking registration, which prevents new people moving in, but allows existing tenants to remain. An occupancy direction may be imposed only if it appears to the authority that a relevant management failure has caused a serious adverse effect on the amenity or character of the area. Once again, the key to the exercise of those powers is management failure.

Before making an occupancy direction, the local housing authority must take into account the interests of the occupants of the house and of the person who has control, as well as those of local businesses and residents. That is an important safeguard, which will ensure that the authority looks at both sides of the problem.

Image Col. 283 The local authority can use special control provisions to require landlords and managers to take all reasonable steps to comply with an occupancy direction. That will have a similar effect to a closure order, which can be made when a house is unfit. Sufficient residents will have to leave, and statutory regimes of security of tenure will be suspended to allow the owner to obtain possession. People whose applications for registration are refused or varied, or have conditions imposed, or who receive an occupancy direction, must be given a written statement of reasons by the local housing authority. The usual rights of appeal to the county court will also apply in those cases. An appeal will, however, suspend the operation of an occupancy direction until it is finally determined by the county court or the Court of Appeal. The courts will have the power to confirm, reject or vary any notice or direction subject to appeal. We hope that the new powers will encourage owners and managers to impose an active management regime on their premises—we suspect that many of the worst HMO hostels are, frankly, not managed at all. For example, often one of the residents is appointed to hold a key, and everyone else is left to fend for themselves. When neighbours complain about anti-social behaviour, we want owners and managers to co-operate with the police and local authorities to try to deal with it.

The provisions are powerful weapons, and we want to ensure that they are used only when necessary. Although the Secretary of State will have the power to include them in model registration schemes, we do not intend to use that power. That means that any local housing authority that believes that it needs to use special control provisions will have to apply to the Secretary of State in order to have them in its armoury. The authority would have to demonstrate a genuine need for using the provisions before consent would be given.

We believe that special control provisions will enable those authorities with the worst problems to tackle them effectively. Targeted wisely, they may serve to inspire—or frighten—the next owner in line to clean up his act and help to reverse the spiral of decline that has occurred in some seaside resorts and elsewhere. I commend the amendment to the Committee.

Indeed, if we take that argument a little further, would not a loophole be produced which could enable an aggrieved proprietor of an HMO to challenge the application of the special control provisions? Such a proprietor could argue that the existence of an HMO was sanctioned under planning powers and, therefore, it was inappropriate for the authority to deem that the HMO's existence was adversely affecting the amenity of the area.

The second strand of the argument is the definition of the behaviour of the residents. The Opposition have no doubt that anti-social behaviour, by whomever it is perpetrated, is completely unacceptable and that action must be taken against it, but anti-social behaviour is not only the preserve of the occupants of HMOs or, indeed, tenants. Anti-social behaviour can be committed by anyone.

Many years ago when I lived in a certain area of London, the owner-occupiers in the house next door Image Col. 285 had an extremely flamboyant lifestyle, involving a large consumption of alcohol and behaviour that was extremely disturbing to everyone else in the street. That anti-social behaviour was not attributable to people living in a multi-occupied house.

Let us consider a hypothetical situation in which there are two houses—either side by side, or in the same neighbourhood—one of which is owner-occupied, the other an HMO. Let us assume that the occupants of both houses are prone either to hold loud, noisy parties where much alcohol is consumed, or to allow their premises to be used for drug abuse. In such circumstances, might not an aggrieved HMO landlord attempt to challenge the local authority's use of the powers under the special control provisions on the grounds that nothing intrinsically more deleterious to the amenity or character of the area is attributable to the HMO's occupants than is attributable to those in the owner-occupied house?

I put those points because we are genuinely worried that the Minister's proposal might make it difficult for effective action to be taken against the genuinely antisocial behaviour of people in inadequately managed HMOs. Amendment (e) would delete the words

"their existence or the behaviour of their residents"

and insert the words

"the way in which the houses are managed".

That comes to the nub of the problem. HMOs in which the occupants are allowed to behave anti-socially, or which are badly managed or not managed—as the Minister highlighted in his remarks—would fall four square within those terms. The owner of the property could not dispute the fact that special control provisions applied. The Minister should consider amendment (e) carefully and give his response to it. Amendment (e) would provide a more accurate and more appropriate definition of the reason for the problem and, therefore, a more effective response to it.

On Second Reading and in Tuesday's sittings, we were given distressing examples of the outrageous behaviour of some unscrupulous HMO landlords who deliberately packed their properties with people on social security, neglected the duties associated with running multi-occupied houses and, therefore, allowed an area or, in some cases, a whole resort to suffer. That is the root of the problem and the special control provisions must address it. They should deal with the failure of certain HMO landlords to manage their properties properly and with those who let them be used for inappropriate purposes.

I should highlight two more amendments. Amendment (g) to new clause 7 seeks to substitute the period of 56 days for 28 days in new section 348D as the minimum period within which a local authority may require a reduction in the occupation level. The reason for that is simple. If numbers are to be reduced, Col. 286 occupants will have to be removed; the question is to where. Those who are removed will not necessarily be the guilty parties. We are talking not about evicting people for anti-social behaviour, but about a landlord being required to reduce the numbers of tenants in a house to comply with control provisions. The landlord could simply pick on one or more innocent occupants and terminate their occupancy.

Allowing such people a little more time to find alternative accommodation is a necessary provision to minimise the possibility of their ending up homeless on the streets because of a lack of alternative accommodation. That would not mean neighbours' having to put up with anti-social conduct for another 28 days. That would be unacceptable. Where there has been a relevant management failure, we should expect the local authority to impose and enforce conditions. Some of our later amendments highlight the need for a duty of care that can be enforced against the managers or owners of HMOs who fail to keep their properties in order. We are not talking about allowing the problem to continue for a further 28 days. We seek a humane provision to ensure that someone, who may be an entirely innocent party, who loses his or her home is allowed slightly longer to find accommodation.

Amendment (h) is related to amendment (g), in that lines 93 to 95 of new clause 7 would allow normal security of tenure provisions to be overriden when an occupancy direction was made to reduce the numbers in a property. We fear that there is clear scope for abuse in that. For example, one might find an HMO in which one secure tenant lives in part of the property and pays a fair rent set by the rent officer which is substantially lower than the rents paid by the other occupants of the HMO. It would be an obvious temptation to a landlord who was subject to a direction on numbers to get rid of the tenant paying the fair rent. That tenant might be an innocent party who had lived in the property for many years, but would be made the victim of the mismanagement of the rest of the property and the behaviour of others who lived there. That would be a clear abuse, but it would be possible under lines 93 to 95 of new clause 7, which would allow the Rent Acts to be overriden by the application of a direction. I hope that the Minister will consider that serious point.

We do not want to hamper the ability of local authorities to take effective enforcement action against people who abuse the social security system and act irresponsibly by letting out slum properties, charging extortionate sums and letting them become places of anti-social behaviour. We want effective action, but we do not want to create innocent victims of a proper regulatory framework. We want to make sure that potential loopholes, such as the one that I have discussed, are plugged.

The amendments are sensible and necessary. The Government's new clause does not go so far as we would like, but it does something to tackle certain problems. I hope that the Minister will accept the amendments in the spirit in which they were tabled.
