Mr. Richard Burden
(Birmingham, Northfield)

The wording of new clause 26 may not be ideal. If Ministers accept that there is a problem, I should be happy to consider a better form of words. However, I am not holding my breath for a positive response.

The Chairman
: Order. A lot of conversations are going on in the Room at the moment. I would like to hear what the hon. Member is saying.

Mr. Burden
: If Ministers follow the logic of the argument that they put to the Committee recently, there is no reason why they should not accept the principle of the new clause. When we were talking about housing in multiple occupation, we urged a national licensing scheme, but Ministers said that they were not centralisers, did not try to impose on local authorities, and trusted them to do the right thing by their communities. We found that somewhat out of character, given the Government's recent performance.

However, if the Government have had a change of heart and now trust local authorities to do the right thing, there is no reason why they should not accept at least the principle if not the wording of the new clause. The trouble is that Ministers have shown themselves too often to be compulsive cappers, capping council tax and local authorities' use of capital receipts. New clause 26 deals with the capping of housing benefit subsidy to local authorities. If the new clause is not accepted, many local authorities will be faced with swingeing cuts in the repair and improvement programmes for their council estates—or council tenants will face quite unreasonable rent rises. That will be the result of policies introduced by Ministers.

Housing benefit subsidy is unlike other housing benefits. It is not paid to council tenants; it is discounted from their rents by local authorities, which are then reimbursed by the Government. The changes in the rules governing housing benefit subsidy were slipped in by the Government at the end of last year. What council house and council flat tenants pay through their rent has become more important during recent years. The capital programme of local authorities has been cut ruthlessly because of the restrictions placed on housing investment by central Government. The housing revenue account, which is ring-fenced, is now much more important in terms of it being used to put right major structural repairs in council housing.

It has been estimated that Birmingham need about £1.3 billion to remedy the problems in its council stock. Borrowing approvals will not meet that cost. Birmingham local authority consulted its tenants to try to solve the problem. It did not like the situation that it was in nor did it agree with the borrowing approvals. More than half of its properties did not have central heating. Too many council tenants were being forced to live in cold and damp JPEG 567 surroundings. An agreement was reached last year between the local authority and its tenants to raise rents by £2 a week to start a 10-year programme to fit UPVC windows and central heating in some of the worst housing blocks.

If the programme continued in the following year and the housing revenue accounts balanced, there would be a requirement of about £19 million. To maintain that programme, rents would have had to be increased by between £3 and £4 a week. However, now the Government have stepped in and said that if a local authority raises the rent for its tenants by more than the guideline rent increase, which was set not by the local authority or the tenants, but by Ministers, they will not reimburse the housing benefit subsidy that is discounted from tenants' rents.

At the end of last November, there was a "consultation"—it is always in quotes. Little publicity was given to it, but its consequences could be serious. Local authorities and tenants will have to make a choice. The poorest tenants who qualify for housing benefit would have to pay more rent, which they could not afford. If they could afford such rent, they would not have qualified for housing benefit in the first place. Alternatively, tenants who are not on housing benefit would have to make up the difference between the housing benefit discount and the refusal of the Government to reimburse it. Under that option, to raise the amount of money that last year's rent increase of about £3 to £4 a week would have raised, council house tenants in Birmingham would have to pay £10.17 a week extra.

The choice that is left is to cut the programme and forget about the central heating and the UPVC. As a result, people will have to continue to live in damp and cold surroundings. There will be a cost to the health service and to the families who have to live in such surroundings.

It is not just Birmingham that will face those problems. If the rule changes had been in place last year, Birmingham would have lost about £10 million, even though Birmingham tenants would probably have paid only an extra 50p a week. If we take the rule changes that will be imposed in the financial year 1996–97 and apply them notionally to what would have happened last year in other local authorities, there would have been a loss in rent income of £380 million to local authority housing revenue accounts. In Brent, for example, rents would have had to have risen by £14.70 a week rather than the £7 actual increase. In Croydon, average rent rises would have been £23.40, rather than £8.85, and in Liverpool average rent rises would have been £10.20, rather than £4.11. That makes a mockery of Ministers' claims that they want to trust local authorities. They do not trust local authorities to consult tenants and they do not consider the local consequences of Government policies—not local authority policies—on cutting capital investment in council housing.

It is not surprising that local authority associations have not welcomed that. George Meehan of the London housing unit said that

"it makes a nonsense of consultation with tenants on the balance between rent and service levels. Any housing policy Mr. Curry may have had has been supplanted by a housing benefit control policy."
