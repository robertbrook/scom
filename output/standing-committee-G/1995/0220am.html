Mr. Raynsford
: This group of amendments covers the extremely important issue of the licensing of houses in multiple occupation. Unless you wish me to, Mr. McWilliam, I do not propose to explain in detail each amendment, otherwise we would be in Committee for a long time. The amendments would introduce a mandatory national licensing scheme for HMOs. It is right to introduce such a scheme and to see it in the context of the current arrangements in multi-occupied houses.

As their name implies, houses in multiple occupation are properties in which more than one separate household lives. In the main, they comprise houses converted into bed-sitting rooms, shared houses or flats, JPEG 197 guest houses and bed-and-breakfast hotels including some that derive their income almost exclusively from people on income support and other social security benefit, households with lodgers and buildings that have been converted into self-contained flats.

The number of multi-occupied houses in England was estimated recently by the Department of the Environment at 638,000, which is slightly less than the estimate that was issued by the Government in November 1994 at the time of the consultation paper to which I shall return in a moment. That figures denotes the large scale of the problem in that 638,000 HMOs contain a greater number of people than that because, in many cases, they contain several tenants.

It is recognised universally that HMOs have some of the worst housing conditions and, thus, put at risk more people's safety and lives than any other housing. The Government's consultation paper that was issued in November 1994 made that point. It stated:

"estimates from the English House Condition Survey suggest that the privately rented HMO stock contains a significantly greater proportionate share of dwellings in poor condition than other forms of housing."

It goes on:

"there is little dispute that HMOs represent a significantly higher fire risk than single family homes."

To put a little more flesh on those bare bones, I must stress that four out of 10 of the traditional HMOs—properties converted into bed-sitters—are known to be statutorily unfit; 40 per cent. of them are unfit for human habitation. Fire and safety hazards are massively greater in HMOs than in housing stock elsewhere. The Campaign for Bedsit Rights has been unstinting in its hard work to draw public attention to the scandal of poor conditions in such properties. In a report that it commissioned from Geoffrey Randall last year, it was estimated that the risk of someone being killed or injured by fire in an HMO was 28 times higher than in other types of accommodation.

The National Housing and Town Planning Council published a report about a year ago, which was entitled "Potential Death Traps"—a title that encapsulates the problem. It outlined a particular problem and stated:

"As we go to the press, we read of yet another two deaths from carbon monoxide poisoning over Christmas, and of a court case following the death of two brothers also from CO after a landlord made a 'dangerously amateurish attempt' to mend a faulty central heating boiler. In the first case, a couple were gassed in each other's arms while watching a video. Their death was only discovered when they failed to turn up for Christmas lunch—they had been fine the previous evening. Unnecessary deaths from fire are so commonplace that they are often not even newsworthy."

The report goes on to highlight several areas, including Brighton. The National Housing and Town Planning Council stresses that Brighton

"is typical of many apparently affluent towns. A house condition survey in September 1994 identified 18,000 HMOs, comprising 15,400 converted flats and 2,600 bedsit houses … According to Trevor Sennett. Brighton's Private Sector Team Manager, all the latter HMO category failed one or more of the issues of fitness under the Housing Act 1985 as amended and the majority require fire safety work. In the converted house category, 80 per cent. require some expenditure to provide satisfactory means of escape and other fire precautions. In many cases this involves upgrading to meet current DoE guidance under circular 12/92."

198 That, in graphic detail, shows the scale of the problem—the poor conditions and the threats to life caused by fire or other hazards.

I do not believe that there is any difference in view between the Opposition and the Government on the extent of the problem. It is widely recognised that conditions in HMOs are unsatisfactory and that action must be taken. Indeed, as I have already mentioned, the Government published a consultation paper in November 1994, which had an interesting title: "Houses in Multiple Occupation—The Case for Licensing". Why were the Government interested in the possibility of a licensing scheme for HMOs? The prime reason, spelt out in that consultation paper and in many other documents published on the subject, can be summed up under two headings.

First, the current legislative framework is unsatisfactory. It is complex and confusing. Seventeen regulations or Acts of Parliament have to be applied, and local authorities do not find the regime easy to operate. Secondly, the current framework is regarded as inherently inefficient because it depends on tenants lodging complaints or on local authorities identifying problems. There is no obligation on the landlords of such properties o obtain licences, which would enable the public authorities, in the process of licensing, to discover whether or no risks existed.

The Government recognised in the consultation paper that such a case pertains. Paragraph 19 sets out the arguments for licensing. Ministers may be worried that I shall be partial; I assure them that I shall shortly deal with the other side of the coin—the arguments against licensing. I shall be utterly even-handed, but the Committee should be aware of what the Government said in their 1994 consultation paper. The Government's arguments for licensing were, first that it would

"secure high standards throughout the licensed HMO sector. As with current HMO powers there would be an emphasis on the more consistent enforcement of fire safety standards, both the provision of the basic amenities to ensure minimum health and hygiene standards would also be included".

Secondly, licensing would

"prevent the undesirable proliferation of hostels catering primarily for benefit recipients in resort areas"—

something of widespread concern, but particularly important to hon. Members who represent such areas. Thirdly, licensing would

"focus any regulatory regime on those areas and types of housing with the worst problems, and hence make possible the removal of planning controls which apply uniformly nationally".

Fourthly, it would

"clarify the regulatory regime so that landlords and tenants know as far as possible which properties need to be licensed and what standards are acceptable".

Finally, it would

"ensure a more consistent approach to the HMO sector, which recognises the value of HMOs in meeting a particular housing need and which is reflected in local housing and planning strategies."

That was the Government's case for licensing HMOs.

In paragraph 22, the Government set out the arguments against licensing HMOs. Against I shall quote in full, and not selectively. Sir Richard Scott need not come to this Committee, as the quotations will be absolutely straight and comprehensive. The first argument against licensing is that JPEG 199

"it would add little to the powers already available to local authorities. These can be exercised flexibly and tackle those properties which represent the greatest risk. Licensing would reduce this degree of flexibility by requiring local authorities to deal with all HMOs, so making it more difficult to concentrate on those properties most at risk".

The second argument against licensing is that,

"as with current registration schemes it would create excessive bureaucracy which would have to be paid for. Only the better landlords would agree to register and the remaining landlords would, as now, refuse to come forward. There would therefore be no significant improvement in the condition of standards of HMO properties as a direct result of licensing".

Thirdly, licensing

"would be introducing to high a degree of regulation into a market where both landlord and tenant are willing to agree on a reasonable standard of accommodation, particularly in relation to matters not directly affecting fire safety. Raising standards to an unrealistically high level in all cases would mean that either the landlord would withdraw accommodation from the market or the tenants would be unable to afford the higher rents that higher standards could imply."

Those were the arguments against a national licensing scheme set out by the Government in their consultation paper. Let us consider those arguments carefully. The first was that licensing is not necessary as

"it would add little too the powers already available to local authorities".

The Government do not believe that. If they did, they would not be introducing the changes proposed in part II of the Bill. By proposing measures to toughen the registration regime, the Government have conceded that the existing framework is not satisfactory and that there is a need to change existing powers.

As to flexibility to deal with the worst cases, everyone agrees that whichever system is operated, be it licensing or registration, it is essential for the body responsible—the local authority—to operate on the principle of first priority being given to the properties in the worst condition, usually on the basis of a risk assessment strategy. Concentrating on the properties in the worst condition will be common to whichever approach is adopted. That part of the argument is no case against a licensing scheme, and the Bill disproves the argument that licensing would add little to local authorities' existing powers.

I turn to the argument that licensing would create excessive bureaucracy. If that it the case, why are the Government taking steps to introduce a stronger registration scheme? Again, the Bill disproves that argument. If the bureaucracy is common to registration and licensing schemes, as is stated in the consultation paper, there is no justification—if the arguments are valid—for part II of the Bill. As far as I know, the Government do not intend to withdraw part II, although they have already surprised us by withdrawing 11 clauses. They accept that part II involves bureaucracy, so they cannot use that as an argument against a licensing scheme.

The argument that only the better landlords would agree to register and that the worst would refuse to come forward so that there would be no significant improvement in standards does not wash. The Minister obviously does not believe that. On Second Reading he clearly and unequivocally argued for an effective scheme that would tackle the worst conditions. He believes that his proposals are the best way to achieve that, and we believe that a licensing scheme is the right 200 way to achieve it, but neither of us doubt that tough and effective action is required to improve standards, which will result from action to tackle the worst problems.

On Second Reading, the Minister said:

"Local authorities must have clear and enforceable rules on safety. They must be able to say 'enough is enough' when it comes to approving new establishments and closing those which are not managed effectively and responsibly. It is stated on the face of the Bill that the registration and control scheme can be refused if the owner is not a fit person to manage the establishments."—[ Official Report, 29 January 1966; Vol. 270, c. 744.]

So clearly the Minister does not believe the argument that a licensing scheme will not tackle the problem—that it will not lead to action on the worst properties in the worst condition and that only the good landlord will come forward—because his registration scheme is designed to tackle the worst problems, and he accepts that there is a case for doing so.
