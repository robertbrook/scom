Mr. Raynsford
: Amendment No. 494 is designed to help to rectify a weakness in the drafting of the Bill. Clause 162 sets out certain factors that a local authority must take into account when deciding whether accommodation is suitable for a homeless family. It states:

"the local housing authority shall have regard to Parts IX, X and XI of the Housing Act 1985"—

which relate to

"slum clearance; overcrowding; houses in multiple occupation".

That is reasonable as far as it goes. Clearly a local authority does not want to put homeless people into accommodation which has been defined as suitable for slum clearance; nor does it want to put people into accommodation which is overcrowded or subject to control orders because of multiple occupation. However, if the Government want to ensure that the local authority takes account of all the relevant duties relating to the condition of property—I assume that they do—they must also have regard to parts VI and XVIII of the Housing Act 1985 because those parts also contain relevant powers relating to the condition of properties. Part VI deals with accommodation which is either unfit or

"in such a state of disrepair that … substantial repairs are necessary".

It is, therefore, analogous to part IX, which relates specifically to slum clearance. It is simply a different approach. Rather than clearing the property under part VI, it is a question of serving repair notices to improve the property. Clearly it would be inappropriate for 832 homeless households to be put into accommodation for which the local authority is either about to or has already served notices to effect repairs.

Similarly, part XVIII contains general powers relating to unfitness and poor conditions. The purpose of amendment No. 494, therefore, is to ensure that the local authority, in taking account of the relevant parts of the 1985 Act, should take account of all the relevant parts of the 1985 Act in relation to the condition of property.

Amendment No. 527 is different, but similarly meritorious, it would require the local authority to take account of the personal circumstances of the applicant and other people who might reasonably be expected to reside with the applicant.

Like my hon. Friend the Member for Greenwich, I want to be helpful to the Minister, and I hope that I shall receive an equally helpful response.

It is pretty obvious that it is not suitable for a person or family to reside in accommodation that is not affordable by that individual or family. That obvious point should be written into the Bill. Local authorities should not be permitted to fulfil their duties under, for example, clause 155, by referring individuals to properties that they cannot afford. It would be most unfair and 834 improper if people who were homeless and in desperate circumstances were sent off to look at properties that were much more expensive than they could afford from the income allowed to them.

So when we talk about suitable accommodation in the private sector, it is relevant and correct that reference should be made to affordability.

There is case law on the subject of what is suitable accommodation, which refers to its affordability. In the case of R v. Tower Hamlets LBC ex parte Kaur in 1994, the court held that accommodation was not suitable unless it was at a rent that the applicant could afford either from his own resources or with the benefit of such public assistance as was likely to be available to him. Hence the particular words used in the amendment.

In the Awua judgment, reference was also made to the fact that other matters, such as whether the occupant could afford the rent, may also be material. So there is case law on the subject, but the case law refers to previous legislation, and case law changes from time to time as the courts take a different view.

So, to be assured that accommodation must be affordable to be considered suitable, it would assist if the wording that we have suggested—or something similar, if the Minister can produce a better draft—appeared in the legislation. Then local authorities would have proper regard to that and especially to the reference to benefits that may be available from public assistance. We must bear in mind the fact that, because of the situation with local reference rents and the recent change in the benefits regime, it is possible that someone entitled to full housing benefit could afford rent but that, if the rent were higher than the local reference rent, there might be a significant gap between the rent payable and the benefit due to an individual, with the result that the property would not be affordable.

I hope the Minister can be equally helpful on this occasion.
