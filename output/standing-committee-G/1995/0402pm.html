Mrs. Diana Maddock (Christchurch): In view of what you have said, Mr. McWilliam, I shall proceed with great speed. Just to recap for members of the Committee, new clauses 85 and 86, to which I was speaking just before lunch, do much the same thing, but deal with different types of tenancy.

New clause 85 seeks to ensure that social landlords who have obtained a suspension order do not enforce that order by means of a warrant for possession without first obtaining the court's leave. That would reverse the decision in the 1987 case of Thompson v. Elmbridge borough council, where it was held that if there was a breach of the terms of a suspended possession order, the secure tenancy would end immediately. What I shall say about these two new clauses also applies to some amendments that I will move later, so that will also speed up our proceedings this afternoon.

Like other members of the Committee, I am not a lawyer and am reliant on legal advice on this matter. I am advised that the procedure in a possession action that relates to secure and assured tenancies is as follows. When a court makes an order for possession against an assured or a secure tenant, the tenancy ends when the tenant is due to give up possession under the order, and not when the tenant is evicted. That was confirmed in the case to which I referred earlier. I believe that the same problems could arise under the Bill's new introductory tenancies.

The two most frequent types of order are, first, possession in 28 days—often called a final order—where the tenancy ends upon the expiry of the 28 days and not when the tenant is evicted. That is normally some months later and often it is not acted on at all. The second is where possession in 28 days is suspended, which might happen because the current rent falls due and although it has been agreed that the tenant will pay off so many pounds a week, he has not done that; or it might be that the condition is that he does not cause a nuisance. The tenancy will not end while the condition of the suspension is kept, but should the tenant be even one day later in payment—which could be because of a delay in receiving housing benefit or illness—the tenancy ends immediately, providing the 28 days have passed. The landlord can then issue a warrant to evict. That leads to various problems.

Such circumstances can last for several years, and it is not infrequent for landlords to issue a warrant five, seven or even 11 years after the order. The problem is that in the meantime, the tenant is not technically a tenant—he is a trespasser. If the landlord does not make repairs, the former tenant cannot obtain an injunction for breach of repair covenant or damages. If it is a former social tenancy, he cannot exercise the right to repair. Moreover, if the former tenant dies, it is difficult for the widow to succeed to the tenancy.

This is a strangely worded new clause and deals with infants' capacity to be tenants of dwelling houses. Hon. Members may have cottoned on to the fact that, as a Liberal Democrat, I care about what happens to young people. The Bill raises several problems in regard to people under 18 and tenancies.

The issue is fairly complicated, but I shall try to explain the issue briefly in view of the time. There are two main reasons for the new clause. First, it is unclear whether a widow of 17 could succeed to a secure tenancy. Section 89 of the Housing Act 1985 says that the tenancy vests in a widow, but section 16 of the Law of Property Act 1925 says that it does not if she is under 18. Section 89 might override section 16, but that is not clear. A significant number of successions take place every year in which the successor, who might be a widow or a member of the family, is under 18. An amendment that makes that clear would prevent a test case from taking the issue through the courts at substantial cost to the public purse.

The problem does not arise in the case of a statutory tenancy under the Rent Act 1977 because such a tenancy is not a legal estate, but a personal right. The same applies to statutory assured tenancies. It should be possible for people to hold a periodic tenancy for their home even though they are under 18. I have moved other amendments that seek to guarantee younger people's rights.

The second reason for the new clause is that substantial numbers of tenancies are granted each year to people aged 16 or 17. They are frequently people who come out of care, but could be students who are renting a room. That brings into effect the provisions of the Settled Land Act 1925. The lease or tenancy is held on trust by the landlord for the infant—someone who is under 18. The landlord does so under a duty to execute a settlement by means of a principal vesting deed and to create a trust instrument in the infant's favour.

That is something of a rigmarole, and, in the case of assured tenancies, the infant will have no security of tenure because the tenant is the landlord. That can cause many difficulties in and out of court. There is no good reason for an infant not to be the tenant in the case of a periodic tenancy or short lease of a dwelling house.

Subsection (2) defines the tenancies that infants can hold. Subsection (3) explains the nonsense that arises from the interpretation of the different Acts, and subsection (4) confirms how they will work together. Subsection (5) sets out exceptions to the new clause.

Secondly, lenders wish to enforce their security only as a last resort. They would therefore have no objection to the principle that the Housing Corporation should have statutory powers to address the problems that will confront an insolvent housing association—that will usually be in the lenders' own interests. However, the use of such powers must be founded on the consent of all the association's secured creditors.

Those are the twin pillars on which the new clauses have been constructed—lenders' desire to make sure that they can protect their lending, but equally their acknowledgement that there is a role to try to mend any difficult situation which has arisen.

Therefore, we have developed an alternative model which replaces the transfer order provisions. The new model addresses lenders' concerns but still gives the corporation a significant statutory role in the handling of an insolvency.

The key features of the proposals are as follows: first, lenders would be required to give two sets of notices to the Housing Corporation, one just before and one immediately after they take steps to enforce their security.

Secondly, the corporation would have a minimum of 28 days after the second notice to develop a proposal that ensures that the landlord's housing remains in the social rented sector. During that period, lenders would not be able to dispose of any of the landlord's housing. Otherwise, there might be nothing left by the time that the corporation had worked up its proposal. All the landlord's secured creditors must agree to the proposal; if they do not, the corporation bows out and insolvency proceeds without further restraint. If all creditors agree to the proposal, they and the corporation make arrangements to implement it. In other words, the Housing Corporation can act only if mandated to do so by all the secured creditors.

Thirdly, the corporation would have powers to appoint a manager under the proposal and to give assistance. Financial assistance would require consent from the Secretary of State. The manager would be equipped with whatever powers were necessary to implement the agreed proposals and the Housing Corporation would spell them out in a statutory order.

Members for Skipton and Ripon (Mr. Curry), for Greenwich, for Christchurch and the right hon. Member for City of London and Westminster, South, because without the co-operation of hon. Mind you, I have been called some strange things in Committee because of the inadvertent use of the second person, but being described as half pregnant by the Minister was the best I have heard. I thank all of you very much.

Bill, as amended, to be reported.
