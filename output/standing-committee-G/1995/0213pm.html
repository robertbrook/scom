The Renton Committee laid out clearly the principles for legislation—that the principles of a measure should be within the Act of Parliament and that only minor variations from it should be in regulations. Therefore, Parliament should have control over it and it should be subject to an affirmative resolution of the House. If such an order is subject to affirmative resolution of the House, it comes into operation on a specific day, after approval by the House. As soon as one wanders into areas of negative resolution—apart from the fact that considerable power has been withdrawn from the House, as the measure must be prayed against—confusion arises about when the order comes into force.

Let us assume that the Government laid the relevant order in June. A prayer against it might follow, but because of the way in which the House works in the approach to a recess, it would be unlikely to deal with it in June. The 40-day period would run until the start of the recess and be carried over to October, so no difficulty would arise over holding the debate and it is just possible, although not likely, that the Government would be defeated.
