I shall now deal with some problems that might arise from the amendment. The subject of double jeopardy has been raised. I do not regard that as an issue because people often receive two punishments for committing one offence. For example, an accountant who defaults his company will probably end up in gaol and, at the same time, lose his job. No one argues that that should not happen if someone commits a serious fraud.

Under the grounds for possession in schedule 2 of the Housing Act 1985, if a person smashes up his home and the homes of his neighbours, he loses his home because he is in breach of the tenancy conditions. That person can also be prosecuted for criminal damage. To protect people who live in the neighbourhood, people who commit such offences should be made to cease occupying their property. We should accept that. We should regard it not as an arbitrary and wilful double punishment but as action taken for proper reasons to protect people in the neighbourhood.
