Col. 606 The three categories do not fit comfortably with the concept of the single standard housing register that we debated this morning. Young children leaving care require access to accommodation when they are discharged from care if they would otherwise become homeless. There is no point in their being told that they can leave care and live on the streets in a cardboard box while their applications are being considered on a waiting list. That would be ridiculous. They need to be able to move directly from care into appropriate permanent accommodation.

At present, a number of local authorities provide specific quotas of secure tenancies to social services departments precisely for such purposes. That is sensible. Young people in care have often experienced much disruption in their lives. If they are fortunate, that may have involved only a move from an unsatisfactory home, or from a home where there has been a tragedy or a disaster, to a specific period in care. However, in many cases such people will have been subjected to a whole series of disruptive moves in their lives and what they require is the certainty of being able to move into permanent accommodation. When good after-care services are available, they must be backed up by secure accommodation so that the young person can settle down and begin to establish an independent life.

For all those reasons, access to permanent low-cost social housing is important for such young people and it is crucial that such provision should continue to be made. In 1993, 6,360 young people aged over 16 ceased to be subject to a care order. In a sense, that is the maximum number of people for whom such provision may be required. Some could secure accommodation through other routes, but a high proportion of them would be dependent on accommodation being secured through the local authority. The number of such people is not large compared with the total number of tenancies nationally, but it is crucial for them that provision is made available. They should have the opportunity of immediate access and not have to go through a period of notionally waiting on a waiting list.

The second category comprises people who are seeking a move under a mobility scheme. Such schemes are important for various reasons. They give people the opportunity to move from one area to another for jobs-related reasons, for social reasons or because they want to move from an area where they have suffered violence or unhappiness and return to another part of the country where they may have friends and relatives who can give them support. In 1990 a national housing mobility scheme—HOMES—was set up to help people find a way through the obviously complicated process of getting an application considered by another authority. Where such agreements apply, it is important that they operate on a basis of understanding between different authorities which will contribute to such a scheme only if they believe that they will get something out of it. Clearly, such a scheme would be entirely disrupted if any allocation under the HOMES process had to pass a separate set of hurdles—the authority's own housing register— Image Col. 607 before it could be considered under the housing mobility scheme, HOMES. For those reasons, we believe that the needs of people seeking mobility nominations should also be excluded.

The third category includes cases in which there are nomination rights to move people from supported accommodation, which is similar to the first example that I described—children leaving care—where there is a need for rehousing directly when the social services authority considers that that is appropriate to ensure that those involved receive continuing care and support. Groups affected would include elderly people, disabled people, people with mental health problems and people with learning disability. When such people need to be rehoused, they must be given appropriate accommodation which is available immediately, not after a period on a waiting list, where they can receive help and support from social services. There are strong, practical reasons for excluding all three categories. I hope that the Minister will accept our case and agree that they should be excluded from the principal arrangements of clause 123.
