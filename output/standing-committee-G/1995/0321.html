Mr. Raynsford: I will, of course, explain the amendments in immense detail, but I shall try to do briefly. Clause 148 defines priority need. It might help the Committee if I give a brief background to how the concept of priority need came into existence. It was first put forward in a circular that was issued in the early months of 1974 and which had bipartisan support. The circular was issued under the then Conservative Government, but was supported fully by the incoming Labour Government. The circular set out broad views on how local authorities should discharge their responsibilities towards the homeless. It advocated the need to take a broad view of responsibility, but it accepted that, in areas where there was a shortage of accommodation, first call must go to the most vulnerable, who were defined as those in the priority need categories.

Those priority need categories were then picked up in the Housing (Homeless Persons) Act 1977. The code of guidance issued in 1977 that accompanied that legislation amplified further how local authorities should discharge their responsibilities towards those in the priority need categories. Nineteen years has elapsed since then; the code has undergone a series of revisions and circumstances have changed. Some new needs have Col. 756 emerged that were not anticipated in 1977, so it is appropriate to review the definitions and the guidance.

The purpose of this group of amendments is to highlight weaknesses in current definitions, identify groups of people who might be considered to be in priority need and, above all, focus the Government's attention on the importance of reviewing the categories to which priority is accorded. We do not expect the amendments to be accepted immediately. Indeed, we would be surprised if they were. However, we hope that the Government will heed what we are saying and pay attention to the categories of need that we are identifying. We want to ensure that provisions for the relief of homelessness are developed in a way which accommodates the range of needs that should be given priority today.

Let me describe the various categories. Amendment No. 423 covers people suffering from a visual impairment. It is based on evidence submitted by the Royal National Institute for the Blind, which has highlighted the extent to which people with visual impairments suffer discrimination in obtaining accommodation. They find it more difficult than others to secure accommodation so they should be given, in the RNIB's view, priority when they are homeless.

The original code of guidance that was issued in 1977 specifically highlighted people who were blind as one of the categories that should be assisted. It referred to people suffering mental illness, handicap or physical disability and stated:

"This includes those who are blind, deaf, dumb or otherwise substantially disabled mentally or physically."

Unfortunately, that clear and precise wording has disappeared from subsequent versions of the code and the RNIB feels—reasonably, I believe—that greater emphasis should be placed on the needs of people with visual impairment.

Amendment No. 448 is about young people aged 16 or 17. The vulnerability of young, single homeless people is not questioned. It is well known that they are, sadly, all too often at risk of exploitation. We hear too many chilling stories of the misfortunes that befall young people who, for whatever reason, find themselves homeless and on the streets. The number of young people sleeping rough increased dramatically during the 1980s; but that is only part of the total. Many more are sleeping on friends' floors, squatting, staying in temporary hostels or bed-and-breakfast accommodation or making other temporary arrangements. They are likely to become homeless—literally homeless—at any time. The majority of them have become homeless through no fault of their own. Many have been discharged from care; others have had to leave the parental home because of domestic conflict—sometimes abuse—or because they were told that when they reached the age of 16 they would have to fend for themselves. It seems right that we should pay more attention to the needs of young single people, who are particularly vulnerable.

Amendment No. 451 seeks to include those who qualify for advice and assistance under section 24(2) of the Children Act 1989 or who are taken to be in need under section 17 of that Act. The amendment deals specifically with young people leaving care. Everyone accepts Image Col. 757 that young people leaving care should be helped to secure good quality accommodation, and not be left to drift between various unsatisfactory and temporary homes. The purpose of the amendment is patently obvious.

Amendment No. 495 is about people who have a housing need assessed under section 46 of the National Health Service and Community Care Act 1990. Such people have a defined need under community care policies. Unfortunately, although one might expect it to be so, such people are not automatically assessed as being vulnerable and in priority need if they become homeless. Sadly, they have to undergo separate tests to establish whether they are vulnerable. Sometimes, if the supporting evidence is poor, applicants may be judged to be not vulnerable, even though a separate arm of the local authority or the social services authority, may have decided that the applicant requires care and support under the community care policy. It is obviously sensible to ensure, whenever possible, that those assessments are made in tandem and that people should not be assessed as in need under one piece of legislation and not under another. The purpose of the amendment is to establish that people who are defined as in need under the community care policy should be treated as vulnerable and in priority need if they are homeless.

The next category is people who are fleeing violence or harassment. I am thinking predominantly about women who have suffered domestic violence, but other circumstances may be covered. In the original code of guidance, the Government of the day took a robust view. They said:

"the Secretaries of State consider that it would be appropriate under this heading for authorities to secure whenever possible that accommodation is available for battered women without children who are at risk of violent pursuit or, if they return home, at risk of further violence".

It is clear that, even in 1977, women, even women without children, who faced the risk of domestic violence should be treated as being in priority need. The problem, as the Minister knows, is that because the priority need categories define families with children as the principle group—of course, there are others—someone who has suffered and fled from domestic violence who does not have a child will not be in the priority need categories: That results in an obvious problem. Those people may be extremely vulnerable and may have suffered traumatic and disturbing experiences. The fact that they do not have a child prevents them in many areas from being considered for rehousing. We believe that their needs should be given greater priority and concern, which is the purpose of the amendment.

Amendment No. 499 covers people who are HIV positive, a group that could not have been foreseen when the legislation was introduced in 1977. People who are HIV positive frequently suffer discrimination in the allocation of housing—if it is known that they are HIV positive, of course. They are also subject to fluctuations in health that cannot easily be anticipated. When the initial assessment is made, they might appear not to have any serious health problems, but their health may deteriorate rapidly. We have received some disturbing illustrations of how an authority may refuse accommo- Col. 758 dation to homeless people who are HIV positive on the grounds that they appear to be in reasonable health, only for that health to deteriorate rapidly within a short time, leading to the need for a reassessment by the authority. In such cases, it would surely be better for those people to be afforded priority at an earlier stage.

The final category—I felt that I owed it to the Committee to explain precisely the purposes behind the amendments—is dealt with by amendment No. 496, which concerns people to whom duties are owed under section 117 of the Mental Health Act 1983. Health and social service authorities are under duty to provide aftercare services for people who have been detained for hospital treatment and are subsequently discharged. In such cases, it is important that those people receive suitable housing. We believe that the amendment is in line with the broad thinking of the original legislation, which specifically referred to people who suffered from mental illness. The amendment would give additional clarity to the responsibilities, and would ensure that people discharged from hospital were not left in the lurch without adequate accommodation.

As I mentioned, we do not expect all the amendments to be accepted—I should be surprised if they were—but they each deal with a category of need, and we expect the Government to take them seriously. I hope that the Minister will show at least a willingness to ensure that appropriate guidance is issued to highlight local authorities' responsibilities and to ensure that a wide and sympathetic approach is adopted to all those categories of people with special needs.
