This is another technical amendment, but a slightly more complex one. I hope that the Committee will bear with me as I try to outline in detail the purposes of the amendment.

Until 1988, when the current financial arrangements for the funding of housing associations were introduced, the housing association grants were calculated in a way that would meet most of the capital costs of a scheme. The calculation worked out what loan was required and what proportion of that loan could be serviced out of the rental income. The capital housing association grant met the rest of the costs of the scheme. That was a first-year calculation and in some ways it was unsatisfactory because, as rents rose with inflation, the return that the association was getting from its rents increased whereas loan repayment charges remained constant and schemes began to show an increasing surplus year on year. Against that background, the Government established a system known as the grant redemption fund to claw back future surpluses arising from those publicly-funded arrangements.

JPEG 157 In the Housing Act 1988, the same arrangements were re-enacted with a change of name from the grant redemption fund to the rent surplus fund. Section 55 of the 1988 Act provides that those arrangements will apply to schemes funded by housing association grant, but the 1988 Act brought in a different approach. Associations were expected to borrow privately, possibly on low start or index-linked terms, with rising loan repayments, therefore, often matching the rising rental income.

Moreover, associations had to make future provision for major repairs out of scheme income whereas in earlier schemes they could return to the Housing Corporation for more housing association grant to cover those costs. Grant rates were set at progressively lower rates, from an average of about 75 per cent, of scheme costs in the early days of the 1988 Act regime to a current average of 58 per cent. As grant rates reduced, rents inevitably had to bear an increasing proportion of the costs.

The result of the new equation was that associations were not expected to make significant surpluses on the schemes developed after 1988 in the same way that they were able to do on schemes developed between 1974 and 1988. Thus, the rent surplus fund rules determined by the Housing Corporation have always specifically excluded such schemes.

The purposes of the amendment, which is designed to amend section 55 of the 1988 Act, is to recognise that reality. There is no purpose in leaving on the statute book a provision dealing with rent surpluses which applies equally to schemes developed under the 1988 Act as to previous ones. That reference is dropped. Equally, there would be no purpose in introducing reference to schemes funded under the new social housing grant provisions under clause 17 of the Bill. The bidding for grant under the Bill will be at least as competitive as the bidding for housing association grant in the past and the principles of no material future surpluses remain as before.

This is a sensible amendment to reflect the changing circumstances, to ensure that there is a consistent approach to the recovery of surpluses and to avoid anomalies being created. I hope that the Minister, in the same spirit that he has adopted towards previous amendments, will accept the amendment. We might expect a change in wording which, I grant him, is far more complex in this than in previous cases. There is far more scope for error on our part, but I hope that at least the spirit of the amendment will be accepted.
