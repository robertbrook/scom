I accept that there is an argument that succession should not be allowed to continue because of the potential for under-occupation of the property. The Minister may advance that argument. However, under-occupation can occur on the first succession—it is not a problem confined to a second or subsequent succession. Often, two people live in a house, one of them dies, and there is an argument about whether the person who remains should succeed. I must raise the peculiar circumstances that can arise with second successions, which the individuals concerned do not understand. It is impossible to explain to them how the law operates. When that is the case, we should question whether we have good law that we should support. In three cases of which I have had experience, neither the law as it stands nor the Bill's provisions are acceptable.

The first is a fairly traditional case, of which many still arise, in which the man in a man-and-wife partnership is the sole tenant. In the 1930s, for example, it was commonplace for people to gain the tenancy of a property together, but for only the man to become a tenant. Although local authorities have since encouraged joint tenancies, that situation remains the case for some. Let us consider the position of a man and wife, with the man as sole tenant, and a child—now an adult in his 40s or 50s—living in the same house. If the wife dies first, followed by the man, there is no problem. The child—now an adult—who has lived in the house all his life will succeed to that tenancy. That is a fairly straightforward case, and the elderly person need not worry about what will happen to his son or daughter when he dies.

When the wife dies, there is no more succession to the child of the relationship, after spending all his life in the property. He has no right to succeed in those circumstances.

That is inexplicable and cannot be regarded as reasonable and proper. We cannot relieve the fears of elderly people that when they die their home will be removed from their child. Even if the Minister cannot accept my amendments this afternoon, I hope that he will agree to do something to address that problem.
