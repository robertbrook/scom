Public Interest Disclosure Bill

10.30 am

The Minister for Competition and Consumer Affairs (Mr. John M. Taylor)
: On a point of order, Mr. Mills. I am aware that there has been doubt or uncertainty expressed about my praying in aid the Pepper v. Hart judgment last week. I would like to take this opportunity to explain my position. Indeed, it is one of the outcomes of that judgment—although it has surely been good practice for much longer—that Ministers should correct or clarify at the earliest possible moment any incorrect or ambiguous statement they may have made inadvertently during a debate in consideration of a Bill. The Minister, for this purpose, is your humble servant, Mr. Mills.

I should say at the outset that the references that I made were based on my own personal knowledge and understanding of that case and not on advice or guidance that I had been given. It was far from my intention to mislead or confuse and I would therefore wish to clarify my position. I believe that I can. I have since taken advice and looked again at the case.

The facts of Pepper v. Hart may briefly be stated. It was a tax case heard by the House of Lords Appellate Committee in June 1992 and concerned the liability of schoolteachers to tax in respect of preferential school fees. I realise that that has nothing to do with whistleblowing, but it has to do with what Ministers say in the progress of legislation.

During the case, the question was raised of interpreting the relevant statute. Until the Pepper v. Hart judgment, the rule of the courts was that references should not be made to debates in Parliament when construing legislation. In any case of ambiguity, it was for the courts, applying a longstanding set of interpretative rules worked out by themselves, to reach a decision on how the legislation was to be interpreted, without consideration of the intentions of Parliament. If that interpretation was not in accordance with the wishes of Parliament, Parliament could amend the legislation clearly to reflect those intentions—but in the meantime, the court's interpretation would apply.

There was support for that rule on both constitutional and practical grounds. My understanding is that the Pepper v. Hart judgment will not change the approach of the courts to interpretation in the large majority of cases where the letter of the law is clear. I am glad to say that, in most cases, there will be no need at all for lawyers to spend long hours rifling through copies of Hansard to discover what was said when legislation was first introduced.

2 The judgment has given judges a new interpretative tool where the wording of the statute is unclear or ambiguous. Inevitably, that is bound to happen from time to time. I have to say that it would, of course, be preferable to rule out all obscurity from all our statutes. I have great sympathy with the Plain English Campaign and I am aware that our legislative draftsmen and women are making strides in that direction and taking pains to ensure that Acts of Parliament are as clear, precise and easy to understand as is possible.

It has been a constant theme of my remarks on this Bill that it is not clear how it may be interpreted. Indeed, the way in which the Bill is structured to depend on the position at common law has the effect that its meaning depends to a large extent on difficult case law. I have to say, however, that it would be difficult—if not impossible—to produce a statute on this subject that was entirely without doubt as to its interpretation. That is not the least reason why I have wished to put on record the Government's intentions in offering amendments.

Whether I was right in my understanding of the Pepper v. Hart judgment raises an interesting point. There is no doubt that the critical passage in the judgment of the House of Lords in Pepper v. Hart lies in the following words of Lord Browne-Wilkinson, with which six out of seven of their Lordships were in agreement:

"In my judgment…reference to parliamentary material should be permitted as an aid to the construction of legislation which is ambiguous or obscure or the literal meaning of which leads to an absurdity. Even in such cases references in court to parliamentary material should only be permitted where such material clearly discloses the mischief aimed at or the legislative intention lying behind the ambiguous or obscure words. In the case of statements made in Parliament, as at present advised I cannot foresee that any statement other than the statement of the Minister or other promoter of the Bill is likely to meet these criteria."

I therefore accept that Lord Browne-Wilkinson was thinking in terms of statements made by the Minister or, if he is not the promoter of the Bill, by its other promoter—in this case, the hon. Member for Islwyn (Mr. Touhig).

Lord Browne-Wilkinson was supported in what he said by Lord Bridge, who said:

"It should in my opinion only be in the rare cases where the very issue of interpretation which the courts are called on to resolve has been addressed in Parliamentary debate and where the promoter of the legislation has made a clear statement directed to that very issue, that reference to Hansard should be permitted."

It follows that the courts will look to the statements of the hon. Member for Islwyn for enlightenment as to the intended meaning behind any ambiguities in the Bill, and not directly, if at all, to my statements. That raises an interesting point, because the hon. Gentleman accepted many of my amendments without making any comment on them. That was so, in deed, in the case of amendment No. 1, on which I first raised the issue of Pepper v. Hart. I take it that as the hon Gentleman said nothing on that amendment and the Committee accepted it, the hon. Gentleman and the Committee must have accepted it for the reasons that I gave.

I shall not delay the Committee for long. I shall not repeat my earlier comments, but I shall remind the Committee of the Government's reasons for rejecting the clause.

First, the provision extends beyond the protection of whistleblowers and would affect a range of matters that did not involve victimisation. Secondly, attempting to put a matter of common law into statute would create anomalies. It would treat a public body differently from a private body. There is no justification for such different treatment. It could lead to a lowering of standards and, perhaps, compromise national security. Staff in public bodies may owe a particular duty of competence because of the nature of their work. Special procedures enable those staff to raise anxieties.

Whistleblowing has been a live issue in the national health service, which is a pertinent example of a public service in which good internal guidance has been provided. The NHS executive issued guidance to staff on relations with the public and the media in June 1993 after extensive consultation. About 3,000 individual comments were considered before the guidance was finalised. A copy of it was placed in the Library of this House and in that of another place. It recommends a framework that all reasonable people would regard as sensible. It balances the rights of staff with the obligations of the employer. Local employers can 6 develop local procedures to handle staff concerns within the framework. It complements the relevant NHS professional ethical guidelines and codes of practice.

National health service staff have a right and a duty to raise with their employers anxieties about health service issues. Under no circumstances are employees who express their concerns in accordance with the guidance to be penalised. The guidance stresses that staff concerns should be taken seriously by managers and receive full and sympathetic consideration. It makes plain the basis on which NHS staff should raise issues of concern about the health service. It recognises the major principles of patient confidentiality and the duty of confidentiality and fidelity to the employer.

NHS employers are required to introduce procedures for handling staff concerns in accordance with the guidance after full consultation with staff and local staff representatives. The responsibility for introducing local procedures lies with the NHS employer. It is also for each employer to ensure that the board and all managers and staff are fully aware of the guidance.

Amendments Nos. 19 and 21 deal with an undesirable state of affairs. An individual would be at liberty to act as if no such terms had been included. That would have no effect on the individual's ability to enforce the remainder of the terms of the settlement against the employer. The overall effect of amendments Nos. 19 and 21 would, therefore, be clearly in line with the Bill's intention. Clause 8
PROTECTION OF SOURCES

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. John M. Taylor
: It is not my intention, the Committee may be pleased to note, to detain it for eternity, but I have a few brief words to say about clause 8.

It may be helpful if I set out the existing law on the disclosure of sources before I explain why clause 8 should not stand part of the Bill. Section 10 of the Contempt of Court Act 1981 provides protection for journalists and others from the disclosure of their sources. Like much legislation on this subject, where there are conflicting rights and freedoms, it performs a balancing exercise. The basic principle is that it is not to be a contempt of court for a person to refuse to disclose the source of information in a publication.

12 There are exceptions. A person may be required to disclose his source if the court considers it necessary in the interests of justice or national security or for the prevention of disorder or crime, all of which can be said to be examples of the public interest.

Clause 8 requires the court to form a view on whether publication of information is in the public interest. If it decides that it is, disclosure of the source of information is not to be regarded as required in the interests of justice. I accept that there may be some cases in which that will be so, but I do not accept that it will necessarily be the case. It would be far better to leave it to the courts to decide according to the facts of each case.

Furthermore, it may well be that in order to decide whether publication is in the public interest the court will need to know the source of the information. The second part of the clause would prevent the court from deciding whether the clause applies at all. The clause is thus illogical. Section 10 of the 1981 Act, by contrast, leaves the questions of disclosure of sources and the various categories of public interest to be considered together.

In my opinion, and that of the Government, the clause is not necessary to achieve the main purpose of the Bill. It is expressed in unacceptably absolute terms. Section 10 of the 1981 Act provides the right balance and does what is needed. I urge the Committee to agree that the clause should not stand part of the Bill.
