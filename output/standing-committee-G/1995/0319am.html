Clause 136
HOMELESSNESS AND THREATENED HOMELESSNESS

Mr. Nick Raynsford
(Greenwich)
: I beg to move amendment No. 472, in page 72, line 20, at beginning insert—

'(1) This Part has effect for the purposes of preventing homelessness among persons threatened with homelessness and securing accommodation for homeless persons.'.

I am delighted to say that I am not entirely confused and that I shall focus my attention on the amendment that relates to clause 136 with which we begin our scrutiny of part VII of the Bill on homelessness.

Homelessness is, by almost any definition, the most extreme form of housing need. It is an affront to any society that aspires to be civilised and humane. It is, therefore, a matter of profound concern that so many people are homeless in Britain today. The official figure that was published recently showed that 120,000 households were officially accepted as homeless in England in 1995. Many more—mainly single and childless households—were homeless, but were not recorded as such. It is one of the most sobering comments on the inadequacy of the official figures that the majority of those people who in the minds of the public are the most obvious symbol of homelessness—those sleeping rough on the streets—do not feature in the official statistics. The reason why is that they are generally single and childless and would not qualify for accommodation from a local authority if they approached it, or they do not choose to do so. The official figure of 120,000 is very much the tip of the iceberg.

It is also a matter of profound regret that the number of homeless people has more than doubled during the lifetime of this Government. I accept that the figure of 120,000 is not the highest that it has ever been. The peal was reached in 1991, when 145,000 households were officially accepted as homeless. Mercifully, since then the number has reduced a little. However, the present 684 figure of 120,000 is more than double the level in 1979 when 55,000 households were officially accepted by local authorities as homeless. The current figure is at roughly the same level that it was in 1989 and I shall return to the significance of that later.

At the same time as there has been such an alarming increase in the number of homeless people during the lifetime of the Government, the output of new homes for renting has plummeted. Over the past five years on average only 28,000 new homes have been started for renting by social landlords in Britain, the overwhelming majority by housing associations. That figure is the lowest for any five-year period since the end of the second world war. It compares with more than 120,000 starts a year during the five years of the last Labour Government. That is a stark reminder of how far the production of new rented housing to meet needs has collapsed under this Government.

Any fair-minded observer looking at those simple statistics would conclude that the problem is based on supply. We are not building enough homes to meet the needs of people who require rented accommodation. That analysis was confirmed by the recent report of the Select Committee on the Environment, three of whose members are members of this Committee. It demonstrated that the scale of need is greater than is currently assumed to be the case by the Government and that it would be prudent for the output of housing to be increased substantially. Again, few fair-minded people could fail to agree with that analysis. The Government's priority should be to get more houses built for people in need.

Perversely, the Government have no plans to expand output. They have damaged the programmes of housing associations by making serious cuts in the Housing Corporations's approved development programme in the previous two Budgets. To all intents and purposes, they have stopped local authorities from building new houses. The Government have brought the output of new social housing to its lowest level since the second world war. They now propose to tackle the problem not by increasing the output of housing but by turning instead on the victims of the crisis that they have created—the homeless.

The Government are trying in part VII of the Bill to rearrange the order of priorities for the allocation of housing to take away the opportunity of homeless people to be housed directly. They are doing that—quite cynically—to conceal their culpability for the inadequate output of rented homes. A fair-minded observer would say that the solution was not to attack the demand side, which is what the Bill does, but to increase the supply of housing. There is an acute shortage, with an inadequate supply of housing, yet the Government propose to attack the victims of that shortage rather than to ensure that more homes are built.

The Government, of course, are somewhat uncomfortable about what they are doing. The more we hear from the Minister, the more we realise just how uncomfortable they are. In today's Financial Times, the Minister is reported as saying that the measures in the Bill are a necessary adjustment to legislation and that they form part of a larger package aimed at securing greater fairness in allocating tenancies. Those wonderfully warm words come from a Minister who wants to continue receiving the plaudits of the Opposition and others for being a reasonable and amenable person, but who is bringing forward measures that would demolish the JPEG 685 framework established by the House 19 years ago to meet the needs of the homeless—measures seen by many to have been a success.

There is no justification for what the Government are doing. There is no evidence that existing legislation is not working. On the contrary, as we highlighted in previous debates, two reviews of the legislation have already been undertaken. The first was conducted under the Deputy Prime Minister, the right hon. Member for Henley (Mr. Heseltine), when he was Secretary of State for the Environment in the early 1980s; the second was conducted in the late 1980s by the late Lord Ridley and the Governor of Hong Kong, Mr. Patten, when they were Secretaries of State for the Environment. The latter review was completed in 1989. It concluded that the legislation was working reasonably well and that there was no need for change. An objective comparison of the situation then and now would show an extraordinary similarity. The number of people accepted as being homeless in 1989 is almost the same as it is today—about 120,000.

What changes have there been to justify the Minister's argument that the measures are a necessary adjustment to the legislation? What has happened to make those adjustments necessary? I look forward to hearing the Minister's justification. Any fair-minded, objective observer would say that the only difference is that the supply of housing has reduced even more since 1989 and that people in need of housing have even less prospect of being housed. That is the problem. The Government's dilemma is that their failure to build enough homes to provide an adequate supply has created an even more acute problem, and they are trying to solve that problem not by tackling the real cause but by taking away from the victims of those policies their entitlement to housing.

One defence that the Minister might use is that a court judgment has brought into doubt the validity of the existing legislative framework—the Housing (Homeless Persons) Act 1977, which is now incorporated in part III of the Housing Act 1985. The Minister might argue that the judgment in the case of the Awua family last summer raised doubts about whether local authorities are under a duty to secure permanent accommodation but, if that is his view, there is a simple remedy, which has already been adopted.

Some years ago, a similar situation arose in the Pulhoffer case, and doubts were raised about the extent of duties. The correct response—prompted in another place, but accepted by the Government—was to say that it was necessary to amend legislation to clarify Parliament's original intention, which was that local authorities should secure permanent housing for people who were deemed to be not intentionally homeless and in priority need under the homelessness provisions.

That is what the Government should do now. There should be a brief amendment—probably one clause only—to restore the pre-Awua position. We would support the Government in that, as it would be the correct response to questions concerning the statutory definition of local authority responsibilities. The Government are not doing that, however. They are making fundamental changes, putting the clock back to the era when local authority responsibilities were to secure not permanent housing for homeless people but only temporary accommodation.

686 In previous sittings, we have explored the provisions under the National Assistance Act 1948—the requirements, which are not clearly defined, on social services to secure temporary accommodation for persons in urgent need. The failure of that legislation in the 1960s and 1970s had appalling human consequences. Homeless families were left on the streets, shunted between areas and treated in ways that no civilised society could possibly contemplate. Families were split; mothers were received into former workhouse institutions and fathers were told to go away. Such conditions persisted until the 1970s, but they were ended by the 1977 Act, which rightly established a framework whereby homeless people were entitled to permanent accommodation if they satisfied the conditions of the Act, and local authorities were required to secure that permanent accommodation.

Everyone who has examined the matter agrees that that is the correct response. Homeless people do not benefit from being placed in temporary accommodation. All the objective studies—the Minister's Department has conducted studies over many years—demonstrate the obvious advantage to families, children's education and to society as a whole of rehousing homeless families in permanent rather than temporary accommodation, which is generally more expensive, often sub-standard and disruptive to family life.

There is no objective good reason for placing homeless families in temporary accommodation, but the miserable piece of legislation that we are debating will take us back to an era when the local authority's duty was not to secure permanent accommodation but when the emphasis was on a temporary solution.

The Government are acutely embarrassed about that. They know that it is wrong and inappropriate. The Minister—who wants to be seen as fair minded—is desperately trying to recover from the impossible inheritance that he has been given, he offers concessions. Last week, we were told that the period for which local authorities would have the duty to accommodate people would be extended from one year to two years. The Minister justified that by saying that figures show that, on average, people remain in temporary accommodation for about 13 months, so that the concession would cover the vast majority of families who would be rehoused before the end of the two-year period.

That is all very well—the Minister is trying to appear reasonable and we welcome the climbdown—but it does not deal with the fundamental issue. A local authority's duties are still being weakened. It has a duty to secure permanent accommodation. That is the right response, rather than the unsatisfactory duty to secure temporary accommodation, albeit for two years.

At first the Government said that that would be unacceptable, but then the Minister began to realise that the Bill would not work in its original form, so concessions had to be made. One of the concessions given was that those on the waiting list who had sufficiently high priority would be eligible to be rehoused directly.

JPEG 687 We then had the even more interesting concession that those on the waiting list with nearly enough priority would also be eligible to be rehoused directly. Despite pressing the Minister, he could not explain how that would work in practice, nor could he explain what would happen to a person on the waiting list with higher priority than someone who had been rehoused because he was homeless or on the waiting list with nearly but not quite enough points. Inevitably that will happen. There will be homeless applicants with slightly fewer points than others on the waiting list. What if someone remaining on the waiting list challenges the local authority's decision to allocate? We have had no answer to that.

There are lawyers on the Government Front Bench and it cannot be beyond the imagination of a clever lawyer to see good scope for a challenge to a local authority's right to rehouse someone in those circumstances. The Government's policy is about the single route into housing, to use their words. What if a local authority is explicitly encouraged by the Minister to rehouse people with slightly lower priority ahead of those with higher priority on the waiting list? That is nonsense and the Minister and his hon. Friends know it. It is the product of embarrassment and the result of a Minister desperately trying to claw himself back from the impossible position he has been put into by the Secretary of State and his predecessors. He had introduced a set of proposals that are fundamentally unsound and which are unworkable in practice.

We had other examples of people who were not on the waiting list and who would not qualify for direct rehousing if they were homeless, such as those who never thought that they would need to be rehoused because their housing was perfectly satisfactory but who suddenly, because of a natural disaster or a terrorist outrage, find that their home is destroyed. Are we seriously expected to believe that such people should not be rehoused directly but should be forced to spend time in temporary accommodation because they were not registered on the waiting list? What nonsense. As my hon. Friend the Member for Denton and Reddish (Mr. Bennett) rightly said, it will give people a perverse incentive to register on the waiting list if they think that there is a possibility of their ever becoming homeless.

What a comment on the Government's bizarre creation. People who become homeless through no fault of their own, in circumstances when any right-minded person would say that direct rehousing must be the right answer, will not qualify, leading to stacks of extra unnecessary applications on waiting lists. Bureaucracy will run riot because that is the only way that people can cover themselves against the possibility of becoming homeless.

We are still waiting for answers on all those matters. I suspect that we shall continue to wait because the Minister knows only too well that they are classic illustrations of the nonsense that the Government are trying to perpetrate. It is a framework that is riddled with anomalies and it will not work in practice. Rather than digging himself into even more untenable positions, the Minister should recognise reality and start the sensible process of trying to restore the good framework that was established in 1977, which has stood the test of time, has provided help to millions of homeless families, and which is recognised by everyone, including local authorities, housing associations, voluntary agencies and churches as a system that works well.

688 Is it not time for the Government to admit that they are totally out of step with public opinion and out of line with with the opinion of all those who know what it is like for people to be homeless, and what is involved in the process of relieving homelessness and of housing allocation? Those who make the present system work and recognise that it is a better system than the one that it replaced do not want us to be pushed back towards the era of temporary accommodation, inadequate safety nets and homelessness leading to people literally on the streets.

The framework established by the 1977 Act had all-party support. It was introduced, as I mentioned last week, by a Liberal Member of Parliament, Stephen Ross, and was supported by members of five political parties, including three Conservative Members of Parliament, two of whom remain Members of this House today and one of whom, the noble Lord Walker, is now in another place. It is a measure of the extent to which today's Conservative party has retreated from social responsibility and lurched to the right that we are presented with these miserable proposals designed to weaken that statutory framework.

We reject entirely what the Government are doing. This part of the Bill is the worst and the most damaging, dangerous and destructive legislation going through Parliament this year. We will fight it all the way. We have tabled a series of amendments designed to limit the damage and ensure that homeless people are still accorded reasonable priority and are still treated humanely. We will press those amendments as hard as we can. We hope that the Minister will go on making concessions, because some concessions are better than none, but his Bill is fundamentally defective. It is not the right way forward; it is riddled with anomalies; it will not ensure fair treatment for homeless people; it will lead to more gross injustices. Most fair-minded people recognise that.

We hope that the Bill will be amended—if not in this House, then in another place, where Members are distinguished by a real care and concern for homeless people, which they have demonstrated over many years, not least by their good sense in introducing the amendments to rectify the problems created by the Pulhoffer judgment. I hope that our colleagues in another place will give the Bill the close scrutiny that it deserves.

It will not be long before the next Labour Government are elected. I assure the Minister that we will regard responsibility towards the homeless as one of our principal priorities. If we inherit an unsatisfactory situation, we will act quickly and purposefully to restore a proper framework based on the sound principles of the Housing (Homeless Persons) Act 1977, which was passed with the support of the then Labour Government and had all-party support, and which, over the intervening 19 years, has provided a framework that works well and has protected millions of homeless people.

It would be nice to think that the Minister would have a rush of common sense and would agree to our amendments, but I fear that he will not. I give him a warning: he will not go down in history with any distinction if his memorial is the undermining of the 1977 Act. It would be a sad memorial for a Minister who has, in many ways, shown himself to be concerned and caring. It is a sad day that he is moving this part of this Bill. I hope that he will have second thoughts.

Mr. Curry

Gentleman wishes, but he knows them as well as I do. 692 He knows perfectly well that first of all—I shall spell this out—a household presents itself as homeless. What then happens? The local authority has an interim duty to accommodate while checking the entitlement. For example, if the household is on the street, that duty will apply. Many people will apply for homeless status from insecure accommodation, which is perhaps about to be repossessed or, shared with a relative.

The local authority then applies successive tests—a person from abroad, homeless, leaseless or threatened with being pushed out from precarious housing. Priority needs such as fire, flood and emergency remain unchanged from the 1977 Act. Another test is whether the homelessness is intentional or involuntary. All homelessness legislation has included some test of voluntariness. If the household passes those tests, the local authority will consider whether suitable alternative accommodation is available—a series of clauses and amendments deal with that later in the Bill. The local authority's duty is to give reasonable advice and assistance in securing accommodation.

What happens when the full duty is to accommodate for two years unless the applicant moves out or is offered permanent accommodation? The sort of property available is suitable private rented sector stock, perhaps accessed through landlord forums giving rent guarantees or fast-tracking housing benefit; housing association stock used on a temporary basis, and local authority temporary stock for up to two years in three. When someone is owed a duty to go straight into permanent housing when the household is already on the waiting list with enough points to get less attractive stock, the local authority has the discretion to promote such a household with nearly enough points.

It is for the local authority to come to conclusions about the precise definition in the light of the chemistry of its own scheme when people are already on an exceptional list. Households with very high needs will be able to go on a list in the morning and be allocated housing in the afternoon, providing the stock is available. That duty ceases after two years. A household can reapply before eviction from a previous property and be reassessed. If the tests are passed, a fresh two-year period of duty starts. If a household is still in priority need and still has no suitable accommodation, the local authority can continue to sponsor accommodation for any period up to two more years. We shall come to the tests for alternative accommodation later.

Those are the routes into housing that the Bill foresees. For a vast number of people, it will be scarcely any different in practice from what it is now.

On any test of reasonableness, it is not acceptable to expect them to continue to occupy such accommodation. That is the purpose of our amendment No. 478.

Amendment No. 479 deals with circumstances in which people are living in accommodation for which the landlord is seeking possession, against which they have no defence. Despite guidance from the Department 718 suggesting that local authorities should be helpful when responding to such circumstances, sadly, some local authorities insist on the people staying in the accommodation until the bitter end, when possession proceedings have gone through the courts and bailiffs have been summoned and executed a warrant.

There are all sorts of potential problems, some of which have been highlighted in briefings from the National Association of Citizens Advice Bureaux. It knows of many distressing cases, one of which involved an elderly couple in Essex. They are in their sixties and were given two months' notice to quit after having lived for a year in an assured shorthold tenancy. They had lived in that assured shorthold tenancy for a period that took them beyond its duration, so they had no defence at all against possession, but despite that, the council refused to accept them as homeless without the matter going through the courts. That upset the couple enormously because they became involved in unnecessarily bad relations with their landlord and were forced to incur significant expense.

There are good grounds for not requiring people who have no defence against a possession order to go through all the processes of the law. That is a bad policy, not only in terms of its impact on the people involved, but for the courts. It increases the load on court business without furthering the cause of justice. At a time when Lord Justice Woolf has undertaken a detailed review of the issue, I should hope that all members of the Committee would regard it as sensible to avoid burdening the courts with unnecessary cases when there is no possibility of an outcome other than possession being granted.

For those reasons, we believe that the amendments are necessary as they would define additional circumstances in which people would not be regarded as expected to continue to live in certain accommodation. The first amendment deals with overcrowding and the second refers to tenants who are subject to possession proceedings but who have no defence against such proceedings.
