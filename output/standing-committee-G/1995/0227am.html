Mr. Clive Betts (Sheffield, Attercliffe): I beg to move amendment No. 253, in page 43, line 40, at end add—

'(3) In section 345 of the Housing Act 1985 (meaning of "house in multiple occupation") insert—


"(3) for the purposes of this section 'single household' shall be taken to mean persons who were living together prior to the occupation of the house and who intend to live together when they cease to occupy the house in question.".'.

Image Col. 323 This is an important amendment. It arises out of the Sheffield city council v. Barnes case, which has affected how a number of local authorities deal with houses in multiple occupation. The Bill amends previous legislation. The subject of houses in multiple occupation forms the basis of all our deliberations. The definitions of HMOs revolves around the definition of a household. We have to go back to section 345 of the Housing Act 1985 to find that a house in multiple occupation is

"a house which is occupied by persons who do not form a single household."

That might seem fairly simple, but what does "single household" mean? No further definition is given in the 1985 Act or in the Bill. Nevertheless, that definition of a house in multiple occupation is the basis for the registration schemes and the exemptions that we have discussed.

In the past, it has been left to the Department of the Environment to advise local authorities. Comprehensive and detailed advice is contained in the Department's guidance notes. Indeed, paragraph 2.2 makes the obvious statement that it may sometime be difficult to tell whether a property is a house in multiple occupation. When considering the Sheffield court case, that is the understatement of the year. It is often exceedingly difficult to determine when a house is in multiple occupation.

Local authorities are asked to have regard to the mode of living test—how a house is occupied by those who live in it, and how they share the household activities. Local authorities have to decide whether the occupants cook together, whether they use the same washing facilities, whether they eat together, and whether cleaning is shared. How that can be established with any degree of certainty is far from clear, as courts have found when asked to decide such matters. Anyone who knows about student households will understand that when one comes to define such activities as cooking, washing and cleaning, household arrangements do not conform to a stable pattern, but fluctuate from time to time.

Like many local authorities, Sheffield city council had assumed, as advised by the Department of the Environment, that those were issues that they should consider when defining HMOs and deciding whether a house should be included in the registration scheme and be subject to the scheme's controls. However, as the officers of Sheffield city council found, the definition is not adequate and has caused enormous problems.

In May 1992, the council served notice on a property that was believed to be a house in multiple occupation. It was occupied by five individuals who led disparate lifestyles—cooking and eating separately, having no common purse, not socialising together, spending most of the time in their own rooms and being unaware of each other's whereabouts. The council believed that it was a house in multiple occupation and took action against the landlord. The landlord contested the claim, and the case ultimately went to appeal and was heard by the Master of the Rolls, Sir Thomas Bingham. He effectively found that, while the mode of living test was one that the local authority should have applied, many other issues were involved, such as origin of tenancy, who took responsibility for the whole house, whether there were Col. 324 locks on doors, who was responsible for filling vacancies, who allocated rooms and the size of the establishment. Mode of living was only one of nine issues to be considered.

That approach was different to that which Sheffield and many other councils had taken. They had thought that mode of living was the key test for shared student households, in particular, when defining whether people were living as a single household. The case was complicated—some male students had previously lived in the property and a group of female students moved in the following year, as frequently happens in student households. In summing up, Sir Thomas Bingham said:

"It is inherent in student life that different students will come and go at different times and that groups will vary in their habits so far as sharing expenses and generally mucking in are concerned, and on the facts of this particular case there is nothing in the way in which the girl students ran the house which leads me to think that they were not one household, all the other indications to my mind showing that they were."

In fact, the girl students led a far more disparate lifestyle and were more separate in their arrangements than the male students who previously occupied the property. The Master of the Rolls said:

"On the facts here it is apparent that the girl students lived rather more independent existences than the boys did. They tended to shop and cook for themselves, to keep their own supplies of food in the kitchen, and to have no very clear plans for a common purse beyond sharing, at least in theory, the costs of electricity."

That was the arrangement that the Master of the Rolls believed existed, but he did not conclude that the house was in multiple occupation. That is a matter of great concern.

The environmental health officers dealing with the case in Sheffield said of the summary:

"It is clear from reading it that the situation at the house in question was a fairly typical student shared house, the kind of which is repeated all over England and Wales in university and college towns and cities. It is also the type typically classified as Category B by the Chartered Institute of Environmental Health's categorisation of HMOs."

The authority knows of 3,000 such houses in Sheffield. The officers continue:

"So it was seen that the mode of living factors carried significant weight in concluding whether a group of tenants formed a household or not."

That was the initial view of Sheffield officers as result of the court case. The officers also said:

"It is important to recognise…what the purpose of HMO legislation was. Clearly the need for it was, and is, as a result of the recognition that certain types of accommodation, because of the way in which it is occupied, provide unacceptable health and safety risks to the occupants."

Multiple cooking and use of facilities, and so on, especially when people in a house are not aware of each other's activities, lead to greater fire risks and the other dangers that we have discussed.

The officers continued that, in the case of Sheffield city council v. Barnes,

"it is seen that the group occupying the house had virtually no such relationship. They were almost totally disparate, and because of this mode of occupancy, it is contended that such a group are indeed within the highest risk category of shared house occupants."

That is important: although such groups are in the highest risk category of shared house occupants, as the law stands because of that judgment they are not regarded as living in houses in multiple occupation, which are subject to registration.

Image Col. 325 I mentioned that Sheffield city council knows of 3,000 such houses in the city. It believes that only between 300 and 500 of them will still fall under the definition of houses in multiple occupation, if the precedent set by the case is allowed to stand. Before anyone thinks that that is special pleading for Sheffield, it is not a problem just for that city. Clearly, the case received publicity in Sheffield, but other council officers are already telling me that landlords are saying, "We will not take any notice of you or comply with any orders. Our houses are not in multiple occupation. The case law stands. Take us to court if you want, because we now think that we can win."

That is a serious problem. Other landlords have picked up on the case and are saying, "We are no longer covered by the law; take us to court." Councils now consider that, in the majority of cases in which they previously believed that houses were in multiple occupation, they would now lose because of the Barnes judgment. The environmental health officers involved in the case in Sheffield concluded:

"In summary, the effects of the ruling are to de-classify from HMO status a vast number of shared houses which pose significant health and safety risks to their occupants."

When Sheffield city council wrote to other local authorities and organisations such as student unions, it received 160 letters of support all over the country. Many organisations have contributed towards the court case because they were so worried by it. Wycombe district council said that there is

"a real need for the law to be clear on the status of shared properties which are occupied by students."

West Somerset district council said:

"We have already been approached by the owner of a House in Multiple Occupation… containing five separate lettings stating that the property is no longer a HMO based on the above judgement."

So the news of the judgment is spreading and authorities are facing real difficulties.

Canterbury city council said:

"The decision of the Court of Appeal seems perverse, to say the least, but the implications are horrendous."

Brighton borough council believes that 2,500 properties in the town will be affected by the judgment.

Perhaps one of the most startling comments was:

"Quite why the Court of Appeal thought it appropriate to condemn the persons in that house and others in similar situations to high levels of personal risk and to conditions which are more akin to Victorian rookeries than to what should be achieved as the 21st century approaches is beyond me."

Those are the words of Brent council—a Conservative council.

He made an interesting, even valiant, attempt to deal with the problem of defining a house in multiple occupation, which, as he rightly said, is the starting point for what we are considering. He was also characteristically modest and sensible in accepting that there may be problems inherent in the amendment that he tabled to raise the matter.

The hon. Gentleman gave a clear and in many ways fair exposition of the present position. I must take issue with him on several points, but he was right to take the matter as the starting point and he was also right in the way in which he considered the definition of HMO, which according to the law is simply a house that is not in single occupation.

The hon. Gentleman referred at some length to the judgment on the Sheffield case. I entirely accept that that is of considerable interest in Sheffield—to students and the local authority—and to local authorities elsewhere. However, he went slightly astray by implying that the judgment was a novel interpretation of the legislative provisions and that it had a novel effect. It is clear from the judgment that the Court of Appeal, under Sir Thomas Bingham, was relying on established case law to consider the problem of what is and what is not a single household.

The Court of Appeal quoted with approval the judgment of Lord Hailsham in the House of Lords in the case of Simmons v. Pizzey in 1979. The starting point of Sir Thomas's analysis was a passage from that judgement and it may assist the Committee if I quote from it. He said:

"The most germane passage is to be found in the speech of Lord Hailsham at page 59F where, having referred to various dictionary definitions and also definitions to be found in a number of authorities, he said: 'I do not find any of these references particularly helpful except to make clear to me what I would have supposed in any case that both the expression "household" and membership of it is a question of fact and degree, there being no certain indicia the presence or absence of any of which is by itself conclusive.'"

The question of what is a single household is the starting point. It is a matter of fact and degree. The hon. Gentleman explained the various considerations which the Court of Appeal applied to the case in Sheffield when it explored the factual circumstances to see whether it was a single household. I shall not reiterate them, but they are contained in the judgement. Those common sense considerations would be understandable to members of Image Col. 327 the public, such as the number of people in the household, the length of time that they occupied the house, how the household came together, the extent of shared facility and the extent of responsibility for the common parts and so on. They are commonsense ways in which to decide whether it was a matter of fact and degree that a house was a single household or a house in multiple occupation.

I submit to the Committee that there are many difficulties when considering individual cases to see whether it was a matter of fact and degree that the house was a single household. But difficulties arise when applying a single test, to incorporate all possible circumstances, to find out whether a household is a single household, which the hon. Member for Attercliffe accepted applied to the amendment. Its underlying sentiment was to deal with matters involving students. However, the amendment would apply that test to every conceivable circumstance. It is the only way in which it would permit a court to determine what is a single household. It would produce some results, which the Committee as well as members of the public might consider to be odd.

A couple could be living together at a parental home and then decide to set up home together—as many couples do. Under the amendment, they would be regarded as people who had not been living together prior to the occupation of their new home. Therefore, apparently they would be living in a house of multiple occupation. That interpretation is clearly arguable under the amendment. The amendment could produce many other results. When considering intention, it is always difficult to know whether people intend to live together. It might be difficult for members of a household to know their future intentions.

The amendment covers future intention. As part of its definition, people who would make up a single household are people who intend to live together when they cease to occupy the house in question. An example comes to mind: I apologise for it being a literary example, but you, Mrs. Winterton, mentioned "Pride and Prejudice" earlier. Let us consider Mr. and Mrs. Bennett who are living in their lovely country home with their many eligible daughters. If asked about their future intentions, I am sure that the daughters would not say that it was their intention to live together as a household unit when they leave their lovely country home. However, if the hon. Gentleman's amendment were applied to such circumstances, their house would have been a house in multiple occupation, something that would surprise Mrs. Bennett.

Various results could be produced from the amendment. Notwithstanding what the hon. Gentleman said, the present approach of the courts is clear. The various factors that they consider are well established and can be applied as a matter of common sense when considering individual cases.
