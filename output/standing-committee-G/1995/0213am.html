'(2A) Consent given under this section, except to a particular landlord or in relation to particular land, may be varied by the Corporation by order under the seal of the Corporation.'.

This is a simple, technical amendment. It would make it clear in the Bill that the Housing Corporation can vary its assent to the disposal of ground when that consent is given. Clause 8 gives the Housing Corporation power to consent to the disposal of land by registered social landlords, either on a case-by-case basis or more generally. The amendment would allow the corporation to vary that consent when it refers to all registered social landlords, a type of social landlord or a type of land. Although the Minister may say that it is clear that the Housing Corporation can do that, we consider it important that such a measure should be outlined in the Bill.

The Chairman
: Some members of the Committee have told me that the Room is a bit warm, so it is in order for hon. Members to remove their jackets if they wish.

The Minister for Local Government, Housing and Urban Regeneration (Mr. David Curry)
: The hon. Member for Leicester, East (Mr. Vaz) rightly anticipated my response. Clause 8 provides that the corporation's consent is required for a disposal. That may be given generally or it may be more specific. In practice, the corporation issues a general consent. Providing that landlords conform to the terms of that consent, they can dispose of property by means of sale, lease, mortgage, charge or any other disposition.

The corporation can, and does, vary the general consent. It updates it from time to time and issues the consent in the form of a circular. The most recent variation dates from last year. The amendment proposes that the corporation may vary a general consent. It is unnecessary as clause 8 follows the current legislation, which has proved adequate. I invite the hon. Gentleman not to persist with the amendment.

Mr. Vaz
: I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Clause 15
RIGHT OF TENANT TO ACQUIRE DWELLING

Mrs. Diana Maddock
(Christchurch)
: I beg to move amendment No. 121, in page 10, line 15, at end add

'except that in section 119 (1) of that Part for the purposes of this section only the words after "period" shall be deleted and there shall instead be inserted the words "of residence in the dwelling of which he is a tenant of at least two years.".'.

Under subsection (1)(c), a tenant has the right to acquire his dwelling if he has been a public sector tenant for at least two years. The amendment would mean that the tenant would have that right if he had been resident in his current dwelling for at least two years. Under the right-to-buy legislation, tenants who would not otherwise have the right to buy their dwellings must have been public sector tenants for at least two years. The Government are introducing the purchase grant to help tenants of registered social landlords who wish to purchase the homes in which they live rather than other dwellings on the private market. Over time, people become attached to their homes and may prefer to buy them rather than other dwellings.

That argument is less strong for tenants who have recently moved into their homes, especially those eligible for the purchase grant. They will probably be the newest of the housing association stock and the amount of help needed to buy property will depend on the length of tenure. A tenant may have moved into a brand new housing association house. If that person had been a tenant of other public sector housing for at least two years, he could immediately receive the maximum grant to buy a home. It is considered by some that there is an increased risk of fraud in such circumstances, including the allocation of new property.

Another risk is that the purchaser may move again as soon as the repayment of discount no longer applies. To minimise that and to ensure that the purchase grant favours long-standing tenants committed to owning their present homes, tenants should be required to have been resident in the home that they want to buy at least two years to qualify for the purchase grant.

The Government may say that the purchase grant legislation should closely model existing right-to-buy legislation and that there is no need to depart from it. They may also say that if tenants did not already have a right to buy, it might be at least two years before they could benefit from the scheme.

The new scheme is rather different from the original right to buy, which was introduced for settled tenants of local authorities or housing associations. Housing association schemes introduced since the Housing Act 1988 do not have eligible dwellings, and local authorities built few properties during the 1980s. Moreover, the purchase grant discount is not related to length of tenure, so tenants in new social landlord dwellings gain no advantage in delaying their purchase.

However, many properties will be purchased for more than their current value because they are relatively new. Much effort has been expended in recent years to create rented dwellings in the social sector. Many fear that early sales would defeat the objectives of much of that work—work done at considerable cost to public funds and to housing association time. Indeed, if the grant injections were small, the associations could make a loss.

In rural and other areas, authorities have also been encouraged to regard the community need for affordable housing as a material planning consideration in formulating development plans. JPEG 107 That has resulted in the negotiation of quotas requiring developers on specific sites to provide an agreed number of units for social housing or the earmarking of sites for social housing development only.

With the encouragement of the Government, authorities have often ensured the availability of properties to successor as well as initial occupiers by involving housing associations in the schemes. Paragraph 43 of PPG 3 states:

"Most commonly, the best way of ensuring that affordable housing will be enjoyed by successive as well as initial occupiers of property is by the involvement of a housing association or other social body (e.g. a trust) providing housing for rent or shared ownership: the body's continuing interest in the property will ensure control over subsequent changes of ownership and occupation."

Authorities have followed that advice and many of the schemes are underpinned by section 106 agreements. In rural areas, the success of the planning policies depend on landowners either donating land free or at considerably less than market value for community benefit, on the understanding that the properties will never be sold on the open market. Moreover, planning permission would not have been given in the first place if that were not the case. That applies also to non-rural areas where other special planning mechanisms have been used to create social housing.
