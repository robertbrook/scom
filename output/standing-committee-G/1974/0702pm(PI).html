Mr. Walter Clegg
: May on behalf of my colleagues and myself, welcome you to the Committee, Mrs. Jeger, and hope that you will be struck by its action and attention to detail.

When we were interrupted at one o'clock I was discussing these two amendments to Clause 27 which, as I said in quoting from the explanatory document, is one of the key clauses to the Bill.

This clause substitutes current use value for the previous market value of other enactments. These amendments will have the effect of leaving intact the market value arrangement of other legislation.

It is very difficult to assess this clause because we know what are the legal exemptions so far as current use value is concerned which we have in Clause 3. But in valuing land, if it is to be taken over by the State, there are certain other permitted matters to be taken into account, not enshrined in the Bill, which will be from time to time covered by the flexible extra-statutory instruments of the Minister.

The clause as it stands causes much concern. We have already seen how charities have reacted to it, but this afternoon I am concerned about the ordinary man in in the street who also suffers under this clause. If the charities suffer, then others will suffer. What they are having to do is 2106 to sell their properties to the State at current use value plus the other elements that are not very easy to determine. Then they have to purchase back from the State at market value.

We are told in other parts of the Bill that local authorities disposing of land must get the best price for it. What then is the position of someone in an area of future development who has to sell his property at current use value, and who after the second appointed day will get nothing other than that, apart from the other matters which I have mentioned? If he is a businessman he has to rehouse and re-establish himself in another development area and pay the full market value.

We know overall what these figures are going to mean because the Explanatory and Financial Memorandum to the Bill says that at this stage, which is the final stage of the Bill, the acquisition of land is going to cost the authorities something between £300 million and £400 million a year, and the estimated value of disposals on the other hand is going to be some £800 million to £900 million annually.

It is obvious that the State is going to make a profit, and that is what the whole Bill is based on. Some people are going to suffer because they will have to sell at one price and buy at another, rehouse themselves and re-establish businesses. We do not, and never have, believed that this is fair. I certainly do not. We have in the past argued that if there is to be a fund raised by this method, those who suffer from the acts of the State should be compensated before the State itself takes any profit. In the main, I am referring to those whose business properties are to be sold at one price and need to be replaced at another price. It is not surprising that one receives many letters on this point, not only from charities but from a wide range of people.

The Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Mr. Gordon Oakes)
: I listened with interest to both the hon. Gentlemen. The hon. Member for North Fylde (Mr. Clegg) was right when he said that I would be resisting the amendments, which are quite fundamental to the conception of "after the second appointed day". The intention of the amendments is to provide that the change in the basis of compensation for compulsory purchase to current use value under this clause shall only apply to compulsory acquisitions under the new acquisition powers in Part III of the Bill and not to acquisitions made under other powers.

The new acquisition powers in the Bill are complementary—I stress this—to the existing powers of local authorities, and it is envisaged that after the second appointed day there will still be acquisitions by local authorities for their own statutory purposes under the powers they have now as well as under the powers in this Bill.

It would be absurd to have two separate and different codes of compensation in those circumstances. It would be absurd for a local authority to pay one price for 2108 one type of acquisition and a totally different price—a market value price—for an acquisition for its own statutory purposes. That is why the words "whether under this or any other enactment" are included in the Bill.

That is the surface effect of the amendment, but much deeper there seems to be a fundamental misconception in the minds of the Opposition as to the ultimate move to current use value. This is our major objection to the amendment. The whole aim of the scheme is to arrive at a situation in which all land changes hands at current use value, except when an authority sells and recoups development value for the community by receiving full market value from the purchaser.

It could be envisaged that after the second appointed day one is in a current use value world. In that situation, when private purchasers will be paying current use value, it would be absurd if local authorities, when using powers other than those in the Bill, had to pay full market value.

The hon. Gentleman referred to the businessman. That is analagous. But a better example was given by the hon. Member for Buckingham (Mr. Benyon) who quoted the farmer and the representations from the National Farmers' Union about their opposition to this Bill. This again is based upon a fundamental misconception. It is true that the farmer would sell his farm at current use value. But he would buy his next farm at current use value, and that would be the division. That would be the last thing that the farmer would want to do, unless he wanted to buy developmentl and—an unusual position in which to be. If his land was acquired for development, he would not consciously seek out other development land for farming purposes. He would, however, buy that land at current use value. Thus after the second appointed day he would be no worse off. He would not be in the position, as both hon. Gentlemen said, of having to sell cheap and buy dear.

If the farmer lost his farm for development through compulsory acquisition by the local authority, not only could he buy another farm at current use value, but he would be entitled under the Land Compensation Act 1973 to farm loss payment or whatever other benefits accrue from that Act.

JPEG 2109 I wish to make it perfectly clear about what the move to current use value is after the second appointed day. We will then be living in a world of current use value. The effect of this clause would be to penalise a local authority for using its continuing, existing powers to acquire land for its own statutory use, whether for housing estates, schools, old people's homes and so on.

Under this amendment, if the local authority were to acquire land for those purposes, it would have to pay a totally different and much higher price than anybody else. Neither side of the Committee would want that situation. I emphasise at the outset that the concept of current use value in the way we envisage it does not mean that a person sells cheap and buys dear. He would sell cheap and he would buy cheap, unless he deliberately opted to buy development land. That is the real position under this Act.
