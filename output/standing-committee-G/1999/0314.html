That, during proceedings on the Sea Fishing Grants (Charges) Bill, the Committee do meet on Tuesdays at half-past Ten o'clock and at half-past Four o'clock and on Thursdays at Nine o'clock.—[ Mr. Elliot Morley.]

The Chairman
: I remind hon. Members that adequate notice should be given of any amendments. As a general rule, I do not intend to call starred amendments, including any that may be reached during an afternoon sitting of the Committee.

Clause 1
VALIDATION OF CERTAIN CHARGES MADE BY THE SEA FISH INDUSTRY AUTHORITY

Question proposed. That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Elliot Morley)
: The Bill is a narrow, technical measure, which deals with charges. Its purpose is to ensure the validity of charges levied by the Sea Fish Industry Authority between 1 October 1981 and 3 May 1996 in connection with its administration of grant schemes made, or treated as having been made, under the Fisheries Act 1981. It also ensures the validity of charges made by the Sea Fish Industry Authority's predecessor, the Herring Industry Board, between March 1972 and October 1981. The Herring Industry Board was abolished in 1981 when it amalgamated with SFIA, to which its liabilities were transferred.

The Bill's main objective, which is outlined in clause 1(1), is to ensure that the validity of the charges levied by the Sea Fish Industry Authority between 1 October 1981 and 3 May 1996 in connection with administering four fishing vessel grant schemes made under section 15 of the Fisheries Act 1981 and an earlier scheme made in 1976 under previous legislation. Under the 1981 Act, the responsibility for administering the latter scheme passed to the SFIA and the scheme was also deemed to have been made under the 1981 Act. The five schemes are specified in subsections 2(a) to (e).

Under the schemes made, or deemed to have been made, under section 15 of the Fisheries Act 1981, grants have been made available to fishermen in the United Kingdom for the construction, improvement or safety of vessles. They have all been administered by the Sea Fish Industry Authority on behalf of Ministers, as provided for by section 16 of the Fisheries Act 1981.

Dr. Norman A. Godman
(Greenock and Inverclyde)
: I asked a question on Second Reading about the purchase of survival suits. In respect of clause 1(1)(d) and (e), do the grants allow skipper owners and small trawler 4 companies to buy survival suits for their crew, or does such equipment not fall within the regulations?

Mr. Morley
: I know of my hon. Friend's long interest in fisheries safety and the value of survival suits in the fishing industry. They did not qualify under the old grant systems because they were not specified in the regulations, and whether fishermen had survival suits was entirely a matter for them. However, we know the value of the suits and are currently discussing a successor to the safety grant scheme, including how it should work and be directed and whether certain provisions should be encouraged, specified or assisted. We shall bear in mind what my hon. Friend said about life jackets and survival suits.

The Bill was introduced because there were concerns about the legality of the charges. It will ensure that the charges are made properly. There have been no complaints about how the charges are levied; those who receive grants signed the SFIA's conditions of approval for their payment, which referred to the deduction of a technical charge from the overall grant. No one objected to it, or to how it was applied.

Mr. David Heath
(Somerton and Frome)
: The Minister knows of my anxiety about the matter, and he said that there were concerns. The explanatory notes state that it is doubtful whether the previous charges were legal. The Minister should explain in what respect there is doubt or concern before the Committee agrees to the Bill. Wherein lies the dubiety?

Mr. Morley
: The legal doubt about whether the authority raised the charges lies in the fact that the Sea Fish Industry Authority levies charges—in this case for the administration of a series of grants to the fishing industry—on behalf of Ministers. There was doubt about whether Ministers had the authority to make those charges. Of course, if Ministers do not have the authority, the SFIA, acting on Ministers' behalf, does not have the authority either. The Bill will ensure that there is no doubt about the matter and that the charges are legal.

A considerable sum of money—about £560 a case, or £7.3 million in total—was levied over 13,000 cases in 24 years, going back to the days of the Herring Industry Board. The human rights issue, which applies now, has been carefully considered and the Bill is compatible with the European convention on human rights.

The Bill is retrospective. As I said on Second Reading, the Government do not take lightly the introduction of retrospective legislation. However, there are precedents for doing so; the Birds (Registration Charges) Act 1997, passed under the previous Government, retrospectively validated charges levied under sections 6 and 7 of the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 for the registration and sale of certain dead wild birds. The Wireless Telegraphy Act 1954 gave authority to past payments made to the Postmaster General for wireless transmission and receiving licences.

The charges were legitimate in respect of the work carried out by the SFIA. As I said, there are no complaints about the charges levied; the Bill makes the powers to levy those charges proper and legal and it must be done retrospectively.
