The fourth type of care given in a care home that would count as personal care is emotional and psychological support. That would include the promotion of social functioning, behaviour management and assistance with cognitive functions.

    Therefore, I hope that it is clear that the meaning of personal care in clause 3 is very wide and covers a whole range of care, both physical and emotional. However, if we were to leave clause 3 as it stands, there is a danger, to which the hon. Member for Meriden referred, that we would include in the definition of a care home all sorts of establishments that could be seen as providing accommodation and personal care in the wide sense that I have outlined to the Committee. For example, without any further limitations, hostels for homeless people, many of whom have drug and alcohol problems, may be required to register as care homes. Equally, an enormous range of establishments that provide any sort of general counselling and psychological support on a residential basis might count as care homes. That is not our intention. We could have dealt with that problem by excluding such places in regulations under clause 3, but we have received many representations on the issue and are aware that it is causing considerable concern. Therefore, we decided that it was best to table an amendment to narrow the definition of a care home, which is why we tabled amendment No. 474.

    Amendment No. 474 makes it clear that, for an establishment to count as a care home, it must provide assistance with bodily functions, where such assistance is required. It is very important to understand that that does not mean that the personal care provided by the home is limited to assistance with bodily functions; as I have already made clear, the meaning of personal care is to be interpreted more widely than that. The amendment will simply mean that that type of assistance must be available and given if required. Clearly, the amount of such assistance that is actually given will depend on the needs of the residents. In a care home for frail elderly people, it is likely to be given fairly often, but in a care home for people with learning disabilities, it might be given only very rarely. However, for an establishment to be registrable as a care home, assistance with bodily functions must be available, even if it is rarely provided. The same requirement is contained in the Registered Homes Act 1984, and, as the hon. Member for Sutton and Cheam rightly said, that requirement was clarified by the court in the case of Harrison v. Cornwall county council.
