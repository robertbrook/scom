Clause 22
FINANCIAL STRUCTURE OF REGISTERED PARTY: ADOPTION OF SCHEME

Amendment proposed [ this day]: No. 5, in page 14, line 33, leave out 'none of the following shall' and insert

'the following may'.

2.30 pm

Mr. Andrew Miller
(Ellesmere Port and Neston)
: Before we adjourned this morning, I was explaining some of my background to the Committee in the context of the speeches of the Opposition Whip. I was seeking to illustrate how trade unions have changed with the times, reflecting the century upon which we are embarking. I was explaining that during my long time as an active trade unionist I had on occasions used the block votes to which the hon. Member for North Dorset (Mr. Walter) referred. I fully understand the expert view of the use of such votes both within trade unions and the Labour party. He is aware from his study of more recent history that trade unions have embarked upon a series of changes that alter their relationship with the political party. The existence of those changes, which are well embodied in the rules of the Labour party and have taken place voluntarily within the affiliated trade unions, means that the hon. Gentleman's attempt to paint the relationship between the trade unions and the Labour party as the same as it was 20 or 30 years ago is wrong.

In his previous incarnation, the hon. Member for New Forest, East (Dr. Lewis) described events that occurred at least 20 years ago in Newham constituency. One acknowledges that from time to time relationships have come under strain because of attempts to push the rules to the boundary. From my equally long experience of friends in the Conservative party, I know that that is true within that party as well.

The hon. Member for North Dorset acknowledged that there was a varying relationship between Conservative clubs and the Conservative party. In my constituency there is a Conservative club in the heart of the strongest Labour ward in the town. I admit to my hon. Friends sitting behind me that I entered the premises on one occasion and I am told by friends who are members of the club that they were not knowingly members of the Conservative party, but joined because the club had the best snooker tables. That seems a sensible basis on which to join the club, whereas its links with the Conservative party are not.

Mr. Robert Walter
(North Dorset)
: The comments by the hon. Member for Ellesmere Port and Neston (Mr. Miller) beg the question: is he trying to suggest 44 that the links with the Labour party that we described—and that Neill described as recently as 1996—no longer exist? Are there no longer trade unions or hon. Members affiliated to the Labour party?

Mr. Miller
: Of course there are affiliated trade unions and long may that remain. I am sure that every one of my hon. Friends will agree. The process of affiliation is based on rules of individual unions and it is not for the Committee to interfere. That is neither the purpose of the Bill, nor a sound remit for the House. A number of mistakes were made in the previous Parliament by interfering with the rulebooks of trade unions. I am saying that that relationship is not the one that the hon. Member for North Dorset described, which I openly acknowledge existed in the early years. He is wrong, because his history needs to be brought up to date.

The political levy that members voluntarily pay does not necessarily reflect an immediate affiliation to the Labour party. Some unions that have political levies are not affiliated to the Labour party because of previous statutes. If a union chooses by balloting its members to affiliate, the size of the affiliation is determined by each union. Different unions have different approaches to that and the decisions of groups of members are, on occasion, surprising.

When the United Commercial Travellers Association—a longstanding trade union going back to before the beginning of the century, whose patron was the first Lord Leverhulme—took a decision to transfer allegiance to ASTMS, the recommendation of its executive, hardly a left-wing organisation, was that their members should contribute to the political levy because the money would be used to influence Governments. It was recognised at the time—and I am sure that Members from all parties and all trade unions would recognise now—that in those days, when the late Clive Jenkins was general secretary, the union had a significant influence on Governments of both persuasions. The UCTA executive made that recommendation because it saw a value in the use of the political fund. It had nothing to do with individual members' wishes to be personally linked to the Labour party. That is an entirely different state of affairs and I am sure that the hon. Gentleman will recognise that.

My point is that the amendment may have been worthy of examination before these transitions started to take place, but it is now irrelevant. The wording of subsection (8) will stand the test of time.

2.45 pm

The amendment changes

"none of the following shall"

 to "the following may" because that is a permissive power rather than a prescriptive one. Had the hon. Gentleman been partisan on that point, I would have expected the amendment to suggest the phrase "the following will". The phrase "the following may" is some recognition that there is an issue here, and indeed there is.

We cannot disguise the fact that there are trade unions that are affiliated to the Labour party and, according to its constitution, are regarded as part of the party. If that were not so, the question of their inclusion in or exclusion from the party's schemes simply would not arise. It is also a fact—we are transparent on this point—that the Labour party receives substantial, albeit declining sums of money from trade unions. Those sums are properly and openly raised and accounted for under distinct statutory arrangements, which exist for that purpose. I assure the Committee that there is nothing underhand or of a party political nature in our consideration of this matter. I want to persuade the Committee that the opposite is the case.

The hon. Member for Beaconsfield (Mr. Grieve) asked what the position would be if his amendment fell. He asked about the nature of the relationship. If affiliated trade unions were included within the schemes for regulating parties' affairs for the purposes of the Bill, there would be two main consequences.

The first, and minor, consequence is that, if trade unions were not outwith the schemes all their financial affairs would have to be included, not just those relating to political activity. Trade unions have changed enormously. Many trade unions now do a great deal of their work around common law claims. Some of the income of the trade union movement comes from insurance schemes and from financial advice and services. That is an indication of the change in the nature of trade unions. However, if we were not to exclude trade unions, all that trading activity would have to be included within the provisions. It cannot be right that such major activities should be accounted for and under the responsibility of somebody who has no say in them. It is a practical issue.

The second consequence is more important. The party would not be obliged to declare donations from a trade union were it not for this clause. The movement of the money would be on a par with, for example, transferring funds within the party from a constituency association to central headquarters. Without this part of the Bill, there would be no clarity and no transparency in respect of the nature of the relationship or the donation to the political party, the Labour party. That is an important principle. It must be right, I have no doubt that people need to JPEG 47 know how much money is coming to the Labour party from the trade union movement. If we were to accept the amendment, that would not be the case. We are on strong ground. It is better to know the nature and the amount of the transaction rather than that money should pass through the organisation without notice being given to anybody.

I hope that that will reassure Committee members. I know that the matter is important to them. I do not seek to dissuade them from putting the amendment to a vote, but I hope that hon. Members will be reassured by the two reasons that I have given—that the other trading activities of the trade union movement would have to be recognised and accounted for, and, more importantly, that subsection (8) establishes clearly, transparently and openly for all to see the nature of the relationship and the amount of money involved.

The comparison between Conservative clubs and trade unions may be slightly spurious, although I would reflect on the fact that my constituency has two Conservative clubs, neither of which, to my recollection, has made any financial contribution to the Conservative party. However, some years ago, I had the good fortune to contest a Parliamentary seat in South Wales against the present European Commissioner, Mr. Neil Kinnock, which had four Conservative clubs. One was the pithead social club at the Britannia colliery at Aberbargoed which did make a contribution to the Conservative party, I am pleased to say, and also served a very good pint of beer.

The hon. Member for Ellesmere Port and Neston mentioned the changing nature of trade unions and the fact that they were no longer the significant influence in the Labour party that they were. The statistics from Neill to which I referred applied to when we did not have a Labour Government; they were from 1996, when I suspect that the trade unions had more influence than they might have today. However, according to the Neill statistics, 3.5 million members of the Labour party have become affiliated by virtue of their trade union membership. If you accept that they are members of the Labour party, which is how they regard themselves—[HON. MEMBERS: "NO."] Well, if one adds them to the direct members of the party, it means that about 90 per cent. of the membership are not covered by the provisions of the Bill because they are affiliated through their trade union organisations. If we are to have transparent legislation, the creation of those political funds should come within the remit of the Electoral Commission.

The Minister's second argument was about transparency. He suggested that it was more transparent for us simply to look at the money passing from the political funds of the trade union to the Labour party—I assume that is the only party about which we are talking here—rather than the way it is collected and spent within the trade unions. That would be fine if the trade unions did not operate their political funds internally as well as passing money to the Labour party and if they did not have their own political campaigns in support of Labour policies.

Mr. Tipping
: Trade unions are regulated by their own legislation. Neill considered that and, by and large, gave it a clean bill of health. If trade unions themselves campaign away from the Labour party once the Bill becomes law, they will become third-party campaigners in their own right and therefore liable to provisions that we shall discuss later.

Mr. Walter

We can congratulate them and the Government who introduced the legislation. We have made a significant point on the provision, but I sense that the Government are not minded to accept our amendment and, therefore, beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

PARTY CEASING TO BE REGISTERED

Mr. Walter
