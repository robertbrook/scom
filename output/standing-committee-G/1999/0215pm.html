Clause 137
SUMMARY PROCEED

Mr. Dominic Grieve
(Beaconsfield)
: I beg to move amendment No. 131, in page 88, line 18, leave out 'three' and insert 'two'.

Clause 137(2) states:

"Despite anything in section 127(1) of the Magistrates' Court Act 1980, any information relating to any offence under this Act which is triable by a magistrates' court in England and Wales may be so tried if it is laid at any time within three years after the commission of the offence and within six months after the relevant date."

The Committee may be familiar with normal procedure, but the standard rule within the magistrates court is that summary only offences have usually to be laid within six months of their commission, albeit that exceptions may be made. Some provisions provide for 12 months. If a matter goes to the Crown court, the prosecution may be brought at any time thereafter.

Schedule 19, which we briefly glossed over, creates the 68 criminal offences to which I alluded yesterday. They include a substantial number of summary only offences. It is worth paying momentary attention to that provision; the offences may be those most commonly committed. For example, the schedule covers:

"failure to deliver proper statement of accounts … On summary conviction: Level 5; failure to deliver accounts within time limits … On summary conviction: Level 5"

on the standard scale, and so on, including acceptance by an ineligible person of office of deputy treasurer, a subject that has been a vexed question in the Committee's proceedings.

There are a large number of offences. Those offences, dare I say it, which are mostly likely to be 428 committed by, I would not say innocent people, but those who fall foul or transgress in a slight way, are, not surprisingly, not triable by a Crown court; they are summary only offences.

Clause 137 provides that, unlike the usual magistrates' court rules, on which the Minister may comment in a moment, prosecutions on summary only offences can be brought up to three years after the commission of the offence and six months after the relevant date:

"the date on which evidence sufficient in the opinion of the prosecutor to justify proceedings comes to his knowledge."

That ties in with my previous comment that in most road traffic offences, the information would have to be laid within six months of the commission of the offence. I am slightly concerned about that. I accept that, by its nature, the sort of audit that the Electoral Commission must carry out will mean that some time is likely to elapse between the commission of an offence and the time when the commission finds out about it. I am willing to concede that, but three years is a very long time and may cause injustice. Why do we set such time limits for relatively minor offences in the magistrates court? It is because, historically, it was considered to be unjust to prosecute people after considerable delay, and the less serious the offence, the less delay is tolerated. Obviously, a prosecution for murder in the Crown court may be brought 50 years after the crime was committed, but in relation to summary offences, the House should be reluctant to establish a system in which, three years after the event, someone may suddenly be prosecuted for potentially minor breaches, which, none the less, could have serious consequences, especially if the person is a treasurer or office holder in a political party.

With that in mind, I substituted two years for three. I am prepared to listen to what the Minister has to say about three years, but in the absence of coherent arguments why three years are required, I would prefer two, which is already 18 months longer than applies to summary offences that are punishable only by a fine. I should be grateful if the Minister would explain why three years are required in this case.

The Parliamentary Secretary, Privy Council Office (Mr. Paddy Tipping)
: The hon. Member for Beaconsfield (Mr. Grieve) introduced the clause and the amendment extremely well. He explained the problem and argued that the three-year period should be reduced to two. In doing so, he acknowledged that, in such circumstances, the usual six-month period should be extended. Amendment No. 131 would reduce the period to two years. The hon. Gentleman asked me to put forward coherent arguments why the three-year period should remain.

It will take six months for audited accounts to be returned to the commission. The commission will have a heavy work load, but it will have to review the accounts and make further inquiries about whether a prosecution should take place. If the commission decides that a prosecution should be brought, the matter must be referred to the police and to the Crown Prosecution Service. Therefore, it is necessary to provide for sufficient time in which to identify whether an offence has been committed to make JPEG 429 further inquiries and, if necessary, to involve the police and CPS, before a prosecution is brought. Therefore, it is arguable whether two years is sufficient. Clearly, one should bring a prosecution as soon as possible, but three years is not unreasonable. A precedent has been set that is not far from the mark. Three years has worked well in respect of section 731 of the Companies Act 1985. It provides the opportunity for extra time. Three years is the outside limit and I do not expect people to wait that long, but the period needs to be longer than two years. The clause is in accordance with section 731 of the Companies Act 1985.

Mr. Grieve
: I am grateful to the Minister for that clarification. We have examined the precedents from which the Bill is derived. It ranges over tax law and company law. I wonder whether they provide good comparables for the Bill. Company law may make similar provisions, but as we discussed with the hon. Member for Hazel Grove (Mr. Stunell), it is a long way from the Morecambe Bay Independents. It will take six months for the audited accounts to be submitted, another six months for the Electoral Commission to identify what might be wrong and a further six months to clarify whether there has been an offence or just an innocent event that it would not refer to the CPS for action. That leaves six months in my two-year timetable and 18 months in the Minister's three-year timetable. Justice delayed is justice denied.

I ask the Minister to reconsider the matter. Three years is a long time. We need only put it in the context of political life, in which three years can put us in another age. We must also have regard to the fact that people should not unexpectedly face charges three years after an event when recollections are genuinely hazy. It could be difficult to recall what went on or who wrongly paid money to a contractor without declaring it to the treasurer for approval and putting it through the books. Such examples will crop up under summary jurisdiction. I would be grateful if the Minister mulls it over with his officials, because two years is more reasonable. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Clause 139
OFFENCES COMMITTED BY UNINCORPORATED ASSOCIATIONS

Mr. Grieve
: I beg to move amendment No. 132, in page 89, line 30, leave out from 'who' to 'attempted' in line 31 and insert

'was ignorant of or'.

The Chairman
: With this we may discuss amendment No. 133, in page 89, line 39, leave out from 'who' to 'attempted' in line 40 and insert

'was ignorant of or'.

Mr. Grieve
: This is my final afternoon of activity—[HON. MEMBERS: "Oh."] The sighs of relief from 430 Labour Members reassure me that I have probably done my job correctly.

The amendments bring us back to the guilt of unincorporated associations and partnerships. The amendment would change subsection (6), which says:

"Where a partnership is guilty of an offence under this Act, every partner (other than a partner who is proved to have been ignorant of, or to have attempted to prevent the commission of, the offence) shall also be guilty of that offence and be liable to be proceeded against and punished accordingly."

That wording suggests that the burden of proof will be on the partner, who will have to prove that he falls into the categories of having been ignorant of the offence or of having attempted to prevent the commission of it. I am not generally in favour of placing burdens of proof on defendants, as they are usually on the prosecutor in English law. The amendment would make it clear that the burden of proof is on the prosecutor to show that the individual partners of a partnership that is guilty of an offence are also guilty.

4.45 pm

This is a serious matter. Partnerships may operate in all sorts of shapes, forms and sizes. As has been pointed out, the Bill properly allows unincorporated associations—partnerships are a form of such association, albeit with a slightly different legal personality—or partnerships to be criminally liable in their own right. Of course, such organisations cannot be locked up; they can only be fined, although those fines can be substantial. Indeed, schedule 19 shows just how substantial they are. However, the Bill goes a step further by ensuring that where a partnership is guilty of an offence, each partner shall be guilty unless he satisfies a court that the offence had nothing to do with him. I question the propriety of that.

We are aiming at a mischief and trying to stamp out improper practices. The penalties can apply to groups of people and will have serious consequences. They are not just raps over the knuckles for a road traffic offence. The offences will criminalise the individuals in question, and I assume that they will be recordable as a criminal record and will appear on people's forms. A few road traffic offences such as causing death by dangerous driving will appear in that way, but most such offences will not.

I am anxious about the matter, and I would like to hear justification of why all partners should have collective criminal liability unless they can persuade a court otherwise. If a prosecutor decides that a partnership has committed a criminal offence, the court will probably have to deal with documentary evidence. I should have expected that such documents would be likely to identify any individual so culpable that he should be prosecuted. Will the Minister justify the arrangements? If he does not think that there is any need for such a procedure, will he revert to the ordinary rules in English law, which ensure that the burden of proof is on the prosecution at all times and it is not for the defendant to exculpate himself?

Amendment No. 133 does exactly the same thing for an unincorporated association. My argument is valid for a partnership, but it is even more valid for JPEG 431 unincorporated associations. Although some partnerships are loose, one would expect them usually to consist of individuals who work together regularly and who know something of each other's activities. That contrasts with unincorporated associations, which would cover every political party. Every member of the committee or other similar governing body would be guilty unless they could prove their innocence. I do not know how Labour party or Liberal Democrat party constituency associations work—

Mr. Andrew Stunell
(Hazel Grove)
: Nor do I.

Mr. Grieve
: The hon. Gentleman makes a good point. I have some understanding of how my constituency association works. I know that it has an executive council, and I assume that the council is the governing body of the accounting unit that my association will become. I know how it operates, and about the voluntary nature of the relationship; it is not tied down by contract, and no one is required to turn up.

It beggars belief that a blanket policy should operate if a transgression is found—I hope that it will not be—so that the group is held collectively liable. The chances are that the only persons liable would be the agent, possibly the chairman and perhaps someone who had played a prominent part in an election campaign. It is nonsense to suggest that other members would know anything about a transgression. They may be able to exculpate themselves, but I doubt whether it is reasonable or proper in those circumstances that they should face prosecution, or that the prosecutor should demand that they satisfy him that they knew nothing about it. That seems a little draconian.

Again, I ask the Minister to review the provisions for unincorporated associations. It could be argued that the provisions should be maintained for partnerships, but not for unincorporated associations, although I think that partners might feel disadvantaged. I hope that the Minister can reassure me that there is a good reason for the provisions. If, having heard my arguments, he thinks that I am right, I hope that he will review the situation.

Mr. Stunell
: This is a rare case of my being impressed by the arguments so far advanced. The amendment was beyond my ken; never having been a member of a business partnership, I could not relate to some of the issues that it raised.

The hon. Member for Beaconsfield certainly struck a nerve when he referred to unincorporated associations. The clause certainly seems to bear the meaning that he suggests. Should that be so, I would be concerned about its ramifications. Mrs. Brown, back in her little branch committee, might find that she has crossed the line. Subsection (1) seems to apply not only to some offences but to any alleged offence. That might visit wrath on a branch committee or constituency executive that goes beyond the bounds of what seems reasonable. An amendment that placed the burden on the prosecuting authorities rather than making the defendants prove that they were ignorant of or had attempted to prevent the commission of the 432 offence would seem appropriate. I shall listen to the Minister's response with some interest.

Mr. Tipping
: The hon. Member for Beaconsfield rightly pointed out that amendment No. 132 relates to partnership, and amendment No. 133 to unincorporated associations. Both have the same effect and intention. The clause is modelled on a precedent—section 734 of the Companies Act 1985. We should focus on amendment No. 133, because the majority of cases will relate to political parties and pressure groups that are unincorporated associations. Subsections (6) and (7) of clause 139 make provisions for individual partners or officers, or certain members of an unincorporated association to be found individually guilty of an offence when the body itself has been found guilty of an offence. An offence will have been committed by the body; proceedings will then be taken separately against individual members. That may be appropriate if individual officers or the management committee as a whole has authorised expenditure in contravention of the Bill's provisions.

The hon. Member for Beaconsfield, as always, has put his finger on it: the amendment seeks to reverse the burden of proof. I am not inclined to promise a new amendment, but I shall certainly reflect on the matter. Interestingly enough, the hon. Member for Hazel Grove came to the same conclusion. The Bill was drafted in that way because a person charged under the provisions of subsections (6) or (7) may escape conviction if he can prove either that he was ignorant of the offence or that he had attempted to prevent the commission of the offence. The first test is that the body has been found guilty. The second test is that the individual challenged was ignorant of the offence or had attempted to prevent it from being committed. As the hon. Member for Beaconsfield pointed out, the amendment would reverse the burden of proof in respect of the first of the two defences.

Mr. Grieve
: I would describe it as restoring the burden of proof. Normally, the burden of proof falls on the prosecution. However, the amendment is changing the system as it would apply under the Bill.

Mr. Tipping
: Hon. Members are always keen to put their amendments in the best light. The hon. Gentleman has done so again. He is right that the amendment would change the burden of proof; it would fall to the prosecution to show that a defendant was not ignorant of the commission of the offence with which he was being charged. The question is whether the defendant or the prosecutor should deliver the burden of proof. The Bill places the responsibility on the defendant. As I said, I am not giving a firm commitment, but having heard the arguments, I shall certainly reflect on the words of the amendment. During the debate it has become apparent that the provision is phrased rather differently than originally drafted. I hope that the hon. Gentleman will allow me to reconsider the wording—to see whether we can protect those people and give them some reassurance—and withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Grieve
: I am reassured that the Minister will reconsider the matter. I accept that it is not completely clear cut. I do not suggest that the Government have done something disgraceful in making such a JPEG 433 provision. The burden of proof is sometimes reversed. The Minister pointed out that the clause is derived from the Companies Acts, but the activities of company directors are a million miles away from the activities of Mrs. Brown and her unincorporated association. We need to think carefully about associations or parties that raise enough money to qualify in light of the realities rather than the precedent of the Companies Acts. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

New Clause 4
TAX RELIEF FOR DONATIONS TO POLITICAL PARTIES

'The following shall be inserted after section 379 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988 (interpretation of sections 369 to 378)—

"Relief for donations to political parties 379AA.—(1) Tax relief shall be available to an individual (the donor) in accordance with this section on qualifying political donations made by him of up to £500 in any year of assessment.
(2) A donation is a qualifying political donation for the purposes of this section if it is made to an eligible political party and—
(a) it takes the form of the payment of a sum of money,
(b) it is not subject to a condition as to repayment,
(c) it is not conditional on or associated with, or part of an arrangement involving, the acquisition of property by the political party, otherwise than by way of gift, from the donor or a person connected with him, and
(d) the donor is resident in the United Kingdom at the time the donation is made.
(3) For the purposes of this section a political party is an eligible political party if, at the last general election preceding the donation in question—
(a) two members of that party were elected to the House of Commons, or
(b) one member of that party was elected to the House of Commons and not less than 150,000 votes were given to

candidates who were members of that party.
(4) If an individual makes a qualifying donation he shall be entitled, on making the payment, to deduct and retain out of it a sum equal to basic rate tax thereon.
(5) Where a sum is deducted under subsection (4) above the sum deducted shall be treated as income tax paid by the person to whom the payment is made.
(6) Any person by whom a qualifying donation is received shall be entitled to recover from the Board, in accordance with regulations, an amount which by virtue of subsection (5) above is treated as income tax paid by him; and any amount so recovered shall be treated for the purposes of the Tax Acts in like manner as the qualifying political donation to which it relates.
(7) The following provisions of the Management Act, namely—
(a) section 29(1)(c) (excessive relief) as it has effect apart from section 29(2) to (10) of that Act,
(b) section 30 (tax repaid in error, etc) apart from subsection (1B),
