The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Paul Boateng)
: These are serious amendments and we are dealing with serious issues. We visited the matter earlier in the course of our proceedings. The effect would be that for those aged 18 or over the offence would carry a quasi-automatic disqualification and for those under 18 it would be at the discretion of the judge.

Our concern has been to ensure that we have a scheme that will be effective for the protection of children. Disqualification is the serious consequence of a serious offence, and should be imposed by the judge wherever conditions warrant it. That will almost certainly be the case where a prison sentence is imposed that is longer than the term prescribed in the legislation. A reduction in the minimum sentence attracting disqualification would risk weakening the entire scheme. Judges would be reluctant to go down that path, and would find reasons for not doing so. We are determined that the scheme should be as strong as possible, and that disqualification should be treated with the seriousness that it warrants. For that reason alone, in the light of representations that were made, and having consulted on the issue, our view of the matter differs from that held by the hon. Clause 30
REVIEW OF DISQUALIFICATION

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to discuss new clause 1—

Reinstatement of Disqualification Order—

'.—(1) This section applies where the Tribunal has directed under the provisions of section 30 that a disqualification order is to cease to have effect in respect of an individual.


(2) A relevant person may at any time apply to the Tribunal for the reinstatement of the disqualification order.


(3) An application under subsection (2) may only be granted if the Tribunal is satisfied, having regard to all the circumstances, that it is likely that the individual may commit a further offence against a child.


(4) Where the Tribunal reinstates an order under this section, it must state its reasons for doing so and cause those reasons to be included in the record of the proceedings.


(5) In this section, "relevant person" means—

  1. the Secretary of State;
  2. a superintendent of police;
  3. the director of social services of a local authority.'.

Mr. Hawkins
: New clause 1 would introduce an arrangement whereby disqualification orders that had ceased to have effect could, in certain circumstances, be reinstated. This is an extremely serious issue, and the new clause would add a helpful provision to the Bill. We recognise that the introduction of disqualification orders will create a useful additional power for the courts. The Government should therefore consider the circumstances in which a tribunal may—understandably—direct, under clause 30, that a disqualification order is to cease to have effect. Under the new clause, a "relevant person"—defined in subsection (5) of the new clause as the Secretary of State, a police superintendent or a local authority director of social services—could subsequently apply to the tribunal for a reinstatement of the disqualification order. The basis for that, which is set out in subsection (3), would be that

the Tribunal is satisfied, having regard to all the circumstances, that it is likely that the individual may—

not will, but may—

commit a further offence against a child.

It is possible that the police, directors of social services or the Secretary of State—from information supplied to him or her—could become aware of a JPEG 167 person who still posed a risk, even after a disqualification order from an earlier ruling of the tribunal had ceased to have effect. In such circumstances, this reserve power would ensure that the disqualification order could be brought back into effect. We think that the public would consider that to be useful to protect children, if a person's circumstances had changed after a disqualification order had ceased to have effect.

I do not want to detain the Committee at length on this matter. I have made the point. We would be surprised if the Government did not feel that, even if they cannot accept the new clause today, something along these lines could be useful, but we wait with interest to hear what the Minister says about it. We hope that he will be prepared to say, as he has already done on a number of issues, that he will consider the matter again with officials, talk to those whom the Government consult and perhaps return to it in another place or on Report.

10.45 am

Mr. Boateng
: Clause 30 provides for the application to a tribunal for a review of a disqualification. The tribunal established under the Protection of Children Act 1999 to deal with appeals will also be the tribunal to which an offender will be able to apply for a review of his disqualification. The right of review, especially where the disqualification is otherwise permanent, is an important element in maintaining justice and fairness for all, while putting the protection of children at the heart of the Bill.

Clause 30 provides for an application to the tribunal by an individual for a review of his disqualification. The conditions of the application are set out in more detail in clause 31. Clause 30 deals with applications from a court-imposed disqualification to the tribunal only. However, individuals should also be able to apply to the tribunal against bans imposed by the Secretary of State for Education and Employment and the Secretary of State for Health. That is provided by amendments to the relevant legislation, which are set out in paragraphs 44 and 45 of schedule 6. Therefore, there is a cross-over between schedule 6 and the clause.

How does the provision tie in with the overall scheme and what would the impact be of the new clause? Although undoubtedly well intentioned, the new clause could have the reverse effect from that intended and weaken the scheme. The Government are committed to ensuring that those who should be disqualified remain disqualified if any risk to children remains. Therefore, an applicant must overcome a considerable hurdle before the disqualification is removed. My fear is that if tribunals are aware that it is always open to them subsequently, at the behest of a director of social services, a superintendent or even a Secretary of State, to reimpose a disqualification, they might be more willing to lift it in the first place. We do not expect tribunals to lift the disqualification unless they are positively satisfied that there is no risk to children and that it is desirable that the disqualification be lifted.

168 Therefore, the process is heavily weighted so that the ban can be lifted only if the person disqualified can positively satisfy the tribunal that he is no longer a risk to children. It is a strong test and, in our view, a substantial safeguard. We would be unwilling to allow anything to interfere with the effectiveness of the scheme as a whole.

The new clause properly sets a high criterion for the reimposition of the ban, because of the impact of the Human Rights Act 1998 and the European convention on human rights. However, again in a perverse way, it could limit the powers that are available to protect children, because it undermines the statutory route, which does not have such a high test as the new clause would impose. If someone from whom a disqualification order has been lifted gives further cause for concern, there are three possible avenues for meeting that concern. If the person is working with children in areas covered by a ban imposed by the Department for Education and Employment or the Department of Health, the ban could be reimposed in the same manner as before, if the person's conduct was sufficiently serious to merit dismissal or removal from work with children. In the case of a sex offender who falls back into former ways, a sex offender order, which could include a ban on work with children, could be applied for. In the case of a violent offender, the precursor activity might itself be criminal; alternatively, an antisocial behaviour order might be sought.

Mr. Boateng

We believe that the new clause, although well intentioned, would have the reverse effect to that which is intended. Setting in statute the high criterion envisaged in the new clause could, in fact, make protection more difficult.

Therefore, although I understand the hon. Gentleman's motives for moving the new clause—and I will write to him on the point that he raised—I am unable to accept it.

Mr. Hawkins
: I am grateful to the Minister for the way that he has approached the matter, and I look forward to his letter.

We want to make it clear that, in moving the new clause, we have no intention of weakening the existing provisions. We do not agree that the new clause would do so, although I understand his point that, if there JPEG 169 were a way of reimposing a ban, the tribunal might think that it could lift a ban more easily. Making the test "satisfied beyond reasonable doubt", as I suggested, would make it clear that the tribunal has to reach the criminal standard of proof, rather than the civil standard. The Minister rightly said that there is much debate about the exact legal connotations of the word "satisfied". I am sure that the Minister will accept that there is a danger that the current wording of the clause will lead a tribunal to conclude that the meaning of "satisfied" is as for the civil standard; that is, on a balance of probabilities. That is another way to approach the matter and make clause 30 stronger. The Minister has kindly said that he would be prepared to consider that further, and that he will also consider what we have suggested. If there is a way of using something along the lines of our new clause, without running any danger of weakening the provisions of the current clause, I hope that the Government will propose it.

Clearly, there will be an on-going debate about the issue—the Minister said that he would be considering the matter—and it would be helpful if it were discussed again in another place and/or on Report. At this stage, therefore, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the motion.

Mr. Boateng
: We are all concerned to ensure that individuals subject to a disqualification order under this part of the Bill are not able to escape easily the terms of the order; that is a given on which we are all of one mind. I assure the hon. Gentleman that we do not believe that the Bill as currently drafted will make it at all easy for an individual to escape the effect of the ban before it is right and appropriate—it ever is—for it to be lifted.

In this clause, the date we are talking about is the point at which an individual can approach the tribunal. It provides an opportunity only for those who are genuinely rehabilitated to approach the tribunal if they wish to have the disqualification order lifted. It is only after that first hurdle has been passed that the individual can ask for the disqualification order to be removed.

The first issue for the tribunal is whether the offender should be given leave to apply; that is far from automatic. I am happy to make it clear that the offender must first demonstrate that his circumstances have changed sufficiently since the order was made for leave to apply to be granted. An example might be where an offender has successfully reared children of his own in the intervening period. Having obtained leave to apply the individual must then prove that he is now actually suitable to work with children by having demonstrated that during his period at liberty in the community. It would not be acceptable for someone to leave prison at the completion of a period in custody—as opposed to the end of the sentence, as the amendment states—and immediately apply to have the ban lifted. Such a person would be unlikely to get past the first hurdle. Even if that were to be achieved, I hope that the hon. Gentleman appreciates that they would not have been able to demonstrate that they were of such trustworthiness as to meet the criteria of the test that I have outlined.

The issue is not whether an individual has ceased to pose a danger to children, but whether he or she is positively suitable to work with them. Unless that rigorous criterion is satisfied, there is no question of the ban being lifted. The critical issue is the period after release from custody. In order to consider whether this leave should be granted in any demonstration of suitability, the tribunal must consider the individual's record in the community.

Clause 32
PERSONS DISQUALIFIED FROM WORKING WITH CHILDREN: OFFENCES

Mr. Hawkins
: I beg to move amendment No. 97, in page 15, line 40 after 'position', insert

'or performs on a casual basis (whether for financial reward or not) any functions which would constitute work in a regulated position.'.

The amendment would insert at the end of subsection (1) words relating to those who perform work of this kind on a casual basis. Again, we seek to toughen up the provision. At our previous sitting we discussed the Minister's and my involvement in the past on a voluntary basis with church youth clubs and that kind of thing. There are a number of casual positions that people can take up when working with children. As the hon. Members for Hemel Hempstead (Mr. McWalter) and for Lancaster and Wyre (Mr. Dawson), who, sadly, cannot be with us this morning, no doubt for understandable reasons, stressed at our last sitting and as the Minister said this morning, child sex offenders are often devious people. They set out to look as if they are helpful to children, they purport to be solid, upstanding and respectable members of the community and they actively seek out such positions. The Minister and other members of the Committee will recognise that the danger is that people may not take paid employment "in a regulated position", to use the words in the Bill, but may seek out casual positions.

We, therefore, hope that the Minister will treat the amendment as a serious and useful addition to the clause. Even if he cannot accept it today, I hope that, as on previous occasions, he will say that he will consider our suggestion seriously with officials and with those whom the Government consult and perhaps table a Government amendment at a later stage. We believe that it would be sensible to extend the wording of the Bill to cover the kind of risk to which I referred, in the light of what the Minister said about the guileful way in which child sex offenders often behave. However we choose to describe that behaviour, we know that such people are deliberately devious and actively seek to work with children in any way possible. I hope that the Minister's response to this amendment will be as constructive as his response to some others.

Mr. Boateng
: Clause 32 will establish new criminal offences that will bite on individuals who seek to work with children while disqualified. This legislation is a JPEG 173 major strand in the provision of an integrated umbrella of child protection measures. All individuals who are banned from working with children—whether through this legislation, or by virtue of being included in lists maintained by the Department of Health, the Department for Education and Employment or the National Assembly for Wales—but who seek to do so while disqualified will be guilty of an offence.

The first offence will be to seek, offer, accept or continue to work with children while under a disqualification order. That offence simply provides for the punishment of a banned individual who tries to ignore the terms of the ban. However, the clause also provides that an individual may offer a defence by proving

that he did not know, and could not reasonably be expected to know,

that he was disqualified from such work. The phrase "reasonably be expected to know" is intended to ensure that an individual cannot escape liability by, for example, moving house to avoid having a notification of disqualification served on him by the Secretary of State.

The second offence will be knowingly to offer work in a regulated position to a disqualified person. That is intended to cover those who know that the persons concerned are disqualified, but nevertheless offer them work in a regulated position. It will catch the predator—the person who deliberately and wilfully employs a disqualified person—but will not bite on the merely inefficient or foolish. The inclusion of the second offence is necessary in the light of the guile, to which I have referred, of people who doubtless operate in rings. If one such person is put in a position of authority, he may employ another, and in doing so groom, use and abuse children.

I should stress that the creation of this offence is not a back-door way of ensuring that people are compelled to carry out checks. In some circumstances, checks are compelled under other legislation. For example, a requirement to check Department of Health lists is imposed on child care organisations under the Protection of Children Act 1999. Outside those areas, the decision whether to carry out checks must be regarded as part of an organisation's overall child protection arrangements.

I should stress that all organisations that look after children should cultivate a culture of vigilance. Our debates, and the Act that one hopes will emerge from this process, are vital to the creation of the correct context, but there is no substitute for an employment and management culture that is itself the depositary of vigilance. Experience shows that, without such a culture, children are at risk. There needs to be a process of interviewing, taking up references and continuous monitoring.

The committing of either offence will incur an appropriately stiff penalty. The ban will act as a barrier to unsuitable people's access to children, and we believe that the offences will prove a meaningful 174 deterrent. It will be evident to those who are disqualified that the law will not allow them to find positions in which they can threaten or harm children.

I understand the thrust of the hon. Gentleman's amendment, but I hope that I can reassure him that it is not necessary to achieving his objectives. We want to ensure that those who pose a risk are prevented from acquiring a position from which they can prey upon children. We have introduced these measures to ensure that, when such people are disqualified, they are banned from a comprehensive range of work in "regulated positions". The new offence should be a powerful disincentive to breaching that disqualification order. The definition specifically covers, by virtue of the definitions in clause 35, work of any kind, whether paid or unpaid. That covers casual work if it falls within the regulated positions in clause 33, so the bite of the provision will not be avoided by casualising labour in a "regulated position".
