The clause would change the provisions of the Local Government Finance Act 1982, of the Local Government Act 1985 and of the Local Government (Scotland) Act 1973 relating to the public inspection, at the time of audit, of the accounts of local authorities and certain other bodies in England and Wales, and in Scotland.

The amendment will mean that purely personal information about employees will not be available for inspection by any persons interested in inspecting the accounts to be audited.

In the new clause we are fulfilling the undertaking that we gave in April 1988 to amend the law relating to the public inspection of local authority accounting records at the time of audit. We gave that undertaking, after consulting the local authority associations and other interested parties, essentially to restore the legal position to that which everyone had thought it was before a High Court ruling in April 1986.

Part of the process of local authority audit involves an authority's accounting records being open to inspection by any person interested, and it is an important element in the overall accountability mechanisms. I need not remind the Committee that the Government attach great importance to the accountability of local authorities, that being part of our purpose in introducing the Bill. This right of inspection enables the public to see how the council has been running its financial affairs, and can provide the means for an elector to obtain the information that he might need for him to exercise his statutory rights of objecting to the auditor about a matter relating to the accounts.

But it would be quite wrong if the right of public inspection enabled a person to discover something about the personal affairs of an authority's employee—for example, that the employee was paying maintenance for children of a previous marriage, or other sensitive information such as contributing to a charity of his choice, or paying dues to his union. It was long thought that the statutory right of inspection would not allow a member of the public to gain access to such information, and clearly, such information had no bearing on how the authority was managing its affairs.

The difficulty was that, in April 1986, the High Court held in a case involving Northampton borough 376 council that the accounting records—in fact, the payroll records—containing such personal information were open to public inspection. That caused great concern. We recognised and accepted that concern, and hence proposed amending the statutory provisions so that once again the law stood as everyone had in the past believed it to be. That is what we are doing today in the new clause.

The purely personal information about employees that the new clause seeks to protect from inspection is information relating specifically to an individual. It is information that appears in the accounts and documents because of the ways in which local authorities and public bodies keep their records—in particular, today, their computerised payrolls.

The information to be protected includes, as I have already indicated, such things as income tax, national insurance contributions, attachment of earnings orders, contributions to charities, and maintenance payments—in practice, the range of deductions from the gross pay, overtime and allowances made to employees and former employees. The provision does not take effect until 1 April 1990 to enable local authorities to make minor adjustments to their computing systems to protect personal information.

I commend the new clause to the Committee.
