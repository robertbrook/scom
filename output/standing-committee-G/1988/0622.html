clause Clause 99
title MEANING OF "CLOSE COMPANY"

Mr. Norman Lamont: I beg to move amendment No. 457, in page 74, line 18, leave out "and section 100 below".

The amendment is a drafting one. The clause is a consequential of the abolition of apportionment. It concerns the definition of "close company", which is used in several places in the tax system. Broadly speaking, a company is a close company if it is under the control of no more than five persons, and for this purpose the immediate members of a family are treated as a single person. Looking at who controls a Image Col. 583 company usually provides a good test of whether the underlying ownership of the company's assets and income is concentrated in a few hands. But it is possible to devise arrangements under which someone is entitled to the bulk of a company's assets and income but does not technically control it. For example, someone might have a minority interest in each company in a chain of companies, so that in total he or she would be entitled to well over half the bottom company's income and assets.

It is right that such a company should be treated as a close company. The current definition does that by using the apportionment legislation to determine the underlying entitlement to the company's income.

The Bill gets rid of apportionment. As the definition of a close company is linked to apportionment, we have had to substitute a new definition, but it is substantially similar to the previous one. The clause provides for that. It looks at the rights to a company's assets if it were wound up. If a few people would be entitled to over half the assets, the company is a close company. The clause deals with cases where there is a chain of companies by looking through to the rights of the ultimate owners.

It is a long-standing feature of our tax system that there are some differences in the tax treatment of similar businesses being carried on by an individual and a company, and in some respects those favour the company, although some reliefs, such as the business expansion scheme and capital gains tax annual exemption, are available only to individuals. The close company apportionment legislation reduces some of the tax advantages available to a company by imposing tax charges on the shareholders. The most important case is the apportionment power that prevents a company accumulating investment income surplus to its business requirements and benefiting from a lower rate of tax than is paid by an individual liable at the higher rates. However, as my hon. Friend the Member for Bridlington (Mr. Townend) is aware, apportionment is notoriously and hideously complex, and although in practice it is applied to only a small minority of close companies, it has to be borne in mind by all companies.

Several hundred thousand close companies with investment income may be in danger, even though trading companies are generally outside the apportionment rules, because their investment income may be brought within the close company apportionment rules. For that reason, we thought it right to remove the vast majority of the half a million close companies from the scope of any special tax charge on their profits and, for the remaining 25,000 close investment companies—close companies that are not trading, but purely investment companies—to abolish apportionment in favour of a relatively simply tax charge.

Even with the top rate of tax down to 40 per cent. there could still be an incentive for some people to hold investments in a close company so as to benefit from the lower rates of tax paid by companies. We felt it necesary to have a replacement for apportionment to avoid giving people the opportunity to reduce their tax on income and capital gains simply by placing their personal investments in a closely controlled investment company. Mr. Lamont: New clauses 30, 31 and 32 introduce provisions to counter devices that enable groups of companies to reduce or eliminate capital gains tax liabilities on the sale of subsidiaries. I emphasise that we are dealing not with ordinary commercial arrangements but with highly artificial avoidance schemes. They are constructed to get round existing provisions that impose a tax charge on gains when assets or companies owning them leave a group.

The basic scheme involves stripping out of a subsidiary unrealised gains in the value of its chargeable assets by means of a tax free intra-group dividend out of artificially created profits, which some in the tax planning industry have come to describe as a drain-out dividend. In that way, the group contrives to shelter from tax what would otherwise be and effectively are realised gains on the sale of the subsidiary. That can even mean a group lending money to one subsidiary to buy an asset from another, to finance a tax-free dividend back to the group.

In attacking those schemes, we have taken steps to safeguard ordinary dividend payments within a group, and a group will not be penalised by these provisions, for extracting profits from a subsidiary before sale, by a dividend payment that is made out of profits and reserves that have already borne tax. The key point is that we are talking about extracting gains from profits that have not borne tax. What is objectionable and what is targeted are cases when the Image Col. 599 dividend is the result of transactions, which convert the crude capital gains into distributable profits on which no tax has been paid.

Artificial they may be, but those schemes are comparatively straightforward and inexpensive to implement. They are capable of being varied in many ways to meet the circumstances of different groups. The potential loss of tax is enormous and runs into hundreds of millions of pounds. So far, the actual loss of tax has been limited, but it has been noticeable that the device of so-called drain-out dividends has begun to be publicised at conferences and in tax advice. Therefore, we have decided to act promptly to deal with the matter. New clauses 31 and 32 follow on from that.

New clause 33 changes the definition of a group of companies for capital gains purposes. At present, a group consists of a principal company, its subsidiaries of which it owns directly or indirectly at least 75 per cent. of the ordinary share capital, any 75 per cent. subsidiaries of those subsidiaries and so on.

The definition of ordinary share capital can include shares which have only restricted rights to the company's profits or assets. The result is that under the existing rules, a group can include companies in which the principal company has only a minimal interest. But all the companies enjoy certain tax advantages associated with membership of a group. For example, they can sell assets to each other without triggering a capital gains charge, because the transfer of the assets is treated as taking place on a no-gain no-loss basis. Mr. Lamont: New clause 39 introduces rules to determine the residence status for income tax purposes of trustees in cases where a settlement has at least one trustee resident in the United Kingdom and at least one who is non-resident. It is necessary as a result of a recent decision of the House of Lords in the case of Dawson v. Commissioners of the Inland Revenue.

The Dawson case concerned a settlement where one of the trustees was resident in the United Kingdom and two were non-resident. The Lords decided that the trustees were not liable to tax on the settlement's foreign income because they were not all resident in the United Kingdom.

If the law was now left unchanged, it would be easy for a settlement with trustees who are currently all resident in the United Kingdom to escape tax merely by appointing an additional trustee who was non-resident and investing the trust funds overseas. I am sure the Committee will agree that that would not be satisfactory.

We are therefore proposing rules which will treat mixed residence trustees as all resident in the United Kingdom if the settlor was resident, ordinarily resident or domiciled in the United Kingdom at any time when he put funds into the settlement. Otherwise, they will all be treated as non-resident. That is simple, fair and objective. It means that a settlement with a United Kingdom settlor will be taxed on its foreign income so long as the connection with this country is maintained by keeping at least Image Col. 609 one resident trustee. The settlor will no longer be able to escape tax on the income of the settlement merely by appointing a non-resident trustee. That is right, because this country can be regarded as the natural home of such settlements.

Conversely, a foreign settlor will be able to appoint United Kingdom trustees and preserve non-resident status for his settlement, by ensuring that he retains at least one non-resident trustee. His settlement will then not be liable to United Kingdom tax on its foreign income. The proposed test will thus do no harm to British professional expertise in the administration and advising of trust, so I think that it achieves the right balance.

When mixed residence trustees fall to be treated under the clause as being not resident—that is, when the settlor is foreign—they will also be treated as being resident outside the United Kingdom. So, when assets are transferred to such mixed residence trustees—that is, their status changes as a result of these provisions—the existing provisions relating to the transfer of assets abroad will apply.

Briefly, the existing provisions apply when assets are transferred and as a result, income becomes payable to a person resident outside the United Kingdom. They apply both to the individual directly involved in the transfer of assets and to those who benefit from such arrangements but who are not directly involved with the transfer.

The clause applies generally to income for the tax year 1989–90 and subsequent years. But there are transitional provisions for settlements which currently have mixed residence trustees. Because we did not make our announcment on this until 15 June and the decision was not made by the House of Lords until May, we thought it right to allow a breathing space, and I am sure that the hon. Member for Islington, South and Finsbury will agree that that is reasonable.

As to the foreign income of such settlements, the trustees are not taxable at present because of the Dawson decision. In some cases, they were not taxable on that income even before Dawson. But they will be taxable in future if the settlement has a United Kingdom settlor. In those circumstances, it could be considered unreasonable for the clause to impose tax on the income which has arisen in the present tax year. Accordingly, we propose giving such trustees a breathing space.
