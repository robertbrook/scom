Local Government and Housing Bill

clause New clause 2
title REMUNERATION FOR COUNCILLORS

"(1) The Secretary of State shall, after consultation with the local authority associations and such other bodies as he considers appropriate, make regulations for the purpose of establishing a comprehensive system of remuneration for councillors elected to membership of a local authority, which takes account of the size and functions of such an authority.

(2) The levels of remuneration determined in regulations made under this section shall be revised annually in line with the retail price index.".—[Mr. Blunkett.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Question proposed [this day], That the clause be read a Second time.

Question again proposed.

Mr. Cecil Franks (Barrow and Furness): When we adjourned earlier today, I was developing the theme of the necessity to improve the calibre of the local council representative. I make no apology for speaking on a subject about which I have felt strongly from the moment when I entered local government more than 25 years ago, in 1960, and I know other Conservative Members who share the same general feeling that I have about it.

I remember my introduction to politics as a young boy, after the war, when my father became a member of the then Salford city council—he remained a member until 1968. I recall listening to the late Labour Member of Parliament for the constituency that was then called Salford, North. He visited my home regularly, and he was also an alderman of the council. As a young boy, I used to listen to them discussing local and national politics, and my interest in politics developed from that.

However, as politicians, we make a mistake in assuming that the wider public are as interested in the world of politics as we are. One has only to ask people, "Can you name your local councillor?" to realise how few can do so. If one takes the exercise further and asks, "Can you name your Member of Parliament?" it can be a salutary exercise.

In an intervention in the Minister's speech, I said that one of the fundamental weaknesses of local government is that no one is trained in local government procedure before becoming a member. It is rather like when one first becomes a Member of Parliament, or one's first day at school: one sinks or swims; one asks the way around.

Col. 398

The Chairman: Order. I am reluctant to interrupt the hon. Gentleman, whose speech is fascinating, but it has little relevance to the remuneration of councillors. I ask him to confine his remarks to the new clause.

Mr. Franks: I appreciate that, Mr. Hunt, but I am attempting to relate my comments to the calibre of the local government representative and, through that, to the necessity, not the option, of placing politics, especially local government politics, on a respectable and responsible level, and of rewarding the job for what it is worth. If a job is worth doing, it is worth paying the proper rate.

The Minister said that he was not in favour of payment in the form of wages for local councillors, but that he was in favour of contributions towards expenses. With great respect to the Minister and his colleagues, those self-same arguments were used 100 years ago in response to the question about whether Members of Parliament should be paid, and local government has not moved on in those 100 years.

The budgets of many local authorities are in excess of those of many small countries with the right to sit in the United Nations assembly—for example, Malta and some of the Pacific islands. Some of the major local authorities have larger budgets than certain Departments of the House, which warrants a more sophisticated approach to how we recruit and remunerate our local representatives.

Opposition Members try to suggest that there has not been a proliferation of meetings for which there is no justification, but in my experience that is a growing trend, which is greatly to be deplored. Opposition Members also try to deny the increasing interference by local councillors in executive functions and in the day-to-day running of councils, and the Minister's remarks were correct. Councillors should formulate policies, give instructions, leave officers to carry out those policies and bring them to book if they do not.

Interference in the civil service of local government is part and parcel of the philosophy of the far Left and reflects such local councillors' lack of experience and training. They cannot differentiate between the policy function and the executive function. Unless we can recruit the right calibre of person to local government, we shall have one local government Act after another, year in, year out, irrespective of the party in Government. We must tackle the root weakness of local government—the fact that the calibre of those who administrate locally is deteriorating rather than improving.

A year or so ago a Labour councillor with Cumbria county council was the Labour party candidate against me at the general election. He lost his job in the local shipyards because his responsibilities on Cumbria county council involved him being chairman of the Lake district special planning board and he did not put in the hours at work that his employers wanted. I appreciate the problems for both the employers and the individual. At about the same time the then Labour leader of Barrow district council lost his job in similar circumstances, as have Conservatives involved in local government.

Image Col. 399 Over the years I have seen councillors from all parties make tremendous sacrifices because of their involvement in local politics. I know of local business men who became hooked on politics and neglected their firms to get into the town hall, with the result that they went bankrupt, their families suffered and their marriages broke up. I have seen other councillors forgo promotion. The choice was theirs, but they could not give all their time to their job because they wished to be involved in local government.

It is wrong in principle that people should have to make such choices. If local government and local politics are worth while and something that we wish to encourage, we must pay fair and proper remuneration. Otherwise we must turn the clock back 150 years and regard local government as parish pump politics that people can pursue in their spare time as a hobby.

Members of Parliament are privileged to be paid to do what was a hobby before we were elected. If we accept the principle that Members of Parliament are entitled to be paid—whether it is the correct rate for the job is an interesting argument for another occasion—the same should apply to councillors in local politics.

Mr. Robin Squire (Hornchurch): I concur with my hon. Friend's comments about the sacrifice that many people make to be involved in local government.

Mr. Franks: People involved in local or national politics must be very rich or very poor. Those in the middle cannot afford to be involved. The figure is a matter of opinion. The new clause makes it subject to the Secretary of State's concurrence.

Reference has been made to the problems of officers. I have worked with many officers in local government over many years and I could count on one hand and have fingers to spare those whom I would employ in a private capacity. The reason for that is that no self-respecting officer would put up with the nonsensical way in which local government is administered. There is a proliferation of committees and increasing interference in executive functions from elected members.

I have friends who are local government officers but, with honourable exceptions, I regard the officers in local government as professional rejects from the private sector.

Mr. Franks: The hon. Gentleman says that that is a bit strong. His comments are moderate; I have heard far worse remarks than what I have said.

Side by side with the deterioration in the calibre of the local councillor is the deterioration in the calibre of the professional officer. I am not surprised that this Government and Labour Administrations have run into difficulties.

I have said sufficient for hon. Members to be crystal clear about where I stand on the principle of the issue. If, tomorrow morning, we went as shareholders to the annual general meeting of a big public company and found that the board of directors was conducting its affairs as they conducted in the House or in local government, we would sack those people on the spot. But we tolerate our political system because we who are involved become "establishmentised".

The reform of our political system in national and local government is long overdue. But the prerequisite is to make national and local politics an honourable profession, which its practitioners can be proud of and which the public can respect.

Most councillors would prefer a salary or remuneration rather than an allowance. We have listened to the pros and cons, but no one has said that claiming allowances is open to abuse. Unfortunately, many councillors abuse the system. If remuneration were paid, there would be no abuse.

I found it embarrassing to claim my attendance allowance. I felt that it was wrong, even though it was legal, as some councillors did not claim. It would be better if local councillors were not subjected to that embarrassment. It was made worse because many unscrupulous people, who did not understand the workings of local government, got lists of claims from the treasurer's department and published them in the newspapers. Although the claims were justified, it looked as though councillors were greedy in claiming allowances.

In Durham it was ironic that councillors who did not claim were sometimes beaten at elections. Although that had nothing to do with submitting claims, that was strange.

As councillors become more senior in local government they attend more meetings and consequently claim more allowances. For me, that became increasingly embarrassing. As a "back bencher" I attended only a few meetings, but as I became more senior I became a member of more committees and claimed more allowances. From what the Minister said this morning I got the impression that he thought meetings were set up so that councillors could attend and claim an allowance for doing so. I objected to attending so many meetings. For 10 years I did not see my family grow up. I have two kids, but sometimes I could not even remember their names because I had been away from Image Col. 401 home for so long. I worked during the day and went to council meetings at 4 o'clock. Often I did not return home until 9 o'clock—that happened almost every night of the week. I did it because I loved it, not because I wanted to attend as many meetings as possible so that I could claim as much as possible. I felt that the Minister was accusing councillors of doing that.

In my local authority we declined the special responsibility allowances because we felt that by accepting them we should be criticised by the electorate of feathering our nests. We were daft, because we were entitled to those allowances, but we did not take them because we feared what the public might think.

Many of my colleagues, particularly those who worked in the collieries, had to chose between tenants' allowances, financial loss allowances and goodness knows what else. It is not right that some people should be out of pocket because they want to serve and do good for their community. A salary would prevent that. Members found that their benefits were affected. They did not know whether they could claim. It is silly that they should have those worries on top of trying to represent their electorate. Ours must be the only country in western Europe to allow that.

I know a man who was active in the Labour party and when he was invalided out of the colliery, we asked him whether he would stand at the local elections. He refused because he was frightened that he might lose his benefits, or that there would be problems with income tax and the Department of Social Security. Although he was quite capable, willing and would have made an excellent candidate, he was restricted because of the fear that he might lose the money that would help to support his family. In a democracy it is not right to take away as the Bill does, the rights of many people to participate in local government because they are worried about their pension or benefit rights. That is appalling.

Col. 412 There is a difference between what is proposed in the amendment and what now happens in Northern Ireland, so it is different from amendment No. 150. The most significant difference between the legislation that applies in Northern Ireland and that in England, Wales and Scotland relates to the power to enforce the recommendations of the local ombudsman. In Northern Ireland there is legal provision for complainants to apply to the county court for an award of damages when an authority has failed to provide a remedy in line with the recommendations. No such provision exists in the rest of the United Kingdom. I advised constituents to go to the local ombudsman who has made recommendations which the local council may not accept. If the local authority refuses to accept the recommendations there is nothing that they can do, other than to kick up a fuss and get some publicity. My constituents face serious financial consequences, including the loss of their home.

The Local Government Act 1974 provides that when the local ombudsman concludes that maladministration has led to injustice to a complainant, a report is produced setting it out and an appropriate remedy is suggested. If the authority fails to take action, a further report is published which specifies a precise remedy for the complainant. If the authority still fails to provide a remedy, the local ombudsman is helpless to enforce the recommendations.

In the first 12 years—to March 1986—of the existence of the Commission for Local Administration in England, 140 further reports were issued and in only 38 cases was there a satisfactory outcome. That is a failure rate of 73 per cent. In about 6.5 per cent. of cases in which both maladministration and injustice were found, no satisfactory remedy was provided. The figure of 6.5 per cent. may not sound high, but it is significant to my constituents who have lost their home, partly as Image Col. 413 a result of what has happened. Even more worrying is the fact that 19 per cent. of local authorities in England—a total of 68—failed to provide a remedy even in the face of a further report. The problem of local authorities not accepting the recommendations of the local ombudsman is extensive, although it does not happen in every case.

In January 1985, faced with the possibility of legislation to allow enforcement of the local ombudsman's decisions, local authority associations urged member authorities to supply the remedies recommended even when they disagreed with them. But since then there has been no marked improvement in compliance. The result has been continued disappointment and disillusionment for individual complainants who have not received the remedies that they deserve and a growing danger that the system will be discredited.

Many bodies with an interest in the ombudsman system have commented on enforcement, including most significantly, the ombudsmen themselves. In the 1984 review of the Local Government Act 1974, the Commission for Local Administration in England called on the relevant Secretary of State:

"to urge all authorities always to provide prompt and positive remedies when injustice is found by a Local Ombudsman".

It said that

"they should introduce legislation for the enforcement of remedies if a marked improvement is not made voluntarily in reasonable time".

It commended the Northern Ireland model, but there has been no improvement. The Committee should draw the same conclusion as the Commission for Local Administration in England, that local authorities should act on its recommendations.

In his annual report for the same year, the ombudsman for Scotland concluded that statutory provision for enforcement had become necessary and added

"My predecessors and I have been very reluctant to come to this conclusion, feeling that it was much better to retain the voluntary system as long as failure to comply were a rarity, but the time has now come to say that the voluntary system is not working adequately."

The Welsh local ombudsman wrote in evidence to the Widdicombe committee:

"it is intolerable that any injustice caused by maladministration identified in the course of an objective and impartial investigation should be allowed to go unremedied."

The Widdicombe committee took up his view by recommending that

"there should be a new statutory right for complainants to apply to the county or sheriff court for a remedy in cases where the local ombudsman has found maladministration leading to injustice and the complainant is dissatisfied with by the remedy offered by the local authority."

Widdicombe recommended that the right should be based on the system in Northern Ireland.

The third report of the Select Committee on the Parliamentary Commissioner for Administration published in June 1986 reached a similar conclusion. The Committee took evidence from local ombudsmen, the PCA, local authority representatives and from Ministers and concluded:

The Justice recommendation referred to in the Select Committee report was made following a study in 1980 of the first five years of operation of the ombudsmen system. In a book published in 1988, Justice concluded:

"enforcement through the courts does offer the best solution to what has become a major problem."

Justice recommended the following conditions precedent on the application to the court:

"(a) a report containing a finding in favour of the person aggrieved that he had suffered injustice in consequence of maladministration;
