Mr. Blunkett

Having said that, though, I must say that this is one of the most despicable proposals that even Marsham Street could imagine. I hope that Ministers will think again about the ring-fencing that they propose before the Bill goes to the House of Lords.

Those who take responsibility for their families and their neighbourhood should—indeed, they would want—to contribute towards management costs, debt charges, and the residual expenses of sheltered housing for elderly people and expensive adaptations for people with disabilities. Special housing is more expensive and falls on the Housing Revenue Account, which we discussed briefly this morning. We must consider which warden schemes should fall under the Housing Revenue Account.

The Housing Revenue Account has been ring-fenced so that ghettos are created. In them, one third of tenants are responsible for the benefits and costs of the other two thirds, who receive income support under the housing benefit scheme. Ministers at what was the Department of Health and Social Security and at the Department of the Environment made it clear to local authorities that income 864 support was no business of theirs and that they should not be engaged in education grants or benefits to support the local community because that was the responsibility of the social security system.

However, the Bill will remove the housing benefit system from council tenants and transfer it to the housing revenue.

However, as later clauses reveal, the housing benefit system will be taken away from council tenants and transferred to the Housing Revenue Account, to be designated a housing cost rather than an income support cost falling on the individual.

I do not believe that Ministers have a rational reason for making the small and dwindling number of council tenants, who can afford full rent, responsible for the ring-fenced benefits and residual costs that arise from community provision, to which we should all contribute and for which we are all responsible.

The Government spelt out three reasons as justification for ring-fencing—the desire to improve the efficiency and accountability of local authorities, the desire to improve the efficiency of central government resource allocation and the desire to influence the level of rents. They clearly reveal the Government's intention, but the last is the true reason. The Under-Secretary of State for the Environment struggled to find the right words this morning, but it is increasing rents, not necessarily to a set market level but to one related to housing sales, arising from the right-to-buy legislation, and the landlords' charter—the so-called tenants' choice; it is hard to remember such an Orwellian term—which are at the centre of this proposal. The measures will force those who can afford to pay full rents to opt out of the system to survive.

It would ensure that people do not fall into the nightmare and poverty trap of claiming, but it does, and increasingly will, result in people just above the benefit threshold—those who are not so badly off and are affording their own rents, who are in work but are struggling to make ends meet—bearing the burden of paying benefits for their neighbours. It amounts to that. These people are always hit because they are just above that crucial poverty trap level of receipt of income support and benefit.

The Bill incorporates that concept. It provides that, when there is no subsidy from the Government, authorities will be able to contribute outwards from Housing Revenue Account to poll tax payers—at that point only, not before. There are good reasons why the Government do not want it to happen before, given the scheme that they are about to implement.

As that concept is clearly on record and the intention is to phase out the national subsidy, who will pick up that part of the subsidy that is currently paid direct as income support to tenants on housing benefit? It will be those council tenants who remain such and who do not receive any benefit. It has to be those who are paying full rents.

There is a twist in the tale here. Eventually, if the Government get their way and sufficient people take up their right to buy, or opt out to housing associations or private landlords, the national bill will increase as they continue to receive personal income support housing benefit, and the number who were in the benefit scheme within the Housing Revenue Account will increase to a point where the Government will have twisted around and eaten their tail. No one who was not entitled to a benefit that they repaid themselves would be in public sector housing, because they would be the only people paying into the Housing Revenue Account. The Government would not be paying because they would have phased it out. The local authority would not be permitted to do so through poll tax payers or the successor option that we shall introduce. They will not receive a local subsidy either, so who will pay?

I assume that, eventually, the one third who are paying full rent will dwindle to one sixth, one eighth or one tenth, and the burden upon them will become greater until there is almost saintdom for the few people left in each street who will be directly responsible for social needs housing for the remainder of the community. That is the bizarre effect that these proposals will have.

Were it not a serious matter, were people not at the receiving end of the proposals and were communities not washing their hands of the housing benefit payment for a large part of the community generally, we would think that it was a bizarre joke, but it is not, and nor is taking away the local contribution from people who are in other forms of sheltered or special needs housing.

That is why we oppose clause 64 and the subsequent clauses which change the system unnecessarily. They do not increase efficiency because higher rents do not equal efficiency. Nor does it lower spending on housing. We have seen the dangers of that, under Labour Governments as well. A lack of investment has resulted in greater costs later in picking up the disrepair, deterioration and lack of interest in investment in stock, whether from subsidy or from rentpayers. It was said earlier that local authority JPEG 867 housing management has much to be proud of in terms of cost and effectiveness vis-à-vis other forms of tenure, including housing associations.

On each count, the proposals are not only unacceptable in terms of housing—they are unacceptable because of their impact on individuals and on families. I note what the Minister said earlier about the need always to be mindful of the impact of legislation on the wellbeing and rights of individuals. It is not acceptable to believe that what is ideologically correct overrides considerations of the consequences for those who turn out to be the victims. I challenge the Minister to say why the Government believe that their proposals will not create ghettos, increase the poverty trap and penalise council tenants on full rents, and will not segregate council tenants from people with all other forms of housing tenure—owner-occupiers, housing association tenants or private landlord tenants. The personal income support that they receive allows them to pay affordable rents and maintain their dignity, and ensures equality, fairness and justice in society, instead of a society based on the desire to eliminate the public sector.
