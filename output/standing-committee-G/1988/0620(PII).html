Interest rates apply to all open charges and when rates are changed, the new rates apply from that day on, and across the board, to all open charges.

On the day on which the new procedures come into effect, there will inevitably be a large number of tax charges in respect of which payment has not finally been made. Some will remain open for a considerable period after that, for instance awaiting the determination of an appeal or a valuation of a property—inheritance tax, in particular, can take a considerable period to determine. Changes to interest rates made after the new procedures come into effect ought to apply both to new charges, made after the new procedures come into effect, and to existing charges, made before the new procedures came into effect, which are still open.

There is nothing sinister about that. If there were an adjustment in interest rates under the existing system, that would happen anyway. Clause 173
BROADCASTING: ADDITIONAL PAYMENTS BY PROGRAMME CONTRACTORS

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Chris Smith
: Clause 173 brings into force schedule 16. I have some more detailed points to make about the schedule, which we can perhaps discuss when we debate it. However, it would be useful to use this clause stand part debate to discuss the principles underlying the change that the Government seek to make to the levy on independent television contractors. I should perhaps remind the Committee, as I usually do when I speak on broadcasting matters, that I have a close association with the Association of Cinematograph, Television and Allied Technicians, the film technicians' trade union, which makes an annual payment to my constituency Labour party. However, the principles at stake are of much more general importance than that. It is in the interests of programme consumers—the viewers at home—that I wish to express concern about what the Government are doing.

528 The change that the Government are introducing will ensure that from 1990 the ITV levy is in two parts. There will be a levy on net advertising revenue of 10 per cent. and a levy on domestic profits of 25 per cent. Both those levies will allow a free slice of income, not subject to the levy, of £15 million on revenue plus the equivalent of the fourth channel subscription and a free slice of £2 million on profits. The levy on profits will be assessed after allowing for payment of the revenue levy. There will be no levy on profits from overseas sales.

The levy has had a chequered history in the past few years. It has been subject to scrutiny by the Comptroller and Auditor General. It has also been subject to scrutiny on a number of occasions by the Public Accounts Committee. I suspect that part of the reason for the Government's action in the clause and the attached schedule is their desire to take account of the PAC's criticisms. The Government appear to be trying to do things through the changes. First, they want to encourage what the Comptroller and Auditor General described as cost-consciousness and programming efficiency. I shall return to that issue in a moment. The Government are shifting the burden of the levy from profits, where it rests entirely at the moment to advertising revenue and profits, with a much lower take on profits than there is now. The idea that appears to lie behind what the Government are doing is that it will encourage cost-consciousness among the programmers and the indepent television stations. That seems to be the first reason for the Government's proposals.

9.15 pm

The second reason appears to lie in the original purpose of the levy, which was to recompense the Exchequer for the monopolitistic position of the ITV channels. It has traditionally been thought that a contribution to the nation, for the use of national assets of television frequencies and stations, should be made by the monopoly producers and suppliers in the industry. Again, I shall return to that because changes are occuring in television and it is important to realise that the justification for that aspect of the levy is diminishing over time.

The third reason for the Government's changes appears to be to make up for lost revenue. That is the point that the Public Accounts Committee was after in its recent reports. It judged that there was approximately a £19 million loss in each of the past two financial years on the previous method of calculating the levy as a result of the current calculation. The Government say that their change would restore the previous position, so that the lost revenue to which the Public Accounts Committee drew attention would be recouped for the Exchequer. However, there is a slight problem. It seems that the new system that the Treasury hopes to introduce will bring in considerably more income than just making good the shortfall would do. Once again, I shall return to that subject.

The Government's proposal is to move to a levy on net advertising revenue coupled with a levy on profits. Of course, things will change again in 1992. We are talking of a period between 1990 and 1992 to which the change will apply. It is a little difficult JPEG 529 to understand exactly what the Government will do in 1992. At that time, if the Government have their way—[ Interruption.] My hon. Friend the Member for Kirkaldy (Dr. Moonie) rightly says that by the time that we reach 1992, there will undoubtedly have been a change of Government so perhaps we should be talking—

Mr. Smith
: So perhaps we should be talking not about what the Government will do, but much more about what they propose to do in 1992, although I hope that they will not have that opportunity.

In their proposals for broadcasting and the franchising of the ITV stations, the Government are unclear about the future of the levy in the new franchising system that they propose to operate. As Members will know, the Home Secretary proposes that, effectively, post-1992, the ITV channels should be up for auction to the highest bidder. He says that stations will have to cross a quality threshold and deposit a bond, but at heart the price mechanism will determine who gets the ITV stations.

Under the proposals that the Home Secretary announced to the House only last week, after 1992 the bid for the franchise will apparently be an offer of a percentage of advertising revenue. It appears that between 1990 and 1992 the Government will place the bulk of the importance on advertising revenue with a bit on profits but that, from 1992, they will move towards a system that depends on a share of advertising revenue as part of the bid that a station makes when it seeks a franchise. We are discussing a change that will apply for two years, and for two years only.

We envisagee considerable problems as a result of the proposals. Problem No. one is that the Home Office press release issued on Budget day estimates that the new levy from 1990 will raise about £40 million a year more than the present levying system. I do not want to beef about extra income to the Exchequer, but £40 million seems considerably greater than the shortfall of £19 million that the Public Accounts Committee identified in its report for the past two financial years. It appears that the Exchequer is seeking to take an even greater slice of income from television stations than it would if it were simply restoring the old system. The extra income will have to come from somewhere, and my suspicion is that it will come from programme quality.

Problem No. two relates to programme quality. The proposed additional imposition on ITV companies will inevitably place downward pressure on programme budgets and, in the long run, on programme quality. ITV stations will be urged at every fiscal corner to cut costs in order to minimise the tax burden imposed by the levy. They will always find it more tempting to buy cheap programmes from overseas rather than to take the expensive step of making their own programmes.

530 The best quality programmes on British television are traditionally made by British-based television companies or independent producers. They cost more than programmes bought off the shelf, which will be a profound disincentive to programme quality under the proposals, when cost will be much more the name of the game than it is now.

There has been considerable response to the Government's proposals from the television industry. The chairman of the Independent Television Association said of the Government's levy proposal:

"The Exchequer will benefit at the expense of viewers as programme expenditure will have to be cut."

That is the nub of the problem. Downward pressure on programme quality and the fact that there will be no investment offset available against the levy payment will inevitably lead to a decline in standards.

Problem No. three is that the benefit for overseas sales, which appears to be built into the new system, will prove illusory in the long run. Overseas sales will be possible only if we have quality programmes to sell. As the downward pressure on cost, budgets and quality begins to bite over the two-year period, the availability of income from overseas sales will tend to diminish.

Then we come to problem No. four. Inevitably, some costs will be passed on to advertisers, and although the Government expects that the bulk of income under the new levy system will come from within the programme stations, I suspect that some of the costs will be passed on to the advertising agencies and companies. That, in turn, will tend to lead to a diminution of the overall pool of advertising expenditure and material. It is happening at a time when there are growing organs of competition within the media for advertising revenue, and if at the same time the cost of advertising is increased as a result of the levy, the question arises of whether there will be sufficiently keen advertisers to take up the spaces available.

Problem No. five: the extra costs will hit television stations differently. Television companies that have high profits but relatively low advertising budgets will do comparatively better than the larger television stations with large advertising budgets. The most obvious example of a company that will gain under the Government's proposed system is TV-am, but other television companies will suffer. Mr. Akers, the media analyst at Citycorp, estimates that Central television will pay about £25.5 million in the new levy in 1990, compared with just over £12 million in the past. He also estimates that LWT, Thames and Yorkshire will pay between 40 per cent. and 45 per cent. more than they are paying at present, and that Harlech television's bill will increase by 72 per cent. So the Government are introducing substantial increases in the amount that they are demanding from independent television companies. It is a near doubling of the amount for Central, almost a 50 per cent. increase in the LWT, Thames and Yorkshire contributions, and a three quarters increase in HTV's contribution. It is quite likely, at the same time, that TV-am's contribution will fall. There seems to be little logic or justice in that.

JPEG 531 Problem No. six: it seems odd that the Government are planning major disruption of the levy system just two years before changes in the franchising are to be introduced. It strikes us as odd that such enormous changes in payments made by television companies should be introduced at a time when the future of those companies is made so uncertain by the changes that the Government suggested in their broadcasting White Paper, to be introduced in their Bill next year.

What will happen if a television station owns an advertising agency—or vice versa—and especially if part of the proposals spills over into the years after 1992? Under paragraph 1(5) of schedule 16, in line 16 of page 233, it seems that it would be possible for a television station and an advertising agency to agree the price for advertising at a rate that would diminish the amount payable to the Exchequer under the levy. The Government do not appear to have foreseen that problem. The case may be hypothetical, but it suggests a possible route for tax avoidance for a television station and advertising agency bright enough to spot it. The Bill gives little protection against a shifting around of rates and charges to achieve such a saving.

We have, therefore, many doubts about the wisdom of the changes that the Government propose. We accept that the arguments of the Comptroller and Auditor General and of the Public Accounts Committee ought to be noted, but the Government are apparently going far further and bringing in changes that will take a great deal of money out of the independent television companies, at the expense of programme-making, investment and programme quality. Ultimately, that will be to the detriment of millions of viewers. I am sure that the Government do not intend that, and that the people do not want it.

The television industry has been trying to gird its loins in preparation for the invasion from space—as the advent of trans-national broadcasting has been called.

The rationalisations have been part of an overall attempt by British television producers to cut costs—the prime purpose of clause 173. Unfortunately, until now that has been done in time-honoured British fashion, not by slicing the 532 inflated salaries of the boardroom members and their star presenters, but by limiting the resources and staff available for the excellent productions that have won this country a reputation as the home of some of the best television in the world.

There is a danger that clause 173 and schedule 16 could encourage this process, when our television producers should be urged to perpetuate and extend these excellent achievements. That is the only way for British television to survive as a major and innovative force in competition with producers, mostly based in America, who can lavish funds on programmes, features and series in the certain knowledge that they have a virtually guaranteed market in the English-speaking—or American-speaking—world.

What tax concessions will they get?

I am uneasy about the clause. We have to be careful. Such is the industry's state of flux that we could be dealing it a severe blow. It may not be a death blow, but it could limit horizons instead of expanding them.

Mr. Norman Lamont
: I shall explain the background to the new clauses because for once, the hon. Gentleman, who is extremely perceptive, has not understood their purpose. As it is important, I shall outline their purpose.

As foreshadowed at the time of the Budget, the new clauses provide the power for the Government to make regulations relating to stamp duty and stamp duty reserve tax. Their purpose is to enable both taxes to be adapted to the new methods of transferring shares that are now being developed in the stock exchange. The most important method is the stock exchange's plan to move to a paperless system of holding and of transferring shares. I am sure that the hon. Gentleman is familiar with the initiative known as TAURUS. We welcome that initiative, as it holds out the possibility of cheaper dealing costs and is likely to give considerable impetus to wider share ownership. But we also need to cater for revised arrangements for the initial period of dealing following a new share issue.

JPEG 577 None of these developments is yet final—they are all still being evolved. The tax rules must therefore evolve with them and, for the time being, will need to be flexible. That is why we are not introducing primary legislation this year. Instead we have proposed regulation-making powers to deal with any transactions under the new methods that take place in the evolutionary period. We have had detailed consultation with all those involved.

With the switch to paperless share dealing, stamp duty will no longer be payable—one cannot stamp something that does not exist. The main tax will therefore become stamp duty reserve tax, which will be charged every time there is an agreement to transfer shares. So, with a system such as TAURUS, which makes provision for errors to be rectified and mismatched bargains to be unscrambled, a single completed bargain could end up paying tax many times over.

The hon. Member for Islington, South and Finsbury also asked about new clause 23, which I shall deal with now. New clause 23 supplements new clauses 21 and 22 by dealing with a narrow administrative point. When information about stamp duty reserve tax liabilities comes from the taxpayer's own computer—or from TAURUS itself—and is in turn processed by an Inland Revenue computer, the Revenue clearly needs to be able to ask for that information to be presented to it in a particular format. For example, the new clause gives the Revenue the power to prescribe the size and type of computer disc and the order in which the information should be set out.

The basic purpose of the new clauses is to cover the move to the paperless system and adapting stamp duty reserve tax to that new world.
