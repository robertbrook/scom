Clause 35
BORROWING LIMITS ETC

Mr. John Battle
(Leeds, West)
: I beg to move amendment No. 273, in page 43, line 15, leave out subsection (2) and insert—

"(2) When determining any question relating to borrowing a local authority shall have regard to any Code of Practice or other guidance issued by a local authority association, the Audit Commission, or the Chartered Institute of Public Finance and Accountancy.".

I wish to ask the Minister why more power is needed. The clause introduces a power for the Secretary of State to regulate borrowing

"in the interests of prudent financial management".

The provision gives the Secretary of State scope that is not needed. We prefer a requirement that is based on self-regulation by local government with independent bodies, such as the Audit Commission, in the background.

The Bill introduces a regulatory regime to determine the borrowing requirements that will be introduced, but that leaves open whether they may be tightened in future. The significant words are

"in the interests of prudent financial management".

The Chancellor sold his Budget this year on the basis that it was prudent, but that word may be interpreted ambiguously. At the very least, there may be controversy about its meaning.

It is wholly unnecessary for the Secretary of State to have a power to interfere when most local authorities follow a voluntary code of good practice. In addition, there are independent back-ups. The new system would confer on the Secretary of State a reserve power, exercisable with the consent of the Treasury, to make regulations at a latter date to conform to the Government's definition of "prudent borrowing" instead of the local authority's or independent body's definition. There may be conflict. Regulations may require that the ratio between the amount of a local authority's short-term borrowing and its annual revenue should not exceed a prescribed level.

A double standard is beginning to emerge on borrowing. The Government often suggest that borrowing is not desirable, yet half the junk mail that we all receive encourages and exhorts people to borrow more and to move towards a cashless society and plastic money. It is striking that one person may be in debt and blamed for it—the debt may be only £150—but if someone else uses a credit card to borrow £150, he is said to be in credit. It seems that one person's credit is another's debt, depending on who makes the judgment although both may have borrowed exactly the same amount.

Local councils cannot play the markets, especially the financial market, because they are fettered. They 646 cannot take advantage of short-term changes in the money rate or interest rates. They are fettered, but 17-year-old computer whizzkids can break into systems and play the market through telephone boxes when councils are not allowed, by law, to make short-term money in the market.

At the same time, the Government would say of the money market that it can and should be self-regulating. Why can there be self-regulation in the city and not in local government? If the answer is that the Government must contain and control local authority borrowing powers in all circumstances, that implies that the Government do not trust local authorities to organise their own borrowing, even within the context set out in the amendment.

At the very least, if the Government intend to leave this clause in the Bill, they should define the exact nature and extent of the reserve powers of the Secretary of state over local authority borrowing to remove any vagueness in subsection (2) and the uncertainty that could be left in the new system.

Will the Minister give details of the powers available to constrain local authority borrowing and say how they will be used? I trust that she will accept our argument that it is greatly preferable to have self-regulation in local government with support from the independent bodies which, I hope we all agree, can provide the necessary background.

: I beg to move amendment No. 62, in page 50, line 47, after "(1)", insert

"not later than three months."

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to take amendment No. 289, in page 50, line 47, leave out

"Before the beginning of each financial year"

and insert

"Before the 30th November in the year preceding each financial year".

Sir George Young
: In reply to an earlier debate, my right hon. Friend the Minister said that it was important for local authorities to plan ahead. No one would dissent from that. The Bill enables the Secretary of State to issue credit approvals up to 31 March, before the financial year begins on 1 April. It would be difficult to plan against that background. Capital budgeting requires careful forward planning and local authorities must know well in advance of 31 March what resources will be available to them.

Central Government budgeting for the financial year beginning on 1 April is usually completed by the end of November, which is when the public expenditure survey committee round normally draws to a close. At present, the RSG supplement figure is usually announced in December and confirmed in January. Local authorities are anxious that their basic credit approvals are notified to them at least by 31 December, which would be the impact of my amendment. The Opposition's amendment is even more ambitious and suggests the earlier date of 30 November. I shall not quibble too much if my right hon. Friend accepts the earlier date.

It is important not to let the Bill go through at present as it allows the Secretary of State to issue basic credit approvals on 30 March. No one could plan ahead sensibly on that basis. I hope that my right hon. Friend can accept the amendment or assure me—as he did so ably a few moments ago—that my anxiety is needless, that another part of the Bill provides the assurance that I seek and that the amendment is not required. I hope that he can allay my anxiety.

Mr. Soley
: The clause is extremely important and our amendments are designed to mitigate some ot its effects on local authorities. I have considerable sympathy with the opening comments of the hon. Member for Ealing, Acton (Sir G. Young) about the importance of local authorities being able to plan ahead. It is clear from the drafting of the clause that the Government still tend not to regard that aspect as important. They pay lip service to the idea, but continue to make local authority planning difficult.

The hon. Gentleman is right to say that our amendment is similar to his, except that it would give local authorities four months' notice, instead of three. It would allow a local authority to have an idea before the Christmas period of what resources JPEG 683 it would receive. Even with the hon. Gentleman's amendment, local authorities would be asked to make decisions on the basis of information that could be given to them as late as the beginning of the new year. It would be better if the authorities received it a month before Christmas when many of them could work out their financial requirements for the following year. Will the Government consider that?

The Minister would not ask a private company to operate on that basis. As part of its normal procedure, it could not be expected to operate its business if it were told the day before the beginning of the new financial year how much it could borrow. A local authority should be given sufficient time to work out its financial requirements for the following 12 months. The more time that is allocated to planning, the more effective such planning would be. That is the aim of our amendment.

684 The proposed systems of captial expenditure controls focus on the control of expenditure financed by borrowing and the control of credit arrangements. The Government seek to achieve their aim through annual credit approvals that allow local authorities to borrow or enter into credit arrangements up to the level of the approval granted by the Secretary of State. We object to the tight timescale in the Bill. It must be borne in mind that local authorities are required to issue their precepts by 1 March. That process must take into account the revenue implications of the capital programme, the levels of which are dependent, in part, on the credit approvals given by the Secretary of State. I think that the Minister is aware of that important point.
