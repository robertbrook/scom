Clause 13
DUTY TO ALLOCATE SEATS TO POLITICAL GROUPS

Mr. George Howarth
: I beg to move amendment No. 143, in page 19, line 44, after "body", insert

"including the governing body of a school"

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to take the following amendments: No. 124, in page 21, line 33, after "applies", insert

"with the addition of school governing bodies".

No. 144, in schedule 1, page 120, line 36, at end insert—

"(i) a governing body of a school.".

Mr. Howarth
: Amendments No. 143 and 144 would extend the requirement for political ballots on council committees to governing bodies of schools. There was public outcry last year when the London borough of Barnet sought to secure 100 per cent. control by its majority party on school governing bodies. The amendment would enable the Government to move forward on an issue that they have accepted in principle. The Minister of State, Department of Education and Science talked about introducing legislation to require local education authorities to include minority party representation on their governing bodies.

The clause provides an ideal opportunity for the Government to legislate without further delay. If the Government are not prepared to accept the spirit of the amendment or to amend the clause, perhaps they will at least confirm the Minister of State's intention to legislate.

The amendments would require a political balance among local authority nominees to the boards of all schools. There are fewer local authority nominees on the boards of voluntary schools and their numbers on the boards of council schools have been reduced by the Education (No.2) Act 1986.

The Bill establishes principles for providing representation of small minorities. The principles are summarised in subsection (5). There appears to be no intrinsic reason why similar principles should not apply to representation on governing bodies.

Mr. Matthew Taylor

The issue is important because there has been clear politicisation of governing bodies since 1985. Mrs. Virginia Bottomley

Amendment No. 143 adds to a reference to school governors the requirement to review representation of party groups on bodies which local authorities appoint. Amendment No. 124 does the same for the ambit of schedule 1 and amendment No. 144 makes the same change in schedule 1 itself.

The Education (No.2) Act 1986 made changes in the constitution of school governing bodies. It provided for a major element of parent representation. Many hon. Members welcomed that distancing from local authority representatives on school governing bodies so that no one group would outweigh the others. It was decided that school governing bodies are a partnership between local authority representatives, parents, teachers and other co-opted members, including people from industry and other local and voluntary groups.

The element of governors appointed by local education authorities is therefore much smaller since the 1986 Act. That is not to say that we do not acknowledge the hon. Gentleman's contention that abuses have occurred in appointments by some local education authorities. We do not regard the way in which some of them behave as within the spirit of the 1986 Act. Such authorities are of more than one political persuasion, as has been said, but I do not think that it would be right to follow the hon. Member for Knowsley, North (Mr. Howarth) in mentioning examples.

Gentleman knows that because it was made clear in a letter to the hon. Member for Blackburn (Mr. Straw). However, deprecating actions do not automatically lead to legislation, in spite of what Opposition Members say. The Government think that, in the new situation, the question of JPEG 349 appointments by local education authorities is that much less significant. "(8) Sections 13 and 14 above shall not apply to appointments made by a relevant authority or committee of a relevant authority to any body established by that authority where the powers of that body are restricted to decisions solely affecting a limited geographical sub-division of that authority and the appointment to seats on that body is limited only to the elected members representing electoral areas (wards or divisions), situated entirely within that geographical sub-division.".

I shall not go into the wording of the amendment in detail because I want to deal with the main issue, which will be of immediate relevance to some members of the Committee. It may be unusual in metropolitan areas, but not in rural district areas, to divide planning issues on the basis of the location that they will affect so that committees represent the councillors of that area and are not divided in proportion to the make-up of the council. In rural areas, the council can cover such a wide area geographically that it is appropriate to divide it in such a way as to allow councillors with an immediate, intimate knowledge of the area to take planning decisions, which are essentially non-political.

That principle is not enshrined in Restormel district council in my constituency. Groups in St. Austell, particularly the chamber of commerce, have pressed for a greater contribution from the councillors who represent St. Austell rather than those who represent Newquay, which is a considerable distance away and has entirely different interests. The Action 2000 group has suggested splitting the committee between Restormel and Newquay. As all St. Austell councillors are Social and Liberal Democrats, that would affect the political composition of the committee. Given the way in which the district works, it would probably not affect planning decisions, which are not made on political grounds.

As drafted, the legislation would not enable a council to reflect in its committees the balance of members representing an area. It also has implications for the experimental neighbourhood councils in Tower Hamlets, which give direct power to people who represent a particular area. It is probably the only area in which Opposition councillors effectively control a budget of millions of pounds. Neighbourhood councils, like district councils, give power to those most involved in the JPEG 353 immediate community. The Tower Hamlets experiment is important and innovative. It has created considerable national interest among those involved in the running of councils and its results are becoming apparent.

That is the aim of the amendment. It refers to the circumstances in which not only the body but its powers

"are restricted to decisions solely affecting a limited geographical sub-division".

That appears to be within the bounds of the Bill's intentions. It will be reflected in the district councils of some members of the Committee.

Clause 14
DUTY TO GIVE EFFECT TO ALLOCATIONS

Mr. Soley
: I beg to move amendment No. 147, in page 22, line 5, after "the", insert "reasonable".

Let me try my luck now. I might be able to encourage the Minister and to take him with me. The amendment inserts the word "reasonable" into the provision that enables a group to have the allocations that it wants on its committee. I draw the Minister's attention to the notes on clauses, which explain that clause 14 provides that where a person has been appointed to a body in accordance with the wishes of a political group under subsection (1), that enables the party to terminate that appointment. I am not objecting to that—I understand what the Minister is seeking to do there. Once the Government have embarked on the process that they have adopted, that step becomes necessary.

However, there is an advantage in including the word "reasonable", because the action might be taken in a way that is damaging to local authorities generally. I shall give some examples of where a problem might arise.

If the member concerned was investigating what he or she believed to be a serious matter of corruption in the local authority, and if that was becoming embarrassing to some members of his or her own party, whichever party that was, and the party chose to remove that member from the committee, that would be unreasonable and could be interpreted at law as being unreasonable. However, if the party decided to turf that person off because he or she was no longer turning up or was not doing his or her job, or if that person simply wanted to serve on another committee, that would obviously be perfectly reasonable and one could not stop it at law.

However, I warn the Minister that on the rare occasions where corruption creeps in, we need someone to be able to retain a seat on a committee if he or she has it on a reasonable basis. The law is used to taking into account the concept of reasonableness and I am not in any way trying to remove the right to take a person off a committee for whatever reason the party desires. As a general rule, it will be because they want to move a person from one committee to another committee, or the person might simply want to come off a committee. A number of similar situations are perfectly proper, and my amendment would not stop people moving in those circumstances. Instead, it would provide a safeguard that could be tested out at law if a person felt that he or she was being removed unreasonably. The Minister should bear in mind cases where a member of a committee is investigating corruption and embarrassing his colleagues because of that investigation.

: The amendment related to the last in a series of clauses which are designed to help local authorities to recognise and come to terms with the growing existence of party politics and the need to accommodate such politics.

The Widdicombe report noted that:

"politics is now the main determining factor in the decisions of most local authorities. It is however not recognised in the statutory arrangements and is often regarded as an alien presence."

Widdicombe said:

"the political process, instead of fitting into a regulated framework within the local government system, is tending to operate outside and at odds with the system. At best this is causing uncertainty and instability. At worst power is being abused."

I hope that the hon. Gentleman will agree that the clauses are designed to ensure that local authorities allocate seats on committees, and make appointments to outside bodies, in proportion to the balance of political groups on the council so that minority parties are able to be properly represented on committees and to play a full role in the council's decision-making process. Taken together with the voting rights of the co-opted committee members, they will prevent the majority parties from being able to use their majorities on the council to influence committee membership in their favour.

The amendment would delete clause 15(3). The hon. Gentleman has expressed anxiety about the subsection. It must be examined within the context of the Bill. The Bill covers not only the ordinary committees of local authorities, but some of the less usual ones. The common feature of the latter is that they are joint bodies, appointed by county councils in conjunction with some other authority. Police committees are appointed by county councils and magistrates, jointly; sea fisheries committees are appointed by county councils, some metropolitan district councils and the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food, jointly; national parks committees are appointed by my right hon. Friends the Secretaries of State for the Environment and for Wales, and by county councils and district councils, jointly.

358 Legally, these bodies are committees of the county councils but, unsually, the powers are vested in the committees, not in the local authority. They are the bodies with which the subsection deals. Because of their joint nature and their special statutory status, the provisions needed to apply the principle of pro rata representation to the sub-committees appointed by these committees are more complicated. Rather than make the clauses more difficult to follow, we thought it right to put the provisions into regulations. We shall, of course, consult on the regulations but, given the limited scope of what is needed, regulations did seem an appropriate method.

I hope that, in the light of that explanation, the hon. Gentleman will withdraw his amendment.
