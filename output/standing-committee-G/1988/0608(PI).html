The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Norman Lamont): Clause 72 and schedule 6 build on and complete the tax reform programme for pensions, introduced in 1987, and make important changes.

First, they deregulate pensions. In future Inland Revenue rules will no longer determine the maximum benefits that an employer may provide. Secondly, they make the tax rules for pensions fairer through introducing an earnings cap on the tax-privileged benefits available to certain very high earners, and other changes will improve the position of ordinary scheme members. They will also make the tax rules simpler, especially in relation to the payment of additional voluntary contributions. I shall deal with those three changes in turn.

On deregulation, at present all occupational pension schemes that give an employee an entitlement to benefits have, in effect, to be approved by the Inland Revenue. Failure to obtain approval of a scheme would put at risk the tax-approved status of any other approved scheme of which the employee was a member. In that way tax approval rules have become a limit on total pensions that an employer may pay. Such a restriction is clearly not appropriate. Employers should have the freedom to offer whatever pension benefits are necessary to recruit or retain key employees. The changes in schedule 6 make that possible by removing the requirement that all schemes for an employee should be considered together to see whether, in total, the tax rules are satisfied. In future it will be necessary to apply the test only to schemes that attract the special pensions tax advantages.

The change will make it possible for employers to set up "top-up" schemes that provide more generous benefits than are allowed under the tax rules. For example, some employers may want to give a pension of more than two-thirds salary; or they may want to give a two-thirds pension where the tax rules do not allow it, such as where 20 years' service cannot be completed with the final employer; or, in the case of new employees who are subject to the Budget changes, employers may want to pension earnings above the limit of £60,000.

Col. 324 Although it is right that employers should have that choice, it is clearly not appropriate that top-up schemes should benefit from any of the special tax reliefs enjoyed by approved schemes, so we propose special tax rules governing payments to, and benefits from, top-up schemes to ensure that they do not enjoy tax privileges. The effect will be that benefits from top-up schemes will normally be charged to tax either as they accrue—if the scheme is funded—or when they are paid in the case of an unfunded scheme. In that way it is possible to allow the schemes to operate without any restrictions on the form and amount of the benefits payable: they will be matters solely for employers and employees to agree between themselves.

On the ceiling on tax relief, now that employers can provide retirement benefits without being restricted by tax rules, there is no longer a case for continuing with the present arrangements under which tax benefits are available to those in approved schemes without any upper limit to the pension payable. The measures announced on Budget day therefore included a proposal for a cash limit based on earnings of £60,000. That limit will allow a tax-approved occupational pension scheme to pay a pension of up to £40,000 a year, part of which may be commuted for a tax-free lump sum of up to £90,000. Beyond that, tax relief will not be available.

That brings the pensions tax relief into line with other tax-received savings arrangements where limits operate similarly, such as personal equity plans and the business expansion scheme. By setting the limit at that level, we have ensured that it affects only those on very high incomes. The vast majority of pension scheme members are unaffected and those who are will still be able to obtain a good level of benefit. Why should the Government, through the tax system, subsidise without limit the pensions of very high earners? That principle operates in other countries and most people will accept that it is an equitable proposal.

The third element of the occupational pensions package relates to the requirements for additional voluntary contributions. For tax purposes, they are treated as an integral part of an employee's occupational pension benefits and are subject to the normal benefit limits. However, the present rules are complex and have some unintended effects. In particular, if the AVC investment does better than expected, the present rules can require the employer's main scheme to reduce its benefits to keep the total benefits—those from the scheme and the AVC together—within tax approval limits. As a result, the incentive to select the best investment is diminished. If it did spectacularly well, the employee might not enjoy the benefit but would instead subsidise the employer's costs. That is clearly not desirable and we have received many representations about it. We therefore propose that if surplus AVC funds arise, they should in future be returned to the employee. But as contributions to the scheme and the investment build-up on its funds will have enjoyed tax relief, the payment to the employee will be chargeable to tax at a special rate. As AVC savings are not intended to be a tax-privileged form of general savings which is sometimes misunderstood, the tax Image Col. 325 charge will broadly recover both sets of tax relief—that on contributions and that on build-up.

To keep the administration of the charge simple, responsibility for deducting and accounting for tax will rest with the AVC scheme. But the amount paid to the employee will be treated as income that has been paid net of basic rate tax, so for everyone other than higher taxpayers there will be no further tax liability.

That new approch to surplus AVC funds has made possible a major improvement to the administrative procedures controlling the payment of AVCs. One of the main complaints was that the administrative rules were extremely complex and burdensome for employers because tax relief pushed the funds above the benefit limit. Many people have found the procedures unduly burdensome and so have been reluctant to co-operate to make free-standing AVC schemes a success. But the new tax charge on surplus AVC funds makes it possible to look again at AVC procedures. We recently consulted pension bodies over proposals radically to simplify the initial requirements. The new procedures will keep to a minimum employer involvement in the employees' setting up free-standing AVC plans. In most cases there will be no involvement at all. The changes have been warmly welcomed by the pensions industry.

Mr. Smith: It seems to us that this is primarily an anti-avoidance provision, so we warmly welcome it.

Mr. Lamont: The purpose of the clause is to remove a tax advantage enjoyed by offshore funds compared with UK unit trusts. It raises the issue of the competitiveness of our unit trust industry and whether it has advantages offshore as opposed to onshore.

Currently, there is a tax charge on UK investors on gains made on switching between different parts of a UK unit trust, but there is no corresponding charge for switches within offshore umbrella funds. That is because the latter come within the share reorganisation provisions that defer any charge for CGT purposes.

With the increased use of such funds, and the UK market being opened to certain investment vehicles within the EC, the Government think that it is right that onshore and offshore funds should compete on equal terms.
