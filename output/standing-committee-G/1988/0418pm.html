Sir George Young (Ealing, Acton): I beg to move amendment No. 384, in page 72, line 2, at end add,

"provided always that no such credit shall be required in respect of the disregard of a war disablement pension or a war widow's pension payable under section 28(6)(a) above".

The amendment deals with the disregard of war disablement and war widows' benefit in assessing entitlement to housing benefit. At present, the first £5 of war disablement and war widows' pensions has to be disregarded in calculating housing benefit. The arrangement obviously increases the housing benefit entitlement of such pensioners and the cost is met by central government. In addition, local authorities currently have discretion to disregard more than £5—up to the full amount of the pension—and that cost is borne locally.

The present position is unsatisfactory for two reasons. The Government will agree with the first: part of their housing policy is to promote mobility and to avoid decisions about where to live being distorted by local authority decisions. That is the level playing field argument to which my right hon. Friend referred. The present system for war widows inhibits mobility. A war widow may wish to move from a town such as Bournemouth, which disregards £60, to a town such as Bristol, which has a less generous policy. It is not right that, because of the grounds on which the Government have chosen to debate housing policy—the level playing field—war widows who may wish to move nearer to their relatives, or for other reasons, should be penalised due to the odd ways in which different local authorities interpret their discretion.

The second reason is that war widows simply do not understand why those in one part of the country receive more benefit than others in another part, although their circumstances—their incomes and perhaps their rents—may be identical and their husbands died for the same cause. Their standards of living may be totally different because of the way in which local authorities interpret their discretion.

At present, 282 of 483 local authorities operate a discretionary variation. War widows are calling for a national unified policy as set out in the amendment. I have seen the results of a survey carried out by the War Widows Association of Great Britain of the present arrangement. That survey reveals bewildering complexity and one sees exactly how various local authorities calculate the entitlement of Image Col. 949 war widows. It varies from nil disregard over £5 in many of them to quite high figures—£53 is disregarded for one war widow in Bradford and £27 is disregarded for another in South Holland. War widows simply do not understand why, when the Government have made a decision on their pensions, the impact of that decision is blurred by the present anomaly.

My right hon. Friend may ask why war widows should be singled out for special treatment in the amendment. The Government have already signalled that war widows are different. During the Standing Committee of the Social Security Bill, my hon. Friend the Minister for Social Security stated:

"To sum up, we are talking about a group of widows who deserve our sympathy and concern and who we all agree should be entitled to preferential treatment in benefits that puts them ahead of other widows."—[Official Report, Standing Committee F, 7 March 1989; c. 1036.]

The Government have already said that war widows are different from other widows. That preferential treatment is reflected in the Government's policy that makes war widows' pensions tax free when other widows' pensions are not. The impact of that decision is that the better-off war widow's income is tax free, so she benefits from the Government's decision that her circumstances are different, but the less well-off war widow has no such benefit because only the first £5 of her war widow's pension is disregarded when her housing benefit entitlement is calculated. No member of the Committee would defend a position in which a better-off war widow benefits and a less well-off war widow is penalised.

Unless the whole of the war widow's pension is disregarded, as proposed in the amendment, there will be differential treatment of well-off and less well-off war widows.

When drafting the amendment, we chose grounds that the Government had already chosen not so long ago and kept the concession very tightly drawn to minimise the cost to the Government. I do not know what the cost of the amendment would be, but I know that there are approximately 57,000 war widows and that the number is declining at the rate of 1,000 a year for widows from the first world war and 2,000 a year for widows from the second world war.

The amendment would provide that there should be no transfer from another revenue account of the authority to its housing revenue account if it decided to exercise discretion and disregard the whole of the war widows' pension. That would increase the deficit on the housing revenue account, which would attract subsidy from the Treasury.

".—(1) This section applies where for any year ('the relevant year') a local housing authority who are required to keep a Housing Revenue Account possess any houses or other property within the account.

  3. such other matters connected with the exercise of the authority's functions in relation to such property as the Secretary of State may direct.
