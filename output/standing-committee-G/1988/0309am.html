Mr. William O'Brien (Normanton): I beg to move amendment No. 95, in page 12, line 13, leave out from beginning to end of line 23 on page 13 and insert—

"(3) Each relevant authority shall determine the number, remuneration, conditions of service and allocation between political groups of any posts created in pursuance of this section by resolution of the full council.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take the following amendments: No. 97, in page 12, line 35, leave out subsection (4).

No. 96, in page 14, leave out lines 11 to 21.

Mr. O'Brien: The amendment would allow authorities to provide support for political groups, subject to the consent of the full council and the advice of the monitoring officer. At our previous sitting, the Committee approved the role of the monitoring officer, and under our amendment the council would have to take note of the monitoring officer's advice on providing support for political groups.

Amendment No. 97 would remove the restriction of £13,500, which should not apply in the light of the arrangements for civil servants. On more than one occasion we have been told that the provisions for political and special advisers to local authorities mirror those in Government Departments and in the Civil Service, so referring to those arrangements in our amendments is relevant to our proceedings. The £13,500 limit is not in keeping with arrangements in Government Departments, and it is an anomaly that should be removed.

Amendment No. 96 would delete the reference to short-term contracts to provide continuity of employment in line with conditions in the Civil Service.

The Widdicombe report accepted that attaching officers to party groups or to leaders of party groups could usefully broaden the basis of advice to officers and promote rather than damage the officer hierarchy serving the whole council. The Widdicombe committee said that allowing the appointment of political and special advisers to provide guidance to political groups on the council and to their leaders could help local government officers and benefit local authority admin- Col. 264 istration. We said that the restriction that means that a person has to resign the post after four years, or just before the election of a local authority, is unfair, unjust and cruel, and ought to be deleted.

Such officers would not exercise executive authority. We accept that, but feel that they should provide a back-up by research, policy analysis, paper writing and advice to the leader, chairman of committees, or groups of the whole council or of the various committees. Widdicombe suggested that, for most authorities, five such appointments would be adequate.

I can understand why Widdicombe considered a group of five. My council, for example, had 66 members. Its political leaders had the task of bringing together 13 former local authorities, a large area of the West Riding county council and two or three rural areas. One can imagine the workload on the administration staff and the political leaders. Widdicombe recommended understandably that special advisers be appointed to advise the political leaders of such large groups.

After taking evidence, Widdicombe made a reasoned suggestion that five appointments be made, which is in keeping with the work of larger local authorities.
