The Minister for Local Government (Mr. John Gummer): I had, I thought, come to the end of what I had to say about amendment No. 16.

The problem with amendment No. 17 is that it incorporates into terms of appointments and conditions of employment of holders of politically restricted posts such guidance, in relation to their political activity, as may be equivalent to any guidance contained within any code of practice applicable to the contracts of service appplicable to all Civil Servants.

There are two problems about that. It was suggested by the hon. Member for Normanton (Mr. O'Brien) that the Opposition should know what the restrictions were based upon. Regulations that affect local government service already operate on the basis of statute. Local authorities have to look up section 80(1) (a) of the Local Government Act 1972 if they want to find out what disqualifications apply to employees in any post to which the local authority appoints or confirms an appointment. Those arrangements are clear and we are following the same procedure in the Bill. There is nothing new here.

It is difficult to believe that the amendment would be suitable on that ground, but I shall go on to the second ground. I do not think that the hon. Member for Normanton would want to achieve the results his proposals would bring about. If I were to remind hon. Members briefly of the rules limiting the political activity of officers in the Civil Service they would see the comparison brought by the amendment into the present legislation.

Col. 76 Civil Servants are "politically free", "intermediate" or "politically restricted" according to grade. Staff in the first group may engage in political activity. Those in the second may participate in politics only with the permission of the Department. Those in the third group may participate in local political activity with the permission of their Department, but may in no circumstances participate in national political activity.

Those rules are derived from the Civil Service pay and conditions of service code. The paragraphs in question are 9923 to 9957, and Departments such as my own will have incorporated those provisions from the code into the Departmental staff rules.

The provisions in the Civil Service code are not guidance. The paragraphs apply to all civil servants covered by the code. They convey to the politically free group permission to undertake political activities, place requirements on other civil servants and set down procedures to deal with the certain other cases. In other words, they are not guidance but lay down the law.

If we take those provisions into account, we will lay upon local government officers a number of requirements that I am sure Opposition Members would not want. We would not have the exception from what is laid upon the shoulders of senior civil servants concerning the right of local government officers in the appropriate tone and form to write about subjects of local government professional interest. Senior civil servants are unable to do that, and if we included the code in that sense it would also exclude local government officers.

We can allow national civil servants to take part in local government in certain circumstances, but not in national government. We are talking about local government officers and it would be odd to restrict their activities in elected posts by including such terms of reference in the Bill. I do not believe that that is what Opposition Members intend.
