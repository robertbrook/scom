Mr. Chris Smith (Islington, South and Finsbury): I beg to move amendment No. 398 in page 82, line 16, at end insert

"or (c) the gift was undertaken with the sole or main objective of achieving a tax advantage.".

On behalf of myself and my hon. Friends I should like to extend our good wishes to my hon. Friend the Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, East (Mr. Brown), who I believe has reached the age of 30 today. We, therefore, wish him many happy returns.

I am tempted to observe that the Financial Secretary's good wishes follow the familiar pattern of the Government—generous rhetoric without any action behind it. It would have been nice to have a fuller explanation from him along with his kind birthday wishes.

Clause 109 deals with cases where an outright gift is made between a husband and wife, in relation to the onset of independent taxation in April next year. The clause ensures that where an outright gift of assets is made, and where income is generated therefrom, the income will be taxed as income of the recipient rather than of the donor.

Two good principals underline the change in the clause. In general, it seems sensible to tax the recipient rather than the giver of a gift or income. It is a principle that would be worthy of detailed discussion in relation to inheritance tax, but I shall not trespass on to that now. However, the principle of taxing recipients is sensible.

The other principle underlying the clause, which is a reasonably virtuous one, is that it would assist in equalising benefits between husbands and wives within a marriage. It should be noted that the Government are quite keen on achieving that for outright gifts in this clause. They are rather less keen on achieving equalisation of benefits between husbands and wives in the married couple's allowance. By way of comparison only, I draw to the attention of the Financial Secretary the answer to a parliamentary question that he gave me yesterday. I has asked him what arrangements the Chancellor of the Exchequer was intending to make

"in order to enable those married couples who wish to share the benefits of the married couples allowance equally between them from 1990 to do so."

The answer from the Financial Secretary was directly negative. He confirmed what we know from the changes that the Government have introduced for independent taxation: the married couple's allowance will be introduced and

"will be given in the first instance to the husband."—[Official Report, 12 June 1989; Vol. 154, c. 271.]

Only if the husband cannot make full use of that allowance himself will any portion of it subsequently go to the wife. The in built disadvantage to the wife in such a relationship is worth commenting on and Col. 398 highlighting. The Government are intent on not giving equal benefit between husbands and wives in the married couple's allowance. The benefit goes principally to the husband.

In contrast, clause 109 establishes a mechanism whereby benefits can be equalised. That must immediately lead us to ask why. The danger that Opposition Members see in clause 109 is that it makes possible an exchange of gifts in either direction between husband and wife specifically for tax avoidance purposes. As far as we can establish, it will be possible to make a gift that will produce income which will then be taxed at a lower rate than it would be in other circumstances, especially where one member of the household is in a higher tax bracket and the other member pays tax at the basic rate. That is the danger in this clause, and that is why we have tabled amendment No. 398.

This clause excludes gifts from the settlement regulations to ensure that the tax falls on the recipient, but the exclusion of gifts is subject to qualifications. Two qualifications are already enshrined in the clause. We seek to establish a third, which is that where the gift is undertaken with the sole or main objective of achieving a tax advantage, it should not be excluded from the settlement provisions and should not be treated as income of the recipient.

We are aware in tabling this amendment that it is difficult for the Board of Inland Revenue to establish whether an action has been taken for the

"sole or main objective of achieving a tax advantage."

The amendment is tabled more in a probing than in an amending spirit, because we wish to draw attention to a potential danger in clause 109. How do the Government intend to ensure that the provisions cannot be abused specifically for taxation purposes? We suspect that they cannot give a guarantee, and we should like to know their thinking. We are worried about the potential impact of some provisions and although we accept some of the underlying principles, we wish to draw attention to the problems that may arise.

Mr. Norman Lamont: The hon. Member for Islington, South and Finsbury (Mr. Smith) said that this was a probing amendment and I shall regard it as such. It is controversial and would not be popular, nor would the new-look Labour party wish to espouse it as it would undermine the very basis of independent taxation. If the amendment were carried, there would be no independence for married couples, nor would people be free to arrange their affairs as they wished.

I shall recap on the object of clause 109 and the thinking behind it. Under the independent taxation provisions we introduced last year, a husband and wife will be taxed separately on their own incomes and each will have their own tax allowance basic rate band to set against their income. Under the present system, where the income for a married couple is all taxed as if it belonged to the husband, it is not necessary to establish how the couple's investment income is divided between the husband and wife in order to calculate the couple's total tax bill. But under Image Col. 399 independent taxation, the division of the income between husband and wife has to be determined so that the tax of each can be separately calculated.

Last year, I explained that where a husband or wife makes an outright gift of assets to the other, the Government's objective was that that should be recognised for tax purposes and that the recipient, not the donor, should be taxed on any income that arises from the assets after the transfer. Provided that a gift of assets between husband and wife is free from conditions and is a complete and irrevocable transfer of the underlying capital as well as the income, we see absolutely no case for imposing a tax penalty on the income from those assets. In any case, there would be great practical difficulty in attempting to enforce such a tax penalty.

I also explained last year that different considerations apply where arrangements fall short of an outright transfer of both income and capital. For example, where a husband seeks to divide his income from the underlying capital and to transfer only income to his wife, while retaining control over the capital, we see no reason why couples should enjoy a tax advantage from the arrangements. We shall come to that in clause 110. Our approach is not new and it follows from what we set out in the Green Paper.

Clause 109, together with clause 110, ensures that the treatment of transfers of income between husband and wife is consistent with our general approach. Clause 109 amends the definition of a settlement for the purpose of certain of the provisions in the so-called "settlements legislation", so that it does not include an outright gift between husband and wife. That ensures that the income from an outright gift will be taxed under independent taxation as the income of the recipient of the gift. But the definition is limited. To fall within the scope fo the definition, a gift of property has to be an absolute and irrevocable transfer of both the income from the property and the underlying capital. Gifts that do not carry the right to the whole of the income from the property transferred, which are effectively transfers only of income, which are subjecto to conditions, or under which the donor may benefit directly, will not come within the scope of the provision. In those cases, the income payable to or applicable for the benefit of the donor's husband or wife will be treated as the income of the donor for all income tax purposes under the new provisions in clause 110.

The distinction between outright gifts and gifts subject to conditions—for example, trusts, which we shall discuss under clause 110—or anything that falls short of complete transfers of both income and capital, is the right place to draw the line in deciding how the income from assets held by husband and wife should be taxed. It ensures that a married couple cannot obtain a tax advantage from a purely artifical transfer of income between them. But if the beneficial ownership of both capital and income is transferred, that genuine change of ownership will be recognised.

The approach proposed in the amendment is different. Even if a husband or wife made a complete and irrevocable transfer of an asset to a partner, under the amendment the income from the asset Col. 400 would continue to be taxed as the income of the donor if the transfer were made with the sole or main objective of achieving a tax advantage. Such a provision makes no sense. Independent taxation is bound to mean that some couples will transfer assets between them with the result that their total tax bill be reduced. That is an inevitable and acceptable consequence of taxing husbands and wives separately. I would have thought that Opposition Members would welcome the result, irrespective of the donor's motive, because that will lead to a more equal division of ownership of property between husbands and wives.

I see nothing objectionable in the provision, provided that the transfers of ownership are genuine. We shall discuss under clause 10 artificial transfers through the use of trusts and the transfer of assets with specified conditions. If those tests are satisfied, it does not matter what the purpose of the transfer of assets is. If there is an outright gift, there are real financial consequences, which we must recognise. On the one hand, the recipient is free to do what he wishes with both the capital and income and, on the other hand, the donor must give up control over the assets.

The hon. Member for Islington, South and Finsbury may ask about a husband and wife who merely swap cash between bank accounts. A husband and wife should be free to do that. If there is evidence of a condition being attached, that would be taken into account. But if a husband decides to give a large part of the cash in his bank account to his wife, that is his decision and, unless there is a specific legal document, he takes the risk that he may not receive the cash back. If one believes in the independence of husband and wife, that is a logical consequence and couples should be free to do so. We have made it clear that we expect income splitting to occur. However, if it is accompanied by specific conditions, the use of trusts and so on which we shall confront in clause 110, that is another matter.

The making of outright gifts would not be undertaken lightly. Husbands and wives will wish to consider extremely carefully all the implications before giving their assets to their partner. That, as I am sure the hon. Member for Islington, South and Finsbury recognises, will affect not only the rich. Up to 90 per cent. of married couples who are liable to tax have some investment income between them, and up to half of those are one-earner couples for whom there may be tax advantages in the transfer of assets between partners.

In those circumstances, it would be completely inconsistent with the basic principle of independent taxation to ignore an outright gift and to pretend that it never happened. But if a transfer falls short of an outright gift, a couple should not be able to obtain tax advantages as a result.

The hon. Member for Islington, South and Finsbury acknowledged that a motive test, which could be implied by his amendment, could not be operated in practice. We are not referring to complicated, artificial avoidance devices from which it might be possible to infer that the main motive was avoidance of tax. We are discussing millions of Image Col. 401 everyday transactions between husbands and wives. If the hon. Gentleman's amendment were carried, I cannot for the life of me imagine how he thinks the Inland Revenue would determine whether the transfer of cash between husbands' and wives' bank accounts was done for a tax motive, given the scope for millions of people to gain a marginal advantage in their tax bills. What would we do: ask them, and accept the answer yes or no? That would not be realistic.

The provisions in the next clause referring to trusts are worth having. They are practical provisions which will prevent abuse on a more significant scale than the amendment of the hon. Member for Islington, South and Finsbury would. I accept that it was a probing amendment, but if the hon. Gentleman had said that that was what the Opposition believed, he would have undermined the very basis of independent taxation. If we are to have independent taxation, we must accept the freedom of husbands and wives to transfer assets unconditionally between them. It is not unreasonable for husbands and wives to be able to do that.
