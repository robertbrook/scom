The clause is based on the findings of the Widdicombe Committee which, as an independent committee examining at the conduct of local government, saw considerable danger in allowing the sort of twin-tracking that we have discussed.

Apart from the difficulty of giving independent advice as an officer of a local authority while at the same time being involved in the imbroglio of party politics, there are two other relevant considerations.

First, we must consider the comfort of those who seek the advice of local authority officers and who expect information to come from the independent staff whom, historically and rightly, they have been able to approach. I place great emphasis on that. Those of us who have access to so many sources of advice must remember that the local authority is the prime and probably only source of advice for many people, especially at the lower end of the scale for Col. 110 individual guidance on housing and social services. I take seriously their need to have the utmost confidence in the advice that they receive.

Secondly, under the present system, local government officers in heavily politicised councils find it increasingly difficult to maintain their status and their reputation for independence. Evidence from the Society of Local Authority Chief Executives, from other organisations and from many individuals shows that many officials feel deeply that when they became local government officers they chose to be non-party political because that was the right role for them. Even if they continue to act in that way, their position becomes extremely difficult if others around, above and below them take an active part in party politics.

I hope that the Committee agrees that the clause should be accepted because of the arguments on high political profiles and twin-tracking between different councils and because of the serious effects on the ability of ordinary people to approach officers as they wish. Just as important is the serious effect on morale among local government officers who, like many civil servants, sought their particular jobs because their forte, interest, ability and concern was in giving impartial, independent advice rather than in party-political argument.

I have a note that was given to me by the chief executive of Knowsley borough council. It is a résume of Mr. Hatton's employment with the council and his simultaneous involvement with Liverpool city council, and it will put the record straight.

Mr. Hatton was employed on 16th August 1976 as an area community development officer on grade SO1. There were 33 applicants for the post. He continued in the post until 1982 when he was redeployed as a management assistant on grade SO1. On 11th November 1983, he was transferred to a new post of voluntary sector liaison officer on grade SO1/2. Mr. Hatton was ultimately summarily dismissed for gross misconduct on 23rd July 1986.

On 8th March 1980, Mr. Hatton's time off for council duties as a member of Liverpool city council—it was Liberal-controlled at the time—and as a NALGO lay official was fixed at no more than six days per month. At the time, there was no formal scheme for time off in existence. Persistent requests by Mr Hatton for more time off were refused by Knowsley council. On 27th November 1981, because of his increasing commitment as a city councillor—he was then shadow deputy leader—Mr. Hatton's time off entitlement for approved council and trade union duties was increased to a maximum of two days per week.

A system of monitoring and control, exerciseable by the chief officer, who was answerable to the council, was introduced specifically for Mr. Hatton. Disciplinary action was to result in the event of non-compliance. These controls necessitated the completing of timesheets by Mr. Hatton.

On 7th December 1981, the council approved a scheme for time off covering all employees. For a council leader, time off would be by negotiation, for the chairman of a major committee a maximum of two days per week and for a member a maximum of one day per week. Under this scheme, 75 per cent. of any attendance allowance received by employees had to be repaid to Knowsley council and the borough treasurer was to monitor the arrangements personally.

On 7th September 1983, Mr. Hatton, now deputy leader of Liverpool city council, was given a maximum entitlement of two and a half days per week. He retained that entitlement until his dismissal.

On 20th November 1984, the district auditor expressed general satisfaction with the council's scheme for time off, including the arrangements for Mr. Hatton. However, the Image Col. 115 district auditor spoke of the need to secure a regular flow of work return forms from him. The chief executive and chief officer tried to obtain the returns. On 25th September 1985, Mr. Hatton was subjected to further proceedings for non-submission of time sheets. Further promises were made but not kept. On 9th June 1986, a final warning was issued to Mr. Hatton by the chief officer. He still did not comply with instructions. On 21st July 1986, Mr. Hatton was dismissed for gross misconduct. The decision was taken by the chairman and vice-chairman of the personnel committee and a subsequent appeal was dismissed by six other members of the council.

I contend that the council took reasonable steps to secure time off for its employees through a formal scheme. It worked well—and still does—for all employees except Mr. Hatton, who abused it and the authority which accorded him the facilities. The abuse constituted grave misconduct, for which he was rightly dismissed.

I have mentioned these matters because argument has centred around Mr. Hatton's case being justification for the Bill's provisions when, in fact, the story demonstrates that he was a difficult person for Knowsley to have on its payroll. The council acted reasonably and tried to help him, but, when he failed to comply with reasonable instructions, he was dismissed.
