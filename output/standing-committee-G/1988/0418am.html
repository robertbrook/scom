DUTY TO PREVENT DEBIT BALANCE ON HOUSING REVENUE ACCOUNT

10.30 am

The Chairman
: With this motion, we shall consider Government new clause 9— Duty to prevent debit balance on Housing Revenue Account—

' .—(1) This section applies where for any year ("the relevant year") a local housing authority who are required to keep a Housing Revvenue Account possess any houses or other property within the account.


(2) The authority shall, during the months of January and February immediately preceding the relevant year, formulate proposals which satisfy the requirements of subsection (3) below and relate to—

  1. the income of the authority for the year from rents and other charges in respect of houses and other property within their Housing Revenue Account;
  2. the expenditure of the authority for the year in respect of the repair maintenance, supervision and management of such property; and
  3. such other matters connected with the exercise of the authority's functions in relation to such property as the Secretary of State may direct.


(3) Proposals formulated by the authority under subsection (2) above satisfy the requirements of this subsection at any time if, on the assumption that the following will prove correct, namely—

  1. the best assumptions that they are able to make at that time as to all matters which may affect the amounts falling to be credited or debited to their Housing Revenue Account for the relevant year; and
  2. the best estimates that they are able to make at that time of the amounts which, on those assumptions, will fall to be so credited or debited,
implementation of the proposals will secure that the account for that year does not show a debit balance.


(4) No assumptions shall be made under subsection (3) above as to the exercise by the Secretary of State of any power except on the basis of information published by him or on his behalf or supplied by him to the authority.

  2. if they determine that question in the negative, they shall make such revisions of the proposals as are reasonably practicable towards securing that the proposals (as so revised) satisfy those requirements.


(7)Where the proposals formulated under subsection (2) above are revised under subsection (6) above, subsections (3) to (6) above shall apply in relation to the proposals as so revised as they applied in relation to the proposals as originally formulated.

The Minister for Local Government (Mr. John Gummer)
: As two of the hon. Members who represent Sheffield are members of the Committee, I am sure that it is our wish to extend to them our especial concern and condolence on Saturday's awful tragedy.

New clause 9 to replace clause 65 is an attempt to make that clause clearer. It is sometimes suggested that we should endeavour to explain why we have second thoughts, but I do not intend to do that. I merely say that the new clause makes the matter much clearer, and has the great advantage of starting at the beginning, moving through the middle, and ending at the end. The original clause was more complicated and difficult to understand because it did not follow—as I would have said to the hon. Member for Torfaen (Mr. Murphy), had he been here—Aristotelian logic.

The purpose of the new clause is twofold. First, it would require a local housing authority to make a budget each year—

Mr. William O'Brien
(Normanton)
: They do that now.

Mr. Gummer
: —in a manner that would ensure that there was no deficit on the housing account. It is not an extra onerous duty because, as the hon. Member for Normanton (Mr. O'Brien) says, housing committees make budgets in any case. All it requires is that the budget must ensure that there is no deficit. Having drawn up the initial budget before the start of the year, the local housing authority would be required to keep the account under review, and to revise the budget, if a deficit seemed likely to arise.

The original clause was less clear and might lead an authority to make mid-term changes, irrespective of whether such changes were possible or realistic. As that is not our intention, the new clause makes it clear tha only mid-term changes that could be carried out realistically would be made. If it appeared likely, mid-term, that the housing account would go into deficit, such changes as could reasonably be made would have to be made, and if there were a deficit at the end of year, that deficit would have to be carried forward and taken into account in the next year's budget. It would still be essential to take whatever steps were reasonable during the course of the year.

The new clause is more easily understood than clause 65. It seems to meet one of the concerns that were expressed about the original clause. Therefore, I hope that the Committee will agree that clause 65 should not stand part of the Bill and that new clause 9 should take its place.

Clause 67
DIRECTIONS TO SECURE PROPER ACCOUNTING

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Gerry Steinberg
(City of Durham)
: The clause is puzzling. Its construction makes it vague. It is general and gives unacceptably wide powers to the Secretary of State. Would that it were as precise as some of the local authority housing accounts that it seeks to criticise. Perhaps the corollary is for local government to confer upon itself powers to direct the Secretary of State to say exactly what he means in proposed legislation. The Minister must explain why the clause is in the Bill?

The Minister and his colleagues agree that most authorities, especially those in the north, are run efficiently and take due account of their electorate. The Minister accepted that, as recorded in column 666 of Hansard on 6 April. Moreover, he has prescribed in the Bill what is to be included in the housing revenue and housing repairs accounts, how housing subsidies will be paid, how to treat debit and credit balances, so is the power contained in the clause necessary?

In view of all the prescription in the Bill, can it be assumed that the Secretary of State anticipates some illegal expenditure? To presume so gives scant regard to other checks and balances on local authority accounts. In addition to the normal accounting conventions and the various codes of practice issued by the Chartered Institute of Public Finance and Accountancy, there is the external auditor. It is his duty, in examining a local authority's accounts, to issue a report on any serious irregularities and to disallow illegal expenditure. Is it to be assumed that the Secretary of State is standing in the stead of the external auditor or, even worse, are we to presume that the Secretary of State will issue a direction that accounts that have been subjected to external audit are to be rectified?

The final safeguard which renders the clause unnecessary is the professionalism of chief finance officers. Legislation requires local authorities to ensure that one officer is responsible for the administration of financial affairs. It requires that he belongs to an approved accountancy body and it has bestowed on him further powers to deal with the possibility of illegal expenditure. After all that effort, is a chief finance officer not now to be trusted accurately to account for the actions of his authority?

Perhaps the Government fear the recent growth in creative accountancy, which has arisen essentially from the severe expenditure restrictions imposed on local authorities. The Government may wonder why local authorities have not yet ground to a halt and intend to pursue that avenue. It is a little sardonic that creative accounting worries the same people who, in announcing an increase in the gross provision for housing expenditure, could in the same calculation reduce the actual new money available.

I should be grateful if the Minister will explain why this extremely puzzling clause is in the Bill.
