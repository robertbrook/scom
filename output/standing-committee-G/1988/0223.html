Clause 1
DISQUALIFICATION AND POLITICAL RESTRICTION OF CERTAIN OFFICERS AND STAFF

Mr. Soley
: I beg to move amendment No. 3, in page 2, line 9, leave out 'or remaining'.

You will have noted, Mr. Hunt, that we tabled two or three other amendments that have not been selected, and I understand why. Although we do not like the implications of the Bill and, of course, of this clause, which we shall oppose, we felt that there was a case for offering an alternative for the Government to consider, even if they did not agree with us that the Bill and clause are unnecessary. I hope, therefore, that we may at least touch on those issues in our debate on amendment No. 1.

Clause 1 states the Government's intentions. Its heading is:

"Disqualification and political restriction of certain officers and staff."

That says it all. The Bill is an undemocratic attack on local democracy, and continues a long line of Government attacks. The amendment would mitigate its effects and make it more consistent. It picks out an inconsistency in the drafting of the Clause. Clause 1(8) provides that an individual holding a politically restricted post who is a member of a local authority within the period of two months after the Bill is passed, may continue to wear both hats until the end of his or her term of office. But clause 1(1) imposes a blanket ban on anyone becoming or remaining a member of a local authority if he holds a politically restricted post. The amendment would remove the words "or remaining", the retrospective element of the legislation, so that someone who is a local authority employee and a local authority councillor may decide to remain in a restricted post or to continue to serve as a councillor until it was logical to choose.

I shall show how the absurdity has arisen by referring to Mr. Widdicombe's detailed report. Volume 1 of the research for that report is central to the thinking behind the clause. It states:

"There is a sizeable body of opinion—probably about half the members and officers—to the effect that officers in the most senior posts ought not to be serving as councillors elsewhere."

It emphasises that:

"Few in fact do. The practice is largely one which involves junior rather than more senior officers. Insofar as this arouses concern, it is more through a vague sense that it may be inappropriate or unwise rather than through any specific evidence of a conflict of interests. The possibility of such a conflict of interests is identified by those opposed to the practice, but clear examples of its operation are rarely, if ever, quoted."

The amendment would allow a person to choose at a logical time whether to give up his job or to give up being a councillor. That is not just tidying up. The Government have failed to produce evidence that there is a problem. Widdicombe said that there is not a significant problem. The Government say that there is and that people are abusing their positions. They have asked for evidence and produced a few isolated cases. We have numerous examples of Government abuse.
