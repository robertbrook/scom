Mr. Matthew Taylor
(Truro)
: I beg to move amendment No. 172, in page 26, line 6, at end insert—

'(3) In section 26 of the Local Government Act 1974 (matters subject to investigation), the following subsection shall be inserted after subsection (1)—


"(1A) Where a complaint as specified in (1) above, is brought to the attention of a Local Commissioner, but other than in the manner set out in (1) above, or where no complaint has been received but a Local Commissioner has reason to believe that a member of the public may have sustained injustice in consequence of maladministration in connection with action of the type specified in (1) above, the Local Commissioner may investigate that complaint or that action on his own motion.".'.

The Amendment would give the local ombudsman the right to initiate investigations arising from evidence that may come to him directly rather than through an individual referring a complaint.

At present, a local ombudsman can investigate complaints only if a member of the public alleges that he or she has suffered injustice through maladministration. If a local ombudsman is aware of what appears to be injustice or maladministration, but receives no complaint, however, he cannot act.

In their 1980 and 1984 reviews of the ombudsman service, local ombudsmen requested the right to act on their own initiative. At the end of March 1985, under the heading "Investigation on initiative," the report of the Commission for Local Administration in England stated:

"Most Ombudsmen outside the United Kingdom can investigate on their own initiative a matter coming to their attention where injustice seems possibly to have been caused by faulty administration. Available figures show that about 5 per cent. of their cases are initiated by those Ombudsmen."

The report also said:

"After a decade's experience it is clear that a number of cases brought to notice in the media seem more significant and serious than some complaints properly referred to the Ombudsmen by individuals."

In my view, ombudsmen should have the power to initiate investigations in such circumstances.

The Widdicombe report recommended that local ombudsmen should have a right of initiative. Under the heading "Power to initiate investigations" the report referred to the case argued by local ombudsmen which I have just outlined. The report stated:

"Research undertaken by JUSTICE in 1976–77 showed a very strong middle class bias among complainants, with over 70 per cent. of complaints being made by non-manual households."

Its conclusion from the evidence presented to it was:

"A power to take the initiative in an investigation could help towards redressing this imbalance. The ombudsman should not pursue a case except where there was good ground for concern, nor conduct an investigation into the general procedures of an authority rather than an individual case where there was reason to suppose that injustice has occurred. Subject to those provisos, we consider that a power to initiate investigations would be a useful addition to the ombudsman's powers.

Under the Heading:

"Recommendations on the local ombudsmen"

the Widdicombe report stated:

"the local ombudsmen should be given new powers to investigate individual cases on their own initiative."

JPEG 425 The Government rejected that recommendation. In my view, they were wrong. That power would be an important enhancement of the local ombudsman's role in tackling injustice. In some circumstances, it would clearly be an advantage. The Committee might not necessarily be inclined to believe me, but I hope that it will take seriously the views that were expressed by the local ombudsmen and Widdicombe. Indeed, the Minister has often said that the Widdicombe Committee was independent and that its advice should be taken seriously.

Although the Government rejected Widdicombe's recommendation, the ombudsmen welcomed it. In his final annual report, Mr. Cook—who was a local ombudsman from 1974 to 1985—stated:

"I know of cases in the region which should have been investigated and the facts brought out into the open. Whether there was anything wrong or not, impartial investigation and report would have been in the public interest. But because I am no more than a response mechanism I have waited in vain for a referred complaint."

There is no question that those best able to judge the usefulness of such a power believe that it would be useful. Moreover, they know of circumstances when an investigation should have taken place but did not because no referral was forthcoming.

The Clae observations on the Widdicombe committee report said:

"It is relevant that overseas ombudsmen who do have the power to investigate on initiative regularly record that they find it most useful to bridge a gap in the service they can otherwise provide to the community."

The power would bridge a clear gap in what local ombudsmen can do to respond to difficulties, and it would address the injustice that is suffered by the people who are least able to complain for themselves—those who do not know how to complain or who are afraid or unable to complain because of age, infirmity or physical or mental handicap.

As I have said, 70 per cent. of complaints come from non-manual worker households, which suggests that those who are most able to complain are also those who are in the best position to use the ombudsman and the most likely to do so.

I think that the Government believe that the amendment would change the role of the local ombudsman and make him more of a free-ranging investigator, but the scope of investigation would remain precisely as it is at present—that is, personal injustice caused by maladministration. Most foreign ombudsmen have that power, but it does not take over their role. Far from it—it accounts for an average of only 5 per cent. of cases. In other words, in 95 per cent. of cases they use the traditional route. Therefore, I do not believe that the initiative would fundamentally alter the type of work which ombudsmen undertake or how they go about it.

There is also the suggestion that the local ombudsman would lose the co-operation and goodwill of local government members and officers. I do not believe that.
