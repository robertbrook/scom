Mr. Gerry Steinberg
(City of Durham)
: I beg to move amendment No. 42, in page 4, line 41, leave out 'reports directly or is directly accountable to' and insert

'regularly deputises in respect of the whole range of duties for'.

 My colleagues on the Front Bench tabled the amendment because we must clarify the position of a deputy chief officer. The clause is extremely vague. Its clarity is seriously flawed. As drafted, it includes many departmental staff, in addition to appointed deputies. Subsection (6) is wildly imprecise. It defines "deputy chief officer" specifically for the purpose of paragraph (d) of subsection (1), which is part of the list of individually designated, politically restricted posts.

A deputy chief officer is specifically caught by clause 2(1)(d) rather than included in the £13,500 mopping-up provision. Therefore, it is reasonable to assume that the definition in the subsection should bear some relation to what is usually understood by the term "departmental deputy" in local government. In a district authority, that would mean either a designated deputy or someone who regularly performs the duties of a chief officer. In a large authority such as a county council, a deputy would normally be someone whose job description includes responsibility for deputising for the whole range of a chief officer's functions. Third tier officers may sometimes act on behalf of the chief officer when he and the designated deputy are unavailable.

It is entirely appropriate for the clause to define "deputy chief officer", but the problem is getting the definition right. An officer who reports "directly to" or "is directly accountable to" a chief officer is not, by any definition, his deputy.

Many aspects of local government are specialised and require the appointed expert to report directly to a chief officer. But the appointee is not a deputy. Perhaps I may give some examples from Durham city council. The first is 184 the chief auditor and the city treasurer. The chief auditor is a fourth tier officer. The second is the town clerk and chief executive and the city solicitor and personnel officer; the former is a chief officer and the latter a third tier officer. The third example is the town clerk, a chief executive and the principal executive assistant, who is a third tier officer. Those examples are from a small authority.

Let us consider the larger authority of Durham county council and its solicitors and secretaries department. As drafted, the clause would bring within the definition of deputy chief officer a substantial majority of that department's staff. Out of 50 employees, about 29—almost 60 per cent.—could be said to report directly, or to be directly accountable to the chief officer. Incidentally, that excludes the designated deputy and also the secretarial, typing and clerical staff, the latter being covered by subsection (7). A considerable proportion of that number would fall below the threshold in clause 2(1)(h). As paragraph (h) is presumably intended to be the catch-all provision, it is illogical for officers who are not caught to be caught by the more restrictive "deputy chief officer" definition. Moreover, the words "reports directly" refer to what happens in practice rather than to the duties of the post.

There is no qualification requiring such reporting to take place on a regular basis, and a similar phrase is used in clause 3. To take an extreme example, one isolated incident of a direct report could conceivably bring an officer within the clause. Surely, that cannot have been intended by the Government when the Bill was drafted?

Since the consequences of holding a politically restricted post are significant, it is impossible for officers to be clear about their positions. That is why we must get the legislation sorted out this afternoon.

Another provision in the Bill again highlights the wide nature of clause 2 and the inconsistent treatment of deputies. Clause 5(6) personally enables the monitoring officer to nominate a deputy to act in his absence; only that person can deputise for him for the purposes of clause 5. That is a narrow definition of a deputy.

The amendment does not purport to make the definition in clause 2(6) as narrow as that in clause 5(6). It attempts to bring the definition within workable and understandable limits.

The amendment seeks to confine the definition of deputy chief officers to those officers who deputise for their chief officers as part of their conditions of service. The real issue is the responsibility given to the post holder. The amendment will make clear who is the deputy to the chief officer.
