Mr. Murphy
: The Opposition believe that clause 137 will weaken the security, and increase the rents, of tenants whose long-term tenancies, including leases, has expired.

There is already widespread and long-established anxiety about the problems of long lease holders. There are 1.5 million leaseholders in Britain whose properties will revert to the freeholders when their lease expires. That might not be a problem in the early years, but, as leases mature, they depreciate in value and become much more difficult to sell. In those circumstances, leaseholders often find that they are unable to buy other accommodation, and 1202 freeholders are often disinclined to invest in maintenance and improvements when a lease approaches its expiry date. On expiry, a leaseholder can claim his rights as a tenant under the Rent act 1977.

The problems related to leases nearing expiry are likely to become more widespread when the many flats that were built during the post-war years, and sold on 99-year leases, become affected. This is a problem in London, of course, but we have also experienced it for many years in the Principality. Many organisations, including the Building Societies Association and the National Consumer Council, have argued that no more leasehold tenancies should be created and that leaseholders should be given the right to own the freehold of their property. The Leasehold Reform Act 1967 gave this right to the leaseholders of houses.

Given the considerable problems that are experienced by long leaseholders, it is wrong to undermine their rights further through clause 137. This argument is strengthened by the fact that the owners of leases that are about to expire are likely to be old people on low incomes who are vulnerable to pressures from unscrupulous freeholders. They face the danger of abuse and harassment by freeholders, as leasehold property is often sold because of its investment value to companies which have little interest in maintaining and managing property and want only to sell it with vacant possession at the earliest opportunity.

Amendment No. 686 would prevent the provision in clause 137 from operating retrospectively. Existing leaseholders have taken on leases on the understanding that they will be converted into Rent Act protected tenancies on expiry. The Housing Act 1988 protected the rights and security of existing tenants, and the principle should apply to leaseholders.

Amendment No. 688 would alter the date from which the clause would catch existing leaseholders from 1999 to 15 January 2019.

I have some difficulty responding to the amendments. In the light of the debate, I might be prepared to consider the amendments to schedule 9. The new provisions will enable them to convert to assured tenancies under the Housing Act 1988 at market rents. The change from fair rents to market rents is an entirely logical adjunct to the measures to deregulate private sector lettings, which we introduced in the 1988 Act.

The case for deregulation was, as I am sure the hon. Member for Torfaen accepts, exhaustively debated when what became the 1988 Act was before Parliament, and we do not need to go over that ground today. Now that we have established the market rent principle for the generality of lettings, it would hardly be right to perpetuate the fair rent regime for long leases that are coming to an end.

We made our intentions on that subject clear as long ago as October 1987, as part of the main deregulation package. We had hoped to include the 1204 necessary provisions in the 1988 Act, but the drafting was more difficult than we had expected. The measures were therefore deferred for inclusion in this Bill. We always accepted the necessity for a long transitional period before full operation of the new regime. We were especially conscious of the need to avoid penalising those who, when the proposals were announced, had recently bought a lease with only a few years to run in the expectation of converting it to a Rent Act tenancy at its termination. To that end, we provided that, whereas the new system will apply to all leases granted after commencement of clause 137, it will apply to existing leases only when they come to an end on or after 15 January 1999—in other words, a full 10 years after the new assured tenancy regime comes into effect. In our view, that provides a perfectly adequate period in which existing leaseholders can adjust to the new approach that we are adopting. We therefore see no reason for the further delays that the amendments would involve.
