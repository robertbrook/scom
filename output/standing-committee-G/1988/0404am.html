10.30 am

Mr. Clive Soley (Hammersmith): I beg to move amendment No. 245, in page 37, line 29, leave out '1990' and insert '1991'.

We come back to what is perhaps the most exciting part of the Bill. It is an extremely important part, although it may be difficult for people outside the House and, indeed, in it to appreciate its significance. In a sense, it shows clearly what has gone wrong with the Government's previous legislation.

The Government passed the Local Government, Planning and Land Act 1980, through which they thought they would solve some problems, but they did not, and they have made a series of changes since then which have made it extremely difficult for local authorities to cope.

In the autumn of 1984, the Government initiated a review of the local authority capital control system that was introduced in 1981–82. It showed the extent of the failure of the 1980 Act. Like so much recent legislation, that Act was produced hastily and drafted poorly. The Government have not learnt much. We are still getting such legislation, such as the Housing Act 1988 and, to some extent, this Bill. Such legislation seems massively to rely on the Secretary of State's discretion, a matter to which we shall return later this morning.

It is absurd, and bad business management, to try to control capital expenditure annually and, equally, to attempt to control gross expenditure, net expenditure and borrowing without emphasis on any particular aggregate. The Minister must know a little about how private business works. Surely he knows that a business which tried to plan its annual expenditure year by year—knowing that the Government frequently move the goal posts—would automatically get into difficulties. A business cannot plan ahead in that way. That is the major problem for local authorities, Labour, Tory, Liberal or anything else—it is difficult for them to plan effectively. I sometimes think that making it as difficult as possible for them to plan is a deliberate part of the Government's policy to make local authorities seem inefficient and ineffective.

The consultation paper, "The Review of Capital Expenditure Control System in England and Wales" was brought out in early 1986. Those proposals were withdrawn, following opposition from local government of all parties. In July 1988, another consultation paper, on capital expenditure and finance was produced. Its proposals form the basis of part IV of the Bill.

The amendment would delay the introduction of this part of the Bill. The Government have waffled around for Col. 572 years, producing consultation papers which have proved to be wrong and Acts of Parliament which have proved to be inadequate and unhelpful to local authorities. It is unacceptable for them suddenly to bring out a consultation paper in the middle of 1988, put the proposals into the Bill early in 1989—which could pass into law by the end of the year—and expect local authorities to adjust to them painlessly and without acute managerial difficulties. That asks far too much of local authorities and it is a totally unreasonable expectation. By giving local authorities at least an extra year, the amendment would enable them to work out some of the problems to which we shall refer in the debate on later clauses.

Another thing which troubles me and local authorities of all parties is the extent of regulation powers that are left to the Secretary of State. The Secretary of State has powers to make regulations under clauses 30(1)(k), 30(3), 31(5), 34(5), 35(2), 39(1)(c), 39(5), 40(2), 40(3), 44(4), 48(6) and 49(3). I wonder why the Government bothered to put those clauses in the Bill—one simple clause which read, "Under this part of the Bill, the Secretary of State will decide whatever he wants to decide and tell the local authorities to do it," would have covered everything. The Bill has nothing to do with local government—it is about central control at the whim of the Secretary of State.

If we look through that group of clauses—perhaps this is more clearly set out in clause 30—we will see that the powers given to the Secretary of State are wide ranging and given in such a way that they will make the job of local authorities budgeting, extremely difficult.

If I were managing director of a company and trying to work out my financial policy for 1990–91 and the shareholders, or another group in a position of influence and control, said, "We are going to change things this year and will tell you how a few months before the change" The company would be in trouble. We all know that, and that the Government are creating problems for local authorities, including those controlled by their party.

Local authorities have no idea of their likely level of resources in terms of basic credit approvals or annual capital guidelines for 1991. They will have great problems with planning. The analogy with the private sector is clear. The Government would not impose such restrictions on the private sector, so why are they imposing them on the public sector?

If the argument is that restrictions will make authorities more efficient and businesslike, why are the Government not giving local authorities the same structure that the private sector needs? If, as I suspect, the Government's real aim is to make it more difficult to plan and to spend in the interests of local electors, we begin to see what the Bill is all about. The Government want more centralised control because they have an intense dislike of local democracy, regardless of whether it is controlled by the Tories, the Labour party or anyone else. Incidentally, that is why the London Boroughs Association and the Association of District Councils are worried about the indiscriminate nature of the Secretary of State's powers.

Many bodies have called for an increase in local authority capital expenditure, not least because they know that the infrastructure of the British economy is being eroded and that that infrastructure is largely provided by local authorities. It is the classic picture of Conservative rule—private affluence matched by public squalor. That is the image of Conservatism throughout this century and previously.

Image Col. 573 In 1985, the Audit Commission said that there was a backlog of £15 billion worth of work to be done. The Government's stock condition inquiry said that the figure was £18·8 billion. How are the Government hoping to deal with that? If they want local authorities to do that work, and they are to introduce these regulations later this year, how on earth will a local authority plan to deal with that work in the timescale necessary? The money they need has been cut or denied them and they are also told that they will not know what the regulations are, or what the law will be, in six months' time? That is the time we are talking about. The Bill could be enacted in six months' time, which is why the amendment must be accepted.

If the Government admit that £18·8 billion worth of work needs to be done, what will they do about it? Are they hoping that, somehow or other, the housing aspect will be lost because houses will be passed over to housing associations? What nonsense. We all know that housing associations are increasingly worried about taking on that burden. Even if they did take it on, they would not have the resources to deal with it. The problem will not go away simply because the Government duck their responsibility and try to pass it on to someone else.

The Government could free the accumulated capital receipts, which in March 1988 amounted to about £6 billion and by next year are estimated to be about £8 billion. If the Minister said today that the Government will release some of that money to local authorities, at least they could begin to do some of the work which they cannot plan for on the basis of the Bill.

The Government are reneging on a commitment given by Lord Jenkin on 19 December 1984, when he said:

"The receipts are and will remain the property of local authorities. They can spend them in future years".—[Official Report, 19 December 1984; Vol. 70, c. 308.]

That pledge is being abandoned. Members on all sides of the House have often said that assurances given by the Government are not to be trusted. The reason is obvious—an assurance given by one Minister is rapidly ditched by his successor, sometimes only months later.

As recently as January 1986, the Green Paper, "Paying for Local Government" stated

"Local authorities have been given the assurance that this amount"—

accumulated receipts—

"will be available in full over time to justify additional capital expenditure."

That assurance is also meaningless. If the Minister agrees to release the funds—although I should prefer him to accept our amendment No. 245—local authorities could begin some of the work that the Government's inquiries show needs to be done. Their assurances so far have meant nothing.

The amendment would provide for implementation in 1991, not 1990. For the reasons that I gave, its acceptance would make the task of local authorities of all political complexions much easier. Without it, their task will be much more difficult. There is a strong suspicion inside and outside the House—across the party divide at times—that the Government are not interested in efficient administration of local government. They want only to prevent local authorities from doing what their electors elected them to do. We also strongly suspect that the Government are dangerously unaware of the serious run-down of the Col. 574 infrastructure of the economy and of the lack of preparation at local and regional level for 1992 and the integrated European market.

I urge the Minister to accept amendment No. 245. If he does, even Conservative authorities will agree that they are being given an opportunity to respond to this difficult, complex and open-ended legislative proposal which allows the Secretary of State absolute discretion to decide how local authorities run their affairs. The power is being given to someone who is not a member of those bodies—something that the Government would be appalled at if it were done to the private sector. They are quite prepared to allow it to happen to local government, though. They should at least recognise that they no longer expect efficiency in local government. They are saying to authorities, "We shall put obstacles to efficiency in your path. If you cannot deliver, that is tough. We are passing the buck to you." I urge the Committee to support the amendment.

Local authorities are giving this emphasis largely in lieu of the Government, who are busy trying to chase private industry, hoping that it will do so instead. Unless a start is made on training, we shall be even more severely disadvantaged.

The proposals will frighten local authorities from doing what the Minister professes he wants them to do. He will live to regret his proposals. For the moment, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.
