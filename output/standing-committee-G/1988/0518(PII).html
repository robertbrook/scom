It would be possible for people to build extra buildings on the site, or to cram them in and take advantage of the relief—something that we did not find acceptable. If a developer did not have planning permission, or merely had a legal contract that was conditional on his obtaining planning permission later, he could not be said to be unconditionally or irrevocably committed to going ahead. Those reasons for introducing the planning permission condition as well as the legal commitment have been largely understood, and although obviously people prefer to have as few conditions as Image Col. 143 possible and to have transitional reliefs that are as long and extensive as possible, they recognise that we have gone out of our way to meet the transitional problems of the construction industry, and in our view, that is the best way to proceed.

Clause 20 deals with fuel and power, and my amendments refer to zero-rating of supplies of liquiefied petroleum gas—propane and butane. The clause, like the others that we have been debating, follows from the infamous decision of the European Court that compels us to change the rules on zero-rating. We still have a certain competence to zero rate supplies that would otherwise be standard rated where they are for domestic or charity use. In general, they are goods and services that are essential as the basis of daily life, as fuel and power certainly are. There are areas that are dependent upon liquiefied petroleum gases for light, heat, cooking and so on.

The problem that we face—it is one that the Treasury has had to find ways round—is that liquiefied petroleum gases have both commercial and domestic uses. It is difficult to find a foolproof system, so that zero-rated supplies do not drift into uses for which they should be standard rated, that is, commercial uses.

The Bill proposes that zero rates shall apply to LPGs in cylinders, where they are supplied directly to the final consumer. If the LPG producers sold direct to the consumers, no VAT would be leviable. If, however, supplies are sold to dealers and stockists—middlemen—in certain circumstances standard rates of VAT will apply.

There is a different regime for bulk supplies of LPGs delivered by tankers. Note (2)(e) provides that

"a supply of not more than 2,500 litres of petroleum gas in a liquid state"

will not be subject to VAT. I understand the Treasury's aim, which is to restrict zero-rating to domestic consumers. Nobody objects to commercial Col. 150 consumers having to pay VAT because, in almost every case, they are able to reclaim it through the usual input-output mechanism.

The Government had to make a choice. They could either zero rate supplies and risk seepage into the commercial sector, or impose a standard rate, applied with a fairly broad brush, requiring domestic users to pay VAT on their supplies when, strictly speaking, the European Court did not require us to do so.

On 6 February 1989 when the draft legislation for the proposal was published, Customs and Excise accepted that gas supplied in cylinder form would almost certainly be used for domestic purposes. That is not entirely the case, as 72 per cent. of gas supplied in cylinders is used domestically, leaving 28 per cent. for commercial use. If we split the gas into propane and butane, we find that almost all butane is used for domestic purposes. A small amount is used for commercial purposes, so there is no danger of seepage of the tax into areas where it should not go. The majority of the sales of propane gas are for industrial or commercial use, but it is not a large majority. Therefore, taken overall, three quarters of the gas is used domestically and about a quarter is used commercially.

A large number of the users of gas supplied in cylinders should not have to pay VAT on their supplies but, as a result of the Bill, they will. I am speaking not only in a personal capacity, but on behalf of the Small Business Bureau of which I am a vice-chairman. It has asked my right hon. and hon. Friends to reconsider the proposal. Various unfortunate consequences will flow from it that will harm small businesses, which are subject to the legislation.

First, traders who are not registered for VAT and who will decide not to register, even after the Bill becomes law, will not be able to recover the VAT. Therefore, they must either absorb the extra cost or pass it on to their customers. Secondly, traders who are forced to register because of the change will have to endure the administration and compliance costs of so doing. Thirdly, registered traders who continue to be registered will face extra cash flow problems—which, in certain instances, may be significant—as a result of having to pay extra VAT to Customs and Excise because of the taxability of their supplies.

In England and Wales, almost all the stockists of LPG are registered, so we need not worry too much about that. But about 5 per cent. of them are not registered for VAT in Scotland, and about a quarter of the stockists are unregistered in Northern Ireland.

We must consider the increase in the financial burden of people who live in peripheral areas. In most cases, they have no alternative other than to purchase cylinder gas to cook, heat their homes and possibly light their dwellings. I believe that there are deficiencies in the proposals for bulk supplies of LPGs. If more than 2,500 litres of gas is delivered in liquid state, it bears VAT, but if less than 2,500 litres is delivered, it is zero rated. A criterion by volume is unnecessary and will create anomalies. It will lead to commercial users obtaining supplies in contravention of the European Court judgment. Commercial users will be able to obtain supplies of less than 2,500 litres, which will be zero rated, in contravention of the European Court's judgment, and domestic consumers, who need not pay VAT, will do so if they receive more than 2,500 litres.

Information on who receives supplies of gas is greater than that on those who receive other forms of fuel. Because the gas is pumped in under pressure and is highly dangerous and explosive, virtually all the tanks used are owned by the oil companies that supply the gas. They know who their customers are and can tell which are commercial and which are domestic. Very few people who use such gas in bulk are domestic customers.

The industry proposes a different criterion of an installed tank capacity of 2 tonnes. That would simplify the administration of the tax because only 3 per cent. of tanks have a capacity of more than 2 tonnes, but they account for 75 per cent. of sales. With a small amount of administration, a large part of the objective would be achieved. Because of the way in which the gas is distributed by tanker, drops of less than 2,500 litres are often delivered to commercial users because the tanks need to be emptied out at the end of the day. We propose a criterion that would be less onerous on those who are supplied and probably better for Customs and Excise.

Why was the figure of 2,500 litres chosen as the criterion for bulk supply? It does not fit in with the calorific equivalents of other fuels mentioned in the clause. Note (2)(f) refers to 2,300 litres of fuel oil, which is equivalent calorifically to 3,335 litres of petroleum gas, so petroleum gas seems to have been unfairly treated compared with oil.

There will be a temptation to reduce the drop size to avoid VAT. That is not good for tax morality, nor for the industry, because it will tend to increase the cost of administration and delivery. In addition, for bulk supplies of more than 2,500 litres, the industry will be obliged in cases of doubt to seek a declaration from the person supplied that he will use it for domestic purposes and not commercial purposes if it is supplied on zero-rated terms. Calor Gas Ltd, which is the largest supplier, has informed me that it will have to seek more than 7,000 declarations a year and that the cost of the changes in its computer software programs to calculate the VAT and change its present systems is estimated to be £100,000. We thought that if we zero-rated them at the final stage of the delivery chain, the fact that a few would go into industrial use would be overlooked by the Commission and that we would not face an infraction process. However, my hon. Friend wants us to go further than that and to zero-rate further back down the chain. That will not affect final consumers at all. The only benefit will be to retailers. He believes that some retailers are unregistered, indeed he gave figures—I am not aware of their source—that suggested that many retailers in Northern Ireland are unregistered traders handling LPG. That does not coincide with our view.

An unregistered trader must have a turnover of less than £23,600, which includes the sale of zero-rated products. It is hard to see how a typical retailer with gross margins of less than 35 per cent. and net margins of much less than that would make a living. I would naturally look at the figures if my hon. Friend can persuade me that they come from a reputable source. I know that he only ever deals in reputable sources.

My hon. Friend was also concerned about cash flow. When a wholesaler or manufacturer supplying Calor gas puts VAT on it, he gets the VAT on average some six weeks before he is required to pay that VAT over to Customs. He is getting a cash flow advantage out of those further down the distribution chain which he enjoys. If he wishes, he can pass on some of that advantage to those further down the chain. If not, he can scarcely complain that they are being hard done by under the VAT system. In general the cash flow effect is fairly small, and unlikely to add more than a tiny amount to the overall cost of the Image Col. 153 product, even if the manufacturing supplier does not pass on any advantage to those further down. We shall keep these and all matters under review in the light of any information that my hon. Friend the Member for Tatton can give me, but I am not persuaded at this stage that it would be right to change the arrangements for cylinders.

On bulk deliveries, my hon. Friend saw no case for a quantitative limit. Rather, he seemed worried that some industrial users might get the benefit of small deliveries of less than 2,500 litres zero-rated. I do not think that we need worry about the possibility. If they do, it is a de minimis problem arising from the way in which we have chosen to help domestic consumers.

For all forms of fuel and power such as gas and electricity where we set a limit and which we believe go to a final consumer we asked the industries what a typical large household supply was, and 2,500 litres was the figure for LPG. The limits are not measured on a calorific equivalent basis from one fuel to another.

My hon. Friend none the less pressed his case on the impact of the procedure. He wished to base the figure not on size of delivery but on the size of the container. If the method that we have chosen proves to have disadvantages in application we are prepared to look at it again. I assure my hon. Friend that in the light of his remarks we shall keep the matter under review. We think it appropriate to go ahead at this stage with the approach that we have adopted in the clause. It is designed to be fairly generous to consumers, and we are not too worried if it is generous to some industrial users as well.

In answer to the hon. Member for Wrexham, we could not avoid applying VAT to LPG or even to butane. We could not single them out. They were clearly covered by the Court judgment and we had to apply VAT, but we have done so in a fairly generous way.

Mr. Lilley: In response to the hon. Member for Kirkcaldy (Dr. Moonie), the question is not one of equity. We are not trying to be equitable between users of different types of fuel and saying that they should all have the same allowance. We are trying to reach a judgment on the normal maximum amount that a household uses or takes as a delivery. Deliveries of coal may tend to be smaller in calorific terms—because of their bulk—than are deliveries of oil. If so, we shall take whatever seems to be the normal maximum that indicates a household delivery and any larger delivery—would probably be going to an industrial firm or commercial business.

That is all that we are trying to do, but if a private user uses more or receives larger deliveries, he can still retain a zero rate by means of declaration. Unfortunately, that user will have to go through that bureaucratic procedure, but we are trying to minimise the need for the supplier to identify who is a final consumer and who is a consumer by means of customer declarations.

Dr. Moonie: The law may constrain the size of tank to which deliveries are being consigned. If the law sets an upper limit of a certain amount on the supply, the chances are that a domestic tank will be of a size below that level. Therefore, the law is defining, a priori, the size of the consignment, so there will be inequitable treatment.

Mr. Lilley: We do not have a tank-based system and calculate on the basis of individual deliveries. There may be legal or other reasons why the normal maximum delivery to a household is what it is in the case of a certain fuel. We are not trying to say what is right or wrong but to make the best pragmatic judgment of when a delivery is going to a household and when it is going to an industrial business. We consulted the industry and originally suggested 2,000 litres for LPG. The industry said that it was more likely to be 2,500 litres and that up to that level most deliveries would be to households. We accepted that figure and put in into the Bill.

The hon. Member for Bridgend (Mr. Griffiths) asked whether there was a danger of a subsequent infraction proceeding. We hope not, but we cannot give an absolute guarantee as it is up to the Commission. We think that we have got it right and that it will agree that it is a reasonable and pragmatic way of operating a ruling. We are trying not to negate the whole impact of the judgment but to retain zero rates for households while applying a standard rate to industry.
