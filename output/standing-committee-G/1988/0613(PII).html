I refer to the speech of my hon. Friend the Economic Secretary, as he seemed to be misleading himself. He said that sometimes we think that retrospection is a good thing and that when it is employed to someone's benefit, it should be treated on the same basis as retrospection when the result would adversely affect an individual.

When someone bestows his own money on somebody else, that is quite different in principle and in kind from forcing him to hand over the money to the Government. No symmetry is involved in that comparison because, if the Government choose to offer tax relief, they are perfectly entitled to do so. The other way round the problem is much more difficult. I have some difficulty with the notion that because it was the general view that the law was as the Revenue thought it was, that is what we should ensure is the law not only for the future, but for the period before the court decided otherwise.

There are many cases in English law, and, indeed, in tax law, in which different consequences could flow from similar circumstances. There was a great case in the late 1970s between Vestey and the Inland Revenue commissioners, which arose out of the provisions of section 478 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act. Mr. Hamilton

That case arose out of facts that almost everyone had accepted as being the law for 30 or 40 years, but one taxpayer challenged the general view and was successful in getting the House of Lords to overturn a previous decision of the other place. When that section was introduced in the Finance Act 1935 or 1936—I forget which—the Chancellor of the Exchequer of the day said that it would not be applied in the way in which the Revenue thereafter sought to apply it.

However, the Padmore case was similar inasmuch as the behaviour of the individuals involved was not influenced by the tax consequences of the court's decision.

It is a fairly dangerous procedure to follow, by which we must look into the minds of individual taxpayers, who are successful perhaps because of the results of other people's litigation, to determine whether the Government are right to introduce retrospective tax legislation. Until the introduction in 1984 of domestic UK legislation that enabled Eurobonds to be issued directly by UK companies without having to withhold tax from interest, a favoured route to avoid the withholding obligation was for a UK company to issue such Eurobonds via a Netherlands Antilles company.
