Clause 139
POWER TO CALL FOR PAPERS OF TAX ACCOUNTANT

4.30 pm

Mr. Nicholas Brown
(Newcastle upon Tyne, East)
: I beg to move amendment No. 419, in page 104, line 35, after "question", insert

"whether or not such description is general in terms".

It would probably help the Committee if I said at the outset that this afternoon we hope to put into legislation a series of recommendations arising from the Keith committee. We were able to discuss the principles involved in this series of clauses in a brief debate in the Chamber on clause 138, which has already been agreed to. I see no reason to trawl through that again. Instead, I hope that we can move through these clauses in a workmanlike way and bring this part of the Bill to a rapid conclusion. As I said on Second Reading, the Opposition are in favour of implementing the recommendations of the Keith committee. If there is any resistance, it will not be from the Opposition Benches.

Our amendment No. 419 is designed to explore the Government's thinking on the main clause, which sets out the descriptions of documents that can be called for by the Revenue. We wonder how explicit the description has to be. It is a straightforward point. We simply seek to ensure that the Revenue is not denied access to a document simply because it has been described in terms that are too general. I am fairly certain that the Government do not intend to allow such a loophole, but I should like to explore with the Financial Secretary the mechanisms that the Government have in place to prevent that. It might be possible to describe a document as a sale contract between X and Y. It may be that the Revenue can only be general in its request, and we should not wish it to be denied access to the papers of a tax accountant because the test was too narrow.

The Chairman
: Before we proceed further it might be helpful if I reported to the Committee now that, following consultations through the usual channels, the Committee will sit next Tuesday in Committee Room 10.

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Norman Lamont)
: I am grateful to the hon. 474 Member for Newcastle-upon-Tyne, East (Mr. Brown) for explaining the purpose of the amendment. As he said, the principles of the Keith report were discussed when clause 138 was debated in the Chamber. In these first few clauses today, we are debating the information powers of the Revenue.

Clause 138 introduces new safeguards for the taxpayer under the Revenue's powers to call for information in the course of inquiries into a person's tax affairs. The new safeguards protect personal records and journalistic material from disclosure to the Revenue and ensure that the Revenue allows not less than 30 days for the papers to be delivered.

Under a separate information power, the Revenue can call for the papers of a tax accountant who has been convicted of a tax offence. The purpose of this clause is to give the public the same new safeguards under this power as they are given under clause 138 in respect of the more general powers.

The Committee will wish to ensure that the same safeguards ae available under both those provisions, and therefore that the clause mirrors the words in the previous clause. It would, therefore, be inappropriate to amend clause 139 and to add words that do not appear in clause 138. However, that is obviously a minor point.

The amendment would allow notices to be given in general terms. A notice puts a legal obligation on the recipient, and he can be penalised if he fails to meet it. It is clearly right that a notice must therefore be sufficiently specific in its terms for the person to know what is required, and that is the effect of the present law. It would not be appropriate to allow notices to be given in any more general terms than is posible under the present law.

I hope that that explains how the powers would work and why notices will be required to be in specific terms. We have discussed and shall discuss what will be excluded from those powers. Obviously, it will be possible to ask for working papers that are relevant to the inquiries that are being made under the provisions, provided that they do not fall in the exempt categories. However, the notice itself will have to be specific, and it can be specific except in so far as the items that have already been mentioned are excused.

Mr. Brown
: As with many of these proposals, we shall have to see how the provisions work out in practice. I accept that to allow notices to be made in general terms is probably going too wide, as the Financial Secretary said. The purpose of our amendment was to explore precisely what the Government intended by the phrase "specific terms". I have listened carefully to what the Financial Secretary has said and although I suspect that we may retrun to the subject in the future, in the circumstances I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Clause 139 ordered to stand part of the Bill.

Clause 140
RESTRICTIONS ON POWERS UNDER TMA SS.20 AND 20A

Mr. Nicholas Brown
: I beg to move amendment No. 420, in page 105, line 15, at end insert

"and for "6" there shall be substituted "12".'.

The amendment relates to section 20B of the Taxes Management Act 1970. Subsection (5) states that a person or company who has been carrying on a business is not obliged to make a document available if it originates more than six years before the date of the notice requiring it. Our amendment is intended to explore with the Government why the provision of six years is set out. Our amendment would extend the period to 12 years, the rationale being that in many instances significant Inland Revenue inquiries can take a long time to come to fruition and can extend back for many years. It is our fear—I hope that the Financial Secretary can meet it—that six years may be unduly restrictive.

Mr. Lamont
: Clause 140 has two purposes. First, it requires the Revenue to inform taxpayers that it is seeking documents from a third party and secondly, it makes changes to the professional privilege for tax accountants, aligning it with that of lawyers.

The hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, East (Mr. Brown) has put forward a case for increasing the power of the Revenue to call for documents that are more than six years old, and he is saying that that period could be increased to 12 years. The power that the Revenue has is subject to several important safeguards to ensure that it cannot be used in an unreasonable or oppressive fashion, and to prevent any unnecessary or excessive invasion of privacy. The Revenue is allowed to call for documents that are more than six years old only where there are reasonable grounds for believing that tax has been lost through fraud.

The hon. Gentleman's suggestion would be seen as oppressive. This clause, like the other clauses that are subject to consultation, has been supported generally by the professions. The existing limit of six years corresponds to the normal limits in general law, for instance under the statute of limitations, and also to the normal time limit under tax law for assessing tax, except where tax is lost through a default by the taxpayer. So, in general, we do not go back more than six years.

There is no statutory limit on how far back the Revenue can go when it calls for information from the taxpayer himself—we shall discuss that point later. That will depend on the circumstances of the case. But it is reasonable that there should be a proper limit where the Revenue calls for information from a third party. One of the major issues is that we are talking about information from a third party. It is not just a matter of putting an obligation and the compliance burden on the taxpayer.

The present limit of six years allows the Revenue access to information that it could reasonably require in the normal course of inquiries. That is the Revenue's view and it is also my view. It is also reasonable that it should be overridden where 476 serious offences may be involved. To extend the limit to 12 years when third parties are involved would be imposing on others an unnecessary obligation and would be seen as unnecessarily oppressive.

Mr. Brown
: The purpose of the amendment was to explore the Government's thinking behind the six-year period. What the Financial Secretary said was entirely reasonable and I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Amendment No. 423 deals with the same issue, but in a different way. An auditor should have a statutory obligation to notify a tax fraud, which he has reasonable ground for suspecting if his attention has been drawn to it during his work for a particular client. Amendment No. 423 seeks to extend that principle and provision.

If our suggestion is not considered appropriate, the problem could be tackled in several ways. For example, we could call for changes in company law to require that an auditor's certificate must state specifically that the audit has revealed nothing that causes him to suspect that a tax fraud has been committed by the company, although there could be opposition to that on the ground that it would hurt the auditor-client relationship. If such concessions are to be made and emphasised, surely it is also right to emphasise the accountancy profession's obligations, in its professional ethics and standards, to the Inland Revenue. We touched on that on Second Reading and I repeat that I am not sure that the analogy between the accountancy and the legal professions is applicable in all aspects. A solicitor's belief in his client's innocence in a criminal case is ultimately tested in an adversarial system in the courts, but an accountant's certification of his client's books or accounts for the year may be all that the Revenue has to go on when assessing whether more tax is due. After all, the Revenue does not have the resources to conduct a detailed investigation into every commercial undertaking, year in, year out.

I am not arguing that accountants have no obligation to their clients or that in all circumstances their obligation should be suppressed in the interests of the Revenue, because that would be nonsense, but a balance should be struck. By protecting, through privilege, accountants' working papers, a major concession has been made. That should be balanced by an obligation to the Revenue, which should be at least as strong as the professional obligations that all professions emphasise when their independence is challenged.

Protection is open to misuse and the hon. But we have taken steps in the provision to prevent that happening by allowing the Revenue access to information that it really needs even if it is to be found only in the audit working papers. There is a specific provision in the clause allowing the Revenue access to fact, showing how a figure in the accounts or in the return has been arrived at, even if it is available only in the audit papers. So a balance has been struck and the position of the Revenue has been safeguarded.

I should not go so far as the hon. Gentleman and agree that auditors should be put under a statutory 478 obligation to report evidence of tax fraud to the Revenue. That would be in clear breach of the duty of confidentiality incumbent upon auditors.

It would also have the undesirable effect of making it more difficult for the auditors to carry out his proper function of protecting the interests of shareholders, as its main effect would be to encourage a dishonest director to go to greater lengths to conceal matters. The issue is difficult, but on balance the arguments weigh against the hon. Gentleman. It would be a highly controversial step to impose such an obligation on auditors.

Mr. Brown

The present statutory de minimis limits therefore avoid the administrative cost of calculating and paying small amounts of interest as it was felt that they were not appropriate to the Revenue's operating on a computerised basis. The reason is simply that statutory de minimis limits rapidly become outdated and, although it is intended, and expected, that these provisions will operate to the advantage of the taxpayer, we shall operate some de minimis amounts administratively.

The clause allows the preent statutory de minimis limits to be removed as procedures for charging and paying interest are transferred to the new computer system. Interest will then be paid on late repayments without a de minimis limit. Demands for small amounts of interest on unpaid tax will, however, be prevented by administrative de minimis limits that can be more easily updated. The clause paves the way to further streamlining of the tax system through the introduction of new and more efficient accounting systems for the collection of tax. As I explained, we expect that the effect of this will be to benefit taxpayers in general. The changes in the clause will, in general, benefit them individually—the removal of the de minimis limits will always benefit them. The effect of the removal of statutory limits, where money is owed by the taxpayer, will depend upon the administrative limits used by the Revenue when the new system is introduced—perhaps in 1922. This will prevent demands for small amounts of interest on unpaid tax. However, I must make it clear that, in some cases, small amounts of interest will be charged jointly with larger amounts of tax, which would not be pursued under existing provisions. Clause 156
TAX CARRYING INTEREST UNDER TMA SS. 86 AND 88

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Lamont
: The clause simplifies the procedure for collecting interest where tax is assessed late. In those circumstances the date that interest starts to run depends on whether the taxpayer or the Revenue is at fault. If the assessment is late as a result of a fault by the Revenue—for instance, because of delays in processing the taxpayer's return—interest starts to run from the date that the tax becomes due and payable under the late assessment. If the assessment is late as a result of a fault by the taxpayer—for instance, because his return was late or incorrect—interest starts to run from the earlier date that tax would have been due if it had been assessed at the proper time.

Clause 97
SURRENDER OF COMPANY TAX REFUND ETC WITHIN GROUP

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Lamont
: The clause relates to the new scheme for charging and collecting corporation tax, known as pay and file. When we introduced the legislation two years ago, we said that pay and file would not come into effect until 1992—a significant year—at the earliest. I announced in April this year that, because of the need to take on board changes in the tax system such as independent taxation, pay and file would not begin before 1993 at the earliest.

The clause is a relieving provision. Interest rates under pay and file will be set in line with the market, charging interest on unpaid tax at an average cost of borrowing, and paying interest on repayments at an average return on deposits. The provision is needed to avoid distortion to the flow of moneys between the Exchequer and the corporate sector, and to prevent round-tripping. If we had no interest differential 492 after pay and file, it would be easy for people to make a turn by simply delaying their payment of tax. The rate charged on late payments will therefore be higher than the rate paid on overpayments, just as the rates charged on borrowing in the market are higher than the rate paid on deposits.

The clause is a relieving provision in that it provides groups of companies with a safeguard against interest charges where there is no net underpayment across the group as a whole. Where one company has paid too much, and another too little, it allows the overpayment to be transferred without interest running either way. Without that safeguard, there would be a net cost to the group as interest would be charged on the underpayment at a higher rate than was paid on the overpayment. The clause also allows groups to rearrange their tax liabilities to make the best use of their available reliefs without exposing the groups to the net costs that would otherwise result from the differential in the interest rates.
