The Paymaster General (Mr Peter Brooke)
: May I allude, in as loud a voice as I can manage, to the pleasure of being in this Room under your chairmanship, Mr. Cormack? The previous time that I was here, I was the Government Whip on the Local Government Finance (No. 2) Bill. Because of an event that occurred—not in this Room but due to the fact that we had been in it—and due to what I shall describe not as a ruse but as a strategy of the right hon. Member for Birmingham, Sparkbrook (Mr. Hattersley), my ministerial career almost ended quickly. Fortunately I survived and I am delighted, nine years later, to be here.

It may help the Committee if I say a word or two about the most substantial change that has been made in schedule 4 as an introduction to this part of the Bill. In 1987, we introduced an important tax relief to encourage employees to make part of their pay profit-related. Registration of profit-related pay schemes started in the autumn of 1987, and by the 262 end of March the number of live schemes had already reached 869 covering more than 122,000 employees. Although I know that the impatient have described that progress as disappointing, the Government see it as clear evidence that the benefits to both employers and employees of flexible pay arrangements linked to profits are becoming more widely known. We have succeeded in stimulating a widespread debate among employers, employees and professional advisers about the need to break rigidities in the British pay system. The provision of the tax relief has prompted considerable interest among employers in creating or developing various profit-sharing schemes.

Inevitably, such an innovative tax relief has also stimulated detailed criticisms. Because we sought to allow employers reasonable flexibility in the design, introduction and operation of their profit-related pay schemes, the legislation is necessarily fairly substantial and appears complex. At the same time, it was essential, if we were to ensure that the tax relief was directed effectively towards the objectives that we intended, that the legislation should contain certain basic requirements. Perhaps, not surprisingly, some employers have found that, in the particular circumstances of their organisations, one or other of the safeguards is inconvenient to them and there have been calls to make changes.

However, we needed time for PRP to settle down and for everyone—both employers and their advisers and the Inland Revenue—to gain experience of how things were working. That is why we deliberately refrained from making any changes last year. Now, in the light of general experience of the first 18 months' operation of PRP, of comments and suggestions received and answers to our questions, we have carefully reviewed the scheme and we have concluded that certain improvements can and should be made, The changes are designed further to increase employers' flexibility and choice in the design of their schemes and at the same time to simplify the requirements as far as possible while ensuring that PRP continues to serve the purposes underlying it.

I do not propose to go into detail on the five significant changes and the group of minor changes, but I wish to mention the material interest test—not least because it comes up in subsequent clauses with which my right hon. Friend the Financial Secretary will deal. My introductory remarks may be helpful.

Employees who have a material interest in their company cannot participate in a registered PRP scheme. In deciding whether an employee has a material interest, the shareholdings of the trustees of a trust under which he may benefit are at present taken into account. Paragraph 9 of the schedule provides that, in future, in certain circumstances they should be disregarded.

Similar changes to the material interest rules are being made for employee share schemes and close company interest relief. The employee share ownership plans provisions also include a material interest test, which is consistent with this change. It may therefore be helpful in considering the change in paragraph 9 to bear in mind its wider relevance.

It might help if I explain briefly what the material interest test is and the reasons for having it. JPEG 263 Employees who have a sizeable or "material" interest in their company cannot participate in registered PRP schemes or approved employee share schemes. For PRP—as for all-employee share schemes—that means an interest in more than 25 per cent. of the ordinary shares of the company. For discretionary share schemes, it means an interest in more than 10 per cent.

There are two reasons for the material interest tests. First, they seek to ensure that the various tax reliefs associated with the schemes cannot be manipulated to their own advantage by those who, by virtue of substantial holdings in the company, may be in a position to do so. Secondly, there would be no justification in giving valuable tax reliefs to people who already have a substantial identification with the success of the business through their existing interest in it. There is probably nothing in the principle underlying the test with which any members of the Committee would wish to disagree. A material interest test featured in the 1978 legislation, brought in by the previous Labour Government, covering approved profit-sharing schemes.

For obvious reasons, the legislation requires that in addition to the individual's own shares, those of his associates have to be reckoned in deciding whether he has a material interest. As the law stands, if the employee has an interest in a trust that holds some of the company's ordinary shares, the trustees are included among his associates and their shareholding has to be added to his and that of his other associates, if any, in determining whether he has a material interest.

Such a rule is clearly appropriate for certain sorts of trusts, for example, where a substantial part of the shares in a family company are held by a trust essentially for the benefit of a few members of the family. But counting trustees' shares can mean that some employees may not participate in PRP or employee share schemes, even though their interest in a trust is in reality remote or small, for example, where a trust has been set up for the benefit of all the employees of quite a large company, and where the benefits that they actually receive or may receive, individually, are small. That difficulty was pointed out by my hon. Friend the Member for Slough (Mr. Watts) during last year's Finance Bill debates, when my right hon. Friend the Financial Secretary undertook to consider whether relaxations might be possible in the material interest tests for PRP and share schemes.

The provisions in this year's bill seek to resolve that difficulty by allowing shares held by an employee benefit trust to be ignored in calculating an employee's interest, provided that certain conditions are met. The form that the relaxation takes is exclusion of the trustees from the associates of the employee concerned.

Clearly, it is not easy to find a sensible path between the two extremes that I have mentioned—the family trust benefiting only a few, and the much wider trust benefiting individuals only to a very small extent. The solution that we have adopted, aimed at providing a relaxation that will apply to as many 264 trusts as possible, is a novel one. Rather then looking at what a trust is empowered to do, which involves attributing to each beneficiary the trustees' total interest—which has been the approach hitherto—we now propose that account should be taken of what the trustees actually do, in distributing benefits to beneficiaries. That seems a more reasonable approach, which takes account of the realities.

I thought that it would be helpful to make those general introductory remarks, but on the rest of the schedule, it may be easier if I reply to points raised by hon. Members.

(Islington, South and Finsbury)

It is worth reflecting on the way in which profit-related pay was introduced. It was first announced in principle in the Chancellor's Budget statement of 1986 and it was fleshed out in detail in the Budget and Finance Bill in 1987. The motives behind its introduction were interesting. In his Budget speech in 1986, the Chancellor attributed considerable virtues to profit-related pay. The major motives for introducing it, as defined in that speech, were, first, that it would enable a major reduction in unit labour costs—unit labour costs were identified as a principal weakness in the British economy. Secondly, it would provide considerable flexibility for both employers and employees when an enterprise went through good and bad times. Thirdly, it would reduce the inflationary effect of increases in wages, and finally it would give employees a personal stake in the profitability of the company for which they worked.

Those motives are to be welcomed, but it is noticeable that profit-related pay has subsequently fallen far short of those principles and aims. The aims spelt out at that stage assumed that profit-related pay would be a fundamental and major part of an employee's income rather than a top-up bonus. That principle was enshrined in the 5 per cent. rule, which stipulated that at least 5 per cent. of an employee's income had to be part of the PRP scheme before tax relief became operative. The 5 per cent. rule is being abandoned, and I shall return to that point shortly because it is the most revealing and important change being made in schedule 4. However, the supposed flexibility and incentive effect, which the Chancellor spelt out at the launch of the idea of profit-related pay in 1986, has failed to materialise.

The Paymaster General—I suspect, a little tongue in cheek—told us that profit-related pay had been JPEG 265 an important change. He said that there was clear evidence that the benefits were becoming more widely known and that it was an innovative form of tax relief. He referred to the figures for take-up, which are instructive. If we consider the number of schemes registered and the number of employees covered by them, we see that take-up is very low. At the end of December 1988, there were 830 registered schemes covering 122,400 employees. That was an increase of 15,100 on the previous quarter, but it is still a relatively small number of the employees in the total work force. A quarter later, at the end of March this year, the latest figures available show that there are 869 registered schemes and that the number of employees has far from increased. It went down marginally in the quarter to March 1989, by 300, to 122,100 employees. That is a reduction over the previous three months.

The figures show that the scheme is not a major innovation that is being taken up on a wide scale and that it is not proving to be of enormous benefit to industry. If we compare the figures with the predictions that the Chancellor made, we see how far short of expectations PRP has fallen. In the 1987 Budget statement, the Chancellor said:

"The cost will inevitably depend on take-up. It could be"—

Admittedly, he used the conditional tense, but it was a bullish Chancellor making reasonably confident predictions about the level of take-up—

"It could be £50 million in 1988–89, building up to substantially more than that, as take-up grows, and as the proportion of an employee's pay which is profit-related rises."—[ Official Report, 17 March 1987; Vol. 112, c. 823.]

Note that the government are not now encouraging raising the proportion of an employee's pay that can be included in a PRP scheme, but are seeking to reduce it by removing the 5 per cent. threshold rule. That predicted figure of £50 million in 1988–89 must also be set against the actual figure of £15 million, which profit-related pay cost the Exchequer in that financial year. The changes in the schedule are estimated to add a further £10 million to that bill. That is far short of the Chancellor's confident expectations when he launched the scheme in detail in the 1987 Budget. The take-up has been disappointing for the Chancellor, whose friends in industry are deserting him on whether profit-related pay has the incentive effect that he claimed it would have.

The survey conducted by Incomes Data Services suggests that most companies with registered schemes are small and in the finance, management consultancy and design sectors of the economy. The three largest participants, which account for some one fifth of employees covered by PRP schemes, are Nationwide Anglia Building Society, Gallaher and Norwich Union Insurance Group. The IDS survey of employers in the autumn of 1987 suggests that even the companies participating in the scheme had considerable doubts about the incentive effect of profit-related pay. The survey says:

"The incentive effect of PRP is being questioned by some companies."

It is clear to us that profit-related pay is being used 266 not as a fundamental part of employees' earnings, not as the incentive motive that the Government claimed that it would be, not as the flexible means of taking employers and employees through good times and bad times, but as a tax-free bonus operation to make available additional funds to employees on the margin. The changes in the schedule do nothing to diminish that view of PRP.

The removal of the 5 per cent. rule takes away the entire basis on which profit-related pay was launched and means that it can be used as a top-up of any kind in relation to earnings. It does not have to be a substantial part of the earnings of an employee. I might accept the argument, which the Government have not yet made, that the 5 per cent. threshold made it difficult to get schemes off the ground, that meeting that threshold was a substantial step facing an employer and an employee, and that to assist schemes to get off the ground, the disincentive effect of the 5 per cent. rule should be reconsidered. But then we must question why the entire 5 per cent. rule is being removed. Why not try a stepping mechanism for new schemes, so that where a firm or an employee first adopts PRP, the 5 per cent. threshold should not apply, but should come into operation within, say, two years? That might be a sensible way to tackle the starting-off problem. But instead of that the Government have decided to scrap the whole principle. That is not a wise step because it means that PRP will be diverted entirely from the supposed fundamental values that the Chancellor had in launching it three years ago.

There is much concern about the removal of the 5 per cent. rule. Will the Government consider a stepping mechanism as an alternative to the total removal that they propose? Secondly, the inclusion of head office and headquarters staff within PRP schemes, as proposed in the schedule, throws considerable doubt on the supposed incentive effects. The principle of PRP was that those directly involved in the production of profit should be reminded of that in their wage packet. That principle of direct involvement is being breached by the schedule. The inclusion of all employees within an enterprise, including those not directly involved in the production of profit, is not a particularly sensible way forward.

We have a few questions about the changes to the material interest test, which the Paymaster General expanded on at some length. We accept the principle underlying the test. The Paymaster General was absolutely right when he said that there was almost certainly no disagreement on both sides of the Committee about that. We have no objection to the principle of the test, nor do we have any objection in principle to the removal of consideration for the shareholdings of trustees in a trust of which an employee is a member. The connection may well be remote. It seems sensible to move in the direction that the Government propose.

However, I have two questions to which I am sure that the Government will give us satisfactory answers. First, why is the figure 25 per cent? I appreciate that it has been at that level for some time. That is not necessarily a reason for keeping it there. The question of where the line should be drawn is endlessly debatable, but it would be useful to hear about the Government's thinking on the choice of one quarter as the test for material interest. It seems fairly high and it might be sensible, perhaps as a quid pro quo for the more generous changes that the Government are making elsewhere, to consider reducing the 25 per cent. threshold. Are the Government absolutely certain that no trust could be deliberately established specifically for the purpose of avoiding the material interest rule? I hope that the answer will be that no trust could be established in such circumstances to avoid the test.
