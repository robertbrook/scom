Mr. Soley
: You may recall, Mr. Hunt, that before we adjourned I was trying to assist the educational process of the Minister. He had just introduced the interesting new legal doctrine that a person should not be allowed to appeal because it caused uncertainty and delay. That was an interesting innovation in what, I accept, is not criminal or civil law.

There is a danger that individuals will lose their civil liberties and their right to participate in political activity in a democracy. Anyone who has that right restricted should be able to appeal, but the Minister gives that right only to those who earn more than £13,500 a year. That is a bizarre and unreasonable way of deciding whether someone should have the right. Those whose rights are restricted in that way should not simply have to accept, in effect, what the Government have said—that they cannot particpate in the party political process or, if they do, they must give up their jobs. That is unacceptable. As the Government have chosen that course, they should provide a right of appeal for everyone and not only for those who earn more than £13,500 a year.

The Minister claimed that none of his friends in local authorities opposed the provision. Before the adjournment I was about to read what the Conservative-controlled London Boroughs Association said, as it seems to sum up its attitude to the first three clauses. It stated:

"The Association is concerned that the definition of politically restricted posts is too widely drawn and in particular that the restriction on dual tracking for officers who deal with the public is drafted in such a way that it would be extremely difficult for local authorities to implement."

The Minister cannot claim that I am using casual quotes from Conservative councillors. That was the considered view of the Conservative-controlled London Boroughs Association.

The association says clearly that the provision is too difficult for local authorities to implement. It is drafted in a peculiar way and is too restrictive. If the Minister will not listen to the Opposition or to other bodies, he should at least listen to Conservative party councillors and allow them to appeal against what is described as a restrictive system, one which

"it would be extremely difficult for local authorities to implement."

I ask the Minister to consider those words. If they represent the reality, it is not unreasonable to argue 220 that, yet again, the Minister is imposing on local authorities rules and regulations that will make their lives even more difficult. In so doing, people's civil rights may be affected because of a local authority's decision. As the LBA rightly said, it is a difficult decision to make; it is not a clear one.

In those circumstances, it is reasonable to assume that mistakes will be made. The Minister says that that may be so, but there can be no appeal. If he persists in that view, no one should have any illusion about what the Government are doing. The core of the Opposition's argument is that the Government have constantly attacked both Labour and Conservative local authorities and are undermining and destroying them. That undermines morale in local authorities and destroys local democracy.

It is for the electorate to decide whether it likes what local authorities do or do not do, not for the Minister. If the electorate does not like them, it should get rid of them. If the Minister introduces new rules and regulations which Conservative councillors describe as too restrictive and, in effect, to complex, it is for him to justify them and to ensure that individuals can appeal against decisions which are almost certain to be wrong in some instances. It is grossly unsatisfactory for him to draw a magic line between those who earn more than £13,500 a year and can appeal and those who earn less than that and cannot appeal, with some ifs and buts in an intermediate position. I return to the Minister's own words when he described an appeals system as causing uncertainty and delay and therefore one which he is not prepared to consider.

If our country constructed legal rules on that basis, no one would have a right to appeal against a conviction in a court of law. All of us would consider that to be wrong. My argument is that this issue is not essentially different. I accept that we are not dealing with criminal or civil law, with a matter being argued before a judge. But the same rights of natural justice apply. The Minister has a duty to ensure that everyone has the right to appeal, without exception. If he removes those rights, at the very least he should allow people to appeal if they think that their rights have been wrongly taken away.
