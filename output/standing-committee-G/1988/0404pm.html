: I shall probably reiterate what was said this morning because I arrived late.

I came into the Room towards the end of this morning's sitting, when I learnt that clause 30 includes the promised provision which allows the Secretary of State to make orders that cover the capital expenditure of local authority controlled and influenced companies.

We knew what the Government's reaction to our amendment would be. The consultation paper, "Local Authorities' Interests in Companies", published in June last year, made it clear that the Government believe that the capital transactions of both types of company defined in clauses 57 and 58 should count against the appropriate control total of the authority.

Despite many responses registering anxiety from individual authorities and their associations, the Government have not moved on influenced companies, nor have they responded to our arguments today. It is apparent that both types of company are to be lumped together.

It was predictable that the Government would insist on the capital transactions of controlled 608 companies being counted as capital expenditure of the controlling authority. The company is in effect an arm of the authority, which can relatively easily exert its power to control decision making. The argument for excluding influenced companies is not that they should be seen solely as independent, but that, by definition, the authority does not have control, only influence.

The legality of authorities' accounting arrangements depending on the actions of bodies over which they genuinely have no control is questionable. Even if a contract exists, and conditions are included in leases, a breach of contract on the part of a company would render the authority in breach of the law. That is a most unusual and unsatisfactory position.

Under clause 58, it would not be difficult for a company to float into and out of the influenced category. Authorities will have to become involved in much additional work, checking whether the companies that they deal with are influenced, and ensuring that contractual protection exists, pursuant to the duty imposed under the order to be made under the clause.

Problems are more likely to occur when a company moves into the influenced category. The duty could have arisen, and the authority's capital arrangements thereby affected to its detriment, without the authority knowing about it. If the Government's purpose is to avoid circumventing controls on local authority capital expenditure, it is difficult to envisage how an authority could use an influenced rather than a controlled company to do that. Enormous ingenuity would be required and there is hardly any evidence of abuse that justifies the Government's line. My hon. Friends and I will oppose the clause because of the Government's attitude to the amendments.

Those of us who have been in local government for many years—I served for 18 years—took much of that time to understand that, and I am not sure that I could put my hand on my heart and say that I understand every aspect of it.

The purpose of the amendments is to ensure that the Government provide clarification and to remove some of the more destructive parts of the clause. It is nonsense to place direct labour organisations in a less competitive position than those with which they must compete. The regulations relating to capital expenditure that will put them in a disadvantageous position not only infringe acceptable practice, but may warrant investigation in the light of EEC competition policy on DLOs and the way in which expenditure on plant and equipment is treated compared with what happens in respect of their competitors in the private sector. We are worried that the proper practices that have been defined in codes of practice for accountancy organisations—and which have been accepted for donkeys' years—are being laid on one side as the policy on capital is now being readjusted and redefined. For example, the word "enhancement" is used in connection with the environment instead of those acceptable words that we have used in the past when judging whether expenditure should be capitalised. I do not want to get bogged down in minutiae and examine the dictionary to see whether words mean what we say they mean, but I believe that there is a considerable difference between the meaning of "enhancement" and the meaning of "improvement". It seems that the Bill will rule out of order those accountancy procedures that have been acknowledged as acceptable for years, and the guidelines for repair and improvement of property that have been included in such expenditure.

We seek justification of the reason why the changes are being introduced and why past definitions are considered unacceptable. What is the intention behind the provisions? We are not interested in the argument of Conservative Members about the unacceptable face of local government. How will the provisions affect the day-to-day lives of people who want local government to sustain the value and the quality of the fixed and physical environment, such as school buildings, roads and the housing stock?

Will the Bill improve the value and quality of life, and not merely increase the monetary value of those assets, which is encompassed by the term "enhancement". Those assets are not simply fixtures that can be deemed to raise money; they are living and breathing facilities used by people daily.
