Mr. Bernie Grant
(Tottenham)
: I shall not detain the Committee unduly, but I am worried about what will happen to people who remain as councillors. The Bill will be tremendously unfair to many people who have given good and valuable service to the community for many years. I know of people who have given up jobs to become councillors—they decided many years ago to work part time in local government to be able to concentrate on a local government political career. The Bill will be detrimental to them.

Many people who work in local government have been refused opportunities of promotion. It is well known in local government circles that a councillor may not be promoted within his local authority area because of present arrangements. Some have been penalised for being councillors in other authorities because of a sort of unwritten rule that promotion is not given.

The present situation is also unfair to the community. The Bill will disfranchise people who have given valuable service for years. When people decided to become councillors, they did so in the full knowledge that they would be able to work in local authorities. It is extremely unfair of the Government to step in now to cut that possibility off. It is similar to the Government deciding to end tax relief on mortgage interest payments. People have made financial decisions on the basis of their being allowed tax concessions. It would be unthinkable for the Government to abolish such relief. Our amendment would provide that councillors may remain councillors. The Minister should support and accept it.

I am also concerned about particular groups of councillors, especially those from black and ethnic minority communities, who are few. Some have jobs in local authorities precisely because local authorities have taken on board equal opportunities policies which give them the chance to become councillors. I suspect that, at a stroke, the Government will be disfranchising black and ethnic minority councillors if they take such a step.

The Minister has said that people will have a clear choice between being a councillor or working for a 40 council. I do not think that that is a fair choice, because it is a choice between doing a voluntary, unpaid job as a councillor and working for a council in a full-time paid job. Not many would be able to choose to become a councillor, especially these days. I do not think that there is genuine choice. The Minister is asking people to relinquish a full-time paid job that they might have held for several years and opt for being a councillor, which is a voluntary position. Admittedly allowances will be paid, but they are in no way comparable to full-time salaries.

If the Minister wants to give people a real choice, he should create full-time paid councillors so that people can choose whether to take that option or to be full-time council officers. It is unfair to expect ordinary working-class, poor people such as some of my constituents to give up careers in local government to accept part-time, unpaid and voluntary jobs.

Mr. Murphy

One serious aspect of the Bill is the ever-increasing use of regulations laid down by the Secretary of State. Perhaps the Minister can say whether the measure will apply to the Secretaries of State for Wales and for Scotland.

There has been an increasing use of regulations in all local government legislation. The Minister mentioned the Adam Smith Institute, of which I know little. However I believe that Adam Smith believed in laissez faire and was not too keen on regulations. If regulations were to be laid for pollution control, I should favour that, but the increasing use of regulations that affect the most important aspect of civil liberties in the United Kingdom must cause grave concern to all who value the liberties that we have won over the centuries. It appears that no affirmative resolution of the House is required for the use of the regulations. A matter of such an importance which affects the liberties of our people ought not to be regulated by regulations laid in the House by the Secretary of State for the Environment and his more libertarian colleagues in Wales and Scotland.

Another cause for concern has been the transition from the White Paper to the Bill of the Widdicombe recommendations on how regulations ought to be laid. Paragraph 6.215 of the Widdicombe report stated:

"We hope that this consensus will be reflected in the reaction to our recommendation and accordingly that it would be practicable to implement it through agreed amendment to local authorities' terms and conditions of service. This is the best approach because political activity … is not a concept readily amenable to legislation and is not the subject of legislation in the civil service context. Flexibility moreover is required, in local as it is in central government, to deal with particular cases such as special advisers appointed on a overtly political basis."

 The point must surely be that, during many months of deliberations, Widdicombe realised that it would be impracticable and dangerous to lay down in law—worse, by regulation—that such matter affected whether a person should be regarded as being in a politically restricted post.

There is no question but that, over the past few decades, local authorities have initiated an intricate and sophisticated method—through the Local Authorities Conditions of Service Advisory Board—of dealing with such matters. We all know about the purple book and the buff book. The purple book, which regulates conditions of service for white-collar workers, states:

"The public is entitled to demand of a local government officer conduct of the highest standard, and public confidence in this integrity would be shaken were the least suspicion to arise, however ill founded, that he could in any way be influenced by improper motives. An officer's off-duty hours are his personal concern but he should not subordinate his duty to his private interests or put himself in a position where his duty and private interests conflict."

 These conditions have been laid down for decades and have served local government well.

The Minister spoke of corruption, and by doing so heightened the debate to an unnecessarily sensational level. The vast majority of local authorities are not 46 in that category. Conservative and Labour authorities regulate themselves effectively by means of the informal and more flexible methods laid down by LACSAB and other bodies. In my view, it is wrong that the regulations should be used if the possibility exists of including the provision in the conditions of service.
