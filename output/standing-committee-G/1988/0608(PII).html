Mr. Lamont
: The new relief for unappropriated surplus is narrowly drawn because it is only in exceptional cases that policyholders have reasonable expectations of benefit that are not already reflected in mathematical reserves set up by the company. As to the reasonable expectations test, no better one was suggested during the extensive consultations. The Revenue expects to be guided in its application by the same considerations to which the regulatory authorities have regard in interpreting the concept for their own purposes. There is every intention that the Revenue should keep in step with the interpretation that holds for regulatory purposes.

The hon. Gentleman asked about relief for solvency margins. Life companies are required to maintain a minimum surplus of assets over liabilities, the precise amount of the margin being determined by set formulae. The solvency margin requirement is essentially a capital requirement that new companies, and indeed more mature companies that find themselves in difficulties, have to put up fresh capital to meet.

There is no presumption that the matter should feature in the calculation of taxable profits. Indeed, the effect of a relief for the solvency margin would be to worsen the relative position of new or loss-making companies as against that of established and profitable ones.

In my introductory remarks I outlined the effect of clause 78. If the hon. Member for Wrexham (Dr. Marek) will bear with me, I should like to take this opportunity, now that we have got down to the important nitty gritty detail, to set out the purpose of this series of clauses.

I have already said that we embarked on a consultation and published a document. Responses showed a broad consensus on the views set out in detail by the Association of British Insurers, and acknowledged important defects in the current rules, but argued that they could be dealt with adequately without the upheaval of introducing fundamentally new regimes. The consultative document contained recommendations, some of which were more radical than others. We looked through the policyholders to individual investors and wondered whether to continue with or to reform the income minus expenses basis of taxation. We decided to reform it.

This years' proposals amend a special tax regime that goes back for decades. Throughout this period the background has been the special way in which 366 the industry operates. Life assurance business is written out of an "undivided fund" that combines the interests of all the policyholders, and shareholders too, where there are any.

The fund takes in premiums and earnings from the fund's investments; pays out expenses, pays benefits to policyholders and transfers of profit to the shareholders; and holds reserves against the obligation to pay future policy benefits. The figures for those various amounts are all relevant.

Two consequences flow from the way that the fund operates, and shape the tax rules. First, there is only one charge to tax—corporation tax, paid by the life office—on the profits, income and gains of the office and its policyholders that are derived from the fund. Policyholders in the main have no further tax to pay on the policy benefits received.

Secondly, a single fund commonly brings together different classes of business that have to be given a different tax treatment. For example, pension business is taxable only on its trading profits, as investment returns earned for policy holders enjoy the tax exemption for approved pension provision. In life assurance, in the everyday sense of the term "basic life assurance business", the investment returns to policyholders are quite properly taxable, as they are for personal savings generally. That is why we have the unique system known as I minus E which brings together contributions from different classes of business calculated according to rules specific to each; the investment income and gains, less expenses, of basic life business, and the trading profits of pension business. The upshot is that both the profits of the office, and the net returns earned for basic life policyholders, are fully taxed. Clause 78 and other proposals would retain that basic structure.

The rules have developed piecemeal over the years and are now flawed by weaknesses that have undermined the performance of the regime and distorted the incidence of tax.

Our objective in the Budget was to provide a tax regime that is more efficient and more equitable. At present, the regime falls unevenly between taxation of life assurance and other forms of saving and between different companies within the life assurance business. We had to take account of the special nature of life assurance—it is a blend of protection and savings, and we wanted a simple system. That is why we put forward our proposals.

The first is to tax pensions and general annuity business on a proper basis by stopping expenses of this business spilling over to reduce the tax due from basic life business. That is ring-fencing the different activities.

Secondly, we propose to broaden the tax base and spread the tax relief for basic life acquisition expenses, which we shall discuss in clause 82. At the same time, to achieve a balance, we have introduced ring-fencing and limited acquisition expenses, which is to be phased in over seven years. We have also reduced the tax rate on gains and income to the basic rate of income tax—25 per cent. Thirdly, we have abolished life assurance policy duty.

The first batch of three measures—putting pension and general annuity profits on the right footing—all, JPEG 367 in a way, involve bringing the profit calculation on to a basis more consistent with the one that the offices use in the accounts that they draw up for themselves and include in their statutory returns to the Department of Trade and Industry. The most important of those proposals—"ring-fencing" expenses—puts back into the profit calculation an ingredient, the trading expenses of the business, which currently does not appear there at all.

The other two involve correcting ingredients that already appear in the calculation, but in amounts that may be quite different from those which the offices use for their own purposes—the contributions on the plus side for investment earnings, which are dealt with in clause 79, and on the minus side for amounts set out aside to cover obligations to policyholders. Clause 78 starts off by tackling that deduction for reserves.

I apologise for that lengthy outline, but it is important to put the clause into context, so that we know what we are discussing.

Mr. Lamont
: This clause is intended to ensure that the profits on which life companies are charged to tax are based on the figures that they show in their accounts and statutory returns. The object is to ensure that there is proper matching between the deductible bonus allocations and liabilities to policy holders, on the one hand, and the incomings which fund those liabilities on the other.

The need for change arises because of uncertainty in the operation of the current rules. Life offices do not have to produce a profit and loss account, so that the usual starting point for calculating tax liabilities JPEG 369 is not available. Instead, their returns under the Insurance Companies Act 1982 form the starting point for calculating the trading profit for tax purposes. This is a satisfactory alternative, provided that all the incomings and outgoings which go into their returns in any year are taken into account on a consistent basis. In practice, however, they may not be in every case. The main effect of the clause is to ensure that taxable incomings and deductible liabilities follow the figures in offices' own accounts, thus ensuring internal consistency and a proper balance between the debits and credits.

On the apportionment of profits, the Bill makes no change to established practice, but we are consulting the industry with a view to possible future action.

The base-broadening that clause 82 introduces helps to pay for the substantial tax cuts elsewhere, to which I referred on the last clause stand part debate. The approach in the Bill aims to base the treatment of expenses firmly on the facts of each case. Where bonds are issued at a discount to redemption price, the discount is an alternative to interest and a compensation for a low coupon. That is easy enough for bonds where the redemption date and terms are certain—commonly known as deep-discount bonds—and clause 89 achieves that, but not where the date, terms and actuality of redemption are uncertain, because it is impossible to assess how the benefits from that potential gain on redemption will accrue over the life of the bond. It has been necessary to classify such bonds as deep-gain bonds and treat them differently, as in clause 90. Clause 91 deals specifically with gilt-edged securities. A problem arises for convertible bonds. Where bonds have variable features—there is an increasing variety of such issues both by public and corporate bodies in the United Kingdom and overseas—it is only possible to split the income and capital elements when the bond has matured. The split could be applied to the investor who purchased the bond at the date of issue and held it to maturity. But that provides no solution for intermediate buyers and sellers. With many of these bonds the uplift realised by investors is to a large extent simply compensation for a low coupon and it is clearly wrong that it should 386 be regarded as capital. These proposed deep-gain rules in clause 90 and schedule 11, therefore, charge the uplift as income. Issuers can avoid this consequence by tailoring their bonds to fit the rules in clause 89.

In attempting to provide this certain tax regime covering all variable bonds we recognise that in one or two respects the net has been cast too wide and embraces certain bonds where the increase in value of the bonds wholly or mainly relates to a link to equity values rather than the conversion of interest income into capital.

The main category concerned is bonds convertible into equities. Let me immediately reassure hon. Members that convertible bonds which simply carry a right to conversion, whether to equities or other sorts of securities, with no options to redeem at a premium, are outside this legislation. But, as drafted, the legislation covers convertible bonds with options to redeeem at a premium. Call options can only be exercised by the issuer, and put options can only be exercised by the investor. Convertible bonds can have either or both of those options.

We have concluded that it would be right to exclude from the legislation all bonds—not only convertibles but some straight bonds—where the only reason that they are treated as deep-gain securities is that the issuer has the option to redeem them at a premium. If the issuer only has an option and is not obliged to exercise it in the investor's favour, the premium cannot be relied on as part of the return on the investor's money and so it is reasonable to tax it as capital gain, not income. That change means that all existing and future bonds that would otherwise be caught simply because of the existence of an issuer's option will not be treated as deep-gain securities.

Where it is the investor who has the option the situation is more complex. In that situation, the premium provides the investor with a guaranteed profit and compensates for a low coupon on the bond. That should be taxed as income. However, some gains on such securities will clearly relate much more to fluctuations in the value of the underlying equity and be much less appropriate to be taxed as income.

We have concluded that, in consulation with interested parties, we must examine in greater depth the correct treatment of increases in value realised on convertible bonds. The intention would be to devise equitable arrangements under which income converted into guaranteed or near guaranteed premiums would be charged to income tax while leaving gains akin to those on equities charged to capital gains tax. We will commence these consultations immediately.

We recognise, however, that investors need to know where they stand in relation to existing quoted convertibles with investors' options. We shall also introduce an amendment on Report excluding from clause 90 all quoted convertible bonds or bonds convertible into quoted equities issued up to today, leaving them to be taxed under the existing regime.

As I have already said in reply to the amendments moved by my hon. Friend the Member for Esher, we JPEG 387 intend also to remove from the scope of the legislation existing issues of bonds that are linked to an index of equity values on a recognised stock exchange and are themselves quoted on such an exchange. We shall also consult on how to tax such bonds issued after today.

I am confident that this will meet the genuine concerns that have been expressed. Reforms are necessary and I am confident that representative bodies will co-operate fully in efforts to achieve a fair and workable system for future issues of convertible and indexed bonds. I am grateful to you, Mr. Crowther, for allowing me to deal with issues that arose on the two preceeding clauses.
