The effect of the clause would be to increase the cost to them. We must consider whether the clause will stop people using their caravans as they do now and also bear in mind that the caravan industry is a substantial leisure industry where small changes in costs can affect severely the number of caravans produced.

I want to link the change, which means that rents for certain pitches and facilities at seasonal caravan sites will be standard-rated for VAT, with the changes that are already occurring in Scotland as a result of the poll tax: that tax will also have an impact in England and Wales in the next year or so. Have the Government considered the issue in its totality, or have they simply considered VAT in England and Wales and the community charge in Scotland?

In Scotland the taxation on caravans is a difficult issue. It is not the case that a caravan is a caravan—caravans are used in different styles for different purposes. When a caravan is used as a main residence in Scotland—some people live in their caravans the whole year—the caravan is taken out of the rating system. As is the intention with the poll tax, a personal poll tax is levied on all residents of the caravan. When a caravan is used as a second home for people who live elsewhere, a standard community charge is levied. I understand that there may be some difference in the English and Welsh legislation, but in Scotland that standard community charge can be set between one and two times, or twice, the personal poll tax.

In my area of Strathclyde, the regional council, as in every other part of Scotland, is levying a standard community charge at twice the level of the personal poll tax. That means a charge on the second home caravan of twice the poll tax—about £600 a year. 104 Those who use their caravans as a second home are faced with extremely high charges—over £10 a week, regardless of rental.

The third type of caravan is the static leisure or holiday caravan, which sounds as though it is equivalent to the caravan to which the schedule refers. Such caravans will be dealt with in yet another way. They will remain on the valuation roll and will be rated. The Glasgow Herald of 16 May reported on the outcome of that for a resident of Argyll:

"A widow of 82 is threatening to take an axe to the caravan she keeps for occasional visits from her family, rather than pay rates for it.

Mrs. Judith Sugden, who lives in a cottage in Ardentinny, Argyll, believes she has no choice but to destroy the caravan she bought second-hand 10 years ago."

 She has the caravan for her children and grandchildren from Canada to sleep in when they visit her. The caravan has never been let for money and its sole purpose is as an adjunct to her home. That has been deemed to be a holiday home, although for that lady it is just a caravan in her garden. It is not on a licensed site. The article reports her as saying:

"'If I had an extension on my house I wouldn't be paying rates on it, and that's what the caravan in effect is.'"

 She now has a rates demand of £71 on top of her poll tax. She cannot understand that and feels that it is a terrible imposition:

"The Duke of Argyll is only paying the same as me with a bloody great castle.'"

 That is the problem with the poll tax.

Here are three kinds of caravan. I do not know whether the poll tax regulations are ready in England and Wales yet but they have already caused considerable problems in Scotland. Three different attitudes have been taken to what is a caravan. In one it is a second home and double the personal community charge of about £600 per year may be levied. Another is the main residence and the person will simply be paying a poll tax and the third kind is a static, leisure or holiday caravan which remains on the rates.

: The need for this amendment arises because the European Commission had not felt able to endorse the derogation which the Government sought under article 27 of the sixth VAT directive. This derogation would have permitted the VAT treatment of building land set out in the original paragraphs 5 and 6 of schedule 6A.

Hon. Members may ask why we should have wanted to apply VAT to building land at all since the European Court judgment did not deal with it. Building land has never been zero-rated in the UK, so the European Court had nothing to say on the matter. Under the sixth VAT directive, articles 4.3(b) and 13B(h), a supply of building land is required to be taxed, but in the past we have taken advantage of a transitional derogation under which building land can be exempted—Article 28.3(b), annex F, item No. 16.

Exemption made sense when new buildings were zero-rated, but once we had been required by the European Court judgment to tax new, non-domestic buildings it would not have been sensible to continue with the building land exemption for this category of JPEG 133 building. This is because retaining an exemption for the largest cost element—or second largest after labour—in the overall cost of a new building would have introduced a gross distortion in the market place. It would have introduced a massive fiscal incentive in favour of self-developments and against buying from a developer or renting from a landlord. When a building is bought or leased, the price or rent paid reflects the open market value of the building including the land on which it stands. People buying or renting accommodation would be paying VAT on the full price or rent charged. By contrast, if they could go out and acquire the land for their new accommodation before a brick had been laid upon it, they would pay no VAT on the land and thus bear VAT only on the cost of construction.

Originally at the time of the European Court judgment last June when the consultation paper was issued, we hoped it would be possible to arrive at a satisfactory definition of building land, bearing in mind in particular that we did not want to apply VAT to what became land for housing. However, the longer the consultation exercise progressed, the more we became convinced that taxing actual sales of building land, however defined, was not the answer. On the one hand, if land assumed the status of building land only after planning permission was obtained, land acquired before planning permission would escape VAT. We would, indeed, be introducing a fiscal incentive to get land for development before obtaining planning permission. Thus the distortion in favour of self-development would still be there. The same thing would happen when buildings were obtained with a view to demolition and redevelopment. At the time the buildings were acquired, the vendor would not know for certain whether redevelopment would be permitted and he could not be said to be making a supply of building land. A similar problem would arise in respect of building land for housing which we wanted to remain exempt. How would a vendor know whether land he was selling was going to be used for offices or other commercial buildings, or for houses, or for a mixture of the two? Even if there were a valid planning permission extant, this could have been revoked or amended before development actually commenced.

We therefore devised the scheme which features in paragraphs 5 and 6 of schedule 6A in the Bill as it stands before the Committee. The essence of this scheme—described by one prominent VAT expert as ingenious—applies VAT at the time construction starts on a self-supply basis. By then it must be beyond all doubt that the land is building land and equally it will be beyond all doubt what is the nature of the building under construction. If it is a dwelling or other building qualifying for the zero rate, no VAT would be charged. If not, the land would be chargeable in principle. In practice, however, if the building was going to be sold or let on a taxable basis when completed, VAT on the self-supply would not be chargeable because the Exchequer would eventually receive the VAT when the building was sold or let. Similarly, if the person constructing the building was going to remain fully taxable, he would always be entitled to deduct any VAT that was 134 charged, and so in the particular circumstances there seemed no need to put him through the self-supply procedure unnecessarily, involving the not always easy task of applying open market valuation.

When I published the draft legislation on 6 February, I made clear that the building land scheme was dependent on getting a derogation from Brussels. We did not anticipate any particular problems with it since we were not asking to collect a penny less in tax than was required under the Sixth directive. We were asking for a scheme which targeted those areas where tax needed to be collected to comply with the spirit of the European Court of Justice judgment and apply VAT to new non-domestic construction and property development in an effective way. At the same time we were legally entitled to retain the derogation for building land and did not want to tax building land for housing. However, the Commission, while not disagreeing with our objective, claimed we were departing too far from the general structure of the tax provided for in the Sixth directive, and it became clear to us that, without the Commission's support, our derogation request could not succeed.

The philosophy behind the new scheme is generally the same as that behind the old one. If we are going to apply VAT to non-domestic construction and property development—as indeed we must because of our Treaty obligations—we should do so in an effective manner, providing a level playing field and preventing distortions and easy avoidance. In some ways the new scheme is more radical than the old one. Instead of tackling the building land problem directly, the new one focusses on the effective taxation of all non-domestic buildings at the time they are ready to go into consumption. The special self-supply charge provided in the new paragraphs 5–7 will ensure that any building which is not let or sold on a taxable basis will be taxed if the developer uses it for an exempt purpose within a period of 10 years from when it was completed. This special charge is within the terms of Article 5.7(a) of the Sixth directive and the only point on which we need a derogation is to apply an open market value to that self-supply instead of cost. On this the Commission has agreed that we can amend our earlier derogation accordingly and, provided that no other member states objects—and they have no reason to—the Commission will support our request. We hope that the formalities should be completed within the next two or three weeks.

From the taxpayer's point of view the new scheme will be undoubtedly less complicated than the earlier one and less uncertain. He will not have to make, at the beginning of construction, a declaration about selling the building. He will not have to make a premature election to tax the rents if he plans to let the building. He will not have to find the VAT on the building land from the beginning of construction. Because the building will always be used to make either taxable supplies or will generate the taxable self-supply charge, all the VAT incurred by the developer during the construction process will be recoverable as input tax. This will be a considerable cash flow benefit compared with the old scheme.
