New clause 26
DUTIES OF LOCAL AUTHORITIES

"(1)(a) It shall be the duty of every local housing authority to cause an inspection of their district to be made from time to time with a view to ascertaining whether any residential property is empty and to keep records of all such inspections.

(b) It shall be the duty of every local housing authority to consider the housing needs of their district with respect to the use of empty residential property and for that purpose to review the information which has been brought to their notice either as a result of inspections and surveys carried out under sub-section (1) above, or otherwise, and as often as the occasion arises, or within three months after notice has been given to them by the Secretary of State, to prepare and submit to the Secretary of State proposals for the use of empty residential property.

(c)A local housing authority shall provide annually to the Secretary of State a statement of its strategy for bringing into use any residential property which it holds or has acquired, and which has remained vacant for six months or longer, and any such strategy statement shall be available for inspection by any member of the public at the offices of the authority at all reaonable hours, and any member of the public may make a copy of such strategy on payment of a reasonable fee.".

(2)(a) Where a local housing authority is satisfied that:

  1. any house, or self-contained separate flat within a house, which is empty, or
  2. any building which contains residential accommodation comprising one or more separate dwellings which are empty
and has remained empty for a period of at least six months they may serve on the owner of the house or building a notice requiring him to submit proposals for bringing the house or building into residential use.

(b) A person upon whom a notice is served under sub-section (1) above shall within 21 days either submit to the authority proposals to bring the premises into residential use, or notify the authority of his intention to do so within a reasonable period of time.

A person need not submit proposals under sub-section (2)(b) above if he seeks exemption for one of the reasons listed in Schedule 12 of this Act and if such reason is accepted by the authority Section (1) shall not apply to such properties.

JPEG 1249

(3)(a) A local housing authority may make an order under this section (in this Part of this Act called an Empty Property Use Order) with regard to any empty residential accommodation within their district if the person or persons having control of that accommodation have failed to provide satisfactory proposals for the use thereof or have failed to implement such proposals once agreed following the service of notice under sub-section (2) above

(b) An Empty Property Use Order shall come into force as soon as it is made and as soon as it is practicable after making the Order the authority shall enter on the premises and take all such steps as appear to them to be required to enable the premises to be rendered capable of providing accommodation that will comply with statutory conditions.

(c) Once an authority has taken steps under sub-section 3(b) above to render any properties capable of use then the authority shall use the premises as residential accommodation and paragraph 6 of Schedule 1 of the Housing Act 1985 shall apply to any tenancy granted pursuant to this subsection.

(d) Any tenancy pursuant to this Section of this Act shall, for the purposes of Section 118 of the Housing Act 1985, be deemed to be a secure tenancy and the provisions of Part V and Scheule 4 of the Housing Act 1985 shall apply to any such tenancy, provided that the right to buy given pursuant to this sub-section and any discount allowable shall not be exercisable in respect of the property occupied pursuant to this Act but shall be excused pursuant to Section 35 of the Housing and Building Control Act 1984.

(e) A local housing authority may discharge its functions under sub-sections (b) and (c) above by making arrangements with a housing association or other voluntary body concerned with the provision of housing accommodation.

(f) An Empty Property Use Order shall last for a period not exceeding five years at the end of which time either—

  1. the authority may, with the consent of the owner, extend the Order for a further period of up to five years, or
  2. the authority may issue a Compulsory Purchase Order on the property, or
  3. the property shall revert to the owner or to any such successor in title to the owner.

4(a) The proper officer of the local housing authority shall make a report in writing to the authority whenever he is of the opinion

  1. that a notice should be served under sub-section (b) above;
  2. that an Order should be made under sub-section (c) above of this Act and that authority shall take into consideration as soon may be any such report made to them.

(b) If a complaint in writing that a notice should be served under sub-section (2) of this Act or an Order made under Section XYC of this Act, is made to the proper office of the local housing authority by—

  1. a justice of the peace having jurisdiction in any part of their district, or
  2. a parish or community council for a parish or community within their district, or
  3. any 50 local government electors for the area,

    the officer shall forthwith inspect the house and make a report to the authority stating the facts of the case and whether in his opinion action ought to be taken pursuant to sub-section (2) or (3) above

(c) The absence of a complaint under this sub-section does not excuse the proper officer of the authority from his duties under this sub-section.".—[ Mr. Matthew Taylor.]

Mr. Matthew Taylor
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

I do not want to detain the Committee, despite the fact that the new clause is lengthy. The text has been lifted from the relevant parts of the Empty Property and Community Aid Bill 1987, which failed to gain sufficient support when it was introduced on 16 November 1987. Before the Minister says it—no doubt he will—I must observe that there are mistakes in the drafting, which arise from trying hurriedly to put the new clause into an acceptable form. The cross-referencing in the new clause is inaccurate.

The first part of the new clause gives councils a duty to inspect their district to see what empty 1250 properties there are. They must think about how empty homes can be used and prepare and make public an annual strategy for their use. By putting these provisions in the Bill we would meet part of what the Government claim is their aim—to put more empty housing, especially local authority housing, back into use. The first part of our new clause embraces that aim.

The new clause would extend further. It mentions the private sector and provides for an examination of the use that is made of private sector housing stock. This is important for a number of reasons, not least because it introduces an element of balance to frequent Government criticisms of local authority housing and draws attention to the fact that much private sector housing lies empty. If there is a problem throughout the country with underused housing stock, it is in the private sector rather than in local authority housing.

4.45 pm

The new clause would require local authorities to be empowered in some circumstances to do something about that problem. That is important because many local authorities face difficulties when tackling apparent housing need. I have argued that some of those difficulties arise from the Government's financial provision for local authorities, but they go beyond that. Many local authorities have difficulties because of a shortage of land. In inner-city areas it is already built upon and what remains available is in private hands; in rural areas, local authorities have little or no land, especially where it may be most pressingly needed such as in smaller villages and rural towns. There is great need, but a virtual inability to meet it at a cost that the council can support because it cannot afford to buy land, and has none itself.

Our new clause would empower local authorities to put empty properties to use. The Minister may not like its terms. I tabled it as a probing new clause rather than something more specific. I want to judge his reactions to this great housing need, empty properties in the private sector and what can be done to put them back into use. The clause would give councils a new power in the form of empty property use orders, which they could serve on long-term empty houses, although only after owners have been allowed to submit proposals to use them themselves. This is an extension of the control order provision of the Housing Act 1964 which applies to multi-occupancy housing. Under this power, an authority would be able to use empty property for five years, either itself or in conjunction with housing associations or self-help groups of homeless people, and then return it to its owner.

The new clause would enable local authorities to obtain vacant possession of the property when EPUOs expired. A safeguard for owners is the proposal that the time people have lived in a property should count, towards the right to buy, so that any discounts accrued can be transferred to other properties in a manner similar to other schemes for housing association tenants.

People would also be able to complain to a justice of the peace if they felt that a property should be the JPEG 1251 subject of such an order, and a JP could require a council to consider proper action. The new clause is about trying to do something about speculators who hold on to empty properties in the hope of making a fast buck at the expense of local people who cannot get housed. It is about helping local authorities to meet the needs of people on the waiting list and their legal obligation to house the homeless in good conditions rather than in bedsits and damp and condemnable caravans—as happens in much of the south-west and other areas.

The new clause is also about trying to even the balance. Local authorities are required to take action, but the same applies to the private sector and to property that is in Government hands. Indeed, the Government's record on dealing with their own empty properties should shame them each time that they condemn local authorities for underusing properties.

I realise that the new clause contains errors. I do not expect the Minister to accept it, but I hope that he will comment seriously on the problem of the under-use of the housing stock outside the local authority sector. What will he do to help local authorities to make use of their under-used housing assets instead of simply launching continual attacks and criticisms of them?

Mr. Trippier
: A central principle underpinning right to buy legislation is that tenants of properties held by local authorities for general housing purposes should have the right to buy their homes. That principle is just as important in rural areas as in major towns and cities.

Where properities are held by local authorities for purposes relating to one of their functions other than the housing function, by and large, they are excluded from the right to buy. There are various exclusions from security of tenure, hence from the right to buy, in schedule 1 to the Housing Act 1985. Those exemptions cover properties which are let to tenants and required for operational purposes. For example, paragraph 8 of the schedule excludes premises on agricultural holdings occupied by persons responsible for the control of the farming of the holding. There are similar exclusions from the right to buy in schedule 5 to the 1985 Act.

There is no current exemption for houses let to tenants on retirement. That is not accidental. A case can be made for excluding from the right to buy a property which needs to be kept available for letting because it is special in some way. If a retirement property is special in that it is particularly suitable for occupation by elderly tenants, it may fall within paragraph 11 of schedule 5. The paragraph excludes from the right to buy properties which are particularly suitable for, and let for occupation by, persons of pensionable age. A house offered to a service tenant on retirement does not necessarily need to be special. For example, within reason, it could be anywhere; there is no fundamental necessity for a smallholder's retirement home to be on the smallholding estate.

Where a county council rehouses a retired smallholder, it is supplying a home to someone who needs it, as distinct from providing housing relating to a person's employment. In that respect, it is carrying out a general housing function in much the same way as a district council would. In principle, there is no difference between properties let to retired smallholders on a smallholding estate and other properties let to retired smallholders or to any other people of pensionable age.
