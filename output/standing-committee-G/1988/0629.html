Mr. Smith
: Having had a healthy and non-controversial debate on Tuesday on subjects such as bingo duty, historic houses and correspondence course training, we can approach friendly societies in the same bi-partisan way. This issue does not divide the parties and we have not tabled the new clauses in a controversial or antagonistic way. Indeed, responding to an Adjournment debate initiated by his hon. Friend the Member for Wyre Forest (Mr. Combs), the Economic Secretary to the Treasury concluded:

'I know that there is much sympathy for the friendly society movement and a desire to see it continue to prosper and perform its essential role in encouraging savings and mutual self help.'—[ Official Report, 4 April 1989; Vol 150, c, 174.]

That sentiment will be echoed on both sides of the Committee. There is wide support in the House for the work of friendly societies, partly because they do valuable work in encouraging saving and prudent provision, partly because that encouragement of saving inevitably has a small but significant counter-inflationary effect on the local economy, partly because they are based upon the sound mutual principle of collective self-help, but principally because they have always performed a crucial role in relation to the small saver. Those on low incomes who none the less wish to make provision for their retirement or death can do so on a small-scale basis with a friendly society. Friendly societies have concentrated on what might be described as the lower end of the savings market. They are an important force in the savings community as a result.

I am indebted to the Friendly Societies Liaison Committee for its assistance in drawing up the new clauses and in preparing these brief remarks, to the Ancient Order of Foresters and to my noble Friend Lord Graham who takes a particular interest in these matters. It is not overstating the case to say that large sectors of the friendly society movement are in a state of crisis today. One of the two examples that come immediately to mind is the Fleet friendly society, which was set up eight years ago and was found to be actuarially insolvent two days before the 1988 accounts went to the printers. It has fortunately since been established that its benefit funds can more than cover the shortfall anticipated by the actuaries, JPEG 703 but its independence as a friendly society is still in doubt.

More recently, there have been reports of the work of the Tunbridge Wells equitable society in helping the Time Assurance Society of Oldham with its problems.

A report in The Guardian of a week ago said:

"The tussle over Time Assurance will further underscore severe pressures placed on friendly societies by restrictive financial regulations, which may push more of them into alliances or force demutualisations."

Many friendly societies now facing financial difficulties would prefer not to choose the demutualisation route, because they fear that they would lose their essential character.

The difficulties of Time Assurance and Fleet exemplify some of the problems of the friendly society movement. Other financial institutions have recently had restrictions substantially loosened, and can compete, often advantageously, against the friendly societies, which are still subject to an ancient legislative framework largely dating from 1879. Little has been done to change that framework since then. In a world of intense competition, the friendly societies lose out because they cannot offer the services and options that other financial institutions can.

The costs of such services have risen greatly because of the increase in demand. The European Community life directive requires that societies be valued annually. Most societies used to be valued every three or every five years. That was satisfactory because the policies issued were small, and relatively few sales were made. Many funds such as the Holloway sickness fund which were not subject to valuation before are now valued annually.

The fees incurred because of the requirements have risen dramatically and often exceed £1 per policy per annum. Friendly society policies are small and require the collection of 12 premiums per year and their allocation to a contract, the yearly issue of benefit statements, other communications from the society, valuation and administrative work, auditing and the posting of valuation information in newspapers, all of which place administrative costs on the societies' work. Those costs have all risen substantially in the past five years, especially compared with the costs of other financial institutions which may not be in the same position when marketing small-scale policies.

Many friendly societies are facing difficulties as a result of competition from groups in the financial community which are not subject to such strict legislative regulation and to increased administrative costs resulting from the Financial Services Act 1986 and the European life directive. A new legislative framework is the ideal solution to such problems.

The hon. Member for Wyre Forest put his finger on the nub of the issue in the Adjournment debate of 4 April, when he said:

"By widening the scope of services, they ought to be able to provide, through the friendly society movement, effective competition or other financial institutions and more effective and efficient services for their members. They ought to be able to 704 increase their access to markets which at present are dominated by insurance companies and pension funds, particularly by those who corner the market by means of tied intermediaries."—[ Official Report, 4 April 1989; Vol. 150, c. 169]

Widening the scope of services is the key to the long-term future of friendly societies. That requires a new legislative framework.

The Economic Secretary has established a review of friendly societies' structures. It is under way, but how long will it take? There is an urgent need for legislation to assist friendly societies in the crises that they face. I accept that the Government must consider carefully, publish proposals and allow a period for wide consultation, but I hope that they will introduce legislation in the year after the next parliamentary session, if not next year. The Opposition will want to scrutinise the Government's proposals carefully, but we shall co-operate to ensure that the friendly societies' regime can be relaxed and that they can provide a wider range of services. I hope that the Government will give us some idea of the timescale of their proposals—when will their review be completed, and when will they introduce legislation?

The new clauses are proposed as an interim measure to fend off the crisis facing the societies between now and the period when primary legislation can proceed. The new clauses seek to increase the tax-exempt limit available to investors in friendly societies from £100 to £200 per year. New clause 50 seeks to establish that new limit; new clause 51 goes on to propose an index linking for subsequent years.

The cost of our proposals to the Exchequer would be minimal. The additional £100 made available to the investor by extending the tax-exempt limit could be invested to yield £12 to £15 per annum. The tax take, disregarding office expenses, would be £3 to £5 per investor per annum, assuming that it was all income. Therefore, the average cost of raising the tax-exempt limit by £100, as proposed under this new clause, would be £4 per person per annum. That is a relatively small price for the Exchequer to pay for a considerable freeing-up of the ability of friendly societies to compete with other financial institutions.

The Economic Secretary has recognised the principle of the tax exempt limit. In a letter to the Friendly Societies Liaison Committee on 26 May, he referred to existing tax relief

"as an encouragement to societies to assist the less wealthy in making a modest provision for their old age and against their eventual death".

He went on to say that the Government recognise that

"such small policies (or contracts) are relatively more expensive to set up and administer than larger ones."

That is precisely the point that we believe justifies the extension of the tax-exempt limit to £300.

In the same letter, the Economic Secretary introduced a caveat. He said:

"It is not realistic to expect the Government to maintain declining institutions at the expense of the taxpayer in the hope that things may get better: that is not the purpose of tax reliefs."

I entirely agree. The way to help friendly societies effectively to compete in the market place is primarily JPEG 705 by legislative change. The proposal for an increase in the tax-exempt limit is a proposal to deal with the acute interim problems faced by a number of friendly societies.

When seeking the increase from £100 to £200 we should compare the relatively favourable treatment that is available to other forms of saving, life assurance and financial services. We must remember that the Finance Bill has increased the personal equity plan fund limit from £2,400 to £4,800, that tax concessions are available to occupational pension schemes and to certain national savings deposits, and that tax-free benefits are available in certain circumstances from investments in gilts. All those are competing forms of saving which the tax system recognises and against which the friendly societies have to compete. They are all items referred to by the hon. Member for Wyre Forest in the debate of 4 April.

Friendly societies need assistance, and the cost to the Exchequer would be minimal. The worth of the market sector that the friendly societies seek to cater for is acknowledged, and the competing forms of saving are in some cases eligible for considerably greater amounts of tax relief than the friendly societies can take up with their present tax-exempt limit.

The current tax-exempt limit of £100 was established in 1987. It effectively dates back to 1984, when the Government first proposed the figure. It should have been inflation-indexed since then, but it has not been. In terms of the equivalent sum assured, the figure of £200 would take us back to 1984. The increase that we are asking from the Government is not great.

Member for Clydebank and Milngavie (Mr. Worthington) felt that the £100 tax allowance was too low for Labour Members. Although the friendly societies movement has 3.5 million members, most earn considerably less than any hon. Member. It is always possible to form a friendly society specifically for members of the Labour party, although that may be the only time when the brothers' party has the characteristic of friendliness among its members rather than to others.

The specific proposal is that the £100 tax-exempt limit should be doubled. I do not recommend that proposal to my hon. Friends, sympathetic though I am, and they are, to friendly societies, for three reasons. First, when the £100 limit was introduced, 708 it represented a considerable improvement on the situation that had existed since 1984. It was welcomed by the friendly societies. I have a letter from the chairman of the friendly societies to my predecessor, my right hon. Friend the Member for Hertfordshire, North (Mr. Stewart). It reads:

"Thank you for your letter of 17th March. I am delighted by the good news it contained, and am sure the changes proposed by the Chancellor will be welcomed warmly by small friendly societies, who believed that the 1984 measures left them with no viable future. So, only two years ago, that was welcomed as a valuable and signficant change. There may be an argument for uprating the limit in line with inflation, which is only 12 per cent. over that period and far less than the proposed doubling.

Secondly, the essential purpose of the relief, as I argued in the letter to which the hon. Member for Islington, South and Finsbury referred, is to encourage societies to assist the less wealthy in making a modest provision for their old age and against their death and to recognise that such small policies or contracts are relatively more expensive to set up and administer than larger ones. The relief is not to subsidise societies in general during a difficult period.

Thirdly, only a minority of business carried out by societies would benefit form the tax change. Most business is taxable business, not all business would be helped and the benefit would be unevenly spread. It would help some societies more than others.

Finally, it is wrong to make a permanent change in the tax system to overcome an essentially transitional problem, as presented by the societies themselves and by the hon. Member for Islington, South and Finsbury and others who have spoken. Their problem is how to continue operating within the present legal framework when it is in their view—and the Government sympathises with them—unsatisfactory. We set up a review to reconsider it. Essentially, friendly societies are constrained by the original Act and are allowed to carry out only the business listed there. Because they do not have a corporate structure, they are not allowed to have subsidiaries through which they could operate other businesses. Those are two central problems that the review that I have set up seeks to resolve.

The other problem encountered by the societies is a much longer secular change that has been going on since the beginning of the century and the establishment of the welfare state initially in 1906, although friendly societies had a role within the original framework, and more intensively since 1945 when they have had less of a role. That has inevitably meant that they want to change and to cease to have quite such a central role as they did in the past. They cannot change their manner of operation until we have changed the legal framework. However, a permanent fiscal change should not be made to deal with a short-term transitional problem.
