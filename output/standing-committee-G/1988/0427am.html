Mr. John McAllion (Dundee, East): I beg to move amendment No. 764, in page 144, line 39, leave out paragraph 1.

This is a probing amendment—I do not want to be accused of being less than charitable towards universities in Scotland—to find out what the Government intend to do about the implications in paragraph 1 of schedule 6.

Clause 118 brings into effect schedule 6, which includes the extension of charitable relief to universities in Scotland. I welcome the Government Col. 1106 showing a new charitable attitude towards universities in Scotland, particularly after years of squeezing spending which has meant staff cuts and departmental closures, and has even threatened the existence of some universities. I welcome the emergence of the Government's new glasnost approach towards universities in Scotland.

There is more to paragraph 1 than first meets the eye. It alters section 4 of the Local Government (Financial Provisions etc.) (Scotland) Act 1962 by removing the exclusion of Scottish universities from the right to claim mandatory charitable relief on non-domestic rates. That means that they will stop paying a large part of the rates which they now pay to local councils in Scotland. Mandatory relief is now levelled at the rate of 50 per cent., and that percentage will be saved when the Bill is enacted.

On 1 April 1990 mandatory relief becomes 80 per cent., which means that universities will save up to 80 per cent. of their current rates bills. Perhaps they may qualify for the same maximum amount of relief as people who receive income support. That may also be a comment on the way in which Scottish universities have been reduced to the poverty line by the Government.

Unless the resultant loss of rate income is compensated for completely by an increase in revenue support grant, all universities' rates bills will be reduced, presumably to ease their funding problems, but the cost to all community charge payers will be increased accordingly. That is the problem. The only source of revenue of local government in Scotland is revenue support grant from the Government, which comes from non-domestic rates and poll tax payers.

The problem is that revenue support grant in Scotland has already been reached for 1988–89 and 1989–90. Unless that revenue support settlement is amended to take account of paragraph 1 in schedule 6 it is likely that poll tax payers will have to pick up the entire burden of rates relief for Scottish universities. I am sure that that is not the Minister's intention, because a signifificant sum of money is involved.

There are eight major universities in Scotland, one of which is in Dundee, and I shall give one example based on current rateable values. The amount of relief which will be claimed in 1991 by the Heriot-Watt university in Edinburgh will be more than £6 million. That is a large sum to put on the backs of poll tax payers in Scotland. Something must be done about that. The Government could give a commitment to attempt to alter the revenue support grant settlements for local government in Scotland to take into account the increased burden on local councils, or they could table an amendment that relief, if it is to be granted, will not be effective until 1 April 1990. As the Bill stands, universities can claim relief back-dated to 1 April 1988—at least that is the information that I have been given by the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities which is concerned about the position.

While I am not suggesting that the Government should force Scottish universities to pay the whole of their rates bills, they should take into account the Image Col. 1107 impact that paragraph 1 of schedule 6 will have on local authorities and their only source of revenue—the poll tax payers. The Government cannot, on the one hand, be happy to extend charitable relief to Scottish universities and, on the other hand, be determined that the people who will pay for that are the poll tax payers and no one else.
