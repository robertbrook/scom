The Minister for Local Government (Mr. John Gummer)
: I was explaining that the matter raised by the hon. Member for Truro (Mr. Taylor) about minority parties is one that, of course, causes some difficulty. But there is no satisfactory means of taking decision-making away from the council without creating considerable difficulties. The regulations will overcome many of the problems. I noted the hon. Gentleman's comments, but we also went round that course when preparing appropriate regulations.

If two minority parties had exactly the same number of members, the full council would decide what to do about the services provided by a special adviser. I wondered whether it should be done alphabetically. But that might have been thought to be somewhat biased and party-political as, in most cases, the Conservatives could get the edge, so I threw that idea aside. The regulations will cover that possibility.

The hon. Gentleman is right. We can all imagine considerable difficulties arising in special and 302 peculiar circumstances. But surely it is better to allow those rare anomalies to be worked out by the council. We shall make the regulations as helpful as possible. The occasions when councils must make decisions in such peculiar circumstances will be limited. Moreover, as the discussions and statements will be public, that will probably keep within reasonable bounds all but the most lunatic and ludicrous—and they are likely to be extremely small in number.

Mr. Gummer

The hon. Member for Truro asked what would happen if the numbers were equal. In those circumstances, the council would decide which group was the official opposition. But the two groups might decide to work matters out on a co-operative basis.

Regulations would certainly make it difficult for two parties that controlled a council to claim that they were both in control and that one was in opposition for the purposes of getting the adviser. I am sure that we can deal with such possibilities.
