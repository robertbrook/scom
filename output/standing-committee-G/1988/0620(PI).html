Since then, I have examined the Housing Col. 502 Associations Act 1985 and it appears that the definition of housing co-operatives in that Act is different from the definition of housing associations. Section 1 of that Act is headed:

"Meaning of 'housing association' and related expressions"

Mr. Lamont: Sometimes the hon. Gentleman is extremely persuasive. When he offers support upon support for our measures, I know that perhaps we have not got it quite right. We are recommending that the clause should not be proceeded with, but perhaps I could first outline the background to the clause and the reasons why we have reached that conclusion.

The present rules allow certain redirections of assets in an estate after death to have retrospective effect for inheritance tax purposes. These rules were introduced in 1972 to meet a particular need—which has ceased to exist—to give time for wills to be rewritten following the introduction of the spouse exemption. Later they served to prevent two lots of tax in quick succession—once on death and a second time on the lifetime rearrangement. But since the ending of a tax charge on most lifetime giving in 1986, that has also ceased to be a major consideration.

In addition, more recently the beneficiaries of some estates have used the current provisions for instruments of variation and distributions from discretionary trusts purely as a means of reducing tax liability. Here the will is rearranged not necessarily because of any change in family circumstances or hardship but simply because of the desire to create a minimum tax bill on the estate, or even no tax at all, and the proceeds of the estate are then in practice distributed in some other way. The most blatant example of that is when, after the instrument of variation, the property is redirected back precisely as its testator had originally directed in the will.

In recent years, the number of instruments of variation has increased, particularly since capital transfer tax was replaced by inheritance tax. The purpose of inheritance tax was that if people retained property and did not take advantage of the opportunity that had been given them to pass on property free of tax, provided that they lived seven years, there should be a tax on death. That was the principle of inheritance tax, and it was because there has been an increasing use of instruments of variation since its introduction, precisely to avoid the charge on death, that we originally decided to bring forward this clause.

I know, as a number of my hon. Friends have pointed out to me, that instruments of variation in many ways serve a socially useful purpose and that sometimes it is difficult when people are elderly to get them to rearrange their affairs in a sensible way. That is often a painful, difficult and embarrassing thing to do. One can draw a distinction between the use of instruments of variation in the sort of blatant tax-planning way that I have described and their use for other purposes.

We have thought about the matter long and hard. There did not seem to be a practicable way of targeting a measure specifically on the abuse; in any case, the continued existence of a facility to rewrite the tax consequences of a death estate retrospectively is itself wrong once the original Col. 508 justification for it had largely gone. So the intention in principle was to prevent changes to tax liabilities after death. That was the thinking, and I still think that there is a strong argument in principle for the reasoning behind clause 167. The clause catered for genuine family need by ensuring that rearrangements using instruments of variation which make appropriate provision for the surviving spouse or other dependants would continue to have retrospective effect for inheritance tax. And instruments of variation—though without the tax consequences—could have continued to be used in other cases.

However, since the Bill was published and since we made the announcement, we have received very strong representations from the Law Society and all the accountancy bodies. We have received a large number of letters, many of them from solicitors and lawyers. In particular I pay tribute to my hon. Friend the Member for Wanstead and Woodford (Mr. Arbuthnot) who wrote a well argued, moderate and cogent memorandum against clause 167, which played a significant part in altering our thinking on the clause. My hon. Friend pointed out, as did the Law Society and the chartered accountants, that in many ways a clause could effectively be avoided by those with access to the appropriate advice, while it would cause major inconvenience to those who had arranged their affairs on the basis of the present law, especially the elderly.

As regards the point that it would be possible for people to avoid the effect of clause 167, I know that some of the consequences of the post-death use of instruments of variation or of discretionary will trusts would be achieved by the appropriate rewriting of the wills themselves. The objective of clause 167 was not to stop that, but to draw a line between arrangements at death, when inheritance tax is meant to bite, and post-death rearrangements. However, the extent to which appropriately drafted wills could achieve what is now by an increasing number achieved post-death is relevant to the judgment of whether the clause is worth while in revenue terms, and whether we would raise the amount of money specified in the Red Book. Another point put to me is that if sufficiently elaborate permutations are written into the will, this can be achieved to a greater extent than I had previously thought.

Given that those with access to the necessary advice would in practice go to such lengths to achieve the best tax results, is the tax saving that will remain sufficient to justify the inconvenience to those concerned in the context of will-making? I have been persuaded that to proceed with clause 167 in its present form might penalise some people without access to the best legal advice, but it would be sidestepped by people with access to the best advice, and thus would not bite on the most wealthy.

This is not an easy matter to quantify or judge, but having heard all that has been put to me, I have concluded that the matter needs further consideration and that we should not proceed with the clause.

I still think that there is justification in principle behind the purpose of the clause, but I want to see whether we cannot find a better, more focused way Image Col. 509 of addressing the problem and specific examples of abuse. It is not easy to find a device to do that, but we intend to keep the matter under review. Next year we may come forward with a more targeted, specific measure to counter abuse of the present facility.

I have been persuaded by the large number of representations, not from people who stood to gain, but from people who felt that their clients would be greatly disadvantaged by the compliance burden and that a vast number of people would have to make adjustments to their wills. That argument persuaded me that in the end what we would get out of the clause would not justify all the inconvenience and hassle involved in it. I advise the Committee not to proceed with it.

clause Clause 168
title ABATEMENT OF EXEMPTION WHERE CLAIM SETTLED OUT OF BENEFICIARY'S OWN RESOURCES

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Lamont: Clause 168 provides rules for the inheritance tax treatment of those claims against a deceased's estate—for example, by a dependant for adequate maintenance—which are settled from an exempt beneficiary's own resources rather than from the estate itself. The exemption from IHT attributable to that beneficiary's share of the deceased's estate is reduced to take account of the disposition made in settlement of the claim.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 168 ordered to stand part of the Bill.

clause Clause 170
title SETTING OF RATES OF INTEREST

Dr. John Marek (Wrexham): I beg to move amendment No. 475, in page 139, line 38, leave out sub-paragrpah (e).

I shall be brief. The powers in the clause that shall be taken to be made by regulation or by statutory instrument seem too great. The amendment deletes paragraph (e) which says: Image Col. 523

"provide for circumstances in which alteration of a rate of interest is or is not to take place".

That paragraph is especially sweeping. It would be acceptable to provide for the principles under which an alteration of rate of interest may be made, but, as phrased, paragraph (e) could provide for very particular circumstances. If that is so, before any such statutory instrument is passed, it should have to be subject to an affirmative order.

Mr. Lamont: The hon. Gentleman may be making slightly heavy weather of the clause, but the Committe can judge when it has heard my explanation.

The interest rates used by the Revenue, for charging interest on late payments and paying interest on repayments, are at present set by statutory instrument. That is a somewhat cumbersome procedure, which makes it difficult to keep Revenue rates closely in line with the market.

The clause introduces a more open procedure under which the formulae by which the rates are set will be published in a statutory instrument. That instrument has not yet been formulated, but it will be laid before the House shortly after the Bill receives Royal Assent. That will lay down the basis on which interest rates are fixed. Rate changes will then be made automatically to follow changes in market rates.

For example, the rate of interest charged on late payments, and paid on repayments, of income tax and capital gains tax is kept in line with the cost of borrowing of an average taxpayer, using a broad average so as to be generally fair overall. At present, the rates are set according to a formula that is not published, based on base rate plus two and a half and adjusted for the tax treatment of the interest charge. The clause will enable the rate setting mechanism to be made more open by allowing the different formulae used for the various Revenue rates to be published.

I apologise for looking rather puzzled at one point because the hon. Member for Wrexham (Dr. Marek) referred to the affirmative or negative procedure, with which amendments Nos. 485 and 488 deal. However, amendment No. 475 deals with the regulation making powers for the formulae by which the rates will be set. It will remove the powers that allow tolerances to be included in the formulae so that Revenue rates do not need to be adjusted whenever there is an adjustment in market rates, however small. The hon. Gentleman was worried about that. When published, the powers will allow the formulae to specify that Revenue rates will not change until market rates move by, for example, one point in total, or that rates will not be changed until the end of the month. That is the reason for the flexibility.

Col. 524 The powers do not allow discretion to be given to the Revenue about whether interest rates should be changed—any discretion will involve merely the parameters within which the rates will change. Their purpose is to allow Parliament greater flexibility in the formulae that may be used, rather than to allow the Revenue flexibility in using the formulae. As I have explained, the present interest rates are specified in a statutory instrument. That is not debated in the House, but because it is a statutory instrument, it must lie before the House for the requisite period before the interest rate adjustment can be made. That is why I described the procedure as rather cumbersome.

The affirmative procedure is usually adopted only where it is appropriate for Parliament to scrutinise the use of the regulation making power—the regulator, for example. It is inappropriate to use the less cumbersome negative resolution procedure elsewhere. It is the usual practice to provide for such administrative regulations to be subject to the negative resolution procedure. There is nothing revolutionary or far-reaching about that, but it will make the system lighter to operate.

Dr. Marek: The Committee is grateful for that explanation. The intention of the regulations is now clear. We shall examine the statutory instrument when it is laid before the House, but I am now much happier about the clause. We are concerned that people should not deliberately fail to pay their taxes simply because they feel that they could have a better rate or could use the money somewhere else instead of paying the taxes that are properly due. If this clause seeks to remedy that, I can say no more than that we shall carefully scrutinise the statutory instrument when it comes before us. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.
