New Clause 8
BINGO DUTY

"(1) Schedule 3 in the Betting and Gaming Duties Act 1981 shall be amended as follows—

  1. in Part I sub-paragraph 2(1)(a)(i) for the sum of '£400' there shall be substituted '£450', and
  2. in Part I sub-paragraph 2(1)(a)(ii) for the sum of '£1,000' there shall be substituted '£1,500'.

(2) This section shall come into effect on 1st October 1989.".—[ Dr. Marek.]

I intend to be brief, but this is an important issue, which does not divide the Committee. On several occasions during our proceedings we have tried to consider, cooperatively, ways to improve the law, and I hope that the new clause provides another opportunity for the Government to see the sense in what we say and to react positively to it. To many people bingo is important; I do not play myself, but I know that it is a social game and an amusement—although it does not necessarily require a great deal of brain energy—and, as such, is loved by many people. The limits for exempt bingo—where duty is not payable—are low, have not been increased for several years, and stand at £400 for one day's prizes, and £1,000 for one week's prizes.

The new clause is modest and seeks only to bring the figures up to date. We have calculated that to do so the £400 limit should be increased to £450, and the £1,000 to £1,500. The sums might have been a little less, but we wanted to produce a sensible new clause, which would restore the position that was created by statutory instrument. There may be 100 or 200 people at a bingo session, playing in one room, at 5p a card, and some people will be sufficiently skilful to take three, four or five cards at the same time and to check off the numbers as they are called. Thus the takings in a house can be £20, £30 or £40, and if six houses are played in an afternoon or evening session, the takings may total £250. Of course, some of the money is retained by the club, but a great deal of it is given out in prizes, and if 630 £250 can be taken in an afternoon session, there is some danger of the £1,000 limit being exceeded in a week.

We do not believe that social clubs should have to pay bingo duty if their activities are social and on a small scale, and if the game is provided for the amusement of their members. I hope that the Committee will not insist that such clubs should have to go to the extra administrative trouble of paying bingo duty. The limit of £1,000 is low. Will the Government increase it by a modest amount? We suggest £1,500. Many of the problems and difficulties of treasurers in social clubs would then be avoided.

Mr. Brazier
: I am most grateful to you, Mr. Cormack, for grouping the three new clauses together, and I am sorry that my hon. Friend the Member for Croydon, South (Sir W. Clark) cannot be here, and that the Committee will have to put up with me once again.

The fact that the three new clauses are being taken together provides me with an opportunity to delve a little into their background. They concern an issue which, alas, cuts across many Government Departments, including the Treasury, the Department of Transport, the Ministry of Defence and the Department of Trade and Industry.

I declare a double interest, in that I am secretary of the all-party parliamentary maritime group, and I am also a management consultant. I used to work for five different ship yards around the world. I have never worked for a shipping company, but our clients in those shipyards were shipping companies, and it saddened me that, year by year, Britain's place in the world ranking fell.

In the past two years, there have been two major reports—one from the Select Committee on Transport, the other from the Select Committee on Defence—on the state of British merchant shipping. It would be wrong to go into those reports in detail, but I want to bring out three essential matters arising from them. First, by no stretch of anyone's imagination is there a level playing field in shipping today. We have the wrong end of the playing field, and I shall expand on that in a moment.

Secondly, and as a result, the tonnage of the United Kingdom-registered fleet shrank—according to page IX of the report by the Select Committee on Transport—by 80 per cent. between 1975 and 1986. Even the UK-owned fleet, much of which is not UK-manned, declined by 60 per cent. in the 10 years up to 1987, a figure taken from the table presented by the General Council of British Shipping.

Thirdly, our economy and defence are suffering serious consequences. In relation to the economy, 636 the sea transport account for 1987, which is agreed between the Department of Transport and the General Council of British Shipping, shows a deficit of £1.1 billion. I believe that it has increased since then. In spite of that net deficit, our revenue from UK shipping still accounted for 10 per cent. of all invisible earnings, showing how important shipping still is in tbe British economy. Indeed, 96 per cent. of all goods shipped to and from this country are carried by sea.

With regard to defence, we are still awaiting the great NATO study on shortages in merchant shipping in the NATO area, which I understand will be available early next year.

The report by the Select Committee on Defence spells out that it is unlikely that we would be able to mount another Falklands-type operation because of the decline in our UK-owned fleet. In times of global tension, for which the whole of our armed forces exist, we would be unable to cover both the requirement for military sealift and for essential economic shipping because we simply do not have the fleet to do so.

I put it to my right hon. and Learned Friends on the Front Bench that while another Falklands and increasing world tension may seem unlikely at the moment, the measures that we take will notice elsewhere and will be part of the way in which potential aggressors will assess our seriousness in defending ourselves and our vital interests.

I refer now to my specific tax measures—you have been lenient with me, Mr. Cormack. New clause 13 refers to rollover relief on corporation tax. It is worth comparing the overall corporation tax position for shipping companies in this country with that abroad. Greece has the only merchant fleet in Europe that has gained ground over the past 12 years. It declined for a while but is now picking up again, and it is now the second biggest merchant fleet in the world. There is no profits tax on merchant shipping in Greece. The only tax of any kind that it pays is a minuscule tonnage tax.

In many other countries, such as Scandinavia, Germany and Japan, there are tax-free reserves. Depreciation allowances are higher in other countries: in France, 31 per cent., and in Germany, up to 40 per cent. There are special reduced rates for shipping in Denmark and, as I have already mentioned, in Greece.

This modest new clause focuses on rollover relief, and I shall explain briefly how it works. When a company sells a vessel, or indeed any other capital assets, the written-down depreciated value of the vessel is taken and the difference between the written-down value and the amount for which the vessel is sold becomes a taxable amount on which tax is paid, unless the money is reinvested in shipping in the same year. The clause seeks only to give the shipowner a little bit more flexibility by allowing him an extra two years in which to reinvest the money in shipping before he has to pay it. That will particularly benefit all shipowners at present as the Select Committee on Transport showed clearly that the age profile of our merchant shipping is poor, as it is in most European countries, and our shipowners would be encouraged to reinvest if payment of tax were JPEG 637 deferred. The cost of doing that would be small. Such a measure already exists in Scandinavia, Germany, the Netherlands, Spain, Japan and the United States of America, over and above the incentives given by other countries.

Before I move from corporation tax and new clause 13, I should stress—because my right hon. Friend the Minister will remind me—that we have a lower rate of corporation tax than most of our competitors in other countries. But the effective rate paid in those other countries is much lower for shipping companies, in almost every case, than it is here because of the array of measures that I mentioned.

New clauses 11 and 12 focus on the constructive British business expansion scheme. The concept of the BES is to encourage people, through a generous income tax allowance, to invest in certain areas. For my sins, the first economic pamphlet I published six years ago called for the abolition of first-year capital allowances and regional investment grants before the abolition of first-year capital allowances became Government policy.
