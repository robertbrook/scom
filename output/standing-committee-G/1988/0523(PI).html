Clause 28
CHARGE AND RATES OF INCOME TAX FOR 1989–90

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Norman Lamont)
: This clause gives effect to the Chancellor's decision to make no change in the basic rate and higher rates of income tax this year. For 1989–90 the basic rate is therefore 25 per cent., the level to which it was reduced last year, and the higher rate is 40 per cent. Under the statutory indexation provisions, the level of taxable income above which the higher rate is charged goes up by £1,400 from £19,300 to £20,700.

My right hon. Friend the Chancellor made it clear in his Budget statement on 14 March and in winding up the Budget debates on 20 March that this was a prudent and cautious Budget, which

"in the firm support it gives to the defeat of inflation by monetary policy, provides a solid base for further economic success, including progress to a basic rate of income tax of 20p in the pound, in the years that lie ahead."—[ Official Report, 20 March 1989; Vol. 149, c.823]

That remains our objective and I commend the clause to the Committee.

: Clause 28, as the Financial Secretary said, enshrines the two rates at which income tax shall be charged on earned income during this financial year. We do not intend to divide the Committee on the clause, but it is relevant and important for us to express our misgivings.

We have two criticisms of the two-rate structure that the Government are seeking to perpetuate. The first criticism is about the higher rate at 40p in the pound. Last year the Chancellor reduced all the higher rates to 40p in the pound. We have since been proved right on both counts. But at this juncture we wish to stress the unfairness argument because the Chancellor has deliberately decided not to put right any of the damage that he caused last year.

Higher earners benefit considerably from the 40p top rate of tax. Since 1979 the top 1 per cent. of earners in Britain have received a total of £26,000 million in income forgone by the Exchequer by means of top-rate tax cuts. The average of that 10-year windfall works out at about £100,000 per person. That is the extent of the gift given by the Government and the Chancellor. Last year's tax cuts and the proposals in clause 28 give wealthier individuals in Britain considerable annual benefits.

The reduction introduced last year gave to the top 1.1 per cent. of the population—those earnings above £50,000—a total benefit of £1,900 million. Therefore, 31.3 per cent. of the total benefit made available in last year's Budget went to 1.1 per cent. of the earning population.

By contrast, at the bottom of the scale, people earning less than £5,000—who constitute not 1.1 per cent. but 12.2 per cent. of the taxpaying population—received not £1,900 million, but only £120 million. That is 2 per cent. of the overall benefit given in last year's Budget. Those figures show the manifest unfairness of what the Chancellor did last year and what he wishes to set in place this year. They show that 31 per cent. of the benefit went to 1 per cent. of the population, whereas at the bottom end of the spectrum, 2 per cent. of the benefit went to the bottom 12 per cent.

The figures are more revealing when one considers the average gain per tax unit per week made after last year's Budget. If one earned over £50,000 a year, one gained £182 per week. If one earned less than £5,000 a year, one gained 92p per week. That starkly identifies the difference of approach taken by the Government to the two sections of earners. Clause 29
AGE ALLOWANCE

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Norman Lamont
: The clause has two purposes. First, it extends the higher level of age allowance previously given to those aged 80 and over and on modest incomes to elderly people aged 75 and over. For many years the system has provided an additional measure of relief for older taxpayers in recognition of the increased cost of living associated with old age. In the Finance Act 1987 we introduced a higher level of allowance for those aged 80 and over, recognising that advancing age brings with it increasing needs due to reduced mobility and failing health. The measure was widely welcomed and received the support of hon. Members on both sides 168 of the House. The age allowances are being increased this year in line with indexation. But we are now proposing to extend the higher level of allowance to those aged 75 and over. The reduction in the qualifying age matches the new social security provision for those aged 75 and over on low incomes which will take effect in October.

Secondly, the clause reduces the rate at which the excess of the age allowance over the corresponding basic married man's or single person's allowance is withdrawn when a person's total income exceeds the income limit. That point concerned many people, including my hon. Friends, after last year's Budget. We have been able to reduce the rate of withdrawal.

In 1989–90 the higher level of age allowance will be £5,565 for a married couple and £3,540 for a single person. About 400,000 single people and married couples are expected to benefit from the extension of the higher level and about 15,000 will be taken out of tax altogether. As a result of the reduction in the age allowance withdrawal rate, elderly households will benefit from age allowance up to higher income levels—£13,420 for a married couple and £12,630 for a single person aged 65 to 74. Some 120,000 elderly households whose total incomes lie within the withdrawal band will see their marginal rate reduced from 41 and two thirds to 37 and a half per cent. This meets a large number of representations that have been made by my hon. Friends and I am sure that it will be welcomed by the Committee.

Mr. Norman Lamont
: With clauses 34 to 42, we come to the part of the Bill which deals with the schedule E receipts basis of taxation. Clause 34 paves the way, and I shall briefly outline what it does. With your permission, Mr. Cormack, I shall elaborate a little on clause 35.

Clause 34 establishes whether earnings are within the three Cases of schedule E. It is then necessary to determine for which year they are to be assessed. In the existing legislation, the rules which determine under which Case earnings are chargeable and the year for which they are to be assessed are intermingled. In the new legislation those two matters are dealt with in separate provisions. Clause 34 restates the Cases of schedule E determining what earnings are chargeable; clause 35 determines the year for which they are to be assessed.

So this clause retains almost exactly the same rules for determining what earnings are chargeable to tax as exist now. At present, the residence and domicile status of the employee when he earns his pay is critical in determining whether all the earnings are chargeable, whether the charge extends only to earnings for duties performed in the United Kingdom or whether there is a charge on foreign earnings but only so far as they are received in the United Kingdom. Under the new receipts basis, it will still be the residence and domicile status of the employee when the pay is earned which determines the extent to which that pay is chargeable to tax.

Before referring to one technical respect in which clause 34 changes the rules, I want to make clear what is the effect of the change in the basis of 182 assessment. It is not a part of the new receipts basis proposals that the nature of the income charged to tax under schedule E should change in any way. It will not be the residence status of the employee in the year of receipt which determines what earnings are chargeable; that will still be determined by his status in the year in which they are earned. Once a charge on earnings is established under one of the Cases, those earnings will be assessed for the year in which they are received unless the charge is under Case III, in which case they will be assessed, as now, for the year in which they are received in the United Kingdom.

However, there is one exception: The clause introduces a small change in the scope of the charge under schedule E. In the recent House of Lords decision in the case of Bray v. Best it was held that, in certain exceptional circumstances, earnings could not be taxed when they came from an employment which ended in an earlier tax year. This was because, on the unusual facts of that case, the earnings could not be attributed to any year when the employee held the employment. The main rule in subsection (2) does not deal with that situation because it provides that the employee must hold the office or employment in the year for which earnings are due.

Subsection (3) therefore provides a supplementary rule which removes the gap exposed by Bray v. Best. That point is an exception; the clause simply restates for the purpose of the receipts basis the three cases of schedule E.

Mr. Lamont
: The clause is the major introduction of a significant simplification, but like many simplifications it seems exceedingly complex. It is a change to a simpler basis of assessment for earnings. From 6 April 1989 the earnings of employees and directors will be assessed for the year in which they are received instead of for the year for which they are earned. Most employees, as was said on the previous clause, do not receive an income tax assessment. Tax deducted from their paye under PAYE is near enough to the correct amount payable. No further action is needed. Even when assessment is necessary it is normally the earnings paid during the year that are assessed, because what is earned and what is paid are the same. So the vast majority of employees are already, in practice, taxed on a receipts basis.

One of the virtues of our proposal is that in practice it alters the treatment of only a small minority of employees and directors—about half a million people who regularly receive part of their pay some time after the year in which it was earned. They will now be dealt with on the same basis as in practice applies to all other employees. The pay assessed for the year will be the pay received in the year and the pay from which PAYE has been deducted.

The principle of the change in the basis of assessment has been fairly widely welcomed. That is not surprising, considering the complexity and delay that can occur under the present system. The new receipts basis of assessment—that is, taxing people when they receive their earnings—will put an end to much confusion and complexity. All employees will know how much they have received during the tax year almost as soon as it ends so it will be possible to complete returns much sooner. Because the earnings 186 from which PAYE is deducted during the year will be the earnings assessable for that year, fewer taxpayers will need assessments. That must mean compliance savings for the taxpayers concerned as well as significant staff savings in the longer term in the Inland Revenue.

To reduce the delay which the strict earnings basis causes, some cases have, by agreement between taxpayer and tax office, been assessed on a non-statutory "accounts basis". Under that basis the directors' earnings for the company's accounting period ending in the tax year are taken as the earnings for that year. That reduces the delay in knowing what income to assess, but technical underpayments arising from unpaid earnings occur under that basis in the same way as under the statutory basis. That is, earnings of the accounting period may not have been paid by the following 5 April. Hence the income is assessable, but PAYE has not yet been applied.

There are other drawbacks. Special rules are needed when the accounts basis is adopted at the start of the source of income. That often results in earnings for a period being assessed twice, and when the source of income ceases, there are further special provisions which result in earnings for a similar period not being assessed at all. This non-statutory basis has existed for a very long time and its very existence shows how complex and unsatisfactory the present earnings basis is.

There is another defect with the accounts basis. When it was introduced, the amount not assessed on cessation probably differed little from the amount doubly charged at the outset. But in more recent times, with increasing levels of profits and earnings, that is unlikely to be the case and a significant amount of the earnings over the life of the employment in some cases may not be taxed at all. This will no longer happen under the new receipts basis and it is estimated that once the transition is complete as much as £50 million extra tax will be payable annually.

The hon. Gentleman mentioned the transitional cost, which is, as he said, £80 million in 1989–90 and £60 million in 1990–91. Those costs arise mainly from the ending of the accounts basis of assessment on the transition to the new receipts basis. Some income will not be assessable under either the old accounts basis or the new receipts basis. Income which relates to the period between the company's accounting date and 5 April 1989 will not be assessed for 1988–89 under the accounts basis. To the extent that it was paid before 6 April 1989, it will not be assessed on the receipts basis either.

We decided against a transitional provision to bring income for this period into charge in accounts basis cases. When the accounts basis started, income for a period similar to that which now falls out of assessment on the changeover to the new basis was assessed twice. Any provision charging tax on all the income which would otherwise fall out of assessment would thus clearly be excessive. Moreover, it would not be easy to derive simple and equitable rules which balanced the doubly taxed income against income which falls out of assessment under the present much lower tax rates.

JPEG 187 We must have an effective definition of when earnings are received if we are to have a system of taxation based on receipts. The clause provides that definition.

In formulating a definition of receipt we began with the PAYE system: one of the objectives of the change is to bring the basis of assessment and collection into line. PAYE applies to payments; receipt is defined in terms of payment. That means that the same definition will pinpoint the time at which PAYE needs to be operated on earnings, and the date at which those earnings are received and are thus assessable under the receipts basis.

Earnings will be treated as received when the first of a number of events occur. In relation to employees generally, it is when a payment of or on account of the earnings is actually made or, if earlier, when the employee becomes entitled to such a payment. For directors, however, for reasons referred to by the hon. Gentleman on the previous clause, we need a different system. The Keith committee recognised the problems in applying PAYE to directors' remuneration. It recommended that, in the case of directors, certain defined circumstances should be treated as payment for the purposes of schedule E and PAYE. We introduced legislation in 1987 which would have enabled that to be done, for PAYE, by regulation, but while the receipts basis proposals were under consideration, we deferred introducing those regulations. As "payment" for PAYE and "receipt" for assessment purposes are so clearly interlinked, it seemed appropriate to deal with both in the Bill. Clause 42 accordingly deals with the question of when payment occurs for PAYE purposes.

Because of the lessons learnt in relation to PAYE, the clause contains further rules to decide when the earnings of directors are received. They follow broadly the circumstances that the Keith committee recommended should be treated as payment. Events that constitute receipt of earnings by a director include the crediting of sums on account of earnings to the director in the company's accounts or records and what the committee referred to as a long stop—the determination of earnings, for example, when fees are voted at an annual general meeting. Earnings will be treated as received by a director when the earliest of any of these events, including those which apply equally to non-directors, occurs.

As I have said, a clear and comprehensive definition of when earnings are to be regarded as received is a feature of the new basis of assessment, and the definition must deal with not only straightforward cases but the special circumstances in which directors are paid. In doing so we must heed the lessons from the Keith Committee. Clause 35 provides such a definition and, to ensure that the basis of collection and assessment is in line, a matching definition of payment for PAYE is proposed in clause 42.
