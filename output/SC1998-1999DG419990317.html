Draft Scotland Act 1998 (Consequential Modifications) (No. 1) Order 1999

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Calum Macdonald)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Draft Scotland Act 1998 (Consequential Modifications) (No. 1) Order 1999.

This is a highly technical order. I shall try not to delay the Committee but I will provide a background explanation.

The order is made under section 105 of the Scotland Act 1998. It makes a variety of amendments to legislation to take account of the Act.

The Order contains the amendments that are required to come into force on 6 May and 20 May, in connection with the creation of the Parliament and the transfer of the Lord Advocate from the United Kingdom Government to the Scottish Executive. It also contains a small number of amendments that will come into force on 1 July but that need to be made now to pave the way for an order to be made under section 126(8) of the Act to define the offices in the Scottish Administration that are not ministerial. A second consquential modifications order will be laid in May to cover the bulk of the amendments that will come into force on 1 July.

The order makes several main modifications. It modifies provision for the manner of taking oaths by the Lord Advocate and other members of the Scottish Executive. It extends to the Scottish Parliament the provision for exemption from postage of some petitions and addresses to Parliament and the limitation of the amount of postage recoverable.

The order also excuses Members of the Scottish Parliament, members of the Scottish Executive and junior Scottish Ministers as of right from jury service in Northern Ireland. The Scotland Act already excuses them from jury service elsewhere in the United Kingdom. The order also excuses the Auditor-General for Scotland as of right from jury service in any part of the United Kingdom. It extends the scope of legal aid to cover references or appeals under the Scotland Act from superior courts to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council in connection with devolution issues.

The order also amends the provisions of the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995 to enable the High Court to grant a convicted person bail, pending determination by the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council of an appeal against the determination of a devolution issue by the High Court, and to provide what will happen following the determination of such an appeal. It amends the Solicitors (Scotland) Act 1980 to ensure that Solicitor Advocates have a right of audience before the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council.

4 The order makes only those amendments that are considered necessary or expedient as a consequence of the Scotland Act. It is essentially a deeply technical instrument. I commend it to the Committee.

4.33 pm

Mr. Oliver Letwin
(West Dorset)
: The Minister described the order as deeply technical. I agree that it is a deep order, but not that it is technical. It contains much that none of us is in a good position to examine or understand in detail. In that sense, it is indeed technical. This is not an attack on this particular Minister or on his Department—orders will increasingly become part of the structure of our legislation—but in constitutional terms, the order is a outrage. I regret to say that previous Administrations of both colours have perpetrated the outrage, so it is not a partisan point.

Mr. Brian H. Donohoe
(Cunninghame, South)
: That will be a first.

Mr. Letwin
: On the contrary, I frequently try make points that are not partisan. However, it is for the Minister to establish the facts, because he is using a statutory instrument to amend primary legislation. That is rightly referred to as a Henry VIII attitude to law, because the fundamental principles of primary legislation are having their heads cut off each time such measures come before us.

I shall make one partisan point. Although previous Administrations have taken similar action—it was an outrage when they did so, too—the frequency and the depth, to use the Minister's term, of such actions has increased greatly, not least because the Government have introduced constitutional legislation of immense significance. The Minister regards the order as merely a modest foretaste and, no doubt, such actions will be much more evident in the larger order—I envisage one of telephone-book dimensions. That is a testament to the wide-ranging nature of the constitutional legislation; it requires the amendment of a tissue of other measures.

I bow entirely to the deep learning and great astucity of other members of the Committee. Indeed, the hon. Member for West Aberdeenshire and Kincardine (Sir R. Smith) was so assiduous in his investigation of the matters that the Committee considered previously that he was able to point out major problems with the voting system, which have since come to light in the press. Perhaps he will show us today that he has spent the better part of a couple of weeks with a group of Queen's counsel and others, studying each of the provisions that are hereby amended. He may dazzle us with his profound understanding of whether each amendment has been correctly drafted, but I regret to say that I am in no position to do so.

I might be misleading the Committee—if so, I apologise in advance—but I doubt that the Minister has spent the better part of two weeks looking at the detail of the order. I suspect that, in normal the way of Ministers, he asked the brilliant officials who serve him to investigate which measures needed amendment and to propose technical amendments—to use the Minister's term—which they assure him are right. They are very probably right and, very probably, most of the order is necessary.

JPEG 5 I shall make a point about one of the provisions, but by and large, I suppose that I have a touchingly naive faith that the Government legal service and the brilliant administrative service have produced appropriate provisions. However, introducing such orders is no way to legislate.

If such matters are the consequence of enormously important constitutional primary legislation and a tissue of other matters needed to be changed, they should have been debated when the original legislation was introduced. They should have gone through the many stages that primary legislation has to go through. There would then have been at least some chance of their being investigated in sufficient depth to enable those elected to Parliament and, indeed, those in the other place to have raised any serious difficulty. That will not happen this afternoon.

I shall certainly not tire the Committee with a request to trawl line by line through the order, but I have made it a principle since being elected to the House never to let slip an opportunity to mention that making such orders is not the proper way to legislate.

Having said that, I turn to paragraph 11 of schedule 1 of the order, which is the only item that struck me immediately as relating to a point of fundamental significance on the devolution legislation. I bow to the superior wisdom of other members of the Committee if I have missed other matters.

As the Minister said, paragraph 11 relates to a fundamental aspect of the devolution legislation. It puts in the hands, not of a new body but of a body whose remit is new—the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council—the power to make a series of landmark decisions about the constitutional balance of the United Kingdom: the balance of power between the Scottish Parliament and the United Kingdom Parliament. The Judicial Committee will have relatively little to guide it in making those decisions.

We do not know yet what legal status the Judicial Committee will accord to concordate. There have been at least three slightly but importantly varying answers from Ministers over the past months as to what that legal status will be. As it is not clear to Ministers whether concordats will he regarded as administerative acts that are subject to judicial review, it is not clear to us whether the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council will begin to think that it ought to give leave to a judicial review of concordats. It would be an interesting development if it decided to close. We doubt whether the Lords who sit on the Judicial Committee have considered that issue, perhaps less the Government.

We are dealing not with the great question of how the Judicial Committee will act, but with the consequential question of how people who feel aggrieved about the balance of powers will be able to take their grievances to it. I refer to the balance of powers established in the Act or, as the Act is vague on the matter, in the transfer of functions order or, as that order is vague about it too, in the concordats, where the meat of the matter will be.

Most people arguing such cases will probably be enormously wealthy—they will probably be the UK 6 Government or the Scottish Government, and I regret to say that they will have virtually bottomless purses, although one will be greater than the other. We envisage that many tens of millions of pounds will probably be spent in hiring the world's most expensive lawyers to argue landmark constitutional cases before the Judicial Committee.

The paragraph is about those at the opposite end of the spectrum. Some people, although perhaps not many, who find themselves needing to go to the Judicial Committee will have exiguous funds. I stand to be corrected if I have misunderstood, but I take it that paragraph 11 is designed to provide for that eventuality.

Mr Macdonald
: I shall begin with the point of principle raised by the hon. Member for West Dorset. I take account of his point, which was non-partisan: he was addressing the previous and the present Government. The Scotland Act has a huge effect on a wide range of matters and makes some changes that are required as a consequence of other legislation. An enormous raft of changes were necessary, but the changes were purely technical, following on from the principles embodied in the Scotland Act. It would not have been practical to have made all the changes in the Scotland Act itself and the order is probably—although the hon. Gentleman does not agree—the best way of doing so. The changes are being made by affirmative resolution, which all sides of the House agreed was the appropriate way of dealing with technical changes that have an impact on primary legislation.

I also understand that the previous Lord Advocate has been kept informed of the various kinds of consequential changes that were considered necessary. Information was made available through the usual channels about the amendments before we reached that stage so that people's concerns could be addressed.

Mr. Letwin
: I do not in the least doubt the hon. Gentleman's sincerity on that; as long as Governments use order-making powers only for genuinely technical amendments, there is no problem. However, does he agree that we are setting a dreadful precedent in that a future Government—of any party—could decide not to make technical errors? That could escape the notice of the House or, if it were picked up, present it with the choice of throwing out the order or accepting it wholesale-which is no way to deal with a point of principle.

Mr. Macdonald
: That could be said of both the previous Government and this one. We do deal with issues of substance in such Committees. To get back to the order, JPEG 9 it is heavily technical and does not change any issues of substance. It merely follows through the consequences of what was agreed as part of the Scotland Act 1998.

I turn now to the individual points that were mentioned. The hon. Member for West Dorset was concerned about the sort of help that would be available for people of modest means who wish to pursue a case up to the level of the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council. The order will make such people eligible for legal aid, as would be the case for usual court procedure in Scotland. Matters will be brought to the Judicial Committee within the scope of the legal aid system.

I am not terribly well up on the Lord Chancellor's proposals because they affect England and Wales rather than Scotland. They are not relevant to this order or what will happen in Scotland. The legal aid provisions in Scotland, as my hon. and learned Friend the Member for Edinburgh, Pentlands (Dr. Clark) said, will be a matter for the Scottish Parliament to decide. I imagine that future changes will have to take account of the possibility of devolution issues and will have to be pursued through to the level of the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council.

Mr. Letwin
: That is most helpful and clarifies the Government's intention. To take it a stage further, if an English or Welsh person had a case to bring to the Privy Council relating to a concordat between the Scottish Parliament and the UK Parliament, would that person be entitled to legal aid to pursue such a case?

Mr. Macdonald
: Anyone who is pursuing a case through the Scottish courts would be similarly eligible for all the aid and protection that is available within the Scottish courts and which is within the scope of the law, as set down by the Scottish Parliament.

Mrs. Eleanor Laing
(Epping Forest)
: My concern, as ever, is for Scottish and other UK taxpayers at a time when the Government's aim is, rightly, to reduce the legal aid bill for the whole country. Has the Minister assessed the likely cost to taxpayers of extending legal aid to this new area of law? I have always said—I am allowed to do so because I am a Scottish solicitor—that the only people who will benefit from the Scotland Act are Scottish solicitors who will make a lot of money from it.

Mr. Macdonald
: We have not made a precise assessment, but I should imagine that it will be a small amount. As the hon. Member for West Dorset said, issues going to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council will involve mainly large bodies and organisations. It would be rare for individuals to bring cases to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council on a dispute over whether a certain law was an appropriate one to have passed in Scotland because it was a devolved or reserved matter. That would happen only in exceptional circumstances.

One can imagine an individual pursuing a civil or criminal matter through the Scottish courts, and part of the the argument being whether the law was appropriate for the Scottish Parliament to have passed or whether it should have been subject to a reserved power. A case could be pursued by an individual all the way to the Judicial Committee, although such instances would be rare.

Mr. Letwin
: The Minister will remember that, under the Scotland Act, there is no question of having to go up the chain to get to the Judicial Committee. All devolution issues are referred immediately to the Committee. Will it not fulfil the same role as the European Court of Justice plays in European law issues, when other courts will have to refer matters to the Judicial Committee as soon as the question whether they are devolutionary issues is raised? Will people not use the system as they started to use the ECJ and European law, in general, to challenge decisions that would otherwise go against them? There may be a flood of such litigation.

Mr. Macdonald
: It is more likely that issues in which legal aid applied would be proceedings that had already been through the courts in which one of the points at issue was whether the matter concerned devolution. The number of cases that incurred liability for legal aid would be limited. All such matters will become clearer in time, but that is the expected position.

It may be helpful for the hon. Gentleman to know that devolution issues will generally go from the law courts to the Court of Session or to the High Court, in the first instance. It will be up to the Law Officers to decide whether it is appropriate for the case to go direct to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council; such procedure is not automatic—point that deals also with the potential cost to the legal aid budget.

Mrs. Gorman
: Will the Minister clarify an issue on page 12 of the order that relates to section 2 of the Abortion Act 1967 and also paragraph 5 on page 13? Powers relating to abortion are reserved for the Westminster Parliament. The two statements in the order imply that the section of the Act under which the abortion legislation is to be administered and the 1991 regulations are to be transferred from the Scottish Office to the new Scottish Administration. Will the Minister clarify the position, given that the new Scottish Administration will not have powers governing abortion?

Mr. Macdonald
: Although the Scottish Parliament will not have such powers and abortion is a reserved matter, the order will not change that. Obviously, certain officers of the Scottish Assembly will have to carry out requirements under the abortion laws and have to apply the legislation appropriately as it applies throughout the United Kingdom. The order defines the particular officers who will have to fulfil such obligations. The officers already have those obligations. All that is being changed is the Officers' title, not their duties. The hon. Lady was concerned about that point, but those duties will remain under UK legislation—they are covered by a reserved rather than a devolved power.

Fire inspectors were mentioned. They will be appointed by Her Majesty on the advice of the First Minister. There is no change, except that the First Minister rather than the Secretary of State will provide the advice. The reference to bail is in parallel to that for legal aid. Provision for bail and legal aid, which would usually be available to people in the Scottish courts, will be available when cases go to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council.

JPEG 11 Data protection will be a reserved power and in parallel with the abortion provisions. Officers and bodies in Scotland will have to fulfil their obligations to the Westminster Parliament under legislation that is reserved at a UK level. The order is designed to ensure that that can happen. I recognise that data protection is of particular interest to my hon. and learned Friend the Member for Edinburgh, Pentlands. I shall write to her setting out the overall picture.

Mr. Gorrie
: Why has the definition of "pre-commencement enactment" been modified?
