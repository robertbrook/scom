Mr. Andrew F. Bennett
(Denton and Reddish)
: I beg to move amendment No. 8, in page 2, line 17, leave out paragraph (b).

This is really a probing amendment. Paragraph (b) sets out the conditions that a member of staff must fulfil to be able to use clause 1 as a defence. It says:

"the member of the staff believes on reasonable grounds that he is so shown in such a register."

It is important to establish what a member of staff has to do to show reasonable grounds. In the last resort, of course, it is a matter for the court to make a judgment, but it should be possible to avoid the point at which such a case is brought to the court, because it is an unpleasant business for the teacher and it is not particularly pleasant for anybody else. To avoid that, therefore, we need to have clear and specific rules—perhaps in the regulations—making clear what the member of staff has to do to show that he has reasonable grounds to believe that the child was on such a register.

There are two sets of circumstances. In the last debate we pressed for corporal punishment to be carried out only by the head teacher, or on his instructions. At present, it is permissible by the head or other members of staff. Let us consider, first, the other members of staff.

I believe that the other members of staff ought physically to look at the register to see whether there is an entry there permitting that child to be subject to corporal punishment. If the teacher looks at the register and there is a mistake, the teacher should not be held liable; it should be the head or the authority responsible for compiling the register. If the teacher has done less than look at the register before he carries out the corporal punishment, in my view he is not carrying out the spirit of the clause. In a big school, he may telephone the school office and ask whether a certain child is on the register.

I am not satisfied with that. It is possible that the child was on the register last week, and the teacher relies on his memory. But we should remember that there are often clashes of name in large comprehensive schools, and it is difficult to be certain which Paul Jones it is. So the only reasonable ground in normal circumstances is that the teacher inspects the register to satisfy himself that the pupil's name is on it. The Minister should say that he accepts that view. In the regulations he should say that in normal circumstances the teacher should inspect the register and satisfy himself that the name is on it. That meets the requirements.

There may be exceptional circumstances when that is not possible. As long as the regulations say "in exceptional circumstances", that narrow area can be tested in the court. The party may be away from school, or there may be difficulty in inspecting the register and the 42 teacher may have to rely on his memory. The Minister should make clear that the only way in which a member of staff can use this provision as his defence is if he has actually inspected the register.

I realise that the position is rather different for the head. He may appear before the court because he carried out the punishment wrongly. He does not have the defence that he simply inspected the register; he also has to establish that he carried out proper and due diligence in preparing the register. That is a different point, although, he ought to be able to clear up the first difficulty and say that he looked at the register. The allegation that the register was not properly maintained, and the question of whether the head or the local authority was responsible for it would be a second argument.

It is far too general and vague an alibi for a member of staff to say that he "believes on reasonable grounds" that the child is shown on the register. I hope that the Minister will say that although he wants the Bill to include such a general provision, he accepts that the guidance to schools must contain a clear statement that the basic duty of a member of staff who wants to use the provisions in the clause is to examine the register and to satisfy himself that the name of the child whom he wishes to punish is on it.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Education and Science (Mr. Bob Dunn)
: I shall reply to the short debate on amendment No. 8. The Committee knows that clause 1(5) (b) is intended to protect a teacher who in good faith uses corporal punishment on an exempt child.

Clause 1(5) (a) will protect a teacher if a wrong entry is made in the register. I tell my hon. Friend the Member for Croydon, North-West (Mr. Malins) that the teacher can 46 rely on the entry. But if the register has been completed in a slip-shod or incorrect way, there may be a case for pursuing the body responsible in the civil court. My hon. Friend knows from reading the notes on clauses that either the governing body or the local education authority is the body responsible, depending on the status of the school.

Clause 1(5) (b) offers protection to teachers who make a genuine mistake of identity, after taking reasonable steps to familiarise themselves with the contents of a register, but, because of an oversight in the established routine of the school, are unaware of a change of entry. I welcome the remarks of my hon. Friend the Member for Staffordshire, South-East (Mr. Lightbown) who, I believe, supports the aim of this part of the Bill.

Subsection (5) (b) refers to a member of staff who "believes on reasonable grounds" that a pupil is shown as liable to corporal punishment. It is right that the Committee should ask what is meant by "believes on reasonable grounds". There are two elements in the clause with the Committee will accept. The first relates to the member of staff's belief. He has to believe that a child's name is on the register as a pupil liable to corporal punishment. He cannot just assume it or not bother; he must believe it.

The second element relates to the grounds upon which the belief rests. They have to be reasonable. If the teacher has taken steps to familiarise himself with the contents of the register, by checking it once or twice a week or as many times as is practicable, or there is a well-run system for notifying teachers of changes to the register, the familiarisation or the system would in themselves provide the grounds for the belief that a pupil was liable to corporal punishment.

Mr. Dunn
: It will go that far, in that sense. Let us look at some examples. Let us say that a teacher is away from school for a lengthy period, perhaps for as long as six months, due to illness, secondment, in-service training or for any other reason. On return to the school, the teacher does not bother to look at the register but uses corporal punishment on an exempt child. The courts would almost certainly take the view that such an action was not founded on a belief of any kind, particularly if the pupil involved had joined the school within the six month period when the teacher was away from school.

Let us take another example of a teacher who wishes to punish a pupil who says that he is John Smith. That might happen in a very large school. The teacher checks the register and finds that John Smith is liable to corporal punishment. The teacher subsequently canes the alleged John Smith. If it then turns out that the pupil involved in the misdemeanour was Alan Smith, who was trying to evade responsibility for his misdemeanour, the courts would probably rule that the teacher had reasonable JPEG 47 grounds for belief that the pupil concerned was liable to corporal punishment.

A third example might be one of a teacher who takes over a class at short notice. The normal class teacher might say, in handing over the class, "No one is exempt from corporal punishment." In the first few minutes, a flagrant act of indiscipline occurs and the temporary teacher reacts by smacking a child who turns out to be exempt. In that case, I believe that the courts could say that the temporary teacher had reasonable grounds for believing that the pupil was liable to corporal punishment.

The question about reasonable grounds has been raised. The concept of reasonableness, as was pointed out by my hon. Friend the Member for Grantham (Mr. Hogg), is an objective one which the courts regularly adopt. A teacher has reasonable grounds only if his grounds are such that it would lead a responsible and sensible teacher to believe that the pupil was shown on the register as liable to corporal punishment.

The hon. Member referred to regulations. I do not advise that we seek to put such guidelines in regulations. There are other means, such as departmental circulars which could adequately state and provide some guidelines, although my hon. Friend the Member for Grantham will be first to point out that such a circular could not be authoritative as such a prescription is a matter for the court.

The hon. Member for Denton and Reddish (Mr. Bennett) raised the question of consulting registers. I believe that he accepts that practice would vary from the small primary or village school to the very large urban comprehensive, where as many as 100 pupils may start at the school in the September term. To consult the register might be feasible in formal cases of caning. In our debates last Wednesday the Committee agreed that in many schools there was a procedure and a punishment book and that a certain mechanical operation took place when a person was sent for punishment.

Smacks are different, as we accept. They may necessarily be applied immediately once an offence has been noticed and the immediate response will be welcomed in certain cases, in certain classrooms by certain children. I am glad to note that the hon. Member for Cambridgeshire, North-East (Mr. Freud) is nodding vigorously at that.

We accept that a smack needs to be administered as quickly as possible if it is to have any effect, and all the Bill asks is that the teacher should act reasonably. I accept that teachers who use corporal punishment in circumstances other than those involving immediate danger to persons or property need to be circumspect. That point was made by my hon. Friend the member for Staffordshire, South-East. They should not use it on pupils they do not know without first checking the register. Forgetfulness or carelessness on their own are insufficient excuses for using corporal punishment on exempt pupils.

I hope that I have dealt with some of the questions asked by hon. Members on both sides of the Committee and .that the amendment will not be accepted.
