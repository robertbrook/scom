: At our last sitting I was referring to the question of environment. In paragraph 6 of the White Paper we find the words:

"the neglect and dilapidation of the common closes and staircases and the back courts have often overwhelmed the good intentions of individual householders".

These are very wise words and I am very glad that attention has been drawn to that aspect, because there is nothing more dismal in the type of house with which we shall be dealing here than the four blocks of tenement buildings round a square, so that the housewife, who normally works at the back, looks on to a most depressing outlook.

We will not sort this out by legislation. It calls for discipline in several ways, including discipline among neighbours co-operating to keep entries, courts and stairheads clear. Unfortunately, many people who have been living in conditions of this kind for many years become inured to them and pay little attention to them. Just as one bad apple will spoil the lot in the barrel, one bad neighbour can create confusion and enmity in these circumstances. I hope that neither the Committee nor the Ministers will think this is an unimportant matter but that where there are a number of these buildings to be improved they will do what they can by suggesting to local authorities that there might be a need for considering the appointment of janitors or caretakers to ensure that these places are kept clean and tidy. There is a need for parental discipline, in this as in so many other fields. It is deplorable to see children quite deliberately attacking the cement and other surroundings of the area and thus adding to the deterioration.

The whole standard of housing is going up at the same time as we are facing the problem of obsolescence in older houses.

Mr. Adam Hunter
: I welcome this Bill, for it has tremendous possibilities, if not in the short term, certainly in the long term. Obviously, the problems vary from constituency to constituency. In my own constituency we do not face the problem of large tenements. Any which we possessed went some time ago because of the forward-looking policies of town councils and county councils which got rid of such properties years ago.

I am particularly pleased to see that the Bill increases the limits for discretionary improvement grants, with the maximum grant to private owners going up from £500 to £1,200. Many owner-occupiers felt that the improvements brought about by the existing maximum were not sufficient to provide a useful long life to older property.

The proposed maximum is an added incentive for younger owner-occupiers who have recently, or a few years back, purchased a house of an older type without the requisite modern amenities. The improvements possible from the new grants will compel many to look towards renovating or modernising an older house rather than burden their lives for thirty years ahead with high mortgage rates for a completely new house or a comparatively recently built one with all the modern amenities. I also welcome the suggested raising of the maximum of the standard improvement grant. This will be received with gratitude by owners of property and also by owner-occupiers.

Part I of the Bill introduces attractive criteria to decide if a house measures up to a tolerable standard. The requirements mentioned in the Bill are positive enough, but one with which I am currently concerned could lead to difficulties, namely that the literal meaning of the word "dampness" is not upheld. Who will decide if a house is substantially free from penetrating damp? It is hardly possible at present to define "freedom from dampness". The usual and ever-recurring explanation on complaints of dampness is that it is caused by condensation. Just two days ago it was reported in the Edinburgh Evening News that the Depute City Architect for Housing in Edinburgh stated that it was a problem not only in new houses in Edinburgh but was a growing problem throughout Britain.

I hope that the Bill will do something positive to help two housing schemes in my constituency, both of which are under 60 the Scottish Special Housing Association. I refer to the schemes in Woodmill, Dunfermline, and the scheme in Rosyth, the former built in 1948 and the latter back in 1914. The Woodmill scheme has a chronic damp problem. Dampness, mould and wet walls are featured in the complaints from many tenants. The seriousness of the position for the tenants is that the houses may be there for another forty years.

The S.S.H.A. at the moment, like some local authorities in other parts of Scotland, has no remedy. A few years ago, in the Woodmill scheme, the system of heating was changed from solid fuel to other forms of heating. The result was soaring heating costs, but the persistence of damp.

The Rosyth scheme is well known to my hon. Friend the Minister of State. He and I have crossed swords on this matter on many occasions, and once or twice he has dropped his guard and been very generous. But he is beginning to see that the sword has a double-edged blade. Even after modernisation in the Rosyth scheme a number of houses which I have seen, two of them as recently as Sunday last, have the undoubted benefits of the improvements of modernisation detracted from due to dampness to a lesser or greater extent. I trust that the Bill will allow more money to be released to the S.S.H.A. to provide a fuller and much more satisfying modernisation programme.

The White Paper, Cmnd. 3598, contains a significant paragraph, namely, paragraph 8, which I should like to read to the Committee:

"The Government consider that worthwhile results will be achieved only if the local housing authorities accept that they have an important role in their districts, not only as the providers of houses for letting, but also as the co-ordinators of all efforts directed to securing satisfactory housing for the whole community. There will be a role for housing associations, and much will depend on the co-operation of owners, factors and residents, but the local housing authorities must take the lead in driving the policy forward in each district. Legislation can give them better powers, better techniques and better financial arrangements. They alone can provide the drive without which all else will be of no avail."

That is a most important paragraph. I can see that in future local authorities will play the role of overlord in compelling all sorts of people—private owners, owner-occupiers and housing associations—to carry out repairs and improve their JPEG 61 property. Despite what housing associations, owners or owner-occupiers think, will the local authority decide what is best for all concerned?

I turn to the question of compensation and the compulsory acquisition of houses. The Bill and the White Paper use different terms in this connection. On the one hand slum clearance is mentioned, and on the other "housing treatment areas". What has happened to town redevelopment schemes? Redevelopment in towns is taking place in areas at the moment and it can be phased. Redevelopment in towns can also absorb perfectly good older houses without modern amenities and others with every amenity. Redevelopment may require, and certainly will require in my area, compulsory purchase.

I wish to ask my hon. Friend what is the position of the owner in a redevelopment scheme when he finds his good house being taken over by compulsory purchase. Is he entitled to the additional payments embodied in the Bill?

Part IV deals with the fixing of fair rents. This provides for the conversion of existing controlled tenancies into regulated tenancies. The 1965 Act had this embodied in legislation. What will take place as a result of the Bill is simply the end of controlled tenancies in some cases and the introduction of regulated tenancies which had previously been deferred. Many people may have some objections to the latter course. However, where improvements have taken place, my hon. Friend's difficulties will not be as great. In the Rosyth modernisation scheme it is well known that as soon as a tenant returns to his house after modernisation he has to bear an increase in rent of £14 to £16 per year, and I understand that this is accepted without demure.

I should like to deal with Part V in relation to environment, which interests me very much. The constituency point which I wish to raise does not go as wide as the point which was raised by my right hon. Friend in introducing the Bill. For environmental purposes, the amount of money is restricted to £100 per house, which, in some smaller schemes, is a great deal of money. I welcome this Part of the Bill for I can see a way in which it will assist with a problem in my area.

I have in mind a controversy which is at present raging in Rosyth where long blocks of housing exist and where the end 62 tenants are being asked now, after many years, to extend facilities to the middle tenants to pass round the end houses for various domestic reasons. This would give every tenant his own entrance, improve the external amenities, and make for a much more harmonious community. I welcome the Bill and hope that it has a quick and smooth passage through the House.
