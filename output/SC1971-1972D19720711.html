NATIONAL INSURANCE BILL
Clause 5
FINALITY OF DECISIONS

Question proposed—( 4th July)—That the Clause stand part of the Bill.

Question again proposed.

The Chairman
: I remind the Committee that we are to consider at the same time the following new Clauses:

New Clause 5—" Finding of fact to be conclusive"—

"The provisions contained in section 75(1) of the National Insurance Act 1965, and the corresponding provision in section 50(1) of the National Insurance (Industrial Injuries) Act 1965, that the decision of a claim or question as there mentioned is to be final shall make any finding of fact or other determination embodied in or necessary to a decision, or on which it is based, conclusive for the purpose of any further decision".

New Clause 6—" Decision on origin of injury or disability"—

"A decision (given under section 48(2) of the National Insurance (Industrial Injuries) Act 1965 or otherwise) that an accident was an industrial accident is to be taken as determining the origin of any injury or disability suffered by the claimant".

"Any person suffering personal injury by accident shall be entitled, if he claims the accident was an industrial accident, to have that question determined and a declaration made…accordingly".

Indeed, that is one of the more lucid passages of the 1965 Act. There is no dispute about those provisoes. The hon. Gentleman went on to say:

"That is the position which obtained in the 1946 Act and in Section 48 of the subsequent consolidation Act."

There followed the classic non sequitur:

This is a crucial factor under Clause 5. The hon. Gentleman owes the Committee a better explanation. I hope that he has had time for reconsideration since he spoke. In one of the cases that I quoted, Mr. Dowling established the hon. Gentleman's three points before the Commission and Mr. Nelson, who awarded him an accident declaration and industrial injury benefit. When Mr. Dowling claimed disablement benefit the medical appeal tribunal decided that he had not had an industrial accident. It was then that the Ministry introduced its strange doctrine that an accident declaration did not mean that the claimant had had an accident, but merely that if he had an accident it would be an industrial accident.

Where and in what form does the hon. Gentleman claim that this strange rigmarole was embodied in the intentions of Parliament? How can he claim that that is the meaning of Section 48? I remind the Committee that the purpose of the Clause is to embody this new, strange rigmarole in our law for the third time. I ask the Committee to compare the two facts—that in the 1965 Act and that which we are asked to adopt this morning. Section 48(2) of the 1965 Act states that:

"any person suffering personal injury by accident shall be entitled, if he claims the accident was an industrial accident, to have that question determined, and a declaration made —"

Section 48(4) continues:

"any declaration under this section that an accident was or was not an industrial accident shall be conclusive for the purposes of any claim for benefit in respect of that accident, —"

It does not say that it shall be valid for an industrial injury benefit claim but invalid for disablement benefit. If an industrial injury benefit is granted it is because an accident declaration has been obtained. That remains final. It is conclusive for the purposes of any claim for benefit in respect of that accident.

The hon. Gentleman claims the right to say, "Mr. Dowling got his accident declaration from the Commissioner on the final point of appeal for the determination of claim. I do not accept that that should be conclusive for the purposes of any 404 other benefit claim, namely, disablement benefit". How can the hon. Gentleman say that that was the intention of Section 48?

Subsection (2)—which is drawn up in somewhat involved language, as these things tend to be—makes it clear that the purpose is to stand Section 48 of the 1965 Act on its head. It says:

"A decision (given under section 48(2) of the National Insurance (Industrial Injuries) Act 1965 or otherwise) that an accident was an industrial accident is to be taken as determining only that paragraphs ( a), ( b) and ( c) of section 48(5) are satisfied in relation to the accident"

Those are the three provisoes that the hon. Gentleman quoted, and which are not in dispute. It says that a declaration of an accident as an industrial accident is to be taken as determining only those three points. It goes on:

"and neither any such decision nor the reference to a medical board or medical appeal tribunal under section 37 of that Act of the disablement questions in connection with any claim to or reward of disablement benefit is to be taken as importing a decision as to the origin of any injury or disability suffered by the claimant".

It is not surprising that the hon. Gentleman hopes that this Clause may slip by us, because to the layman, at first glance, it is difficult to understand. In simple language, it means that an accident declaration is no longer to be taken as determining that an accident had taken place. If an accident did not take place in Mr. Dowling's case, why did the Commissioner award him industrial injury benefit? One does not award industrial injury benefit out of the blue because one is sorry for a chap; it is given to him, by definition, because an accident has taken place.

I suggest that it is nonsense to say that we are only re-establishing what was intended originally by Section 48. How can anyone who is honest and clear-headed say that the intentions of those two texts are not diametrically opposed?

The intentions of the originators of this legislation are even more irrefutable in terms of the finality of decisions. At our last Sitting I started to quote from the Explanatory Memorandum issued in conjunction with the 1945 Industrial Injuries Bill. On the determination of claims to benefit it says that:

"Appeals will lie from the decision of the local Tribunal to an Industrial Injuries Commissioner whose decision will be final."

The Explanatory Memorandum, referring JPEG 405 to assessment for pension and not for industrial injury benefit says that:

"The workmen will have certain rights of appeal against the decision of the Medical Board to a Medical Appeal Tribunal and the decision of that Tribunal will be final."

All that is summed up in Section 50 of the 1965 Act.

But what does Clause 5 say? Subsection (1) provides that:

"The provision … that the decision of a claim or question as there mentioned is to be final shall not make any finding of fact or other determination embodied in or necessary to a decision, or on which it is based, conclusive for the purpose of any further decision."

If that is not standing the original intention of the 1946 Act on its head, what is? What has been the effect of accepting the hon. Gentleman's interpretation of the original legislation, and the interpretation which his Department has pursued through the courts with such ferocity?

The effect of high unemployment is shown in two ways. First, the absolute numbers of those who receive neither unemployment nor supplementary benefit have increased. Not only are the figures high but what is particularly alarming is that as the numbers of male unemployed have risen, so the proportion not in receipt of unemployment benefit has also risen. This is an extremely serious situation. I believe that we are able to counter it and clearly we ought to counter it.

There is a part of the new Clause to which I have not referred, and that is the earnings-related supplement part. We suggest that the earnings-related supplement should be extended from the present six-month period to a year. I should like to indicate why we think that ought to be done.

First, according to the latest figures, barely a third of those with unemploy- JPEG 435 ment benefit are receiving the earnings-related supplement. This is an extremely small proportion. It is in many cases more valuable than the flat-rate benefit, it more than doubles it in a significant number of cases. The cut-off point after six months has nothing to do with need. Clearly it is those who are unemployed for longer than six months, as their other resources dry up and as their psychological resistance to this most harsh time of their lives probably decreases, whose need for increased money is greater.

Secondly, we have very little information about the size of additions in earnings-related supplement received by men at different earnings levels. We know the entitlements for a man at a given earnings level to earnings-related supplement, according to the regulations. What we do not know is how many men at different earnings levels claim and are in receipt of earnings-related supplements at particular levels. So far as I know, the Department of Employment has never published these statistics. What we do know—and this is significant and is a cause for considerable concern about the earnings-related supplement—is that the lowest paid workers pay in contributions, which they are obliged to do if their earnings are above a certain level, but they do not receive benefits in line with the level of their contributions. This is because of the 85 per cent. ceiling rule. They are not entitled to receive more than 85 per cent. of their earning power taking into account both the flat rate for themselves, their wife and children and the earnings-related supplement part.

If one includes the flat rate part for the man, wife and children and adds on the earnings-related supplement part, then it is easy to see that for low paid workers their entitlement with the earnings-related supplement is greater than their previous low earnings. Therefore, many low paid workers do not receive their full entitlement to the earnings-related supplement. This is a cause for considerable concern, although the point is not wholly covered by the new Clause. We feel that not only ought the period to be extended but that the operation of the earnings-related supplement ought to be looked into.

I shall be very brief about the third point I want to make. A number of other workers do not receive earnings-related supplement because of previous spells of 436 unemployment or sickness benefit. If one has had successive spells of being off work; then one is not entitled in some cases to earnings-related supplement; in other cases, it is reduced. In other words, the conditions for receipt of earnings-related supplement are very stringent and operate against those with a number of previous periods out of work.

The crucial point in this new Clause, apart from these other considerations about why it operates, in our view, rather unsatisfactorily, is that, first, the cut-off point at the end of six months is not related to need and the longer a man is unemployed the more he needs the extra resource which he would receive from the earnings-related supplement.
