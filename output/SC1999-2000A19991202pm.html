Mr. Flight
: The clause deals with recognised schemes that are constituted in other EU states. Under the Undertakings for Collective Investments in Transferable Securities directive, as implemented by clause 235, such collective investment schemes are required to be recognised if they satisfy various prescribed requirements. One of those is set out in subsection (3)(a), which states that the notice that is given to the FSA must be accompanied by a certificate from the authorities, in the relevant state, that are responsible for the authorisation of collective investment schemes. The certificate must make it clear that the scheme complies with the conditions that are necessary for it to enjoy the rights that are conferred by any relevant community instrument—in practice, the UCITS directive.

There have been several instances, particularly in relation to the use of subsidiary companies and derivative instruments, where the relevant authorities in Ireland and Luxembourg have interpreted the UCITS directive requirement more liberally than those in the UK and, indeed, differently. For example, a Dublin UCITS could do something that could be freely marketed in the UK, whereas a UK-based scheme doing the same thing could not get recognised here.

The amendment would require the FSA to notify the relevant authorities of the state in question, should it consider that the scheme does not comply with the conditions necessary to enjoy the rights conferred by the community measure to which the certificate relates. However, that is a double-edged sword On matters on which Dublin and Luxembourg accept certain items and the UK does not, I believe that Dublin and Luxembourg are right and the UK is wrong. However, it is the other way round in relation to acceptance of other items. It is healthy to have such issues resolved 1118 out in the open. Therefore, the amendment would not necessarily lean on Dublin, Luxembourg and other EU states to change their rules, but would have differences out in the open so that pressures may resolve them one way or another.

Amendment No. 846 would further require the FSA notice to be published with a view to bringing it to the public's attention. It would require the FSA to make public those instances in which it believes that an EEA state has interpreted the UCITS directive differently. That gives effect to what I said earlier about amendment No. 801 opening up the matter to public discussion.

Mr. Flight
: The amendment would not breach any Community obligations. It does not confer the ability not to accept the passported UCITS; it merely requires the FSA to make public the fact that there is a different interpretation from one EEA member to another.

Two examples spring to mind of where, broadly, debate has taken place for almost nine years without becoming public, although it is of some import. In Dublin, the authorities permit the keeping of accounts on money funds on an amortisation basis. This means that money funds can maintain a constant price while investing in financial paper, which will fluctuate in value over time, with interest rates. The same applies to United States money funds. The UK interpretation is that one has to value such financial paper to market daily, therefore the price of the money fund fluctuates.

People wishing to use money funds expect to have a constant principle. Therefore money funds managers feel that they cannot use financial paper but have simply to invest in deposits. The reality is a mechanism where the industry has kept at bay the competition of money funds, which have been so effective in the United States. If that issue had been brought to light, the debate would have been much more healthy and Mr. Cruickshank would not be so busy.

As I said earlier, it is a two-way street. It is unsatisfactory that significant differences are not publicly debated. Another such difference is the use of what is known as the Mauritian sandwich, used for funds invested in India. Quite correctly, I believe, 1120 Luxembourg and Dublin permit this as falling under the UCITS rules, which allow the use of derivatives as well as structures in order to make a fund more efficient. That is to allow investors to benefit from double taxation agreements between India and Mauritius. The UK authorities take the view that it cannot be done because it is fund-to-fund.

I have mentioned two quite big issues that have not been resolved by the Securities and Investments Board, the FSA, the Treasury or the Commission; they have just been sitting there, suppressed. It would have been much better if the UK authorities had forced those matters out into the open for debate and resolution. It could, however, be the other way round; we could have stimulated debate about the potential misuse of the UCITS directive by others.

I repeat that the crucial issue is whether the clause is in breach of EU directives. I believe that the answer is no, because it is not stopping or impeding the local marketing of passported UCITS. It is a method of forcing some degree of pan-EEA rationality.
