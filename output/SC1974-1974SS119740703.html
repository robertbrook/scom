Friend will withdraw the amendment.

It was a difficult operation because, after all, hon. Members who were present 8 during the Committee stage of the previous Bill will recollect that the Conservative Government were quite willing to consider other ways in which the feudal package could be preserved. This was a perfectly respectable legal view to hold and the Lord Advocate of the day, now Lord Wylie, took that view and expounded it very convincingly to the Committee.

When the Labour Government came into office, I took a different view. I took the view that we could do it a different way, and this is the other way we have found which would be effective and safe.

There are two conflicting requirements. One is the requirement of keeping the feudal package together. There are those who say there must be a reddendo for that, although, frankly, I do not agree, but one cannot put their view at nought. That is a valid legal view which may have some effect at the end of the day if the matter ever came to the courts in litigation. On the other hand, there is a desire on which we are united. There are differing views on reddendo, but we are united in the desire to excise feuduty from the feudal package now and not leave it until phase 3.

Whereas it is easy enough to say that by statute we will abolish feuduty, it is not altogether a simple matter and not a matter which can be satisfactorily dealt with by the amendment. It is not satisfactory to patch up the common law in the same way. As it now stands, the clause seeks to say that feuduty goes, but the package is preserved in such a way that any common law requirement that may continue for a reddendo is covered and if to say that it is a legal fiction to say that the package continues as if there was a reddendo, I have to accept it as a JPEG 9 fiction but to say that it is the best, most economical and least damaging way to do it.
