DRAFT WEIGHTS AND MEASURES ACT 1963 (SUGAR) ORDER 1974

12 noon.

The Minister of State, Department of Prices and Consumer Protection (Mr. Alan Williams)
: I beg to move, That the Committee have considered the draft instrument.

I presume that in an atmosphere of reasonableness hon. Members will wish to approve the draft order. I hope that hon. Members have had copies of documents that we decided to make available because of the printing dispute. We were not sure whether the necessary documents would be available and so we have provided copies of correspondence and the Statutory Instruments Committee report so that hon. Members would have an opportunity to see, in advance, precisely what the main points of controversy would be.

Mr. Jerry Wiggin
: I am not a member of this Committee and that places me at a disadvantage. I appreciate the Minister's gesture of good will, but I wonder whether there is a spare copy available now so that I may catch up.

Mr. Williams
: Indeed, I am sure that we shall with the utmost good will provide the hon. Member with a library. I I shall be trying to touch on most of the points contained in the documents during my speech.

Mrs. Sally Oppenheim
: Hon. Members are at a considerable disadvantage, through no fault of the hon. Gentleman, because we have not received a report of the debate in another place.

Mr. Williams
: The debate in the other place, as the hon. Lady will know, was adjourned because certain papers were not available. The other place also wanted further consideration to be given to the recommendations of the Select Committee on Statutory Instruments. That is what the Government have under- 4 taken to arrange. I felt that it was better to put it to this House first so that hon Members would have the opportunity to consider the changes.

The purpose of the order is fairly clear. However, I suspect that our discussions may wander beyond the men parameters set by this order if hon Members are imaginative enough. The purpose is to allow the sale of pre-packed sugar in prescribed metric quantities, a: well as in prescribed Imperial quantities where those quantities are above 100 grammes and do not exceed 5 kilo grammes. Packages covered by the legislation down to the size of 50 grammes will have to have net weigh marked on the container.

Opposition Members will know that the previous Government initiated the consultations on which the order is based. They are also probably aware that there were two rounds of consultations.

Under Section 54(2) of the Weights and Measures Act 1963 there is a duty to consult, and more than 70 organisations were consulted. Those organisations have to be representative of interests substantially affected—that means consumer as well as industrial interests Bodies such as Age Concern, for example, were consulted, as were the main processors and those involved in distribution.

Not only must consultations take place and be reasonably exhaustive, but there is a duty for them to be considered. In fairness to the previous administration it should be said that because the consultations revealed dissatisfaction on the part of certain consumer interests at the lower ranges then provided in the initial draft order, that is, the lower ranges of sizes for prescribed packages, the initial order was withdrawn. After consultations with the trade, not only this order but the EEC Directive, were altered to take account of the issues raised by Age Concern and other bodies on behalf of consumers. The request was for a size below 250 grammes, which was in the original order and in the original EEC Directive. The trade accepted the requirement and the metric scale now goes down to 125 grammes, as prescribed in the order and in the EEC Directive.

JPEG 5 The order is necessary because, of course, we have to meet an EEC Directive, but we had to change the law relating to the sale of sugar in this country by 1st January 1975 so that sales of the pack sizes could be permitted here by 1st January 1976.

Mr. John Gorst
: Does the Minister now feel that the various bodies to whom he referred have been satisfied by the alterations? Have the criticisms now been met to their satisfaction?

Mr. Williams
: To the best of my knowledge, that is so. If it were not, I should certainly have tried to accommodate them. I understand that the proposed order will meet the requirements.

Mrs. Oppenheim
: Will the hon. Gentleman make it clear whether the date of 1st January 1975 is in relation to the EEC Directive or to the order?

Mr. Williams
: The requirement under the EEC Directive is that member States amend their legal requirements by 1st January 1975 to enable the sale of the prescribed packs by 1st January 1976, which is what the order achieves. Therefore, the operative date of the order is 1st January 1976. We actually put it at 1st October, for technical reasons connected with the time of the sugar crop, to help the industry. The industry would have liked an even earlier date, but we did not feel that that would be a practical course.

A further reason why the order is necessary is that the industry itself is eager for the change—[ Interruption.] The hon. Member for Weston-super-Mare (Mr. Wiggin) may make sedentary interruptions as he wishes. The fact remains that for four years the industry itself and the Confederation of British Industry have been saying that they wanted the facility for metrification in respect of sugar.

I can only go on the advice that the Government have received in the consultations undertaken by my Department, under both this and the previous administration. The industry needs time in which to order plant, to instal it and actually to produce a stock of metric packs in time to meet the directed requirements.

The Government take the view that it would be better for the consumer if only 6 one type of measure were used, and we hope, therefore, that from 1st January 1976 supplies—domestically provided and those imported—will be in metric measure. It would be confusing to the housewife if there were prolonged periods in which the two types of pack were side by side on the shelves, because they are not identical in size.

As hon. Members will be aware from their studies of Imperial and metric measures, the precise equivalent, ignoring decimal points, of the 1-lb. pack would be a 453 gramme pack. If we want to allow the housewife to make choice comparisons, we want relationships that are straightforward in the quantity on sale. Instead of switching to a 453 gramme absolute equivalent of the 1-lb. pack, it seemed logical to switch to the 500 gramme pack so that direct and clear arithmetical relationships between the 500 and the 1,000 pack, or down the other way, as outlined in the order, could be achieved.

The Secretary of State has asked for assurances from the industry that as the packs will be larger, no advantage will be taken when switching to larger packs to charge a higher unit price for sugar. We have had assurances from the trade that as the packs hold 10 per cent. more, it will be the industry's intention to alter the price only by that 10 per cent. On the present price scale, therefore, the 1-lb. pack new equivalent, that is, the 1 kilogram, which is 10 per cent. larger, would cost one penny more.

Mr. Milne

Mr. Williams
: The hon. Gentleman cannot have a record of the proceedings while the proceedings are still going on; there is a degree of spontaneity which is not practically possible.

The hon. Gentleman will bear in mind the representations that we have had from consumer groups about metrication generally, as well as in relation to sugar, suggesting that it would be confusing to the housewife if one had both packages side by side. Although there is a difference, the difference is so small, 10 per cent., that housewives could be confused about the comparative prices and deciding which was the better buy.

Therefore, our intention is that eventually only one system of measurement will be used. This is inevitably because the industry, at its own wish, will be replacing or modifying existing plant so that it will produce for metric quantification. The Joint Committee on Statutory Instruments made a series of points and, in fairness to the Committee, we should deal with each.

First, the Committee rejected certain words in Article 3 of the order:

"Sugar which is not pre-packed shall be sold by retail only by net weight."

as being ambiguous.

This we find difficult to accept, because none of the Departmental records—going back to the original use of these words— 8 suggests that there has been any confusion in the past. Our key standard work of reference on the 1963 Act does not give any evidence or in any way suggest that there has been any difficulty of interpretation. These words were not commented on by any of the 70-plus organisations that took part in consultations.

The words appear in several places in the parent Act and have caused no difficulty there. They were used by the previous administration when the pasta and salt orders were produced. Hon. Members will accept that the then Minister for Consumer Affairs is a man whose knowledge of technical law is widely respected on both sides of the House. The order which he presented for consultation and for acceptance by the House contained the same words.

If he now suggests that we should depart from precedent, we are in danger of throwing doubts on the validity of all previous legislation in which these words were used. As there has been no evidence whatsoever of confusion arising from those words, we feel that the Select Committee's contention is not substantiated by evidence.

Secondly, the Committee said that it could not see the practical effect of a phrase in Article 4—

"made up in or on a container for sale".

Section 58(1) of the 1963 Act defines a container and makes clear that the container may wholly or partly enclose goods and that "pre-packed" is made up in advance for retail sale "in or on a container". Therefore, in the event of containers not necessarily being fully enclosed in future, it is logical that we cover such a situation. As a container does not have to be a completely enveloping pack, we are allowing in the wording, as in the original Act, for the fact that someone may say that there is a loophole because they are only putting it on the container, not in a container. Therefore, this provision merely ensures that no loophole exists.

Mr. Williams
: I would ask the hon. Lady what she thinks we should do. The packaging industry has changed enormously within a short time. Would she want us, in the event of the sort of changes she envisages in the packaging of goods other than sugar, to leave what we consider to be a possible loophole by which consumers would be deprived of the protection of prescribed quantities? I am sure that she will accept that it is right that we should make this provision as totally encompassing as possible.

It has been suggested that an inadvertent penalty could arise from the fact that the lowest Imperial weight is 12 ozs whereas on the scale the lowest metric weight is 125 grammes, namely, about 5lb. In discussions with the trade it emerged that in recent times the smallest pack of sugar that has been made—this was for speciality sugars—was 12 ozs. No. further packs of this size are being made not even for speciality sugars.

Therefore, as the industry is about to go metric, and as imports will be in metric quantities, it seemed to us in meeting representations by those concerned that if a demand for small packages existed we should ensure that it was met. We felt that this point was fully covered by altering the metric scale. As from 1976 supplies will be in metric. We should therefore take the scales down to 125 grammes, namely, about ¼lb.

A further request was that there should be rough equivalents. In the explanatory memorandum, the Select Committee did not ask for precise equivalents. It wanted rough equivalents of the upper and lower limits of the order as set out in Article 5, namely, a 5 kilograms and a 100 kilograms measure. It also wanted a rough equivalent of the lower limit for marking quantities in containers, namely, 50 grammes.

The suggestion that the equivalents should be rough rather than precise was sensible. To give precise equivalents would have necessitated going into multiple decimal points and would have been virtually incomprehensible to the average member of the general public. Therefore, 10 the rough equivalent seemed to be the better way of meeting the communication need that it was felt the explanatory memorandum in its original form did not meet.

We have re-written the explanatory memorandum. The new wording is laid down in one of the papers that we have distributed to hon. Members. Five kilogrammes appears as approximately 11 lbs. and 100 grammes appears as approximately 3½ozs., and 50 grammes appear as 1¾ozs.

In addition, therefore, to the rough equivalents to which I have referred, the precise words requested by the Statutory Instruments Committee have been inserted, namely,

"…and these provisions shall be replaced by this order."

It is to be hoped that the Committee will agree that it was right that the Department cancelled the original meeting of this Committee so as to take into account the representations made by the Statutory Instruments Committee, and that the Government have tried, where possible, to meet reasonably points that were, in themselves, reasonable.
